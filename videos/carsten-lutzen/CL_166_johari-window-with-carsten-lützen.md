# "Johari Window" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten revisits the Johari Window model — a four-quadrant framework describing what is known and unknown to self and others — and reframes it as a dynamic tool rather than a fixed state. He explores how feedback, coaching, and intentional disclosure can actively expand the "arena" quadrant, helping individuals and teams develop greater self-awareness and mutual understanding.

## Methods & Techniques

### The Johari Window Model (ab 00:30)
- **What:** A two-axis, four-quadrant model mapping what is known/unknown to self against what is known/unknown to others, producing four zones: Arena, Façade, Blind Spot, and Unknown.
- **When to use:** Self-awareness conversations, coaching sessions, feedback workshops, or any context where you want to help someone reflect on their own visibility and growth edges.
- **Group size:** Works in 1:1 coaching or group settings.
- **Duration:** Can be introduced in minutes; reflection and application take longer.
- **How it works:**
  1. Draw two axes: "Known to self / Not known to self" and "Known to others / Not known to others."
  2. Identify the four quadrants: **Arena** (known to self + known to others), **Façade** (known to self, hidden from others), **Blind Spot** (unknown to self, visible to others), **Unknown** (unknown to all).
  3. Explore what would shift each boundary and what a larger Arena would enable.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=C28imCqzXhE&t=30

### Expanding the Arena Through Feedback (ab 02:30)
- **What:** Using feedback from others to shrink the Blind Spot — making things known to self that were previously only known to others — thereby enlarging the Arena.
- **When to use:** Feedback sessions, retrospectives, "I see you at your best when…" formats.
- **How it works:** When someone receives feedback about a strength or behaviour they weren't aware of, the boundary between Blind Spot and Arena shifts. The Arena grows, and both the individual and their environment have a more complete picture.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=C28imCqzXhE&t=150

### Expanding the Arena Through Disclosure (ab 03:30)
- **What:** Consciously sharing something from the Façade — a secret, a vulnerability, or something previously hidden — to push the boundary down and enlarge the Arena.
- **When to use:** Trust-building moments, intimacy-creating exercises, or contexts where psychological safety is high enough to support vulnerability.
- **How it works:** Sharing something meaningful (a fear, a personal struggle, a value) moves it from Façade into Arena. Carsten cautions to be intentional — not everything needs to be disclosed, and context matters.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=C28imCqzXhE&t=210

### Coaching as an Arena-Expanding Tool (ab 04:15)
- **What:** Using coaching questions and perspective shifts to help a coachee surface insights from their Unknown or Blind Spot into the Arena.
- **When to use:** 1:1 coaching conversations focused on self-reflection and growth.
- **How it works:** Carsten suggests prompts like "What would [someone else] say about this?" to shift perspective and surface blind spots. Coaching agreements (Vegas rules) protect what moves from Façade — nothing from the session is disclosed externally.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=C28imCqzXhE&t=255

## Key Principles
- The Johari Window is not a fixed snapshot — it is a dynamic model that can be actively shaped through feedback, disclosure, and coaching.
- A larger Arena is always better: the more that is mutually known, the more effective collaboration and self-understanding become.
- The Unknown quadrant (not known to self or others) should be approached with care — it may involve suppressed experiences and is not territory for untrained practitioners.
- Transparency is generally valuable, but disclosure should be intentional and context-sensitive — not everything needs to be shared.
- Feedback is a two-way mechanism: receiving it shrinks the Blind Spot; giving it shrinks the Façade of others.

## Facilitation Tips
- When introducing the model, anchor each quadrant with a concrete, low-stakes example (e.g., "I know I'm wearing a green t-shirt, and so do you — that's the Arena") before moving to more personal territory.
- Use the "I see you at your best when…" format as an entry point for feedback that targets the Blind Spot specifically.
- In coaching, use perspective-shift questions ("What would your manager say about this?") to help coachees access their own Blind Spot without confrontation.
- Always establish a coaching agreement before moving into Façade territory — confidentiality creates the safety needed for disclosure.
- Avoid actively probing the Unknown quadrant unless you are trained for it; acknowledge it exists and move on.

## Best Used When
- Running feedback workshops or retrospectives where you want a conceptual frame for why feedback matters.
- Coaching individuals who are stuck or unaware of how they are perceived.
- Building psychological safety on a team by helping members understand the value of transparency.
- Onboarding new coaches or Scrum Masters to the role of self-awareness in facilitation.
