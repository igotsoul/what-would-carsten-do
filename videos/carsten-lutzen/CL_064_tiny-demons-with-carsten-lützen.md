# "Tiny Demons" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten Lützen introduces "Tiny Demons," a Liberating Structures exercise in development (shared with him by Barry from The Liberators) that uses drawing and metaphor to help participants surface and humanize their fears or anxieties around a specific topic. By transforming written fears into illustrated monsters — and then reimagining those monsters in a playful, party setting — participants make their concerns more approachable and easier to discuss. It works well as a warm-up or opening exercise before deeper reflection or problem-solving activities.

## Methods & Techniques

### Tiny Demons (ab 00:30)
- **What:** A creative warm-up exercise where participants write down fears or anxieties, draw them as monsters, then redraw their chosen monster in a fun/party setting to shift perspective and invite conversation.
- **When to use:** As an opening exercise to surface concerns about a product, professional development topic, or any team challenge — particularly useful before deeper reflection exercises like What/So What/Now What or 5 Whys.
- **Group size:** Not specified; works individually then in pairs.
- **Duration:** Approximately 15–20 minutes total (2–3 min writing, 3–4 min drawing monsters, 4–5 min party redraw, plus pair sharing).
- **How it works:**
  1. **Write fears (2–3 min, individual, silent):** Ask participants to list at least four specific fears, anxieties, or concerns related to a chosen topic (e.g., the product they're building, their professional growth). Four is a target but fewer is fine.
  2. **Create a monster template (1 min):** Take a new piece of paper and divide it into four quadrants using two lines. In each quadrant, draw a simple starter shape: top-left = squiggly line, top-right = closed shape (triangle, square, etc.), bottom-left = a line, bottom-right = any free-form shape.
  3. **Turn shapes into monsters (3–4 min):** Add eyes, fangs, horns, teeth, arms — whatever makes each shape feel like a scary monster. The drawing quality doesn't matter; playfulness is the point.
  4. **Name the monsters (1–2 min):** Assign one fear to each monster, giving it a name (e.g., "The Monster of Broken Builds").
  5. **Redraw one monster at a party (4–5 min):** Pick the monster they most want to get to know or understand better. Redraw it on a fresh piece of paper, but now it's at a party — dancing, holding a drink, having fun. The monster is still itself, but in a positive, approachable context.
  6. **Pair share:** Exchange the party monster drawing with a partner and briefly explain: which monster is it, what does it represent, and what's the underlying concern behind it.

## Key Principles
- Externalizing fears through drawing creates psychological distance, making them easier to name and discuss.
- Giving a fear a silly or monstrous visual form reduces its emotional charge — it becomes something you can look at, rather than something that looms over you.
- Reimagining the monster in a party setting shifts the relationship with the fear from avoidance to curiosity ("getting to know it better").
- Carsten notes a useful facilitation cue: watch for when people lift their pen from the paper as a signal to move on, rather than relying on a fixed timer.
- The exercise pairs naturally with deeper reflection structures (What/So What/Now What, 5 Whys) as an opener that warms up honest conversation.

## Facilitation Tips
- Invite participants to write "at least four" fears but normalize writing fewer — the goal is engagement, not compliance.
- When giving drawing instructions, keep the starter shapes simple and non-prescriptive; Carsten emphasizes "it's not that important" what exact shape people draw.
- Use the pen-lift signal (when participants finish drawing) rather than a rigid timer to pace transitions naturally.
- The party redraw step is key — it's where the emotional reframing happens. Give it enough time (4–5 minutes) and encourage genuine creative play.
- The pair share doesn't need to be long; a brief exchange of "what is this monster and what's underneath it" is sufficient to open the door for further dialogue.

## Best Used When
- Opening a session where a team needs to surface concerns, blockers, or anxieties before diving into problem-solving.
- Working with topics that carry emotional weight (e.g., product direction, team change, skill gaps) where direct conversation might feel threatening.
- You want a creative, low-barrier warm-up that also generates real substance for follow-on exercises.
- Halloween — Carsten specifically approves.
