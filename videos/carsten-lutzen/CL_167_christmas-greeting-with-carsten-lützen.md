# "Christmas Greeting" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten shares a simple but high-impact end-of-year practice: writing personalized, handwritten letters to each team member. Inspired by receiving one himself from a product owner, he explains what makes these letters meaningful and encourages others to adopt the tradition.

## Methods & Techniques

### Personalized Handwritten Year-End Letters (ab 00:20)
- **What:** Writing individual, handwritten letters to each team member at the end of the year — not a template, but unique content per person.
- **When to use:** End of year as a closing ritual; any moment where you want to acknowledge individuals in a deeply personal way.
- **Group size:** Individual (one letter per team member); Carsten wrote 22+ letters across his teams.
- **Duration:** A couple of hours total for all letters; manageable despite sounding time-intensive.
- **How it works:**
  1. Reflect on each team member individually — what stood out about them this year.
  2. Write a short handwritten letter (not typed) with unique, personal content.
  3. Include: highlights from the past year, how you worked together, personal achievements, fun anecdotes, and positive expectations for the year ahead.
  4. Keep the tone positive and encouraging throughout.
  5. Send or deliver before the end of year.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=jUjnEeovNcA&t=20

## Key Principles
- Handwritten beats typed — the physical, personal nature of a handwritten letter carries emotional weight that digital communication cannot replicate.
- Unique content per person signals genuine attention; a template undermines the whole point.
- Personalized recognition creates energy and positive atmosphere in teams — Carsten notes "you can't buy this."
- If you struggle to find things to write about a team member, treat that as a signal: you may be too distant from that person or the team.
- The practice is reciprocal in value — it benefits the giver as much as the receiver through the act of reflection.

## Facilitation Tips
- Use past-year highlights, shared anecdotes, individual achievements, and forward-looking encouragement as your four content buckets per letter.
- Don't overthink length — a few sincere, specific words matter more than volume.
- If you're stuck on what to write for someone, that's diagnostic information worth sitting with.

## Best Used When
- End of year or end of a major project cycle.
- When you want to close a chapter with intentionality and warmth.
- When you want to strengthen individual relationships within a team without a group format.
- As a complement to team retrospectives or celebrations — this is the private, one-to-one counterpart.
