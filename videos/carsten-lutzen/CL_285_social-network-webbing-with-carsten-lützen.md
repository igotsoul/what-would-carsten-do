# "Social Network Webbing" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten introduces Social Network Webbing, a Liberating Structure for visually mapping a team's network of collaborators and stakeholders. The exercise helps teams identify who they work with, who can boost or hinder their productivity, and what existing relationship paths they can leverage to expand their influence and access.

## Methods & Techniques

### Social Network Webbing (ab 00:30)
- **What:** A Liberating Structure in which a team creates a visual "spider web" on a flip chart or virtual board showing all the people they work with, their relative distances from the core team, and the connections between them.
- **When to use:** When a team wants to make their stakeholder and collaboration network explicit — especially useful at the start of a project or when trying to understand how to gain influence or access new contacts.
- **Group size:** Works for a team (small group); pairs and groups of four are used during the exercise.
- **Duration:** Not specified explicitly, but the 1-2-4-All structure suggests 20–40 minutes depending on team size.
- **How it works:**
  1. Each team member writes their name on a sticky note and places it in the center of a large paper or virtual board — this is the "core group."
  2. Individually and in silence, each person writes down people they work with regularly (stakeholders, collaborators, anyone with a stake in the project).
  3. Pairs compare their lists: find overlaps, add missing people, then pairs merge into groups of four and refine again (1-2-4-All structure).
  4. Place all identified people on the map, using **distance from the center** to indicate how closely the team collaborates with them — close collaborators near the center, infrequent contacts further out.
  5. Individually and in silence, reflect: who can boost our productivity? Who can hinder it? Who would we like to get closer to?
  6. Repeat the 1-2-4-All process for this reflection, then share and cluster insights.
  7. Draw connections between people on the map, using visual cues (e.g., color or line style) to indicate whether connections are boosting or hindering performance.
  8. Use the mapped connections to identify existing relationship paths that can be leveraged to reach new or hard-to-access contacts — rather than building relationships from scratch.

## Key Principles
- Making the network **explicit and visible** is the core value — most teams have an implicit network they've never mapped together.
- **Distance as data:** the physical placement of names on the map communicates relationship closeness more intuitively than any list.
- **Leverage existing paths:** before trying to influence or reach someone new, look for who in your current network already knows them. Use those bridges.
- The map is **not static** — it will be partially outdated the moment it's created. Plan to update it regularly.
- Color coding by team or function allows at-a-glance pattern recognition when standing back from the map. Carsten emphasizes this as "super super nifty."
- The exercise surfaces both opportunities (people to get closer to) and risks (people who may hinder the work).

## Facilitation Tips
- Use **1-2-4-All** as the core participation structure throughout — it ensures individual reflection before group convergence and prevents groupthink.
- Keep the **core group's sticky notes in one consistent color**, and use a distinct color per external team or function. This makes the web readable from a distance.
- Place the finished map **somewhere visible** so it remains a living reference rather than a one-time workshop artifact.
- Carsten recommends the article by Christian from The Liberators (on Medium) for deeper facilitation guidance on this structure.
- In virtual settings, a large virtual board (e.g., Miro or Mural) can replace the physical paper roll or flip chart.

## Best Used When
- A team is starting a new project and needs to understand their stakeholder landscape.
- The team feels stuck or blocked and wants to identify who might be hindering progress or who they need better access to.
- There's a need to build shared awareness across the team of who knows whom.
- You want to help a team move from reactive relationship-building to deliberate network strategy.
