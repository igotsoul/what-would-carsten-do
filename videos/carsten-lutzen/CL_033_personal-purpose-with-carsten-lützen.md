# "Personal Purpose" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten Lützen explores the concept of personal purpose — what it is, what it isn't, and why it matters for coaches, Scrum Masters, and knowledge workers. He argues that purpose is not a career goal or job title but a compass that guides direction, helps with prioritization, sustains motivation, and builds resilience. He also shares practical tools for discovering and evolving your own purpose.

## Methods & Techniques

### Ikigai (Western Adaptation) (ab 03:40)
- **What:** A self-reflection framework for finding the overlap between what you love, what you're good at, what the world needs, and what you can be paid for — adapted for a Western coaching context.
- **When to use:** When an individual wants to explore or articulate their personal purpose in a structured way.
- **Group size:** Individual or 1:1 coaching
- **Duration:** Varies; typically used as a reflective exercise over multiple sessions
- **How it works:** Carsten refers to a separate video he made on the western adaptation of Ikigai and recommends it as a starting point for purpose discovery.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=j48hUBrrvgk&t=220

### Coaching Conversations for Purpose Discovery (ab 03:50)
- **What:** Using structured coaching conversations to help an individual surface and articulate their personal purpose.
- **When to use:** When someone feels reactive, unmotivated, or disconnected from their work — or is proactively seeking clarity on direction.
- **Group size:** 1:1 (coach + individual)
- **Duration:** Multiple sessions typically needed
- **How it works:** A coach asks questions to help the individual uncover what already exists inside them. Carsten is explicit that a coach cannot install or give someone a purpose — the coach can only help the person realize it themselves.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=j48hUBrrvgk&t=230

### Liberating Structures — "What, So What, Now What" (W³) for Purpose (ab 04:10)
- **What:** Using the Liberating Structures practice W³ (or similar formats) to help individuals or groups dive deeper into personal purpose through structured reflection and dialogue.
- **When to use:** In team or group settings where exploring individual purpose collectively can strengthen psychological safety and mutual understanding.
- **Group size:** Small groups or pairs; can scale
- **Duration:** 20–45 minutes depending on depth
- **How it works:** Carsten mentions Liberating Structures as a tool for ongoing purpose exploration without specifying a single structure, pointing to the broader LS repertoire as a resource.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=j48hUBrrvgk&t=250

## Key Principles
- Purpose is a **direction, not a destination** — think compass, not map.
- Personal purpose is **not fixed**; it evolves over time, and that is healthy and expected.
- Purpose is **not** a career goal, mission statement, productivity tool, or job title.
- Signs you may be disconnected from your purpose: always feeling reactive, saying yes to everything, difficulty prioritizing, or experiencing burnout.
- Purpose supports three practical outcomes: **prioritization** (does this align with my purpose?), **motivation** (am I energized by this?), and **resilience** (does this help me push through difficulty?).
- Passion is related to purpose but carries risk — it can lead to over-investment of time and energy. Purpose helps balance that.
- A coach can help you **uncover** your purpose; they cannot give it to you.
- Carsten's own purpose shifted from "I want to work on things that are actually used" (as a developer) to "I want to help teams and individuals bring their authentic, full self to work and grow a human-friendly work environment."

## Facilitation Tips
- When working with individuals on purpose, explicitly frame it early: purpose is not a goal or title — this reframe reduces resistance and confusion.
- Use open, curious questions rather than prescriptive exercises. The goal is to help the person surface what they already know, not to construct an answer for them.
- Normalize that purpose changes — this reduces the pressure people feel to "get it right" and encourages honest reflection.
- Watch for burnout or reactive behavior as signals that a purpose conversation may be valuable and timely.

## Best Used When
- Coaching an individual who feels stuck, reactive, or disengaged
- Preparing someone for a role change or career transition
- Running a team workshop on values, identity, or working agreements where individual purpose feeds collective direction
- Supporting someone experiencing or recovering from burnout
