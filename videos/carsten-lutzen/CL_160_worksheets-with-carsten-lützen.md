# "Worksheets" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten shares a simple but effective facilitation technique: handing participants a printed worksheet (A3 or A4) instead of relying on laptops or tablets during workshops. The idea is to reduce digital distractions, keep participants focused, and create a reusable physical artifact that integrates naturally with sticky-note-based methods like Liberating Structures.

## Methods & Techniques

### Printed Worksheets for Workshop Focus (ab 00:30)
- **What:** A pre-designed paper sheet (A3 or A4) given to each participant, containing just enough structure for the workshop task — no more, no less.
- **When to use:** When facilitating deep-dive sessions such as user interviews, team goal exploration, or stakeholder mapping where digital tools create distraction risk.
- **Group size:** Any; each participant receives their own sheet.
- **Duration:** Fits within any workshop segment; reusable across multiple interview rounds.
- **How it works:**
  1. Design a minimal worksheet — title, a name field, and a grid or simple structure relevant to the task.
  2. Print on A3 or A4 paper and distribute to all participants.
  3. Participants use sticky notes directly on the sheet (e.g., to capture pain points, sentiments, or observations).
  4. At the end, transfer sticky notes to a shared flip chart or wall to identify patterns across participants.
  5. The blank worksheet can be reused for subsequent interviews since sticky notes are removed each time.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=wFxQl_lEh-k&t=30

### Observations vs. Solutions Column (ab 02:30)
- **What:** Adding a dedicated "notes" or "potential solutions" column on the worksheet to capture solution ideas that pop up during listening, so they don't hijack attention.
- **When to use:** During user interviews or pain-point discovery sessions where participants tend to slip into solution mode while listening.
- **How it works:**
  1. Include a separate column on the worksheet labeled "potential solutions" or "notes."
  2. When a solution idea surfaces, participants write it down immediately and let it go.
  3. This clears mental bandwidth so they can return to active listening.
  4. Observations and solutions remain decoupled — observations drive the analysis, solutions are parked for later.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=wFxQl_lEh-k&t=150

## Key Principles
- Simpler is better — a worksheet does not need to be sophisticated; minimal structure is enough to serve its purpose.
- Physical artifacts reduce digital distraction: no chat notifications, no Reddit, no context switching.
- Worksheets create unbiased individual input — participants cannot see each other's work, preserving independence before a group synthesis.
- Decoupling observations from potential solutions protects listening quality and keeps discovery honest.
- Worksheets are a means to an end, not an end in themselves — the goal is participant focus and quality output.

## Facilitation Tips
- Keep worksheets reusable by having participants use sticky notes rather than writing directly on the paper.
- Combine with Liberating Structures (e.g., 1-2-4-All) to scale individual worksheet outputs into group insights on a shared flip chart.
- Carsten recommends A3 as the preferred format — large enough to hold a grid and sticky notes comfortably.
- Design the worksheet after you know the method, not before — let the activity structure dictate what fields are needed.

## Best Used When
- Running multiple user interviews in parallel and wanting to synthesize patterns afterward.
- Participants are likely to be distracted by devices (remote teams, open-plan offices).
- You want to protect independent thinking before group discussion.
- Working with Liberating Structures or any sticky-note-based method that benefits from individual pre-work.
