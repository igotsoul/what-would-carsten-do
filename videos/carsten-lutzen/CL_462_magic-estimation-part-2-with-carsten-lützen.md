# Magic Estimation Part 2 with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten Lützen revisits his Magic Estimation technique and extends it beyond estimation into **backlog cleanup**. He recaps the original silent Fibonacci-based estimation mechanic, then shows how the same turn-based, silent sorting format can be repurposed to triage an entire backlog — categorizing items as still valid, needing refinement, duplicates, already done, or no longer needed. He reports cleaning up ~350 items in under 45 minutes using this approach.

## Methods & Techniques

### Magic Estimation Recap (ab 00:30)
- **What:** Silent, turn-based estimation using Fibonacci scale (1, 2, 3, 5, 8, 13, 20). Team members take turns placing or moving backlog items. No talking.
- **Baseline:** Pick one mid-sized item as a reference point before starting.
- **Oscillation rule:** If an item keeps bouncing between two values, call a 5-minute timeout to discuss the difference in understanding — often reveals missing requirements or already-done work.
- **Stability:** The round ends when every team member's turn results in no action — the board is stable.
- See [Magic Estimation Part 1](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=8SjyyQ8o4G4) for the full original technique.

### Magic Estimation for Backlog Cleanup (ab 04:15)
- **What:** The same silent, turn-based mechanic applied to backlog triage instead of estimation. Team members sort printed backlog items into categorization columns.
- **When to use:** When your backlog has grown stale, is full of unknowns, duplicates, or items of uncertain relevance. Carsten specifically mentions backlogs where the average age is growing and nobody has a complete overview.
- **Group size:** Whole Scrum team
- **Duration:** 30–45 minutes for ~350 items
- **How it works:**
  1. Print out the entire backlog (one item per card/post-it).
  2. Set up categorization columns instead of Fibonacci numbers:
     - **Backlog** — item is still valid, stays in the backlog
     - **Needs refinement** — missing info, needs to go back to PO/PM or a developer for clarification
     - **In progress** — we've actually already started on this
     - **Duplicate** — seen this before; check if any info should be merged, then close
     - **Not needed** — ship has sailed, no longer relevant; close with comment
     - **Already done** — this was completed but never closed; close it
     - **No clue** — the description is so vague (e.g., a single sentence) that nobody understands what was meant; set aside for author clarification
  3. Team members line up and take turns, same as Magic Estimation: pick one card from the pile, read it silently, place it in the appropriate column.
  4. Continue until the pile is empty.
  5. Process the results: close duplicates and done items, send "needs refinement" items back, archive "not needed" items.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=3nZKZ2ogUCg&t=255

## Key Principles
- Reuses a mechanic the team already knows (Magic Estimation) for a different purpose — lowers facilitation overhead.
- The silent, turn-based format keeps it fast and prevents endless debates about individual items.
- Carsten is candid that he's "not necessarily a big fan of estimation" when used for velocity planning — he values the technique for alignment and shared understanding, not for generating numbers.
- The categorization columns surface systemic backlog hygiene problems (lots of duplicates = intake process issue; lots of "no clue" = story writing quality issue).

## Facilitation Tips
- Works best in the same room with physical cards; virtual whiteboards work but lose some energy.
- For remote/virtual sessions: clearly define turn order and use a visual indicator for whose turn it is.
- If it's the team's first time, use an arrow or pointer to help people track whose turn it is.
- The speed comes from silence — don't let it devolve into discussion per item.
- After the session, the distribution across columns is itself diagnostic: lots of duplicates or "not needed" items tells you something about your intake and grooming process.

## Best Used When
- The backlog has grown unwieldy and nobody has full overview.
- Average age of backlog items is increasing.
- The team suspects there are many duplicates, stale items, or already-completed work lurking in the backlog.
- You want to clean up fast without spending hours debating each item individually.
- The team already knows Magic Estimation — the familiar mechanic makes this easy to introduce.
