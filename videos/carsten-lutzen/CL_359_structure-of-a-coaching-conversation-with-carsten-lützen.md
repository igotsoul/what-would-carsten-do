# "Structure of a Coaching Conversation" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten walks through a backbone structure for coaching conversations, applicable to 1-on-1 coaching, group coaching, and retrospective settings. The structure guides coaches and facilitators from establishing a shared agreement on outcomes, through defining a compelling preferred future, to identifying concrete first steps grounded in existing resources. It draws on solution-focused approaches and can be adapted for team workshops and working agreement sessions.

## Methods & Techniques

### Coaching Agreement (ab 00:45)
- **What:** An explicit upfront alignment on what a good session outcome looks like, including confidentiality ground rules.
- **When to use:** At the start of any coaching conversation or facilitated session to create psychological safety and shared purpose.
- **Group size:** 1-on-1 or group settings
- **Duration:** 5–10 minutes
- **How it works:**
  1. Ask: "If this session is over in an hour and it was really good — what would you say was the best part?"
  2. Have participants reflect and write down their answer.
  3. Use responses to agree on what "good" looks like for this session.
  4. State the Vegas Rule: everything discussed stays in the session (coach will not share content externally).
  5. Always ask for permission before proceeding.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=8i9zQwOmYPg&t=45

### Preferred Future (ab 01:50)
- **What:** A solution-focused exploration of what success looks, feels, and sounds like — investigated before jumping to actions.
- **When to use:** After the coaching agreement; when the coachee or team needs a compelling vision to move toward rather than just fixing current pain.
- **Group size:** 1-on-1 or group
- **Duration:** 10–20 minutes depending on depth
- **How it works:**
  1. Ask: "What does the preferred future look like? What does it feel like? What are the signs?"
  2. Be curious — ask follow-up questions to enrich the picture.
  3. Reframe the perspective: include stakeholder viewpoints (partner, user, CEO) to make the future compelling and multi-dimensional.
  4. Only move forward once the preferred future is clearly defined — you cannot navigate toward an undefined destination.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=8i9zQwOmYPg&t=110

### First Signs of Progress (ab 02:55)
- **What:** Identifying the smallest observable changes that indicate movement toward the preferred future — distinct from actions or tasks.
- **When to use:** After defining the preferred future; to make progress tangible and coachee-driven.
- **Group size:** 1-on-1 or group
- **Duration:** 10–15 minutes
- **How it works:**
  1. Ask: "What is the first small change you can see or feel in your environment that tells you you're closer to the preferred future?"
  2. Key distinction: these are *signs* (observable indicators), not actions or to-dos.
  3. Ensure each sign is actionable by the coachee themselves.
  4. Carsten suggests using the Liberating Structures **15% Solutions** exercise as a facilitation format for this step.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=8i9zQwOmYPg&t=175

### Resources & Forerunners of the Solution (ab 03:45)
- **What:** Surfacing existing strengths, assets, and past glimpses of the solution that the coachee or team already possesses.
- **When to use:** After identifying first signs of progress; before planning concrete next steps.
- **Group size:** 1-on-1 or group
- **Duration:** 5–10 minutes
- **How it works:**
  1. Ask what resources (skills, relationships, experiences) the coachee/team already has.
  2. Identify *forerunners* — moments where something resembling the solution has already happened, even partially.
  3. Carsten's key insight: "We would not suggest an idea unless we have already seen some version of it working."
  4. Use these forerunners as the foundation to build the path from current state to first signs of progress.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=8i9zQwOmYPg&t=225

## Key Principles
- The structure has two axes: **time** (past / present / future) and the **session itself** as a contained grey box within that timeline.
- Always establish a **Coaching Agreement** before doing any coaching work — clarity on outcomes and confidentiality is non-negotiable.
- Always **ask for permission** before moving to the next phase.
- The **Preferred Future** must be defined before identifying actions — you can't navigate without a destination.
- **First Signs of Progress** are signs, not actions. This distinction keeps the coachee from jumping to solutions before the future is clearly felt.
- **Forerunners** (precursors of the solution) are proof that the desired change is already possible — build on what's already working.
- The structure is solution-focused: it looks forward and builds on strengths rather than analyzing problems.

## Facilitation Tips
- Use the "best part of this session" question as a lightweight, conversational way to establish the coaching agreement without it feeling formal or bureaucratic.
- When exploring the preferred future, actively reframe to include multiple perspectives — don't let the coachee stay stuck in their own viewpoint.
- Be genuinely curious during the preferred future phase; resist the urge to jump to solutions.
- When facilitating groups, "Best Hopes" and "What's In It For Me" (WIIFM) are good exercises for the coaching agreement phase.
- Apply the Vegas Rule explicitly — naming it creates a shared, culturally understood shorthand for confidentiality.
- The 15% Solutions Liberating Structure maps naturally onto the First Signs of Progress step.

## Best Used When
- Starting a 1-on-1 coaching engagement or series of coaching conversations
- Facilitating group coaching sessions or team retrospectives with a coaching intent
- Working on team working agreements where you want the team to own the outcome
- Any session where you want to move from complaint/problem mode to solution/future mode
