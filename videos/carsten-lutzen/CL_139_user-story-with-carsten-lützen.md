# "User Story" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten Lützen provides a concise introduction to user stories — the classic agile format for capturing requirements — and adds practical coaching nuance around how to write them well. He emphasizes that the "so that" value clause is often omitted but is actually the most important part, and that "user" is too generic a role type to be useful.

## Methods & Techniques

### Writing Better User Stories (ab 00:30)
- **What:** A structured format for capturing requirements from the perspective of a specific user: "As a [type of user], I want to [action], so that [value]."
- **When to use:** When defining work items in a product backlog, particularly to anchor developer work to user needs and enable value-based prioritization.
- **Group size:** Typically product owner, team, and stakeholders
- **Duration:** Ongoing — part of backlog refinement
- **How it works:**
  1. Replace the generic "user" with a specific role or persona (e.g., "registered user," "admin," "finance approver")
  2. Define the action the person wants to perform
  3. Always include the "so that" clause — this is where the value is articulated and where the team and product owner can have a meaningful prioritization conversation
  4. Add acceptance criteria to define when the story is done and how the persona will recognize value
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=-uhya_PHMvg&t=30

### Using the "So That" Clause for Prioritization (ab 02:00)
- **What:** The value clause of the user story format is the lever for backlog prioritization. Carsten frames it as the key conversation starter between the product owner, team, and stakeholders.
- **When to use:** During backlog refinement and sprint planning when the team needs to decide what to work on next.
- **How it works:** Once the "so that" value is made explicit, the team can ask: "Is this value big enough to warrant doing this now?" This surfaces trade-offs and enables informed prioritization across a long backlog.

## Key Principles
- The Scrum Guide does not mandate user stories — they are a common choice, not a requirement. Teams should not feel locked in to the format.
- "User" is too broad a role. Always push toward a specific role type or persona to make the story meaningful and testable.
- The "so that [value]" clause is frequently omitted but is the most important part: it enables value conversations between the team and stakeholders.
- Carsten references Alistair Cockburn's framing: "Stories are promises for conversations." A user story is not a complete specification — it is a placeholder that signals a conversation still needs to happen.
- Acceptance criteria are necessary to move from a story to something actionable: they define what "done" looks like from the persona's perspective.

## Facilitation Tips
- When coaching teams on user stories, challenge vague roles immediately — ask who specifically gets out of bed wanting this.
- Use the "so that" clause as a facilitation prompt with stakeholders: make the value explicit before committing to building something.
- Don't let teams skip the value clause even in obviously small stories — the discipline of articulating value builds good habits.

## Best Used When
- Introducing user stories to teams new to agile or Scrum
- Coaching teams who write stories without a value clause or who use "user" as a blanket role
- Backlog refinement sessions where prioritization conversations feel stuck or shallow
