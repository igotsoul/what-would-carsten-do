# "Resource Detective" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten introduces the Resource Detective, a structured trio exercise for practicing and receiving feedback on coaching conversations. Participants rotate through three roles — coach, client, and resource detective — and use a technique called "resource gossip" to surface what worked well. The exercise is designed to build coaching confidence and sharpen conversation skills through positive, strengths-based feedback.

## Methods & Techniques

### Resource Detective (ab 00:45)
- **What:** A trio-based coaching practice exercise where one person coaches, one is coached, and a third silently observes and later facilitates structured positive feedback using "resource gossip."
- **When to use:** When coaching skills practice and peer feedback are desired — suitable for agile coaches, Scrum Masters, or anyone wanting to improve conversation and coaching skills, even without formal coach training.
- **Group size:** 3 per group (coach, client, resource detective); multiple groups can run in parallel.
- **Duration:** ~10 minutes coaching + feedback rounds per rotation; full exercise scales with number of participants.
- **How it works:**
  1. Assign roles: **coach**, **client** (coachee), **resource detective**.
  2. Coach conducts a coaching conversation with the client for ~10 minutes. Resource detective stays silent throughout — no comments, no questions.
  3. **Online:** Resource detective keeps camera off and acts as timekeeper via chat. **In person:** Resource detective sits out of sight of the other two.
  4. During the conversation, the resource detective watches for:
     - Powerful questions or moments that sparked reflection in the client.
     - Resources in the coach (great questions, body language, facilitation moves).
     - Resources in the client (strengths, signs of resilience, capacity to overcome the issue).
  5. After time is up, **first resource gossip round — about the client:**
     - Client turns off camera/steps out of the conversation.
     - Coach and resource detective talk about what they noticed in the client: strengths, signs of capability, what gives confidence the client can handle the challenge.
  6. **Second resource gossip round — about the coach:**
     - Client rejoins; coach turns off camera/steps out.
     - Client and resource detective discuss what the coach did well: pivotal moments, great questions, quality of the coaching agreement, closing, follow-ups.
  7. Coach rejoins. Rotate roles. Repeat until everyone has been in each role.
- **Optional:** The coach can brief the resource detective in advance with a specific focus (e.g., "notice how I open the session") — without the client knowing.

## Key Principles
- **Strengths-based feedback:** The entire structure is built around surfacing what worked — not critiquing what didn't. This creates a safe environment for practice.
- **Resource gossip as a format:** Talking *about* someone while they listen (or are temporarily absent) allows honest, unfiltered observations that are often more impactful than direct feedback.
- **Dual observation:** Both the coach and resource detective independently watch for resources in the client — reinforcing that the client's own strengths are central to the coaching work.
- **Separation of feedback rounds:** Keeping the two gossip rounds separate (one about the client, one about the coach) prevents conflation and gives each person a dedicated moment of positive focus.
- **Focused attention as a coaching tool:** Coaching can be sharpened by pre-assigning a specific observation cue to the resource detective — a light form of deliberate practice.

## Facilitation Tips
- Keep the resource detective truly silent during the coaching conversation — no reactions, no nodding into the chat. Silence protects the authenticity of the session.
- Online: use the chat for timekeeper signals to avoid interrupting the audio. In person: physical separation (resource detective out of sight) helps all three stay in their roles.
- Remind resource detectives to look for *both* coach and client resources during the session — they are observing on two tracks simultaneously.
- The resource gossip rounds work best when they stay appreciative and specific — "when you asked X, the client visibly shifted" is more useful than general praise.
- Carsten credits this exercise to Jesper and Daniel from a coaching course — worth acknowledging the origin if sharing the exercise.

## Best Used When
- A team of Scrum Masters, agile coaches, or facilitators wants to practice coaching skills together.
- You want a safe, low-stakes format for peer coaching with built-in feedback.
- Building confidence in newer coaches — the strengths-only format removes the fear of being criticized.
- Running a coaching skills workshop or training program where participants need repeated practice rounds.
