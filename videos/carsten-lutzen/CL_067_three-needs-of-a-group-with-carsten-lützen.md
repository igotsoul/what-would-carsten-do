# "Three Needs of a Group" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten Lützen introduces a mental model called the "Three Needs of a Group," originally from Krina Kelson's professional agile coaching course. The model identifies three interdependent needs every group has — Process, Content/Subject Matter, and Relations — and clarifies the facilitator's distinct role in serving the group. It also provides a useful diagnostic lens for understanding why a session did or did not work.

## Methods & Techniques

### The Three Needs of a Group (ab 00:30)
- **What:** A mental model that describes three overarching needs present in any group setting: Process, Content/Subject Matter, and Relations. Balancing all three is essential for effective group work.
- **When to use:** As a preparation and reflection framework for facilitators — before designing a session, during facilitation to stay oriented, and after a session to diagnose what went wrong.
- **Group size:** Any group
- **Duration:** Applicable as an ongoing mental model, not a timed exercise
- **How it works:**
  1. **Process** — The group needs a structured process designed to help them reach their goal. It can be highly structured or loose, but some clarity on "what is going to happen" is required.
  2. **Content / Subject Matter** — The topic or problem the group is working on. Crucially, the group owns this — not the facilitator.
  3. **Relations** — How group members interact with each other; whether they feel psychologically safe and included.
  - Each pairing without the third creates a dysfunction:
    - Process + Content without Relations = lone wolves following steps but not truly collaborating
    - Content + Relations without Process = shared goal and trust, but no alignment on how to get there
    - Process + Relations = where the facilitator operates — guiding without owning the content
  - The facilitator's zone is deliberately Process and Relations only. Content remains with the group.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=XRxfHAsnU6w&t=30

### Post-Session Diagnostic with the Three Needs (ab 03:30)
- **What:** Using the Three Needs model to retrospectively analyze why a session underdelivered.
- **When to use:** After a session where results were not achieved or the dynamic felt off.
- **How it works:** Ask which of the three needs was insufficiently met. Was the process unclear? Were relations underdeveloped — did people feel unsafe? Was the subject matter unclear or outside the group's ownership? This helps identify the root cause rather than assigning blame.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=XRxfHAsnU6w&t=210

## Key Principles
- The facilitator is an **impartial steward of process and relations** — not a content expert, not responsible for outcomes.
- The group is fully responsible for the subject matter and the results they achieve.
- A facilitator can guide, coach, and create safety — but cannot be held accountable for whether the group reaches its goal.
- The Three Needs model functions as both a **design lens** (before) and a **diagnostic lens** (after).
- When all three needs are balanced, the session becomes a dance between facilitator and group — each playing their distinct role.

## Facilitation Tips
- Carsten recommends keeping this model "in the back of the head" during facilitation as a real-time orientation tool.
- Deliberately stay out of the content. The facilitator's value lies in holding process and relations — stepping into content undermines the model.
- When a session fails to produce results, resist the impulse to take personal responsibility. Instead, use the three needs to locate where assumptions broke down.
- Create psychological safety and inclusion as part of tending to Relations — this is an active facilitation responsibility, not a given.

## Best Used When
- Preparing a workshop or group session and wanting to ensure all three dimensions are designed for
- Feeling lost during a session and needing a quick mental anchor to reorient
- Debriefing a session that did not meet its goals
- Explaining the facilitator role to stakeholders who expect the facilitator to be accountable for outcomes
- Onboarding someone new to facilitation and helping them understand their scope
