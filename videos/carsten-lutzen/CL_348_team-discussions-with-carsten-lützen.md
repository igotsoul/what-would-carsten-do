# "Team Discussions" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten demonstrates how to use question cards from the Zombie Scrum First Aid Kit (by The Liberators) as a structured team discussion tool. By applying prioritization mechanics — either one-dimensional (importance) or two-dimensional (importance × effort) — teams can surface what matters most and identify hidden impediments. The resulting map becomes a rich conversation starter well beyond the session itself.

## Methods & Techniques

### One-Dimensional Question Card Prioritization (ab 00:45)
- **What:** Teams rank question cards from a specific category (e.g., "Ship It Fast") by relative importance, building a scale collaboratively.
- **When to use:** When a team needs to align on what aspects of their agile practice matter most right now.
- **Group size:** Small to medium teams (implied by team discussion context)
- **Duration:** Not explicitly stated; works as a focused session segment
- **How it works:**
  1. Pick a category (e.g., "Ship It Fast") or draw cards randomly.
  2. Place the first card in the middle as an anchor.
  3. Each subsequent card is placed higher or lower than existing cards.
  4. If the first card turns out to be an extreme, shift the whole scale — no problem.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=QTBMDcjjaIg&t=45s

### Two-Dimensional Importance × Effort Mapping (ab 01:30)
- **What:** Cards are placed on a 2×2 grid with Importance (low→high) on one axis and Effort to improve (low→high) on the other, creating an actionable priority map.
- **When to use:** When a team wants to not only identify what's important but also decide where to invest energy first.
- **Group size:** Small to medium teams
- **Duration:** 20–45 min (implied)
- **How it works:**
  1. Set up axes: Importance (horizontal or vertical) and Effort (the other axis).
  2. Choose a placement mechanic — either:
     - **Magic Estimation style:** Participants place cards individually; the next person may move any card before placing a new one.
     - **Lightning Decision Jam style:** Place card in center, team votes directionally on each axis (lower/higher) one axis at a time.
  3. Continue until all selected cards are placed.
  4. Debrief the resulting map as a facilitator.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=QTBMDcjjaIg&t=90s

## Key Principles
- **High importance, low effort = start here.** The classic prioritization rule applies: focus first on what matters most and costs least.
- **High importance, high effort = impediment investigation.** Cards in this quadrant are conversation starters: what is blocking us from making this easier?
- **Low perceived importance can reveal team health issues.** If "we help each other when someone is stuck" scores low on importance, it may signal the group is a collection of specialists rather than a real team.
- **The debrief is where the real value is.** The map itself is less important than the conversations it surfaces — both in-session and in a structured debrief afterwards.
- **Relative ranking is more honest than absolute scoring.** Comparing cards to each other forces clearer thinking than rating each in isolation.

## Facilitation Tips
- Use the Zombie Scrum First Aid Kit question cards as ready-made prompts — they span four categories: Self-Organize, Ship It Fast, Improve Continuously, and Build What Stakeholders Need.
- You can focus the session on one category or mix across all four depending on what the team needs to explore.
- Watch for cards that cluster in unexpected quadrants — those are your coaching opportunities.
- If a team rates "helping each other when stuck" as low importance, explore whether this is a team-formation issue before moving to content.
- If "working with management to ship faster" lands high importance / high effort, use it as an entry point to discuss specific impediments with the team.

## Best Used When
- A team is showing signs of Zombie Scrum (going through the motions without real agility).
- You want a structured but non-threatening way to surface what's working and what isn't.
- A retrospective or team health check needs a fresh format beyond the usual formats.
- You want to connect day-to-day practices back to underlying agile principles.
