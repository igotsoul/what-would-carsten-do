# Positive Retrospective with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten presents a lightweight retrospective format inspired by Alistair Cockburn's two-column Keep/Try approach, then extends it with a third space dedicated to peer appreciation and team-level recognition. The format is designed to be simple enough to internalize quickly while keeping a positive, solution-focused energy throughout.

## Methods & Techniques

### Keep / Try / Appreciate Retrospective (ab 00:10)
- **What:** A three-column retro board combining Cockburn's minimalist Keep/Try format with an added appreciation column for peer recognition and team shoutouts.
- **When to use:** When teams need a lightweight, positive retro format that surfaces both process improvements and interpersonal dynamics. Especially useful for teams that tend to focus only on problems or struggle with psychological safety.
- **Group size:** Any team size; designed for standard Scrum teams.
- **Duration:** Not specified; format is concise enough for a short timebox.
- **How it works:**
  1. Set up three columns: **Try** (what should we experiment with or stop doing?), **Keep** (what is working well that we should continue?), **Appreciate** (what did a teammate do that helped you or the team?).
  2. Team members write Post-its for each column.
  3. The Appreciate column prompts specific, named peer recognition — e.g., "It was great that Brian helped me unblock my task" or "Carsten's facilitation of the cross-team interaction was really valuable."
  4. Cluster and discuss as usual, but ensure the Appreciate column gets dedicated airtime.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=hl1v-gDXeEc

## Key Principles
- Alistair Cockburn argues retrospectives should be kept as simple as possible — a two-column Keep/Try captures most of what teams need.
- The Try column implicitly covers "stop doing" items — teams can frame these positively as experiments rather than complaints.
- Solution-Focused Coaching aligns naturally with the Keep column: reinforcing what works is as important as identifying what to change.
- Teams are not just individuals working in parallel — explicit attention to how team members support each other strengthens cohesion.
- Peer recognition doesn't happen naturally; creating a dedicated space for it ensures it's not crowded out by process topics.

## Facilitation Tips
- Keep the format lightweight — its strength is in its simplicity. Resist the urge to add more columns.
- Prime the Appreciate column with an example before the exercise starts (e.g., write one yourself) so participants understand what level of specificity is expected.
- Encourage naming individuals in appreciate items — vague appreciation ("the team was great") has less impact than specific recognition.

## Best Used When
- Teams are stuck in a cycle of problem-focused retros and need a reset toward positive reinforcement.
- Team cohesion and mutual support feel weak or underacknowledged.
- You want a quick, low-prep retro format that still covers improvement, continuity, and relationship dimensions.
- Working with teams familiar with solution-focused approaches who want a compatible retro structure.
