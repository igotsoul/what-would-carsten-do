# "Oscars" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten introduces a retrospective format called "Oscars" (also known as Stories Cast or Award Ceremony) that uses the completed Sprint backlog as its raw material. Instead of open brainstorming, the team nominates and votes on backlog items across creative award categories, then dives into structured discussion to extract learnings and improvement actions.

## Methods & Techniques

### Oscars / Award Ceremony Retrospective (ab 00:30)
- **What:** A structured retrospective format where teams nominate and vote on Sprint backlog items across award categories — turning the completed work into an award show.
- **When to use:** When the team benefits from a more focused, structured retrospective rather than open brainstorming; great for shifting perspective on the Sprint's work and making reflection more engaging.
- **Group size:** Suitable for a standard Scrum team (implied by Sprint context)
- **Duration:** Not specified precisely; Carsten suggests ~5 minutes per category for discussion
- **How it works:**
  1. Print out (or display) the completed Sprint backlog
  2. Define 2–3 award categories. Classic options:
     - **Best Story** — what was the best story in this Sprint? What can we celebrate and learn from it?
     - **Most Annoying Story** — what caused the most pain, stress, or heartache?
     - **Third category** — decided by the team (e.g., "Best Lead Actor", "Best Supporting Actor", "Best Visual Effects", or whatever fits)
  3. Each team member nominates a story per category
  4. Use dot voting (or another voting method) to determine the winner per category
  5. Timebox a discussion (~5 minutes) per winning story: what can we learn, what should we celebrate, what actions follow?
  6. Capture action items to feed into the next Sprint
- **Energizer variant:** Before settling on the third category, run a short brainstorming: "If this Sprint were an award show, what would be the most surprising award?" Use the winning suggestion as the third category.

## Key Principles
- Open brainstorming formats (e.g., Starfish, Retro Wheel) are valuable, but sometimes a more structured format helps teams look more specifically at the actual work done.
- Using the Sprint backlog as the source material grounds the retrospective in concrete reality rather than general impressions.
- Award categories should balance celebration ("Best Story") with honest reflection ("Most Annoying Story") — both are sources of learning.
- The team can and should customize the third category — creativity in the format increases engagement.
- Success factors from the best story should be explicitly identified so they can be reused in future Sprints.

## Facilitation Tips
- Timebox each category discussion to approximately 5 minutes to maintain energy and focus.
- Use the energizer variant (brainstorming a surprising award) as a warm-up before selecting the third category — it adds playfulness and team ownership.
- Encourage teams to name the third category themselves; this increases buy-in and makes the format feel fresh each time.
- Always capture action items from each category discussion, not just the "most annoying" one — the best story can also yield reusable insights.

## Best Used When
- The team tends toward vague retrospective discussions and would benefit from grounding reflection in specific backlog items.
- You want to add a celebratory or playful element to a retrospective without losing focus on improvement.
- The team has been running the same retrospective format for a while and needs a fresh angle.
- You want to balance celebration and problem-solving in the same session.
