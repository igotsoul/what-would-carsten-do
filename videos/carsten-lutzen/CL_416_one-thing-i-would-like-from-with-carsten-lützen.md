# One thing I would like from with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten introduces a retrospective format built around a single recurring question: "One thing I would like from…". By varying the subject (the retrospective, the team, the process), the facilitator creates a focused, consistent structure that surfaces both interpersonal needs and process pain points, then funnels them into measurable outcomes and concrete actions.

## Methods & Techniques

### "One Thing I Would Like From…" Retrospective (ab 00:40)
- **What:** A full retrospective format using one repeating question with different subjects ("…from this retrospective", "…from the team", "…from the process") to generate insights, prioritize them via dot-voting, and derive measurable outcomes and actions.
- **When to use:** As a regular team retrospective, especially when teams need a fresh structure that balances personal needs, team dynamics, and process issues in one session.
- **Group size:** Works for small to mid-sized teams; Carsten suggests pair-work as an option for larger groups.
- **Duration:** Fits within a standard retrospective timebox (implied ~60–90 min for a full sprint retro).
- **How it works:**
  1. **Opening — Set expectations:** Ask *"One thing I would like from this retrospective"*. Each person writes one post-it silently, then presents it. This tunes the group into what they each need from the session.
  2. **Round 2 — Team needs:** Ask *"One thing I would like from the team"*. Same format: silent individual writing, then share. Examples: punctuality, empathy for personal circumstances, willingness to try new tech.
  3. **Round 3 — Process needs:** Ask *"One thing I would like from the process"*. Surfaces bottlenecks, ceremony issues, or ideas for change. Post-its go on the whiteboard alongside round 2 results.
  4. **Prioritize:** Dot-voting on all team + process post-its. Take the top 3.
  5. **Define outcomes:** Teams (or pairs) pick the 2–3 most interesting items and define 2–3 measurable outcomes — things they'll know they've achieved.
  6. **Define actions:** For each outcome, generate 1–2 concrete actions. Vote again on most likely to succeed. Commit to 2–5 actions per sprint.
  7. **Closing — Reflect:** Ask *"One thing I got / learned from this retrospective"* and compare against what participants wanted at the start.

## Key Principles
- A single consistent question format reduces cognitive overhead and keeps the retrospective focused.
- Starting with personal expectations ("what do I need from this retro?") creates psychological buy-in before diving into team and process topics.
- Outcomes should be measurable — the team must be able to tell whether they've met them.
- Dot-voting is sufficient for prioritization; keep it simple.
- Closing the loop at the end (comparing actual vs. expected takeaways) reinforces learning and accountability.

## Facilitation Tips
- Have everyone write post-its **individually and in silence** before sharing — this prevents anchoring and ensures all voices are heard.
- Carsten sometimes uses the first question as a free brainstorm to warm up the group before applying the format fully.
- For larger groups, consider having people **pair up** during the outcome-definition phase to reduce noise and increase focus.
- Keep the action list short: **2–5 actions per sprint** is the target range.
- The closing question ("one thing I got out of this") can be varied slightly ("one thing I learned") — the important thing is revisiting individual expectations set at the start.

## Best Used When
- The team needs a structured but flexible retrospective that covers multiple dimensions (personal, interpersonal, process) in one session.
- You want to move away from the classic 4Ls or Start/Stop/Continue formats without introducing high complexity.
- The team has a habit of generating lots of ideas but struggles to commit to actionable outcomes.
- You want a closing ritual that naturally evaluates whether the retro delivered value.
