# "Confirmation Bias" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten explains confirmation bias — our tendency to seek information that confirms existing beliefs rather than challenging them — and why facilitators and agile coaches need to be aware of it when working with teams. He shares a quick, hands-on workshop exercise using a number sequence puzzle that reliably surfaces confirmation bias in action, making the concept tangible for participants.

## Methods & Techniques

### Number Sequence Exercise (ab 01:45)
- **What:** A short group exercise where the facilitator gives participants a starting number sequence and asks them to discover the hidden rule by proposing their own sequences. It reliably reveals confirmation bias in the room.
- **When to use:** At the start of a workshop or coaching session when you want to build shared awareness of confirmation bias before diving into deeper work.
- **Group size:** Works for any group size; individuals take turns proposing sequences.
- **Duration:** 5–10 minutes.
- **How it works:**
  1. Tell participants there is a rule behind a number sequence and they need to figure out what it is.
  2. Give the starting sequence: **2, 4, 8** — and confirm it follows the rule.
  3. Participants take turns proposing three-number sequences. The facilitator only responds "yes, that follows the rule" or "no, that does not follow the rule" — no hints.
  4. Observe: most participants will keep proposing sequences that *confirm* their hypothesis (e.g., 20, 40, 80 or 1, 3, 9) rather than sequences designed to *break* it.
  5. Encourage falsification: try sequences like 1, 1, 1 (no), 1, 1, 2 (no), 1, 2, 3 (yes), 4, 5, 6 (yes).
  6. The actual rule: **each number must be greater than its predecessor** (any ascending sequence).
  7. Debrief: ask who fell for the confirmation bias and how that connects to their work.

## Key Principles
- Confirmation bias is universal — everyone is affected to some degree, making self-awareness essential rather than optional.
- In hypothesis testing, the correct approach is to try to *falsify* a hypothesis, not prove it. If you cannot disprove it, you gain stronger evidence it may be valid.
- Social media algorithms amplify confirmation bias by feeding users content aligned with their existing views, reducing exposure to opposing perspectives.
- When coaching teams, confirmation bias can lead a coach to selectively notice data points that support a pre-formed diagnosis of "what's wrong" — a risk Carsten flags explicitly.
- Making the bias visible through an experiential exercise is more effective than just explaining the concept.

## Facilitation Tips
- Run the number sequence exercise *before* any diagnostic or problem-framing work to prime the group for more open, falsification-oriented thinking.
- Only give binary feedback (yes/no) during the exercise — resist the urge to hint or guide. The discomfort of not knowing is part of the learning.
- After the exercise, explicitly connect the experience to the team's real work: "Where might we be doing this same thing in our process or in how we look at our data?"
- Carsten invites sharing alternative exercises for teaching confirmation bias — this is a good signal to crowdsource similar activities from the team or audience.

## Best Used When
- Opening a retrospective or problem-solving session where assumptions about root causes risk going unchallenged.
- Coaching a team or leader who tends to jump quickly to conclusions and ignore contradicting signals.
- Building psychological safety and epistemic humility before an honest team health check or diagnosis.
- Introducing critical thinking norms to a newly formed team.
