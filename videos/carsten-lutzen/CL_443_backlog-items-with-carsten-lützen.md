# Backlog Items with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten gives a concise overview of the four main backlog item types he uses in Scrum: user stories, tasks, spikes, and bugs. He explains what distinguishes each type and how classifying items helps the Product Owner understand where the team's work is focused — feature development, maintenance, investigation, or bug fixing.

## Methods & Techniques

### Four Backlog Item Types (ab 00:30)
- **What:** A simple classification system for backlog items using four categories — stories, tasks, spikes, and bugs — to give the Product Owner better visibility into the nature of the work.
- **When to use:** When setting up or refining a Scrum backlog to improve transparency and Product Owner decision-making.
- **Group size:** Whole Scrum team
- **Duration:** Ongoing practice, not a one-time exercise
- **How it works:**
  1. **User Stories** — Business-facing requirements in the classic "As a [user], I want [action] so that [outcome]" format. Captures user intent and is refined with acceptance criteria (functional + non-functional).
  2. **Tasks** — Technical work raised by the dev team, e.g. addressing technical debt. Can be written as user stories to help the PO understand intent, but sometimes a plain description suffices if the team knows what's needed.
  3. **Spikes** — Time-boxed investigatory work. Used when the team wants to evaluate a tool or approach without committing. Carsten recommends hard time-boxing (e.g. 4 hours) to maximize value and avoid open-ended exploration.
  4. **Bugs/Defects** — Can follow a zero-bug policy (fix immediately) or be prioritized alongside features. The latter allows the PO to consciously weigh impact (e.g. affects 1% of users) against new feature value.

### Strict Single-Priority Ordering (ab 00:45)
- **What:** In a Scrum backlog, each item has a unique priority rank — exactly one priority 1, one priority 2, etc. No ties allowed.
- **When to use:** When teams or POs resist forced ranking and default to multiple "priority 1" items.
- **How it works:** Enforce that the backlog is a fully ordered list. The PO is responsible for deciding the single most important item at any given time.

## Key Principles
- A backlog is simply a prioritized to-do list — nothing more. Treating it as anything grander adds unnecessary complexity.
- Item classification (story/task/spike/bug) gives the PO a quick diagnostic: is the team mostly doing new features, maintenance, investigation, or firefighting?
- Tasks originating from the dev team (e.g. tech debt) should ideally be framed as user stories to make their value legible to the PO — but pragmatism applies when the intent is obvious to everyone.
- Spikes are underused. Carsten notes he doesn't use them as often as he should — time-boxing exploratory work is a high-value habit.
- Bug prioritization is a legitimate Product Owner decision, not a failure. A bug affecting 1% of users may genuinely rank lower than a high-value feature.

## Facilitation Tips
- When introducing this classification to a team, use the category breakdown as a retrospective lens: look at the last sprint's items by type to surface patterns (e.g. "we spent 60% on bugs — is that intentional?").
- Encourage dev teams to write technical tasks in user story format when presenting them to the PO — it bridges the language gap without requiring deep technical knowledge from the PO.
- For spikes, always define the time box and expected output before the sprint starts to avoid scope creep.

## Best Used When
- Onboarding a new Scrum team or Product Owner who needs a simple mental model for backlog structure.
- Backlog refinement sessions where teams mix item types without clear distinction.
- Retrospectives focused on work balance and team health (feature work vs. debt vs. bugs).
