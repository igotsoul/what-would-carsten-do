# "If x was a language" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten Lützen shares a quick, low-prep training opener that helps participants self-assess their proficiency in the topic about to be taught — framed as language fluency levels. The technique surfaces the group's self-perceived skill distribution at the start of a session and can be extended with a "best hopes" element to anchor learning goals and revisit progress at the end.

## Methods & Techniques

### "If X Was a Language" Proficiency Check (ab 00:20)
- **What:** Participants self-assess their knowledge of the training topic by selecting which "language fluency" statement best describes them, written on a sticky note.
- **When to use:** At the start of a workshop or training to gauge the group's self-perceived skill level and tailor facilitation accordingly. Works for any topic — agile, coaching, facilitation, programming languages, etc.
- **Group size:** Any size; participants work individually and in silence.
- **Duration:** 5–10 minutes.
- **How it works:**
  1. Frame the topic as a language (e.g., "If agile was a language…").
  2. Present four proficiency levels (displayed or read aloud):
     - *"I can order a beer and ask where the bathroom is"* — very basic, rudimentary knowledge.
     - *"I can lead an everyday conversation"* — some working knowledge, no strong theoretical foundation.
     - *"I can write and lead a professional conversation"* — solid understanding of theory, able to distinguish signal from superstition.
     - *"I can teach or negotiate"* — expert level, able to instruct and influence others.
  3. Ask each participant to write their name on a sticky note in silence.
  4. Have participants place their stickies on the board next to their chosen level.
  5. The trainer now has a visible map of self-perceived skill distribution to inform the session.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=y4wnVTlNiGc&t=20

### "Best Hopes" Extension (ab 01:45)
- **What:** An optional add-on where participants also write their best hope for the day on their sticky note, creating a personal learning goal that can be revisited at the close of the session.
- **When to use:** When you want to combine the skill-level check with expectation-setting and a closing reflection loop.
- **Group size:** Any size.
- **Duration:** Adds 2–5 minutes at the start; brief reflection at the end.
- **How it works:**
  1. Use a larger sticky note so participants can write both their name and their best hope for the day.
  2. Participants place stickies as above.
  3. At the end of the training, return to the board and invite participants to reflect: Did we meet your best hope? Did you move up a level? What would help you continue moving forward?

## Key Principles
- Self-assessment is self-perceived — Carsten explicitly notes that the data reflects how participants see themselves, not an objective measure. This framing keeps it safe and non-threatening.
- Silence during the exercise is intentional — participants write individually without discussion, preventing social influence on self-assessment.
- The technique is topic-agnostic — the "language" metaphor can be applied to any domain, making it reusable across very different training contexts.
- Closing the loop matters — the real value is in returning to the stickies at the end to celebrate growth and identify what support is still needed.

## Facilitation Tips
- Don't explain or comment while participants are writing — Carsten emphasizes staying silent during the self-assessment phase.
- Use the visible skill distribution to adjust your facilitation in real time: if most people cluster at the low end, slow down and build foundations; if there are clear experts in the room, consider drawing on them.
- Celebrate movement between levels at the close — acknowledging that someone moved from "no words" to "ordering a beer" makes growth tangible and motivating.
- Keep the proficiency labels light and humorous; the beer-and-bathroom phrasing lowers the stakes and makes even low self-assessment feel non-embarrassing.

## Best Used When
- Opening a training or workshop where participants arrive with mixed experience levels.
- You want a fast, visual way to set expectations and signal that the session will meet people where they are.
- You need a low-prep, no-materials-beyond-stickies opener that still generates meaningful data.
- You want to build in a natural closing reflection without designing a separate retrospective exercise.
