# "We Predict" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten Lützen shares a short workshop exercise called "We Predict" that uses a 1-2-4-All structure to surface participant predictions about an upcoming topic. It aligns expectations, activates prior knowledge, and increases engagement before diving into new content — and creates a revisit opportunity at the end of the session.

## Methods & Techniques

### We Predict (ab 00:20)
- **What:** Participants make individual predictions about an upcoming topic or concept, then share and build on them through progressively larger groups, ending with a plenary harvest. Predictions are posted visibly and revisited at the end of the session.
- **When to use:** At the start of a workshop module or learning segment, before introducing a new concept, technique, or problem framing. Effective for both training contexts and collaborative problem-solving sessions.
- **Group size:** Any size; structured in individuals, pairs, groups of four, then full plenary.
- **Duration:** Approximately 5–10 minutes depending on group size and topic depth (individual round: ~1 minute).
- **How it works:**
  1. Introduce the topic to be explored (e.g., roadmapping, value stream mapping, process improvement).
  2. Individually and in silence, participants write predictions. Carsten suggests prompting with questions like: Where can we use this in everyday life? What content do you think is included? Where does the knowledge come from? What data or statistics back this up?
  3. Share and discuss in pairs.
  4. Share and discuss in groups of four.
  5. Facilitator harvests predictions in plenary and posts them visibly on a wall.
  6. At the end of the session or module, revisit the predictions together — identifying which proved true, which were myths, and what falls in between.

## Key Principles
- Activating prior knowledge and surfacing assumptions before new content increases participant engagement and ownership.
- Making predictions public creates a lightweight learning contract — participants become invested in seeing whether their prediction holds.
- The revisit at the end transforms the exercise into a reflection loop, reinforcing learning and making myths visible.
- Using 1-2-4-All structures the social scaffolding so quieter participants have space to think before being exposed to group dynamics.

## Facilitation Tips
- Give at least one minute for silent individual prediction writing — don't rush this step.
- Provide prompting questions to reduce blank-page paralysis: applicability, content scope, knowledge sources, and supporting evidence.
- Write the harvested predictions on a shared wall or board so they remain visible throughout the session.
- Close the loop explicitly at the end: return to the predictions wall and facilitate a brief debrief on what held up and what didn't.

## Best Used When
- Opening a training module or workshop segment on a new concept or technique.
- The facilitator wants to activate participant thinking before presenting content rather than starting with a lecture.
- The group needs alignment on what they already believe or assume about a topic.
- There is a defined end-point to the session where predictions can be revisited.
