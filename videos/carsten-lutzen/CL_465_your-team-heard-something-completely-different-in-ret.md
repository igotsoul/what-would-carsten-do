# Your team heard something completely different in retrospectives

## Summary
Carsten walks through a full communication-themed retrospective design built around a central insight: team members often don't register what they are expressing outwardly, because they only have access to their own intentions. The format strings together five elements — a chair-game warm-up, a structured follow-up on previous actions, the "Neighbors" sticky-note exercise (writing on behalf of someone else), a vote, and a postcard closer — to surface the gap between what people say and what teammates actually hear.

## Methods & Techniques

### Chair Game — Communication Warm-Up (ab 01:00)
- **What:** A physical exercise where groups with mutually incompatible missions try to arrange chairs at the same time, exposing how poor communication leads to friction even with no bad intent.
- **When to use:** Opening a communication-focused retrospective in a co-located setting; works well as a debrief starter.
- **Group size:** Full team, divided into 3 sub-groups.
- **Duration:** 10–15 minutes including debrief.
- **How it works:**
  1. Divide participants into three groups, each with a different mission: chairs upside down / chairs in pairs / chairs in a circle.
  2. All groups attempt their mission simultaneously in the same space.
  3. Debrief: Why did this feel frustrating? What would have helped? What does this mirror in our sprint communication?
- **Note:** Carsten has a dedicated video on the Chair Game (linked separately).
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=3dNH2DiGu0g&t=60

### Structured Follow-Up on Previous Retro Actions (ab 02:00)
- **What:** A disciplined opening ritual for the retrospective proper that forces accountability on last sprint's action items before generating new ones.
- **When to use:** Every retrospective — Carsten treats this as a default step, not optional.
- **How it works:**
  1. A **driver** (assigned in the previous sprint) debriefs: what happened with the action item?
  2. Group votes or discusses: is this still relevant?
  3. If still relevant → carry it into the current retrospective. If resolved or irrelevant → close it explicitly.
- **Why it matters:** Demonstrates that retrospectives lead to real change, which builds trust in the format.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=3dNH2DiGu0g&t=120

### The Neighbors Exercise (ab 03:30)
- **What:** Participants write sticky notes **on behalf of their neighbor** rather than themselves, using whatever format the team uses (retro wheel, LOFT, starfish, etc.). This forces team members to recall what their neighbor has actually expressed during the sprint.
- **When to use:** Retrospectives focused on communication, listening, or perception gaps. Particularly powerful when a team suspects members aren't truly hearing each other.
- **Group size:** Any size; works in a circle (physical or virtual).
- **Duration:** 10–15 minutes for writing, plus discussion time.
- **How it works:**
  1. Keep the same retrospective format from the previous sprint (continuity helps).
  2. Instead of writing your own perspective, write sticky notes for **one specific neighbor** (e.g., go clockwise).
  3. When presenting: frame every note with "I think [Name] would say…" — making it explicit this is a **best guess**.
  4. After presenting: give the neighbor space to correct, add nuance, or flag what was missed.
  5. Optional Step 3: each person adds their own sticky notes for anything the neighbor missed that they want on the board.
- **Why it works:** Forces active listening during the sprint. Surfaces the gap between what people intend to communicate and what colleagues actually register. Can bring new topics onto the board that the team hadn't voiced explicitly.
- **Facilitation note:** Watch for shifts in room dynamics — sometimes unexpected items appear because the proxy framing unlocks what people felt unable to say for themselves.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=3dNH2DiGu0g&t=210

### Signs of Improvement Instead of Actions (ab 05:30)
- **What:** After voting on a focus area, the team defines **observable signs** that improvement is happening, rather than committing to specific concrete tasks.
- **When to use:** Any retrospective, but especially communication-focused ones where behaviour change is hard to task-ify.
- **How it works:** After the vote, ask: "How would we know that more [X] has happened? How would we experience it? How would our users, managers, or other teams notice?" The discussion itself surfaces the team's implicit expectations.
- **Why Carsten prefers this:** Concrete actions often get lost or feel mechanical; signs of improvement create shared awareness and let the team self-regulate organically.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=3dNH2DiGu0g&t=330

### Postcard Closer (ab 06:30)
- **What:** Each participant writes an **encouraging postcard** to the person they wrote sticky notes for — framed as a message from that person to themselves — which the facilitator distributes during the sprint (not immediately).
- **When to use:** Closing activity for communication-themed retrospectives; also effective as a mid-sprint morale touchpoint.
- **Duration:** ~5 minutes writing.
- **How it works:**
  1. Participants spend ~5 minutes writing a short, positive postcard addressed to their neighbor, as if **from that person to themselves** (e.g., "Dear John, you should be proud of…").
  2. Facilitator collects all postcards.
  3. During the sprint, the facilitator hands out each card at a moment when the recipient seems to need encouragement — or simply at a set point mid-sprint.
- **Why it works:** Reinforces positive communication as a team value; creates a concrete artifact from the retrospective that arrives asynchronously and feels personal.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=3dNH2DiGu0g&t=390

## Key Principles
- People operate from their **intentions**; teammates only experience **behaviours and words**. The gap between these is where communication problems live.
- Changing perspective structurally (writing for someone else) reveals more than asking people to "be empathetic" — the exercise does the cognitive work.
- Retrospectives must demonstrate follow-through: always close or carry forward previous actions explicitly before generating new ones.
- Signs of improvement > specific action tasks for behaviour-change goals: they create shared awareness and are harder to game.
- Positive framing at closure (the postcard) anchors the retrospective in growth rather than complaint.

## Facilitation Tips
- Use the **same retro format as the previous sprint** for the Neighbors exercise — continuity reduces cognitive load so attention goes to the neighbor's perspective, not the format.
- When presenting neighbors' sticky notes, **explicitly reinforce** the "best guess" framing before anyone speaks — without this, recipients may feel put on trial.
- Always give the neighbor an explicit **right of reply** after their sticky notes are presented, and offer the optional Step 3 so no one feels unheard.
- As facilitator, **observe the room** during the Neighbors exercise: shifts in energy, laughter, or silence are signals that something real is surfacing.
- Collect the postcards yourself; do not leave it to participants to hand them out. The facilitator's judgment on *when* to deliver creates the most impact.
- This full arc (Chair Game → Follow-up → Neighbors → Vote → Signs → Postcard) runs long — plan for 90 minutes if all elements are included.

## Best Used When
- The team has recurring tension around communication, information sharing, or "we didn't know that was happening"
- Previous retrospectives surfaced communication as a theme but generated only vague actions
- The team is experienced enough with retrospectives to handle the Neighbors exercise without defensiveness
- A facilitator wants a format that generates both insight (what are we communicating?) and a lasting artifact (the postcard)
