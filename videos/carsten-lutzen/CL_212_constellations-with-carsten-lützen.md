# "Constellations" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten introduces the Constellations exercise, a physical group activity that uses spatial positioning to reveal how team members relate to a series of prompts or statements. It combines silent individual reflection with group debriefing to surface patterns, tensions, and team dynamics in a low-pressure way.

## Methods & Techniques

### Constellations (ab 00:20)
- **What:** Participants physically move closer to or farther from the center of the room in response to a statement — proximity to center signals agreement, distance signals disagreement. Everyone moves simultaneously, creating a visible map of the group's collective stance.
- **When to use:** When you want to surface team sentiments, uncover disagreements, or create a starting point for honest conversation — especially in groups where direct verbal input may feel risky.
- **Group size:** Works for teams and larger groups; a neutral third-party facilitator is recommended.
- **Duration:** Flexible — run as many prompts as time and energy allow. Total session length scales with number of questions.
- **How it works:**
  1. Designate a center point in the room ("center of the universe").
  2. Read a loaded statement aloud (e.g., "We are always working on what is most important").
  3. Everyone moves simultaneously — closer if they agree, toward the wall if they disagree.
  4. No one waits to see where others go first; all movement happens at once.
  5. After positioning, invite those closest to the center to share their perspective, then those farthest away, then those in between.
  6. Debrief the pattern, then repeat with the next prompt.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=QBe7Td8waTI

## Key Principles
- Simultaneous movement prevents social mirroring — people choose their position independently, not in reaction to others.
- The exercise starts silently, reducing the pressure of immediate verbal disclosure before the group is ready.
- The depth of the debrief depends on the level of psychological safety in the room — facilitators should read the group and calibrate accordingly.
- Loaded, specific prompts yield more revealing constellations than vague or neutral ones.

## Facilitation Tips
- Use a neutral, third-party facilitator to help draw out patterns without influencing participant positioning.
- Choose prompts that are genuinely meaningful to the team — statements about priority, collaboration, or shared values tend to generate useful spreads.
- After positioning, interview participants in layers: those nearest the center first, then those farthest, then the middle. This gives voice to the full range of perspectives.
- The number of rounds is up to the facilitator — continue for as many prompts as the session warrants.

## Best Used When
- Kicking off a retrospective or team session with silent, low-stakes reflection before open discussion.
- You want to make invisible dynamics visible without putting anyone on the spot verbally.
- The team has varying levels of openness and you want to create a safe entry point for honest conversation.
- You need a quick read on team sentiment across multiple dimensions.
