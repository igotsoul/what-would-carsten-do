# "4-2-1-SNAP" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten introduces 4-2-1-SNAP, a reverse variation of the Liberating Structure "1-2-4-All", designed to close down a discussion rather than open one up. The format moves from group conversation to pairs to individual reflection, ending with a shared snap gesture to signal collective closure. It's a quick and effective way to help participants mentally let go of a topic and shift focus.

## Methods & Techniques

### 4-2-1-SNAP (ab 00:20)
- **What:** A reverse version of the Liberating Structure "1-2-4-All" that helps groups converge and close down a discussion. Instead of building from individual to group, it narrows from group to individual, ending with a shared snap to signal readiness to move on.
- **When to use:** After presenting data, reviewing interview findings, or any discussion that needs a clear closing — when you want participants to reach personal closure before moving to the next topic.
- **Group size:** Groups of four, splitting into pairs for the second round. Works with any number of parallel groups.
- **Duration:** ~7 minutes total (4 min + 2 min + 1 min + snap)
- **How it works:**
  1. **4 minutes** — Form groups of four. Discuss the invitation (e.g., "What stands out to you when you look at this data?"). Full group conversation.
  2. **2 minutes** — Split into pairs within each group. Continue the discussion, diving a bit deeper.
  3. **1 minute** — Individually and in silence. Reflect, write down final thoughts or remarks on the topic.
  4. **SNAP** — Facilitator counts down from 3 to 1, everyone snaps together. This signals collective closure — the topic is done, thoughts are captured, time to move on.

## Key Principles
- Convergence needs structure: without a deliberate closing ritual, discussions can linger in participants' minds even after the group moves on.
- Writing down the last thought before letting go is a key mechanism — it makes it safe to release the topic because nothing is lost.
- A shared physical gesture (the snap) creates a collective, synchronised moment of closure that is both playful and effective.
- This is a variation of "1-2-4-All" — Carsten credits Barry Overeem for the reminder, illustrating how practitioners build on and remix existing Liberating Structures.

## Facilitation Tips
- Choose an invitation question tied to the specific content just discussed (e.g., "What stood out to you in that interview?" or "What stands out when you see this data?").
- Keep the time boxes strict — the narrowing structure only works if each phase actually ends and transitions.
- The silent individual minute is important: resist the urge to fill it with talk. Let participants finish their thought in writing.
- The countdown-and-snap can feel playful; lean into that — it helps shift energy and signals a clear transition.

## Best Used When
- You have just shared data, research findings, or interview results and need to close the sense-making conversation.
- A discussion has been running long and needs a structured, respectful way to end.
- You want participants to feel heard and finished — not cut off — before moving to the next agenda item.
- You need a short (~7 min) closing ritual that works without materials or preparation.
