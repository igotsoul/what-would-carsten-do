# Lack of Trust by Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten addresses the first and foundational dysfunction in Lencioni's Five Dysfunctions of a Team — absence of trust — and how it manifests in real teams. He explains the root causes (new teams, change events, regression to storming phase) and offers a practical toolkit of techniques to help teams rebuild psychological safety and open communication.

## Methods & Techniques

### Market of Skills (ab 02:15)
- **What:** A structured exercise where team members articulate what skills and perspectives they bring to the team and what they hope to gain from it.
- **When to use:** With newly formed teams or teams that have undergone significant change; especially useful in the forming/storming transition.
- **Group size:** Full team
- **Duration:** Not specified; Carsten links to a dedicated video
- **How it works:** Each team member reflects on and shares their contribution to the team and their expectations from it, making individual value visible to the group.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=YjF_fDb74Yc

### Back-to-Back Listening (ab 02:35)
- **What:** A paired exercise where two people stand back-to-back, listen to the same piece of music, and share what they felt — then swap perspectives.
- **When to use:** When a team lacks empathy or members aren't genuinely hearing each other; works well as a trust-building warm-up.
- **Group size:** Pairs
- **Duration:** Short exercise, suitable as a warm-up
- **How it works:** Partners stand back-to-back, listen to a song together, each shares what they felt, then they switch and reflect on the other's experience.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=YjF_fDb74Yc

### Personal Quiz (ab 02:50)
- **What:** Team members take turns creating a short quiz about themselves, sharing personal facts they would not normally mention.
- **When to use:** To build vulnerability-based trust; especially effective when the team is guarded or overly professional in their self-presentation.
- **Group size:** Full team
- **Duration:** Not specified
- **How it works:** The facilitator goes first as a role model, sharing things they would not normally bring up — demonstrating vulnerability. Team members then do the same in subsequent sessions.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=YjF_fDb74Yc

### Feedback Culture Workshop (ab 03:10)
- **What:** A structured conversation about how and when the team gives feedback, surfacing individual preferences and establishing healthy norms.
- **When to use:** When distrust or conflict may be rooted in an unhealthy or absent feedback culture.
- **Group size:** Full team
- **Duration:** Not specified
- **How it works:** Facilitate a discussion around: When do we give feedback? How do we give it (constructive vs. positive)? Individual preferences (1-on-1 vs. in plenum)? Then establish shared norms.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=YjF_fDb74Yc

### Ways of Working Alignment (ab 03:30)
- **What:** A team agreement session that explicitly defines how the team collaborates — when to ask questions, when to work together, and how decisions are made.
- **When to use:** Early in team formation or after disruption; critical when implicit working norms are a source of friction.
- **Group size:** Full team
- **Duration:** Not specified
- **How it works:** Facilitate a conversation to surface and document shared working agreements. Treat them as living norms — "set in stone for the time being."
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=YjF_fDb74Yc

### Troika Consulting (ab 03:50)
- **What:** A Liberating Structures exercise where one person receives structured peer consultation from two others on a real challenge they are facing.
- **When to use:** To build a culture of helping each other; effective once minimal trust exists and the team is ready for structured peer support.
- **Group size:** Groups of 3
- **Duration:** Typically 15–30 min (per Liberating Structures standard)
- **How it works:** One person is the "client" and shares a challenge; the two "consultants" ask clarifying questions, then the client turns away while consultants discuss ideas; client turns back and reflects on what was useful.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=YjF_fDb74Yc

### Trust-Focused Retrospective (ab 04:10)
- **What:** A closed-team retrospective where psychological safety and open dialogue are the explicit goal — with non-team members excluded.
- **When to use:** When a team has enough trust to begin honest reflection but needs a protected space to surface what's really going on.
- **Group size:** Team only (no external observers)
- **Duration:** Standard retrospective timebox
- **How it works:** Remove anyone not on the team. Create a safe container and facilitate an honest, open retrospective focused on team dynamics and how people are feeling — not just process or outcomes.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=YjF_fDb74Yc

## Key Principles
- Absence of trust is the foundational dysfunction (Lencioni's Five Dysfunctions of a Team) — nothing sustainable is achievable without it.
- Trust problems often appear in two contexts: newly formed teams in the storming phase, or existing teams that have regressed into storming after a significant change (new member, departure, restructuring).
- Key warning signs: people hiding their work or feelings, non-constructive confrontation, and — most critically — silence. When people stop contributing, trust has broken down significantly.
- There is no silver bullet. Rebuilding trust requires a combination of techniques, patience, and consistent facilitation attention.
- Getting people to talk is the single most effective lever. Everything else serves that goal.
- The facilitator must model vulnerability first — going first in a personal quiz or sharing something personal sets the tone for others to follow.

## Facilitation Tips
- Do not wait for trust problems to resolve on their own — Carsten is explicit that this never works.
- Sequence matters: start with lower-stakes exercises (quiz, back-to-back listening) before moving to more exposing formats (Troika, open retrospective).
- Acknowledge that some techniques may feel "feely feely" — normalize this with the team before running them.
- In feedback culture discussions, explicitly address that people differ in their preferences (1-on-1 vs. group feedback) and build norms that accommodate this.
- Use the retrospective as the primary container for trust work, but only once the team is ready. If not ready, use the smaller individual exercises first to build toward it.
- Exclude non-team members from trust-focused retrospectives to protect the safety of the space.

## Best Used When
- Onboarding a newly formed team through the storming phase
- A team has experienced significant change (member joins or leaves, restructuring) and has regressed in cohesion
- A Scrum Master or agile coach notices reduced participation, rising conflict, or people becoming guarded
- Preparing for a team health check or working agreements session
- A retrospective feels flat or unsafe and the facilitator suspects low trust is the underlying cause
