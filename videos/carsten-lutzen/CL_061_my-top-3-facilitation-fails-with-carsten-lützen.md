# "My top 3 facilitation fails" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten Lützen shares three personal facilitation failures as a way to model openness about mistakes and normalize learning from things that go wrong. The video covers a participant walking out during a Liberating Structures exercise, a workshop where Carsten misjudged what the client actually needed, and a broader reflection on how to process and move on from facilitation missteps.

## Methods & Techniques

### Back-to-Back Listening (ab 01:10)
- **What:** A Liberating Structure exercise where participants listen to each other without facing one another, designed to deepen attentive listening.
- **When to use:** With groups that are open to unconventional, experiential activities. Not ideal for all audiences or without proper stage-setting.
- **Group size:** Not specified, but used in a group of senior engineers.
- **Duration:** Not specified.
- **How it works:** Not described in detail in this video; referenced as an activity within a broader session that also included Open Space and the Law of Two Feet.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=j73NjBoTORY&t=70

### Law of Two Feet (ab 01:00)
- **What:** An Open Space principle giving participants explicit permission to leave any session or activity where they feel they are neither contributing nor learning.
- **When to use:** In Open Space sessions and self-directed workshops. Carsten notes it can produce unexpected outcomes when introduced before more structured exercises.
- **How it works:** Participants are told upfront: if you are not adding or receiving value, use your two feet and go somewhere else.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=j73NjBoTORY&t=60

### "Be a Goldfish" — Letting Go of Fails (ab 05:30)
- **What:** A mindset practice borrowed from the Ted Lasso character (also referenced in a separate Carsten video) for processing mistakes without ruminating. The goldfish metaphor represents a short memory — you acknowledge the failure, extract the learning, and move on.
- **When to use:** After any facilitation session where something didn't go as planned.
- **How it works:** Acknowledge the fail. Ask: what can I learn from this? Then consciously move on rather than collecting or dwelling on failures.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=j73NjBoTORY&t=330

## Key Principles

- **You cannot please everyone.** Some exercises will resonate with some participants and not others. That is normal, not a disaster.
- **Stage-setting matters.** When using unconventional exercises, clearly communicating the "why" helps participants understand purpose and reduces resistance or walkouts.
- **Alignment before the workshop is critical.** Two of Carsten's three fails trace back to insufficient upfront alignment on what the client or participants actually wanted to get out of the session.
- **Leave your ego at the door.** Carsten reflects that in one case he was too attached to his own vision for how the workshop should run, and failed to truly listen to what the client needed.
- **Facilitation and teaching are different stances — know when to use each.** Carsten notes his preference for facilitation over teaching, but acknowledges that sometimes participants need concrete tools and direct instruction, not reflective dialogue.
- **Normalize failure in the facilitation community.** Carsten explicitly frames sharing failures as a community responsibility — there is much to gain from open conversation about what doesn't work.

## Facilitation Tips

- When introducing the Law of Two Feet or other Open Space principles, be intentional about scope — clarify whether it applies to the whole session or only specific parts, to avoid confusion during structured exercises.
- Before designing a workshop, invest more time in alignment conversations: what does the client actually want? What outcome are they expecting? What format (facilitation, teaching, hybrid) fits that need?
- If an exercise falls flat, try it again with a different angle before abandoning it entirely. Only retire a technique after a truly spectacular failure.
- Be honest with yourself about your facilitation preferences and where they may create blind spots (e.g., a bias toward dialogue over instruction).

## Best Used When

- Facilitators are reflecting on their own practice and looking for permission to talk openly about failures.
- Preparing to use Liberating Structures or unconventional exercises with an unfamiliar group.
- Coaching new facilitators on the importance of client alignment and ego management.
- Discussing the difference between facilitation and teaching as professional stances.
