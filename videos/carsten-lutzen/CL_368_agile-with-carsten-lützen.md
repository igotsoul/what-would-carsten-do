# "Agile" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten makes a short but sharp observation: "Agile" is just an adjective — and an empty one unless explicitly defined. Before any conversation about agile practices can be meaningful, teams and individuals need to align on what the word actually means to each of them. The video serves as a reminder to question fluffy, positive-sounding language in organizational contexts.

## Methods & Techniques

### The Adjective Substitution Test (ab 00:35)
- **What:** Replace the word "Agile" with another adjective — Carsten uses "beautiful" — to reveal how vague and meaningless the term often is in practice.
- **When to use:** When facilitating or coaching a team or organization that claims to "be Agile" or "do Agile" without a shared definition. Useful as a quick provocation or coaching question.
- **Group size:** Works in any size conversation, especially 1-on-1 coaching or team workshops
- **Duration:** A moment — it's a reframe, not an exercise
- **How it works:** When you hear "We are doing agile processes", mentally (or out loud) swap "agile" for "beautiful". The resulting absurdity — "We are doing beautiful processes" — exposes that the original statement carried no real information either.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=MZGR3JDiJjU&t=35

## Key Principles
- "Agile" is grammatically an adjective and semantically nearly empty without a shared definition
- No two practitioners necessarily share the same definition of "Agile" — alignment must precede discussion
- Positive-sounding adjectives ("agile", "lean", "beautiful") are often used to signal intent without communicating substance
- Carsten's provocation: whenever someone claims to be "oh so Agile", ask what Agile means to them — that question itself is more valuable than the claim

## Facilitation Tips
- Use the substitution test ("replace Agile with beautiful") as a quick icebreaker provocation in coaching conversations or team sessions where Agile terminology is thrown around loosely
- Ask "What does Agile mean to you?" as a grounding question before any discussion of agile adoption, transformation, or assessment
- Don't assume shared vocabulary — surface the definition before building on it

## Best Used When
- Starting an agile coaching engagement with a new team or organization
- Facilitating a workshop where participants have mixed or assumed understanding of "Agile"
- Challenging agile cargo-culting or buzzword-heavy culture in a non-confrontational way
- Preparing for an alignment conversation about what "done" or "good" looks like in an agile context

## Related Concepts
- Wittgenstein's language games — meaning is use, not definition
- The Agile Manifesto (the original definition many practitioners unknowingly diverge from)
- Shu-Ha-Ri — different stages of agile understanding lead to genuinely different definitions
