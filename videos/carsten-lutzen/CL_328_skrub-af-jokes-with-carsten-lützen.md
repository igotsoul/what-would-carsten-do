# "Skrub af Jokes" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten introduces a lighthearted meeting-closing technique he calls "Skrub af Jokes" (Danish for "go home jokes") — ending a meeting with intentionally terrible dad jokes to get people laughing and signing off on a positive note. The technique is inspired by the Danish party tradition of serving a late-night snack ("skrub af mål") as a gentle signal that the party is over.

## Methods & Techniques

### Skrub af Jokes / Go Home Jokes (ab 00:30)
- **What:** End a meeting a few minutes early with a round of deliberately bad dad jokes or puns, so participants leave laughing.
- **When to use:** At the close of any meeting or workshop — especially useful for remote meetings where sign-off can feel abrupt or flat.
- **Group size:** Any size
- **Duration:** 2–5 minutes at the end of a meeting
- **How it works:**
  1. End the meeting agenda a couple of minutes before the scheduled end time.
  2. Announce you're going into "go home jokes" mode.
  3. Tell one or two terrible puns or dad jokes (examples: "Why did the dad joke cross the road? To get to the other side." / "Did you know tennis players have a high divorce rate? Because love means nothing to them.")
  4. Optionally invite participants to share their own bad joke before signing off.
  5. Let the groan-worthy humor be the last thing people experience.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Io2v8J9Ezww&t=60

## Key Principles
- Ending on a laugh — even a reluctant one — leaves people with a positive emotional memory of the meeting.
- The "gentle push out the door" concept: a playful signal that the session is over, inspired by Danish party culture (the midnight snack as a soft close).
- Counterintuitive but effective: jokes so bad they work *because* of how cheesy they are, not despite it.
- Inviting participants to contribute their own joke increases engagement and gives shy closers an easy way to participate.

## Facilitation Tips
- The worse the joke, the better — the collective groan is part of the experience.
- Keep a small repertoire of reliable dad jokes ready so you're never scrambling.
- Inviting others to share a joke creates a participatory close and can surface unexpected humor.
- Works in both in-person and remote/virtual settings.

## Best Used When
- You want to close a meeting on a high note without a formal debrief.
- The meeting has been heavy or intensive and you want to shift the emotional register before people leave.
- You're running a series of recurring meetings and want a signature closing ritual.
- Remote meetings where the ending often feels abrupt — this gives a clear, warm signal that it's over.
