# "Battery" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten introduces the Battery exercise — a check-in or energizer that helps individuals and teams reflect on their energy levels. The key twist he adds is shifting focus from activities that charge or drain the battery to *signs* of charge and drain, making the exercise more self-aware and actionable. This is especially useful when teams are returning from vacation, transitioning back to office, or showing early signs of stress and burnout.

## Methods & Techniques

### Battery Check-In — Signs of Charge & Drain (ab 00:30)
- **What:** A check-in exercise using a battery as a metaphor for personal energy. Rather than asking what activities charge or drain you, Carsten reframes the questions around observable *signs* of change in energy — making it more concrete and actionable.
- **When to use:** Returning from vacation, back-to-office transitions, when team members may be feeling worn out or stressed, or as a regular team health check-in.
- **Group size:** Works for individuals and teams; suitable for remote, hybrid, or in-person settings.
- **Duration:** Short check-in activity; fits within 10–15 minutes depending on group size.
- **How it works:**
  1. Introduce the battery metaphor — your energy level as a battery charge.
  2. Instead of asking "what charges/drains your battery?", ask people to identify *signs*:
     - What are the signs that your battery is getting charged? How do you notice it? How does your team notice it?
     - What are the signs that your battery is being drained? What are the early warning signals?
  3. Carsten's personal example: when he turns off the radio during his commute (normally he listens to podcasts), that's a telltale sign his battery is draining.
  4. Once signs are shared, move to actions: given these signs, what concrete steps can you take?
     - For charge signs: what can you do to create more of those moments?
     - For drain signs: what can you change, hand over, or ask for help with?
  5. Encourage people to say the signs out loud — some may be subtle or easier for others to spot.

## Key Principles
- The classic version of this exercise ("what charges/drains your battery?") tends to surface generic answers — time off, fun assignments vs. tedious chores — which don't lead to useful team conversations.
- Reframing around *signs of change* makes the exercise more specific, personal, and actionable.
- Early warning signs of drain are critical to name explicitly — they help people catch burnout before it becomes a crisis.
- Some warning signs are internal; others are more visible to teammates. Sharing them creates shared awareness and opens space for support.
- The exercise bridges self-reflection and team action: from noticing → to naming → to doing something about it.

## Facilitation Tips
- Use the signs-first approach before discussing activities: "What does it look like when your battery is charged/drained?" before "What causes it?"
- Invite people to share personal examples to model vulnerability and make it safe for others (Carsten models this by sharing his own radio-in-the-car sign).
- After surfacing signs, always close the loop with action: what can I do, delegate, or ask for help with?
- A thermometer can be used as an alternative metaphor if battery doesn't resonate with the group.

## Best Used When
- Teams are returning from a break or transitioning back to office and need a re-entry check-in.
- There are early signals of stress, low morale, or unsustainable pace in the team.
- You want a low-barrier opening exercise that also has real depth if people engage authentically.
- You want to build psychological safety around mental health and energy without making it feel heavy.
