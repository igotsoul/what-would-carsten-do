# "Resource Gossip" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten introduces "Resource Gossiping," an exercise he learned from a coach named Elvira, designed to surface and align expectations when a new team member joins. By letting the team speak openly about their expectations — while the newcomer listens but cannot be seen — the exercise creates transparency and prevents misalignment from growing into frustration.

## Methods & Techniques

### Resource Gossip (ab 00:20)
- **What:** A structured exercise where a team openly discusses their expectations about a person (typically a newcomer) while that person sits with their back turned to the group and listens.
- **When to use:** When a new team member is joining and the team has already started forming unspoken expectations. Ideal for making implicit assumptions explicit before they create problems.
- **Group size:** A team (small to medium group); one person is the subject of the exercise.
- **Duration:** Not specified; suited for a short team session given the 2:27 video format.
- **How it works:**
  1. Have the team sit in a circle (around a table works well).
  2. The person being "gossiped about" turns their back to the group — they can hear but not see who is speaking.
  3. The team discusses openly: Who is this person? What are their strengths? What do we expect from them?
  4. The subject does not intervene during this round.
  5. When the round is complete, the person turns back around and shares: What are you taking away from this discussion? What surprised you most?
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=xH-hZqKuITQ

## Key Principles
- When a new team member is announced, people naturally start forming expectations (via googling, word of mouth, etc.) — these silent expectations can grow into misalignment or frustration if left unspoken.
- Making expectations explicit and discussable is more powerful than leaving them implicit.
- The physical setup (back turned) serves two purposes: the subject can hear authentically, and the rest of the team is not distracted by reading the subject's emotional reactions.
- Attribution: Carsten credits this exercise to a coach named Elvira.

## Facilitation Tips
- The exercise "might seem a bit extreme" (Carsten's words) — prepare the group by framing it as a positive, constructive activity, not a critique session.
- The closing reflection question is key: ask the subject what they are *taking away* and what *surprised* them most — this personalizes the learning and closes the loop.
- Ensure the gossip round stays focused on strengths, expectations, and hopes — not concerns or criticism — to keep the tone constructive.

## Best Used When
- A new team member is about to join or has just joined.
- The team has started forming expectations informally and those need to surface.
- The team wants to create psychological safety and openness from the start of a new working relationship.
