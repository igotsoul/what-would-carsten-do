# "The IKEA Effect" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten Lützen introduces the IKEA Effect — a cognitive bias described by Michael Norton in 2011 — and explains how facilitators and change agents can deliberately leverage it. The core insight is that people assign disproportionately higher value to things they helped create, which means involving participants in shaping workshops and conversations directly increases their commitment and perceived value of the outcome.

## Methods & Techniques

### Co-creating the Workshop Agenda (ab 02:30)
- **What:** Instead of presenting a pre-set agenda, open the session by asking participants what they want to have happen, then build the agenda together in the room.
- **When to use:** At the start of any workshop or team session where buy-in and engagement are critical.
- **Group size:** Any size.
- **Duration:** 5–10 minutes at session opening.
- **How it works:** Ask "What is it that you would like to have happen today?" Capture responses visibly. Because participants have heard, seen, and been part of shaping the agenda, they invest more effort into making the session valuable.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9rXeHiIUdXE&t=150s

### 1-2-4-All Content Engagement (ab 02:45)
- **What:** A structured conversation format where participants first reflect individually, then in pairs, then in groups of four, before sharing with the whole room — followed by a debrief.
- **When to use:** When introducing a topic or prompt where you want broad engagement and deep ownership of the ideas surfaced.
- **Group size:** Any size; scales well with larger groups.
- **Duration:** Roughly 8–12 minutes (1 min solo, 2 min pairs, 4 min fours, debrief).
- **How it works:** 1. One minute individually and in silence. 2. Two minutes in pairs. 3. Four minutes in groups of four. 4. Share out to the full group. 5. Debrief. The progressive build-up means participants arrive at group discussion having already invested effort, increasing perceived value of the conversation.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9rXeHiIUdXE&t=160s

### Best Hopes Check-in (ab 03:10)
- **What:** Opening a session by asking participants "What is your best hope for today?" to surface expectations and co-own the session goal.
- **When to use:** At the start of workshops, team sessions, or coaching conversations to establish shared intent and increase participant investment.
- **Group size:** Any size.
- **Duration:** 5 minutes.
- **How it works:** Ask "What is your best hope?" at the opening. Follow up at the close with "What will you take away as the best use of your time?" The bookend structure reinforces ownership and reflection.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9rXeHiIUdXE&t=185s

### How Might We (ab 03:20)
- **What:** A reframing technique that challenges or deepens an idea by prefixing it with "How might we…?" — keeping ownership with the group rather than the facilitator.
- **When to use:** When you want to probe or stretch an idea without taking ownership of it away from participants; useful in problem-framing and ideation phases.
- **Group size:** Any size.
- **Duration:** Varies; can be used as a brief interjection or as a structured exercise.
- **How it works:** When a topic or idea arises, reframe it as "How might we do this or that?" The phrasing signals openness and collective ownership, deepening involvement without the facilitator steering the conclusion.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9rXeHiIUdXE&t=195s

## Key Principles
- The IKEA Effect (Norton, 2011): people assign approximately 60–65% more value to things they helped create compared to an equivalent pre-made product.
- Effort creates perceived value — the same applies to workshops, change initiatives, and team agreements: participation inflates perceived worth.
- Teams are more committed to outcomes they helped shape. This is the foundational argument for co-creation as a facilitation default.
- Teaching from the back of the room is more effective than delivering from the front precisely because it activates the IKEA Effect.
- Organizations like IKEA and LEGO have built their business models on this bias — participants/customers value the experience of building more than receiving a finished product.
- Carsten's guiding principle: "We love what we build — so let's build together."

## Facilitation Tips
- Default to co-creation over presentation wherever possible — even small acts of co-creation (choosing the agenda, naming norms) increase commitment.
- Use the 1-2-4-All structure to give every participant a chance to invest effort before group discussion; this raises the perceived value of the conversation for everyone.
- Use "How might we?" as a non-directive challenge tool — it keeps the group's ownership intact while still deepening the thinking.
- Bookend sessions with Best Hopes (opening) and takeaway reflection (closing) to reinforce participant ownership of the session's value.
- Silence at the start of reflection (the individual minute in 1-2-4-All) is intentional — let people invest effort in their own thinking before socializing it.

## Best Used When
- Designing workshops where participant buy-in and post-session follow-through matter.
- Leading change initiatives where teams need to own the direction, not just receive it.
- Opening any session where energy or commitment feels low — co-creating even part of the agenda shifts the dynamic.
- Coaching or training facilitators on why participant involvement is not just a nice-to-have but a psychological lever.
