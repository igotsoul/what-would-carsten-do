# "Sprint Goal" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten Lützen makes the case for always starting with a Sprint Goal before building a Sprint Backlog, arguing that the goal — not the list of tasks — is what keeps a team focused and aligned. He shares two practical techniques for crafting meaningful Sprint Goals: the Miracle Question from solution-focused coaching and 10 Powerful Questions from The Liberators. He also offers a reframe for daily stand-ups centered on the Sprint Goal rather than the board.

## Methods & Techniques

### Miracle Question for Sprint Goal Setting (ab 01:20)
- **What:** A solution-focused coaching technique adapted for Sprint Planning. The team imagines their Fairy Godmother completed the Sprint overnight — they wake up not knowing it's done. The question is: what signs would you, the team, and stakeholders notice that the Sprint was a success?
- **When to use:** During Sprint Planning when the team struggles to articulate a meaningful goal, or when the team tends to focus on task lists rather than outcomes.
- **Group size:** Full Scrum Team
- **Duration:** 10–15 minutes as a framing exercise within Sprint Planning
- **How it works:**
  1. Set the scene: "Imagine you go to bed tonight. During the night, your Fairy Godmother visits and the Sprint is magically done — a success."
  2. Ask: "You wake up tomorrow not knowing the Sprint is finished. What would be the signs?"
  3. Explore: How would *you* notice? How would the *team* notice? How would *stakeholders and users* notice?
  4. Use the answers to identify which outcomes matter most and build actions (backlog items) that move the needle toward those outcomes.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=urBqCmgJoAE&t=80

### Sprint Goal–Centered Stand-Up Reframe (ab 02:50)
- **What:** A simple facilitation move to refocus a Daily Scrum that has drifted into task-reporting or board-walking. Instead of asking "what did you do / what will you do", Carsten asks: "What have we done to move us closer to the Sprint Goal?"
- **When to use:** When the daily stand-up feels scattered, when people talk about everything at once, or when the team is losing sight of the Sprint Goal mid-Sprint.
- **Group size:** Scrum Team
- **Duration:** Replaces or supplements the standard daily check-in format
- **How it works:**
  1. Notice that the conversation is drifting from the Sprint Goal.
  2. Ask: "What have we done to move us closer to the Sprint Goal — and why?"
  3. Center discussion on that question rather than the board.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=urBqCmgJoAE&t=170

## Key Principles
- Set the Sprint Goal *before* building the Sprint Backlog — goal first, tasks second.
- Don't obsess over completing every Sprint Backlog item. The Sprint Goal is the real measure of success. Unfinished items may simply mean you avoided unnecessary work.
- A Sprint Goal creates **coherence** — it helps the whole team move in one specific direction rather than pulling in different directions.
- Sprint Goals work even outside Scrum; any timebox with a defined goal serves the same focusing purpose (call them iteration goals if you prefer).
- Good Sprint Goal questions focus on what **stakeholders and users will be able to do** after the Sprint — outcome, not output.

## Facilitation Tips
- When the team gets stuck defining a Sprint Goal, shift to outcome-oriented questions: "What will our stakeholders be able to do after this Sprint? How will they notice it's done?"
- The Miracle Question works because it forces a shift from task-thinking to outcome-thinking — the team describes success before defining the work.
- Carsten references **10 Powerful Questions for Sprint Goals** by The Liberators as a complementary resource (link in video description).
- Use the Sprint Goal as an anchor throughout the Sprint — in stand-ups, in conversations, in scope decisions.

## Best Used When
- A team is new to Sprint Goals and needs a concrete technique to formulate one.
- Sprint Planning sessions tend to jump straight to tasks without agreeing on a shared outcome.
- Daily stand-ups have become board-walks disconnected from the Sprint's purpose.
- A team wants to reduce wasteful work by staying focused on what actually matters for the Sprint.
