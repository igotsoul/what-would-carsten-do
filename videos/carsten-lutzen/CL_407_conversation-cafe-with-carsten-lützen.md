# Conversation Cafe with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten introduces Conversation Cafe, a Liberating Structure for structured group dialogue. The format ensures every participant gets equal time to form and express their opinion without being dominated by extroverts. It uses a talking stick and four sequential rounds to move from individual reflection to open discussion and concrete takeaways.

## Methods & Techniques

### Conversation Cafe (ab 00:20)
- **What:** A structured dialogue format using a talking stick and timed rounds to give every participant equal voice before opening into free discussion.
- **When to use:** When a group needs to discuss a meaty question and you want to prevent dominant voices from crowding out quieter participants. Works well as a team discussion format within a larger workshop or retrospective.
- **Group size:** Small to medium groups (chairs in a circle implied)
- **Duration:** ~40–60 minutes total (depending on open discussion length chosen)
- **How it works:**
  1. **Pose the question** — Choose a focused prompt relevant to the team (e.g., "What would we like to achieve?" or "How do we overcome this obstacle?").
  2. **Set up the space** — Chairs in a circle, talking stick ready (can be a ball, pencil, or any object).
  3. **Round 1 — Sharing (1 min/person):** Pass the talking stick around. Each person has 1 minute to share their thoughts on the question. Skipping is allowed. No discussion — sharing only.
  4. **Round 2 — Reflections (1 min/person):** Pass the stick again. Each person shares reflections on *what they heard* in Round 1. Still no discussion.
  5. **Round 3 — Open discussion (15–40 min):** Free discussion. The team can choose to keep or drop the talking stick. Carsten notes he may reintroduce it if quieter voices are being left out.
  6. **Round 4 — Takeaways (5–10 min):** Structured closing round to capture concrete takeaways from the session.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zzQfRklEbQk

## Key Principles
- The talking stick is a device for equality, not control — only the holder speaks.
- The first two rounds are explicitly non-discussion: they create space for reflection before reaction.
- Participants may skip their turn if they have nothing to add, reducing pressure.
- A timekeeper (separate from the facilitator or the facilitator themselves) enforces the 1-minute limit.
- The facilitator monitors *process*, not content — Carsten's framing: "I will not monitor the content but I will make sure that everybody is heard."

## Facilitation Tips
- Appoint a dedicated timekeeper if running multiple groups simultaneously.
- In Round 3, offer the talking stick as an option — only reintroduce it actively if you observe people being talked over or unable to speak.
- The question framing matters: make it open enough for reflection but specific enough to be relevant to the team's current situation.
- The final takeaway round prevents the conversation from dissolving without actionable output.

## Best Used When
- A team needs to discuss a challenging or sensitive topic and quieter voices risk being drowned out.
- You want structured dialogue before moving into open discussion.
- A group tends to jump to debate before everyone has had a chance to think and speak.
- As a contained segment within a larger workshop or retrospective agenda.
