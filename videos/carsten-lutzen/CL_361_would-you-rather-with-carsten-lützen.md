# "Would You Rather" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten introduces "Would You Rather," a simple icebreaker he reacquainted himself with through a colleague named Kasper. Participants pass a (virtual or physical) ball and answer dilemma questions — choosing between two unusual situations — while explaining their reasoning. The format works in-person and online.

## Methods & Techniques

### Would You Rather (ab 00:10)
- **What:** An icebreaker where participants receive a ball and must choose between two dilemma options, then explain their reasoning. Questions range from playful to work-related.
- **When to use:** At the start of a meeting or workshop to warm up the group, spark reflection, and get people talking.
- **Group size:** Any team size
- **Duration:** ~5–10 minutes (flexible)
- **How it works:**
  1. Prepare dilemma questions in advance (e.g., on slides or cards) — don't improvise on the spot.
  2. One person holds the (virtual or physical) ball and receives a question.
  3. They choose between the two options and, crucially, explain *why*.
  4. They pass the ball to the next person, who gets the next question.
  5. Repeat until the group is warmed up.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=I27aE8IufIc&t=0s

## Key Principles
- The "and why?" follow-up is the most important part — it surfaces reasoning and reveals how people handle impossible choices.
- Questions should be prepared in advance to keep energy and flow up.
- Questions can be work-themed (e.g., "Product Owner or Scrum Master?") or purely playful (e.g., superhero dilemmas) — mixing both works well.
- The strangeness or absurdity of the dilemmas is a feature, not a bug: it lowers the stakes and makes participation easy.

## Facilitation Tips
- Prepare all questions before the session — having them ready in a PowerPoint or slide deck makes online facilitation smooth.
- A virtual ball (e.g., naming who "receives" it next) works just as well as a physical one.
- Keep questions varied in tone; blend team-context dilemmas with light, imaginative ones.

## Best Used When
- Opening a team meeting, retrospective, or workshop and needing a low-barrier, fun warm-up.
- Working with remote or hybrid teams where physical energizers are not possible.
- You want to generate light conversation and learn something about how teammates think.
