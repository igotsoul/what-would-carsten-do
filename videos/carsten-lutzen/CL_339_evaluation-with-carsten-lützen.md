# "Evaluation" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten shares a lightweight session evaluation format adapted from the 4L retrospective framework. It helps facilitators collect structured feedback on what worked, what participants learned, and what was missing — closing with a Fist of Five vote on overall time well spent. The method works both in-person and online.

## Methods & Techniques

### 3L + Fist of Five Session Evaluation (ab 00:30)
- **What:** A post-session evaluation format using three feedback quadrants (Liked, Learned, Lacked) adapted from the 4L retro format, followed by a Fist of Five vote replacing the "Longed For" quadrant.
- **When to use:** At the end of any workshop, training, or meeting where you want structured participant feedback and a quick overall rating.
- **Group size:** Any; works for small and larger groups.
- **Duration:** 5–10 minutes depending on group size and depth of discussion.
- **How it works:**
  1. **Liked** — Participants write post-its on what they enjoyed or found valuable. Use this to identify what to continue doing.
  2. **Learned** — Participants note what they're taking away from the session — content insights, tools, or new ideas. Carsten notes it can be surprising what people pick up (e.g., praising the facilitation tool rather than the content).
  3. **Lacked** — Participants note what was missing — more breaks, deeper coverage of a topic, something not addressed. Carsten values this both as a venting outlet and as concrete input for improving future sessions.
  4. **Fist of Five vote** — Instead of "Longed For", participants rate the session 0–5. Zero = waste of time, Five = really great time well spent. In-person: raise hands or post-its. Online: pre-prepared numbered post-its on the virtual whiteboard that participants drag to their chosen number.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=AdunzpGUozA

## Key Principles
- Adapted from the 4L retrospective — familiar structure repurposed for session evaluation rather than team reflection.
- The "Lacked" quadrant serves a dual purpose: it gives participants a safe outlet to express what was missing, and it gives the facilitator concrete, actionable improvement points.
- The Fist of Five framing matters: Carsten frames it as "return on time invested" — did the time spent create the biggest possible impact? Time is framed as the most precious resource at work.
- How you define 0 and 5 shapes the responses — invest time in making those anchors meaningful for participants.

## Facilitation Tips
- Prepare numbered post-its in advance on the virtual whiteboard for the Fist of Five vote when working online — participants simply drag their number in.
- In-person: either use post-its for all quadrants or do the Fist of Five as a live hand raise.
- Don't be surprised if "Learned" captures feedback about facilitation tools rather than session content — it's valid, even if not what you hoped for.
- Use the emerging numbers from the Fist of Five cluster visually to read the room quickly.

## Best Used When
- Closing any workshop, training, or structured meeting where you want more than a thumbs-up/thumbs-down rating.
- You want to continuously improve your facilitation from session to session.
- You need a format that works equally well in-person and remotely without major adaptation.

## Related Concepts
- **4L Retrospective** — the source framework (Liked, Learned, Lacked, Longed For); Carsten has a separate video on this format.
- **Fist of Five** — a consensus-checking and voting technique used here as an overall session rating.
- **Return on Time Invested (ROTI)** — the framing Carsten uses for the Fist of Five anchor definition.
