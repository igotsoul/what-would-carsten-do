# "Ladder of Inference" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten introduces the Ladder of Inference, a mental model created by Chris Argyris, designed to help people slow down before jumping to conclusions or solution mode. The model walks through six sequential steps — from gathering raw data to taking action — encouraging practitioners to surface assumptions, challenge biases, and build well-grounded beliefs before acting.

## Methods & Techniques

### Ladder of Inference (ab 00:30)
- **What:** A six-rung mental model that structures the journey from observable data to action, preventing premature conclusions by making each reasoning step explicit.
- **When to use:** Whenever a team or individual is at risk of jumping to solutions too quickly, during problem analysis, retrospectives, conflict resolution, or any situation requiring shared understanding of a complex issue.
- **Group size:** Works as an individual mental model or as a group worksheet/facilitated exercise.
- **Duration:** Not specified; can be used as a quick reflective check or a full structured workshop exercise.
- **How it works:**
  1. **Available data** — Observe the system or environment and gather data as objectively as possible. Write it down.
  2. **Selected data** — Review what data you have and notice gaps. Ask: Why is there no data from this source? Check for unconscious omissions driven by bias (e.g., "this isn't important" or "nothing can be done about it anyway"). Add missing data before proceeding.
  3. **Interpretation** — Ask: Am I reading this data objectively, or do I already have a conclusion in mind? Generate multiple possible meanings for the data, including perspectives from others in the group or environment. Acknowledge that several truths may coexist.
  4. **Assumptions** — Test your assumptions: Is there data to back them up? Why are you assuming this — is it based on past experience elsewhere that may not apply here?
  5. **Conclusions** — Draw conclusions grounded in your interpretations and data. Document why you are concluding what you are concluding.
  6. **Beliefs & Actions** — Let conclusions form beliefs about the problem at hand. Only then take action, because wholehearted action requires solid belief. (Carsten references the Pyramid of Results model as a companion here.)

## Key Principles
- The model exists to interrupt the human tendency to jump directly from observation to conclusion or solution — a habit Carsten acknowledges in himself.
- Each rung of the ladder must be climbed in sequence; skipping steps is the core failure mode this model addresses.
- Biases operate silently: data gets excluded without conscious awareness. Making the selection step explicit helps surface what was quietly omitted.
- Assumptions need evidence. Carsten stresses asking "why" — why do we assume this, and does the data actually support it?
- Beliefs precede effective action. Acting without grounded beliefs produces half-hearted or misdirected effort.
- The model was created by Chris Argyris and is intended as a shared thinking tool, not just a private checklist.

## Facilitation Tips
- The Ladder can be used as a **mental model at the back of your head** (for coaches and facilitators running sessions) or handed out as a **worksheet** for groups to work through together — Carsten explicitly invites practitioners to choose the mode that fits their context.
- Connect the Ladder to **What / So What / Now What** (a Liberating Structure): the "What" maps to data gathering, the "So What" covers interpretation and assumptions, and the "Now What" maps to action — making the Ladder a useful underpinning for that structure.
- When facilitating the interpretation step, actively invite multiple perspectives. Carsten notes there are "probably several truths" in any data set — voicing this normalizes disagreement and opens dialogue.
- Encourage groups to **document assumptions and conclusions** in writing, not just discuss them verbally, so reasoning is visible and challengeable.

## Best Used When
- A team is rushing to solutions before fully understanding the problem.
- Retrospectives or reviews surface data that different people interpret very differently.
- Conflicts arise from people operating on unstated or unexamined assumptions.
- A coach wants to model rigorous thinking or teach critical reasoning to a team.
- Facilitating a What / So What / Now What session and wanting a deeper framework to anchor the "So What" phase.
