# Kanban with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten Lützen provides a concise practitioner overview of Kanban in IT contexts, contrasting it with Scrum's sprint-based approach. He explores the psychological and motivational trade-offs of working without fixed iterations, and highlights WIP limits as a key mechanism for maximizing flow and value delivery.

## Methods & Techniques

### WIP Limits (ab 04:20)
- **What:** Constraining the number of items that can be "In Progress" simultaneously — Carsten's default is one item per team member.
- **When to use:** Whenever a team tends to multitask or pull in new work when blocked, reducing throughput and focus.
- **Group size:** Any team size
- **Duration:** Ongoing practice, not a time-boxed activity
- **How it works:**
  1. Define a WIP limit for the "In Progress" column (e.g., 1 per person).
  2. When a team member is blocked, the constraint forces the team to swarm on the blocker rather than pulling new work.
  3. This keeps flow moving through the system and maximizes value reaching the customer.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=RtOcNVlmmp4&t=260

### Kanban Board for Non-IT Contexts (ab 03:10)
- **What:** Applying a simple To Do / Doing / Done board to everyday planning contexts outside software development.
- **When to use:** Any context requiring continuous flow of tasks — journalism, event planning, family scheduling — where sprint iterations would be unnecessary overhead.
- **Group size:** Individual to small group
- **Duration:** Ongoing
- **How it works:**
  1. Create three columns: To Do, In Progress, Done.
  2. Prioritize the backlog continuously.
  3. Pull the highest-priority item whenever capacity is available.
  4. Refine backlog item format as you learn what information is needed to sustain flow.

## Key Principles

- **Pull vs. push:** Original Toyota Kanban is pull-based; IT Kanban often becomes push-based as development teams push work forward rather than customers pulling it. Being aware of this distinction matters.
- **No iterations = faster course correction:** Without fixed sprints, teams can reprioritize at any moment, making Kanban better suited for support, maintenance, or rapidly changing priorities.
- **The Sisyphus problem:** Without sprint boundaries, engineers can lose their sense of progress — the backlog always looks full. Carsten identifies this as a real psychological cost of Kanban that teams and coaches must actively address.
- **Gamification through sprints:** Scrum sprints provide a natural sense of accomplishment ("this much got done!") that Kanban does not replicate automatically. Kanban teams need deliberate practices to create equivalent recognition.
- **Low process overhead as a feature:** Kanban's simplicity means less ceremony and explanation — teams can start executing immediately and let the process evolve organically.
- **Single-threaded focus:** Humans are effectively single-threaded; WIP limits enforce this reality and prevent the illusion of productive multitasking.

## Facilitation Tips

- As a coach or Scrum Master in a Kanban context, Carsten emphasizes the importance of **actively reminding team members of the progress they are making** — since the board doesn't provide the same visual "done" milestone a sprint does.
- Celebrate pushes to production or customer deliveries explicitly to compensate for the absence of sprint review ceremonies.
- When introducing Kanban, let the backlog item format evolve through usage rather than over-specifying it upfront.

## Best Used When

- Teams need to respond to continuously changing priorities (support, maintenance, ops).
- The business cannot or will not commit to sprint-length planning horizons.
- Overhead of Scrum ceremonies outweighs their benefit for the team's context.
- Introducing agile practices to non-technical audiences (the simplicity lowers the barrier to entry).

## Related Concepts

- **Scrum / Sprint cadence** — contrasted throughout as the iteration-based alternative with built-in achievement milestones
- **Toyota Production System / Original Kanban** — the manufacturing pull-system origin of the practice
- **Flow efficiency** — underlying principle behind WIP limits; maximize throughput by reducing context switching
- **Gamification** — referenced as the motivational mechanism that sprint completion provides and Kanban must compensate for
