# "Speech Bubbles" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten walks through how to draw speech bubbles in multiple styles for use on flip charts during facilitation sessions. He covers classic speech bubbles, thought clouds, exclamation and question variants, and conversation threads — all practical tools for visually capturing what participants say during workshops.

## Methods & Techniques

### Classic Speech Bubble (ab 00:45)
- **What:** A standard rounded speech bubble used to capture spoken contributions from participants on a flip chart.
- **When to use:** When noting down what someone has said during a session — distinguishes spoken input from other written content.
- **Group size:** Any
- **Duration:** Seconds to draw
- **How it works:**
  1. Write the text first — so you know how much space the bubble needs.
  2. Decide where the speech tail comes from (left, right, center).
  3. Draw the outline all the way around, adding the tail pointing toward the speaker's direction.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=RbkllPNSBDI&t=45

### Exclamation Bubble — Powerful Statement (ab 01:45)
- **What:** A speech bubble with a large exclamation mark in the corner to signal a strong or important statement was made.
- **When to use:** When a participant says something particularly impactful that you want to visually emphasize.
- **How it works:**
  1. Write the text first.
  2. Draw a large exclamation mark in the corner where the speech tail will be.
  3. Draw the bubble outline so the exclamation mark sits at the edge — inside or outside the bubble.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=RbkllPNSBDI&t=105

### Question Bubble (ab 02:30)
- **What:** A speech bubble with a large question mark in the tail corner, used to distinguish questions from answers or statements.
- **When to use:** When capturing open questions during a session so they're easy to scan and revisit.
- **How it works:**
  1. Write the text first.
  2. Draw a large question mark angled slightly — Carsten suggests leaning it toward the tail end.
  3. Place the question mark consistently in the same corner as the tail, regardless of direction, so all question bubbles are easy to scan at a glance.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=RbkllPNSBDI&t=150

### Thought Cloud (ab 03:15)
- **What:** A cloud-shaped bubble with small circles as the tail, representing a thought rather than spoken words — inspired by comic book conventions.
- **When to use:** When capturing what someone is thinking or reflecting on, rather than what they've said out loud.
- **How it works:**
  1. Write the text first.
  2. Draw the outline as a bumpy cloud shape instead of a smooth oval.
  3. Connect to the thinker using a series of small circles rather than a pointed tail.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=RbkllPNSBDI&t=195

### Shout / Provocation Bubble (ab 03:50)
- **What:** A jagged, spiky bubble shape used to represent shouting, strong emotion, provocation, or negativity.
- **When to use:** When someone says something provocative, negative, or expressed with intensity — the visual form signals the emotional charge.
- **How it works:**
  1. Write the text first.
  2. Draw the outline with sharp, angular points rather than soft curves.
  3. Match the tail style to the spiky shape so it looks consistent.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=RbkllPNSBDI&t=230

### Conversation Thread (ab 05:30)
- **What:** A sequence of connected speech bubbles used to capture a back-and-forth dialogue between participants.
- **When to use:** When documenting a conversation or exchange where the sequence and relationship between contributions matters.
- **How it works:**
  1. Plan the layout in advance — this requires more space than a single bubble.
  2. Draw the first bubble, then the second, connecting them visually to show sequence.
  3. For a reply, draw a bubble from the other direction and link it back to the original bubble it responds to.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=RbkllPNSBDI&t=330

### Walk the Talk Illustration (ab 04:30)
- **What:** A visual metaphor combining an icon (e.g., a ladder or thumbs up) inside or alongside a speech bubble to replace text with a symbol.
- **When to use:** When reinforcing a concept like "walk the talk" visually — or any time you want to make a point without words.
- **How it works:** Draw a speech bubble and place a relevant icon inside it instead of text. Carsten's example uses a ladder to illustrate "walk the talk."
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=RbkllPNSBDI&t=270

## Key Principles
- **Text first, always.** Write the content before drawing the bubble — this prevents bubbles that are too small or too large for the text.
- **Consistent placement of markers.** Carsten always places exclamation or question marks in the same corner as the speech tail, making flip charts scannable at a glance.
- **Visual form carries meaning.** The shape of the bubble (soft vs. spiky vs. cloud) communicates the nature of the contribution — statement, thought, question, or emotion — without extra words.
- **Walk the talk with your visuals.** Carsten emphasizes using icons inside bubbles as a way to model the principle of showing rather than just telling.
- **Color adds contrast.** Carsten tends to color speech bubbles yellow to make them stand out from the rest of the flip chart content.

## Facilitation Tips
- Use speech bubbles to visually distinguish participant contributions from facilitator-written content on the flip chart.
- Standardize your bubble types across a session so participants can quickly read the visual language (cloud = thought, spiky = strong emotion, etc.).
- Plan layout before drawing conversation threads — they need more horizontal space than single bubbles.
- Adding shading and color after drawing the bubbles makes them pop and adds visual hierarchy to the flip chart.
- Speech bubbles can double as banners for headings or titles.

## Best Used When
- Capturing participant voices during workshops, retrospectives, or facilitated discussions.
- Creating process illustrations or visual summaries on flip charts.
- Differentiating between statements, questions, thoughts, and strong feelings in a visual record.
- Working in low-tech, in-person settings where flip charts are the primary visual medium.
