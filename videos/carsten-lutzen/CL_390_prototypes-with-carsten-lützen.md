# Prototypes with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten introduces three lightweight prototyping approaches — paper prototypes, Wizard of Oz, and pragmatic/custom prototyping — with the unifying principle: get learnings as fast and cheaply as possible. The video challenges teams to resist over-engineering their prototypes and instead match the technique to what they actually need to validate.

## Methods & Techniques

### Paper Prototype (ab 00:30)
- **What:** A low-fidelity prototype using physical paper to simulate a user interface or flow. The user "clicks" by pointing, and the facilitator swaps paper sheets to simulate screen transitions.
- **When to use:** When you want to quickly validate a user flow or test whether a user is willing to take a specific action — before any digital implementation.
- **Group size:** Works best 1:1 or in very small groups, since the facilitator must manually swap paper sheets.
- **Duration:** Can be run in minutes per user session.
- **How it works:**
  1. Draw your screens/states on individual sheets of paper.
  2. Ask the user to point to where they'd click or interact.
  3. Swap to the appropriate next sheet based on their input.
  4. Observe and collect data on whether the flow makes sense.
- **Limitations:** Does not scale — requires the facilitator to be physically present with each test participant.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=1-Vfz2RQZ_Q&t=30

### Wizard of Oz Prototype (ab 01:50)
- **What:** A technique where a human (e.g., the Product Owner) operates "behind the scenes" to simulate a system that has not yet been built, while the test subject believes they are interacting with a real product.
- **When to use:** When testing interaction-heavy products like chatbots, digital assistants, or other AI/automated systems where the backend is complex to implement but the interface can be mocked.
- **Group size:** Requires at least two people: one test subject and one "wizard" operating behind the scenes.
- **Duration:** Varies by scope of the interaction being simulated.
- **How it works:**
  1. Build or mock the front-end interface only.
  2. Have a team member (the "wizard") sit hidden and manually provide the system's responses in real time.
  3. The test subject interacts as if the system is real.
  4. Observe behavior and collect feedback without having built the actual backend.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=1-Vfz2RQZ_Q&t=110

### Pragmatic / Custom Prototyping (ab 02:50)
- **What:** A mindset prompt to be creative and choose the right prototyping technique for your specific validation goal — whether that's a high-fidelity prototype, wireframes, or a minimal proof-of-concept.
- **When to use:** When neither paper prototyping nor Wizard of Oz fits your needs, or when you're testing technical feasibility rather than user flow.
- **How it works:**
  1. Define clearly what you want to validate: user flow, technical feasibility, desirability, etc.
  2. Research available techniques suited to your goal.
  3. Choose the most lightweight option that still yields meaningful data.
  4. Implement just enough to get the learning — no more.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=1-Vfz2RQZ_Q&t=170

## Key Principles
- The goal of any prototype is to **get learnings as fast and as cheaply as possible** — not to build something impressive.
- Prototyping is a broad term; resist defaulting to one approach. Match the technique to what you actually need to validate.
- If a prototype takes years to build, it may have made more sense to build the real thing — **know when to stop prototyping**.
- Distinguish between what you're validating: **user flow**, **user willingness**, or **technical feasibility** — each may call for a different technique.
- Engineers often use prototypes to validate feasibility of algorithms before committing to full implementation — this is equally valid as user-facing prototyping.

## Facilitation Tips
- When running a paper prototype session, physically swap paper sheets in response to user input — the simplicity is the point, don't be embarrassed by it.
- For Wizard of Oz tests, ensure the "wizard" is completely hidden or their role is not revealed until after the session to avoid biasing the participant.
- Carsten credits Ole Rich Henningsen from Syndicate for introducing him to the Wizard of Oz technique — worth attributing if you share it further.

## Best Used When
- A team is tempted to build a full product before validating whether the concept works.
- You need quick user feedback on a flow or interaction model without engineering investment.
- You're exploring a technically complex feature (e.g., chatbot, AI assistant) and want to test desirability before feasibility.
- A team is unsure which prototyping approach to use and needs a simple mental model to decide.

## Related Concepts
- **Lean Startup / Build-Measure-Learn** — the underlying philosophy Carsten's advice aligns with.
- **Wireframing** — mentioned as a valid lightweight alternative to high-fidelity prototypes.
- **Proof of Concept (PoC)** — distinguished from user-facing prototypes; used primarily to validate technical feasibility.
- **High-fidelity prototype** — mentioned as one end of the spectrum; looks like the real thing but has no real backend logic.
