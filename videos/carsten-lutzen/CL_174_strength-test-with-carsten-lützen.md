# "Strength Test" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten introduces a simple decision-quality exercise called the "Strength Test," learned from a colleague named Fleming. Using a three-circle Venn diagram, teams visually surface what a decision was actually based on — facts, user feedback, or gut feeling — creating transparency and sparking honest conversation about how sound their decisions really are.

## Methods & Techniques

### Strength Test — Decision Quality Check (ab 00:30)
- **What:** A quick Venn diagram exercise that reveals the basis of a team's decision by having participants dot-vote on whether the decision was driven by facts, user feedback, gut feeling, or a combination.
- **When to use:** After a decision has been made in a workshop or meeting, to surface assumptions and build shared understanding of why the decision was made.
- **Group size:** Any team size; works well in workshops and meetings.
- **Duration:** Very short — a few minutes to run, longer if it sparks deeper conversation.
- **How it works:**
  1. Identify a decision the team has already made.
  2. Draw three overlapping circles (Venn diagram style) labeled: **Facts** (hard data known to be true), **User Feedback** (input from actual users requesting the feature or direction), and **Gut Feeling** (intuition of the decision maker or team).
  3. Each participant individually and silently places a dot in the zone that best represents why they believe the decision was made — a single circle if one driver dominated, or an overlap zone if multiple drivers applied.
  4. Reveal the pattern of dots and use it as a conversation starter: How strong is this decision? Is the team aligned on its basis?
  5. The ideal outcome is a dot cluster in the center — all three drivers present.

## Key Principles
- Many decisions are made without conscious awareness of *why* or *on what basis* — this exercise makes that visible.
- Gut feeling is not inherently bad; the key is being *aware* that gut feeling is the driver.
- Gut feeling can be refined further to identify *whose* gut feeling — e.g., the decision maker's personal intuition.
- The strongest decisions sit at the intersection of all three: facts, user feedback, and gut feeling.
- This is not an exact science — its value lies in starting an honest conversation, not in producing a precise score.

## Facilitation Tips
- Instruct participants to vote **individually and in silence** before revealing results, to avoid groupthink.
- Frame the exercise as a conversation starter, not a judgment — avoid making anyone feel their decision was wrong.
- The overlap zones in the Venn diagram are just as valid as the single circles; encourage participants to use them.
- Keep it fast — the exercise itself is lightweight; the value is in the discussion it generates.

## Best Used When
- A team has just committed to a project, feature, or task and you want to pressure-test the decision before moving forward.
- There is a sense that decisions are being made on autopilot or without clear rationale.
- You want to build psychological safety around questioning decisions without directly challenging individuals.
- Time is limited but you still want meaningful reflection on decision quality.
