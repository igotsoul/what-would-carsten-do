# Why do retrospectives feel like a waste of time?

## Summary
Carsten diagnoses three common reasons retrospectives lose their value — nothing ever changes, format fatigue, and the facilitator wearing too many hats at once — and offers concrete strategies to address each. The video is a practical troubleshooting guide for scrum masters and agile coaches who want to turn retrospectives from a reluctant obligation into a meeting teams genuinely look forward to.

## Methods & Techniques

### 15% Solutions (ab 01:30)
- **What:** Reframe retrospective action items as small things individuals can do tomorrow — without extra time, money, or authority. Shifts focus away from big systemic fixes that require someone else to act.
- **When to use:** When teams repeatedly generate ambitious action items that never get implemented, or when "nothing changes" is the dominant complaint.
- **Group size:** Any
- **Duration:** Can be integrated into any retrospective format
- **How it works:** When an action item is proposed, ask: "What can *you* do tomorrow — without more hours or money — that moves this forward?" Pair this with a solution-focused question: "How will we know this problem is solved?"
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=LnL4tXonsw8&t=90

### Deliberate Format Rotation (ab 03:00)
- **What:** Proactively change the retrospective format before fatigue sets in, choosing each new format for a specific observed team need rather than picking randomly.
- **When to use:** When team energy is dropping, when you notice the first signs of "not this again," or when you observe a specific gap (e.g., poor peer feedback, difficulty expressing needs).
- **Group size:** Any
- **Duration:** Ongoing practice, not a one-off
- **How it works:** Observe what the team needs. Match a format to that need — Carsten suggests Know Your Neighbor when teams struggle to express needs to each other, or the Retro Wheel when appreciation and recognition are lacking. Run a format long enough to get value, then switch at the first sign of fatigue or when a new focus emerges. Use retromat.org or a curated playlist to build a format backlog.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=LnL4tXonsw8&t=180

### External or Rotating Facilitator (ab 05:00)
- **What:** Remove the scrum master or agile coach from the facilitator role so they can participate fully as a team member, eliminating role confusion.
- **When to use:** When the person facilitating is also a core team member who has opinions, ideas, or a stake in the outcome.
- **Group size:** Any
- **Duration:** Permanent structural change to how retrospectives are run
- **How it works:** Arrange a swap with a sister team — their agile coach or scrum master facilitates your retro, and yours facilitates theirs. No external consultant required. If a swap isn't possible, use a highly structured format like 1-2-4-All that does the facilitation work itself, allowing the scrum master to temporarily set down the facilitator hat and participate.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=LnL4tXonsw8&t=300

### 1-2-4-All for Silent Reflection (ab 05:45)
- **What:** A Liberating Structures exercise that builds in individual silent thinking before group discussion, reducing the need for active facilitation and allowing the facilitator to participate.
- **When to use:** When the facilitator also needs to be a participant, or when the team defaults to dominated group discussion without individual reflection.
- **Group size:** Any
- **Duration:** Varies; built into the structure of the exercise
- **How it works:** Participants think alone (1), then in pairs (2), then in fours (4), then share with the whole group (all). The structure drives the process so the facilitator doesn't have to manage every step.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=LnL4tXonsw8&t=345

### Best Hopes Opening (ab 06:30)
- **What:** Start a retrospective with no predetermined format by asking the team what would make this session the best use of their time, then build the session on the spot from their answer.
- **When to use:** When you're unsure what the team actually needs, when trust is low around retrospective value, or when standard formats feel disconnected from real concerns.
- **Group size:** Any
- **Duration:** Question takes 5–10 minutes; session length as agreed
- **How it works:** Ask: "At the end of this hour, what would need to have happened for this to have been the best use of your time?" Listen to the answers, then select exercises from your facilitation backpack (Liberating Structures, retromat, etc.) that address what the team has named. Arrive prepared with a range of exercises, but let the team's stated hopes shape which ones you use.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=LnL4tXonsw8&t=390

## Key Principles
- When retrospectives feel like a waste of time, Carsten places responsibility on the facilitator, not the team.
- Big solutions that require someone else to act are a trap — actionable personal commitments are more likely to produce visible change.
- A solution-focused lens (asking "how will we know this is fixed?") is more productive than dwelling on the problem itself.
- Format choice should be intentional and tied to a specific observed team need, not rotated for novelty's sake.
- Role clarity matters: a facilitator who is also a team member and also a process owner creates confusion about whose voice is speaking at any moment.
- Having a "facilitation backpack" — a ready set of exercises you can pull from on short notice — enables responsive, adaptive session design.

## Facilitation Tips
- Watch for early signs of format fatigue (sighs, low energy, "not this again") and change formats before resistance becomes entrenched.
- When choosing a new format, anchor the choice to something specific you've observed: "I noticed we struggle with X, so I'm trying Y."
- If you can't get an external facilitator, use a self-running structure like 1-2-4-All so you can participate without abandoning facilitation entirely.
- Don't go into the Best Hopes approach unprepared — bring a backpack of exercises so you can respond to whatever the team surfaces.
- Use retromat.org or a personal playlist of formats as a practical resource for format variety.

## Best Used When
- A scrum master or agile coach suspects their retrospectives have become routine or are generating low team engagement.
- Teams are producing action items that never get completed.
- The facilitator is embedded in the team and struggling with role separation.
- You want a diagnostic framework for troubleshooting retrospective health before redesigning the process.
