# "Discovery & Action Dialogue (Liberating Structure)" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten Lützen walks through the Discovery & Action Dialogue (DAD), a Liberating Structure designed for diving deep into a specific challenge and generating volunteer-driven action. The structure uses seven solution-focused questions to move a group from problem awareness through root cause exploration to concrete next steps — and is largely self-facilitated within small groups.

## Methods & Techniques

### Discovery & Action Dialogue (DAD) (ab 00:45)
- **What:** A structured group dialogue using seven sequential questions that guide participants from identifying problem signals through to volunteering for concrete actions. Solution-focused in orientation, it avoids dwelling on problems and instead surfaces contributions, root causes, inspiration, and ownership.
- **When to use:** When a team or group has a specific challenge they want to explore deeply and move toward action on. Works well after a brainstorm has surfaced a problem worth pursuing.
- **Group size:** 5–15 people per group. Carsten recommends erring toward smaller groups to allow more speaking time per person.
- **Duration:** 25–70 minutes. Carsten notes he rarely finishes in 25 minutes — 30–40 minutes is more typical in his experience.
- **How it works:**
  1. Define the challenge the group is working on and set the invitation.
  2. Assign one person in each group as the recorder/facilitator.
  3. Work through the seven questions (individually, then in pairs if large, then as a group):
     1. **How do you know the problem is present here?** — Identify observable signs that the problem exists and signals that it's improving.
     2. **How do you contribute to the solution?** — What can each person do to reduce or remove those signs?
     3. **What prevents you from doing this all the time?** — Surface barriers and underlying root causes.
     4. **Do you know someone who has solved it?** — Find inspiration inside or outside the team/company.
     5. **Do you have any ideas?** — Open space for creative suggestions that have surfaced during the dialogue.
     6. **What needs to be done, and who volunteers to do it?** — Identify concrete actions; only volunteers from within the group can take ownership.
     7. **Who else needs to be involved?** — Identify external people to bring in, while keeping internal volunteers accountable.
  4. Recap within groups; optionally debrief in plenary depending on workshop format.

## Key Principles
- The structure is **solution-focused**: the questions deliberately redirect energy away from problem analysis and toward contribution and action.
- **Volunteers, not voluntolds**: ownership is explicitly chosen, not assigned. This ensures that the people taking action are genuinely motivated to improve the situation.
- The dialogue is **largely self-facilitated** within each group — the seven questions do the heavy lifting, freeing the lead facilitator to move between groups rather than anchor any one conversation.
- Starting with "how do you know the problem is present?" forces the group to **operationalize the problem** — defining observable signs — before jumping to solutions.
- The question "what prevents you from doing this all the time?" is a powerful route to **root causes** without framing it as a blame exercise.

## Facilitation Tips
- Run the questions **individually first, then share in the group** (and in pairs if the group is large) — this prevents groupthink and gives quieter participants space to think.
- Keep groups toward the **smaller end of the 5–15 range** for richer conversations and more airtime per person.
- Set a clear **invitation** at the start: explain what the group will do, what the challenge is, and that seven questions will guide them.
- As the lead facilitator, you **don't need to be present in each group** throughout — the structure carries itself. Use your time to circulate and observe.
- Leave time for a **group recap** at the end; a plenary share-out is optional but useful depending on the overall workshop format.

## Best Used When
- A team or group has already identified a specific problem and is ready to move from diagnosis to action.
- You want to generate **volunteer-driven ownership** rather than top-down task assignment.
- You need a structure that can **run with minimal facilitation intervention** inside each group.
- The challenge being explored is one others may have faced — allowing the "do you know someone who solved it?" question to add real value.
