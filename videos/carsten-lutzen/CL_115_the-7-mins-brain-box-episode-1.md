# The 7 mins Brain Box - Episode 1

## Summary
Francesco (Frankie) and Carsten introduce the "Brain Box" — a collaborative experiment in which two practitioners independently visualize the same prompt under a strict 7-minute time constraint, then compare results. Episode 1 has Francesco thinking out loud as he sketchnotes "a coaching conversation with a leader," demonstrating how timed constraints and visual metaphor selection work in practice.

## Methods & Techniques

### The Brain Box Format (ab 00:20)
- **What:** Two or more people independently visualize the same randomly selected prompt within a strict 7-minute time limit, then share and compare their outputs asynchronously.
- **When to use:** Warm-ups for visual facilitation training, exploring how different practitioners interpret the same concept, or energizing creative ideation in pairs or small groups.
- **Group size:** 2+ (works well in pairs)
- **Duration:** 7 minutes per round; comparison and debrief as needed
- **How it works:**
  1. Prepare a set of prompt cards (e.g., "illustrate a coaching conversation with a leader").
  2. Draw one prompt randomly.
  3. Each participant sets a 7-minute timer and works independently — sketching, drawing, or visualizing whatever comes to mind.
  4. Participants share their outputs and discuss similarities, differences, and surprises.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=RhuAXrC1Pyc&t=20

### Thinking Out Loud as a Visual Process (ab 01:30)
- **What:** Narrating your internal reasoning while sketching — articulating metaphor choices, dead ends, and pivots in real time — as both a learning tool and a transparency practice.
- **When to use:** When demonstrating visual facilitation to others, in coaching or training contexts, or when you want to make implicit creative decisions visible.
- **How it works:** As you draw, verbalize every decision: what you notice, what metaphors you're considering, why you reject or accept them. Treat the commentary as equally valuable as the drawing.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=RhuAXrC1Pyc&t=90

### Visual Metaphor Selection for Abstract Concepts (ab 02:30)
- **What:** A micro-process for translating abstract concepts (coaching, leadership, conversation) into simple visual symbols by interrogating each word separately, then combining the results.
- **When to use:** Whenever you need to represent a complex or intangible idea in a sketch, graphic recording, or visual summary.
- **How it works:**
  1. Break the prompt into its component nouns/verbs (e.g., "coaching," "conversation," "leader").
  2. For each component, brainstorm candidate symbols (e.g., a leader → dollar sign, light bulb, column/pillar).
  3. Choose symbols that work together compositionally and carry the right connotation.
  4. Use layout and directionality intentionally — Francesco notes that in left-to-right reading cultures, placing the speaker on the left implies they initiate; swapping creates a different dynamic.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=RhuAXrC1Pyc&t=150

## Key Principles
- **Constraints unlock creativity.** Francesco and Carsten frame the 7-minute limit not as a restriction but as a tool — pressure collapses perfectionism and forces instinctive choices.
- **Asynchronous co-creation generates surprise.** Working independently before comparing amplifies divergence and makes the debrief more generative than if both had collaborated in real time.
- **Directionality carries meaning.** In visual communication, where figures face and which direction speech flows signals relationships and roles. Layout is not neutral.
- **Unexpected discoveries are data.** Francesco notes mid-sketch that he would never have predicted a certain element capturing his attention — treating surprises as insights rather than distractions is part of the practice.
- **Identity informs visual choices.** Francesco consciously chooses the more "active" coach configuration to reflect his own professional shift — visual metaphors express practitioner values, not just neutral descriptions.

## Facilitation Tips
- When running the Brain Box with a group, randomize the prompt selection visibly — the element of chance reduces overthinking and makes the constraint feel playful rather than imposed.
- Encourage participants to narrate their process, not just produce a drawing. The verbal layer reveals assumptions about concepts that are worth surfacing in the debrief.
- Keep the comparison phase open-ended: ask "what surprised you about the other person's interpretation?" rather than evaluating whose drawing is "better."
- Use monochrome or grayscale deliberately when figures are secondary to the concept — as Francesco does to signal "these people matter less than the dynamic between them."
