# "Pen Nibs" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten Lützen, agile coach and Scrum Master, walks through three types of pen nibs and when to use each for flip chart work. The video provides practical guidance on matching pen tip to purpose — writing for readability, precise drawing, or artistic illustration — helping facilitators get better visual results with minimal effort.

## Methods & Techniques

### Wedge Tip for Flip Chart Writing (ab 00:20)
- **What:** A wedge-shaped nib that produces bold, variable-width strokes naturally suited for large, readable text on flip charts.
- **When to use:** Any time you are writing text on a flip chart intended to be read at a distance.
- **Group size:** Any — this is about output legibility for an audience.
- **Duration:** N/A — a tool choice, not a timed activity.
- **How it works:** Hold the pen with fingers at the marked grip points so the wedge sits at the correct angle. The nib's shape creates natural thick-and-thin variation that makes letterforms bold and easy to read from afar. Fine lines are possible but require practice.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=73Jf4i8GO2Y&t=20

### Round Brush Nib for Precise Drawing (ab 01:40)
- **What:** A round-tipped nib that produces uniform line width regardless of stroke direction, making it ideal for diagrams, shapes, and detailed drawings.
- **When to use:** When drawing circles, icons, diagrams, or any visual where consistent, even lines matter more than bold text weight.
- **How it works:** Because the tip is symmetrical, line width stays constant no matter the angle or direction of the stroke. Carsten contrasts this directly with the wedge nib, which produces thicker lines on certain stroke directions.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=73Jf4i8GO2Y&t=100

### Brush Nib for Expressive Drawing (ab 03:00)
- **What:** A flexible brush-style nib capable of producing a full range of line widths from very fine to broad in a single stroke.
- **When to use:** When you want artistic, dynamic visuals — e.g., motion lines, organic shapes, illustrations with depth and expression.
- **How it works:** Vary pressure to control line width. Light pressure yields a very fine line; heavier pressure creates a broad stroke. Carsten notes it is not well suited for writing, as the soft tip risks damage if pressed too hard against paper repeatedly.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=73Jf4i8GO2Y&t=180

### Fine Detail Brush for Small Elements (ab 04:10)
- **What:** An extra-fine, soft brush nib for adding small details and precise marks to visual work.
- **When to use:** When you need fine detail that larger nibs cannot produce cleanly.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=73Jf4i8GO2Y&t=250

## Key Principles
- Match the nib to the job: writing, precise drawing, and expressive drawing each have an optimal tip type.
- Readability at distance is the primary criterion for flip chart writing — the wedge nib serves this best.
- Uniform line width is not always desirable; the natural variation of a wedge nib improves text aesthetics, while round nibs are better when consistency matters.
- Protecting your tools matters: using a brush nib for writing can damage it over time due to the pressure required.

## Facilitation Tips
- Carsten's go-to for all flip chart writing is the **wedge tip** — default to this unless there is a specific reason to switch.
- Keep a **round nib** available when you know the session will involve drawing diagrams or models alongside text.
- Reserve the **brush nib** for moments when you want to add visual energy or illustration quality to your outputs.
- Finger placement on the marked grip points of the wedge nib is key to getting the correct angle and consistent results.
