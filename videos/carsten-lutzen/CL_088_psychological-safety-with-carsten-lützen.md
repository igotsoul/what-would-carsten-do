# "Psychological Safety" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten Lützen presents a two-dimensional model for understanding psychological safety, mapping Safety/Danger on one axis and Comfort/Discomfort on the other. The model generates four cultural quadrants and helps coaches and facilitators identify where their team currently operates, where they want to move, and how cultural biases affect individual perceptions of safety.

## Methods & Techniques

### The Two-Dimensional Psychological Safety Model (ab 00:45)
- **What:** A 2x2 model plotting Safety vs. Danger on the x-axis and Comfort vs. Discomfort on the y-axis, producing four distinct cultural quadrants.
- **When to use:** When diagnosing team culture, opening a conversation about psychological safety, or helping a team understand why certain behaviours are happening.
- **Group size:** Any team size; individual reflection recommended before group discussion.
- **Duration:** Suitable as a 10–20 minute framing exercise or discussion anchor.
- **How it works:**
  1. Draw or share the 2x2 grid with axes: Safety–Danger (horizontal) and Comfort–Discomfort (vertical).
  2. Explain each quadrant (see Key Principles below).
  3. Ask team members to individually place themselves on the grid (anonymously if safety is low).
  4. Facilitate a discussion about the spread of responses and what it means for how the team works together.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=1WZoLwEnlhg&t=45

### Anonymous Self-Placement Exercise (ab 05:30)
- **What:** Team members anonymously indicate where they personally sit on the psychological safety model, surfacing individual variation without requiring public vulnerability.
- **When to use:** When you suspect the team lacks sufficient safety to have an open conversation about safety itself.
- **Group size:** Any.
- **Duration:** 5–10 minutes.
- **How it works:** Distribute the model (physically or digitally), ask each person to mark their current perceived position anonymously, then aggregate and discuss patterns as a group.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=1WZoLwEnlhg&t=330

## Key Principles

- **Safety ≠ Comfort.** Comfort and safety are independent dimensions. You can feel comfortable in a dangerous environment (repressive culture) and uncomfortable in a safe one (constructive dissent).
- **Four cultural quadrants:**
  - *Comfort + Danger* → **Repressive culture**: people can be hurt but nobody speaks up.
  - *Comfort + Safety* → **Consensus culture**: agreement is required for everything; the team goes nowhere.
  - *Discomfort + Danger* → **Hostile culture**: the worst place to be; avoid at all costs.
  - *Discomfort + Safety* → **Constructive dissent**: where growth and innovation happen; the target zone.
- **Oscillation is healthy.** Carsten notes that people cannot sustain constructive dissent indefinitely. Moving back to the comfort/safety quadrant to recharge is normal and necessary, not a failure.
- **The cliff effect.** Even when operating in constructive dissent, only one or two poorly-phrased remarks can accidentally push a team member into the hostile culture quadrant. The move from constructive to hostile is short; the reverse is long.
- **Recovery requires sequencing.** There is no direct path from hostile culture to constructive dissent. Teams must first rebuild comfort, then rebuild safety, and only then deliberately introduce productive discomfort.
- **Perception is individual.** A facilitator or coach may believe they are operating in constructive dissent while a team member experiences the same interaction as hostile. Safety is felt individually, not collectively.
- **Cultural bias matters.** National and organisational culture influences default quadrant. Carsten uses Denmark's consensus-leaning culture as an example of a structural pull toward the comfort/safety quadrant.

## Facilitation Tips

- Be aware of your own default quadrant as a coach. Carsten acknowledges his natural gravitational pull is toward consensus culture (comfort + safety) as a people-pleaser, and that moving to constructive dissent requires a deliberate choice.
- Do not assume your team members share your self-assessment. Run the anonymous placement exercise before drawing conclusions about where "the team" is.
- Use this model as a conversation starter, not a diagnosis. Its value is in giving teams a shared language to talk about experiences that are often left unnamed.
- If the team is in or near hostile culture, do not try to jump straight to constructive dissent. Design activities that first restore comfort, then slowly re-introduce safety before pushing into productive discomfort.
- Normalise the discomfort in the constructive dissent quadrant explicitly — tell people it is supposed to feel uneasy and that the discomfort paired with safety is exactly where growth lives.

## Best Used When

- Kicking off a psychological safety conversation with a team that has no shared framework yet.
- Debriefing after a retro or session where something felt "off" in team dynamics.
- Coaching an agile coach or Scrum Master who is stuck in people-pleasing behaviour.
- Designing a team intervention after a period of conflict or low trust.
- Helping leadership understand why "just make it safe to speak up" is not actionable without addressing the comfort dimension separately.
