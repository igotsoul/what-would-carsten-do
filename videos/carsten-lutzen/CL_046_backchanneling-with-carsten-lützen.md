# "Backchanneling" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten Lützen introduces the concept of backchanneling — the verbal and nonverbal signals a coach uses to show active listening and encourage the coachee to continue. He outlines a progression from passive to more active backchanneling techniques, highlights the risks of mechanical or over-done responses, and emphasizes the importance of adapting to each individual coachee's needs.

## Methods & Techniques

### Passive Backchanneling — Facial Expressions (ab 01:00)
- **What:** Using facial expressions and subtle nonverbal cues to signal attention without interrupting the flow of conversation.
- **When to use:** Throughout any coaching conversation as a baseline presence signal.
- **Group size:** 1:1 coaching
- **Duration:** Continuous throughout conversation
- **How it works:** Maintain attentive, responsive facial expressions that gently encourage the coachee to continue speaking without breaking their train of thought.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=sITAD8dg02c&t=60

### Paraphrasing (ab 02:00)
- **What:** Reflecting back what the coachee said in slightly different words to make them feel seen, heard, and respected.
- **When to use:** After the coachee shares something significant; helps deepen their sense of being understood.
- **Group size:** 1:1 coaching
- **Duration:** Brief, a sentence or two
- **How it works:** Listen carefully, then restate the essence of what was said back to the coachee. Research supports this as effective for making people feel heard.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=sITAD8dg02c&t=120

### Mirroring and Reflective Questions (ab 02:40)
- **What:** Demonstrating deeper listening by mirroring language or asking questions that reflect the coachee's own words and feelings back to them.
- **When to use:** When you want to help the coachee explore what is really going on beneath the surface.
- **Group size:** 1:1 coaching
- **Duration:** Interspersed throughout conversation
- **How it works:** Pick up on specific words or themes the coachee uses and mirror them back, or form a question that reflects their deeper meaning. This is verbal but serves the backchanneling function of showing attentiveness.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=sITAD8dg02c&t=160

### Engaged Brief Comments (ab 03:10)
- **What:** Short, supportive responses that acknowledge what the coachee is saying and invite them to continue — e.g., "That sounds really important to you — what happened then?"
- **When to use:** When you want to show engagement and keep momentum without shifting into a full coaching question.
- **Group size:** 1:1 coaching
- **Duration:** Very brief — a sentence at most
- **How it works:** Carsten distinguishes these from formal coaching questions: they belong in the backchanneling category because the primary purpose is to signal active listening and invite continuation, not to redirect the conversation.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=sITAD8dg02c&t=190

## Key Principles
- Backchanneling encompasses both nonverbal (facial expressions, nods) and verbal (paraphrasing, reflective questions, brief comments) signals.
- The core purpose is to signal active listening, make the coachee feel seen and heard, and build psychological safety.
- When backchanneling becomes mechanical, it signals the coach has stopped truly listening — the technique itself undermines its own purpose.
- Variety matters: using the same response pattern repeatedly (e.g., always saying "awesome" or always asking "what else?") feels robotic and erodes trust.
- Over-affirming everything — nodding at everything, responding with "great!" to every statement — feels insincere and damages the coaching relationship.
- Different coachees need different levels of affirmation: some need frequent backchanneling to feel supported; others need only minimal prompting and will drive their own thinking.

## Facilitation Tips
- Carsten warns against letting backchanneling become automatic habit — staying genuinely curious about the coachee is the foundation that keeps it authentic.
- Avoid over-nodding or blanket affirmations ("awesome", "great") for everything — reserve them for moments that genuinely warrant them.
- Do not repeat the same technique in a loop (e.g., "what else? what else? what else?") — vary your responses to demonstrate real engagement.
- Read the individual coachee: calibrate how much active backchanneling they need. Some will interpret minimal responses as sufficient support; others need more explicit affirmation to feel safe enough to open up.
- Watch for the moment your backchanneling shifts from genuine listening to waiting for your next question — that is the mechanical trap to avoid.

## Best Used When
- Preparing for or reflecting on 1:1 coaching conversations.
- Coaching supervisors or anyone who regularly conducts 1:1 conversations.
- Training new coaches or Scrum Masters in active listening fundamentals.
- Debriefing a coaching conversation where connection or trust felt off.
