# "Christmas Cards - Part 2" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten reflects on the practice of writing personal Christmas greetings to select colleagues as a year-end appreciation ritual. Inspired by receiving a thoughtful handmade gift from a former team, he encourages practitioners to invest time and effort into meaningful, personalised gestures for the people they work with.

## Methods & Techniques

### Year-End Personal Appreciation Gestures (ab 00:10)
- **What:** Writing handwritten Christmas or year-end greetings — or crafting something personalised — for colleagues you genuinely want to thank.
- **When to use:** End of the year, or any natural closing moment in a team's journey (project completion, team disbanding, offboarding).
- **Group size:** One-to-one or small group; works best when the gesture is tailored to the recipient.
- **Duration:** Low time investment with high relational impact; the effort itself is part of the message.
- **How it works:** Identify a select group of co-workers you are genuinely grateful for. Create something personal — a handwritten card, a made object, or a personalised keepsake — that reflects the shared experience. Deliver it as a closing ritual for the year.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=2O3La79zJxY&t=10

## Key Principles
- The effort and intentionality behind a gesture matters more than its material value — people can feel how much thought went in.
- Personalisation signals that you see the person, not just the role; Carsten's gift included a deliberate blank page for him to "continue his story," which made it specific to him.
- Appreciation rituals strengthen relational bonds that outlast formal team structures; the gift came from a team Carsten was no longer officially part of.
- Selectivity is intentional — writing to "some select co-workers" keeps the gesture meaningful rather than routine.

## Facilitation Tips
- Use this as a prompt when closing a team, ending a programme, or wrapping a year — invite team members to write personal notes to one another rather than doing it yourself as a facilitator.
- Encourage specificity: a note that names a concrete moment or contribution lands far better than a generic "thank you."
- A shared team gift (like the book Carsten received) can be a powerful collective act — consider facilitating a session where a team co-creates something for a departing colleague or coach.

## Best Used When
- Closing out a year or a significant chapter with a team.
- Recognising contributors who often go unacknowledged.
- Building or sustaining psychological safety and relational trust within a team or community of practice.
- Offboarding a Scrum Master, coach, or team member in a way that honours the relationship.
