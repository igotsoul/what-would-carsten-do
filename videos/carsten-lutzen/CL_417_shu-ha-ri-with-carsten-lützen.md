# Shu-Ha-Ri with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten introduces Shu-Ha-Ri, a Japanese martial arts concept applied to learning new skills and frameworks. Drawing on Martin Fowler's framing, he explains the three progressive stages of mastery — following rules, bending rules, and breaking rules — with Scrum as the primary example. The video offers a clear mental model for coaches and practitioners to understand where learners are in their journey.

## Methods & Techniques

### Shu-Ha-Ri Learning Model (ab 00:20)
- **What:** A three-stage model of skill acquisition: Shu (follow the rules), Ha (bend the rules), Ri (break the rules). Originally from Japanese martial arts, popularized in agile contexts by Martin Fowler and Alistair Cockburn.
- **When to use:** When coaching someone learning a new framework, method, or practice — to calibrate how much deviation from "the book" is appropriate at their current stage.
- **Group size:** N/A — conceptual model applicable to individuals or teams
- **Duration:** N/A — describes a developmental journey spanning months or years
- **How it works:**
  1. **Shu** — Follow the rules to the letter. Read the Scrum Guide, apply it faithfully. Don't deviate yet; internalize the foundations.
  2. **Ha** — Begin to bend the rules. You've developed a feel for how things relate; you can identify where optimization or adaptation makes sense.
  3. **Ri** — Break the rules that no longer make sense. You've internalized the principles so deeply that you can discard or replace practices, creating your own process that adds real value beyond the original framework.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=mkBp6vJLqZg&t=20

## Key Principles
- Always start at Shu — even experienced practitioners should begin by following a new framework faithfully before adapting it.
- Bending rules (Ha) is only productive once you have genuine confidence in your understanding of the underlying process.
- Breaking rules (Ri) does not mean ignoring the framework arbitrarily — it means having internalized it deeply enough to know which rules no longer serve the purpose.
- The end state at Ri is not "broken Scrum" but a genuinely custom process that delivers real value.
- The model is universal: Carsten notes it applies equally to learning to ride a bike or learning to read and write.

## Facilitation Tips
- Use Shu-Ha-Ri as a diagnostic lens when coaching teams: ask "where are we on this journey?" before pushing for process changes.
- When a team wants to deviate from a framework early, Carsten's framing suggests gently redirecting them back to Shu — build confidence first.
- Reference Martin Fowler and Alistair Cockburn for teams that want to go deeper; this video covers the simple, accessible version.

## Best Used When
- Coaching a team that is new to Scrum or another framework and eager to "customize" before they've internalized the basics.
- Explaining to stakeholders or leaders why following a process by the book is a necessary first step, not rigidity.
- Reflecting with an experienced team on whether they've earned the right to break certain rules — and whether they're at Ha or Ri.
- Onboarding new Scrum Masters or agile coaches who want a mental model for their own learning journey.
