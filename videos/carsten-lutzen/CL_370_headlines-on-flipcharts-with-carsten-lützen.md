# "Headlines on Flipcharts" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten Lützen demonstrates three practical techniques for drawing visually engaging headlines on flipcharts. The video focuses on hands-on, step-by-step instructions for creating banners, tape-style frames, and curly paper effects — all achievable with basic markers and optional color. The core principle is simple: write the text first, then add the border.

## Methods & Techniques

### Classic Banner Headline (ab 00:45)
- **What:** A rectangular banner frame around flipchart text, enhanced with a thin-line highlight and optional shadow using a grey marker.
- **When to use:** General-purpose flipchart headlines; adds a polished, structured look without much effort.
- **Group size:** N/A — preparation technique
- **Duration:** 2–3 minutes per headline
- **How it works:**
  1. Write the text with a thin marker first.
  2. Draw a rectangle around the text.
  3. Add small "end caps" on both sides of the rectangle.
  4. Draw a thinner line along the top and sides (same marker, not the thick one) to create a 3D effect.
  5. Optionally: add a grey shadow — thicker on the bottom/left, thinner on top/right.
  6. Optionally: add color with crayons, using lighter tones on the face and darker tones on the shadow triangle.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=7Esc_9NHfDM&t=45

### Tape-Corner Frame (ab 02:30)
- **What:** A border that mimics pieces of tape holding the text to the flipchart, giving a casual, pinboard feel.
- **When to use:** Informal or creative workshop contexts; adds personality without heavy structure.
- **Group size:** N/A — preparation technique
- **Duration:** 2–3 minutes per headline
- **How it works:**
  1. Write the text first.
  2. Draw small rectangular "tape" pieces at each corner — precision is not required.
  3. Connect the corners with straight lines to form a border.
  4. Add a simple shadow with the thick marker.
  5. Color the tape pieces in a different color from the text border (grey, blue, etc.) to distinguish them.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=7Esc_9NHfDM&t=150

### Curly Paper / Scroll Effect (ab 04:00)
- **What:** A headline that appears to be written on a curling or scrolled piece of paper, giving a wishlist or storybook feel.
- **When to use:** Thematic flipcharts (e.g., wishlists, vision boards, storytelling formats); adds visual drama.
- **Group size:** N/A — preparation technique
- **Duration:** 3–4 minutes per headline
- **How it works:**
  1. Write the text first.
  2. Draw a spiral at the top-left, extend across the top, and mirror with a spiral at the top-right.
  3. Add vertical stripes below the headline area to suggest depth.
  4. In one stroke: draw down, curve around the bottom, and back up to close the scroll shape.
  5. Add shadow and color — open to interpretation, precision not required.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=7Esc_9NHfDM&t=240

## Key Principles
- **Text first, border second** — always write the content before drawing any frame or decoration. This ensures the design fits the text, not the other way around.
- **Imprecision is acceptable** — Carsten emphasizes repeatedly that headlines don't need to be precise or accurate; participants view flipcharts from a distance.
- **Minimal tools, high impact** — two markers (thin + thick) are sufficient. A grey marker and some crayons elevate the result but are optional.
- **Sign your work** — Carsten always adds his initials in the corner so participants know who to ask if they have questions about the content.
- **Experimentation is encouraged** — there is no single right way; creativity and play are part of the practice.

## Facilitation Tips
- Use a thin marker for text and outlines, reserving the thick marker only for borders and shadows — not for lettering detail.
- Grey as a shadow color adds a "finished" quality to any headline style without requiring artistic skill.
- Keep colors light on the front face of banners and darker on shadow sides to mimic a 3D effect.
- Don't aim for perfection — a slightly irregular banner reads as human and warm, not sloppy.
- Adding initials to flipcharts is a simple accountability and navigation habit worth building into every session.

## Best Used When
- Preparing workshop materials, retrospective boards, or visual agendas on physical flipcharts.
- A facilitator wants to elevate the visual quality of their materials without investing in graphic design skills.
- Running sessions where analog tools (flipcharts, markers) are the primary medium.
