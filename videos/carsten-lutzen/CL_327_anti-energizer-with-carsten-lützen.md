# "Anti Energizer" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten challenges the default assumption that meetings should always start with a high-energy game or icebreaker. He introduces the concept of the "anti-energizer" — a calm, low-stimulus opening activity that slows the pace down instead of ramping it up. The core insight is that teams showing signs of stress benefit more from settling and centering than from more stimulation.

## Methods & Techniques

### The Anti-Energizer (ab 01:30)
- **What:** A calm, intentional opening activity designed to bring participants' energy *down* rather than up — helping stressed or overstimulated teams settle before focusing.
- **When to use:** When participants show signs of stress or mental overload; when the team's default state is already high-tempo and adding more energy would be counterproductive.
- **Group size:** Works for both in-person and online settings; no specific size constraint mentioned.
- **Duration:** Implied to be short (comparable to a typical energizer, a few minutes).
- **How it works:** Replace the opening game with a slow, low-demand activity. Examples Carsten draws on:
  1. **Silent Dino (online or in-person):** Participants silently arrange Post-its into a dinosaur shape on a digital whiteboard or physical surface — no talking, just quiet movement and presence.
  2. **Mindfulness moment:** A brief guided or self-directed mindfulness exercise focused on arriving in the room and connecting to the meeting's purpose.
  3. **Yoga or Tai Chi:** Short physical grounding practice introduced at a team offsite — slow, embodied, centering.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=ZE2dd754s7A

## Key Principles
- The goal of any meeting opener is to get people *mentally present and ready to focus* — not necessarily to raise energy levels.
- An anti-energizer can achieve the same outcome as a traditional energizer (engaged, focused participants) via a completely different route: calm instead of stimulation.
- Watch for subtle stress signals in your team; they are an indicator that the default high-energy opener may be the wrong choice.
- Facilitators should read the room's energy state *before* defaulting to their favorite format.

## Facilitation Tips
- Notice small signs of stress in participants before the meeting starts — these are cues to pivot away from a high-energy opener.
- Silence as a facilitation tool can feel strange at first but becomes comfortable quickly; don't be put off by the initial awkwardness.
- The silent Post-it dino exercise works well online (e.g., Miro or Mural) and requires zero explanation or facilitation once set up.
- Pair the activity with a brief framing: "We're going to take a moment to slow down and arrive before we begin."

## Best Used When
- Teams are visibly stressed, overloaded, or running back-to-back meetings.
- A workshop or retro follows a high-pressure sprint or demanding period.
- Participants need grounding before a sensitive or complex conversation.
- The facilitator senses the group is physically present but mentally scattered.
