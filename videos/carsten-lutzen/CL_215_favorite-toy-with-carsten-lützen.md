# "Favorite Toy" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten introduces "Favorite Toy," a lightweight energizer that invites participants to share what their favorite toy was as a child. It requires minimal preparation, works well both online and in person, and sparks authentic conversation without intruding into people's private lives.

## Methods & Techniques

### Favorite Toy Energizer (ab 00:30)
- **What:** Participants are asked "As a kid, what was your favorite toy?" and share an image of it with the group — either by posting it to a virtual whiteboard or showing it on their phone in person.
- **When to use:** At the start of a workshop or meeting as a warm-up; especially useful for diverse groups where brand names or cultural references might not land universally.
- **Group size:** Works for small and large groups; for larger groups, combine with 1-2-4-All to keep it manageable.
- **Duration:** 10–15 minutes (including finding, posting, and discussing).
- **How it works:**
  1. Pose the question: "As a kid, what was your favorite toy?"
  2. Online: participants do an image search and post the image to a shared virtual whiteboard.
  3. In person: participants find a photo on their phone to show the group.
  4. Each person briefly shares their toy with the group.
  5. For large groups: use 1-2-4-All to speed up sharing — start with individual reflection, then pair discussion, then only the most interesting items surface to the full group.

## Key Principles
- A good energizer creates connection without crossing into personal or sensitive territory — "favorite toy" stays light and safe while still revealing something genuine about people.
- Using images instead of just naming toys bridges cultural and linguistic diversity in mixed groups.
- A wall of childhood toys on a virtual whiteboard creates a visible, shared artifact that reflects the group's playful side and what made them happy as kids.

## Facilitation Tips
- Always ask for an image rather than just a name — it works across cultures and removes ambiguity around brand names.
- In virtual settings, the whiteboard result itself becomes a conversation starter; let people browse it before discussing.
- For large groups, default to 1-2-4-All and only invite sharing of particularly interesting or surprising toys to the full group, keeping timing tight.
- You can run a shorter version by skipping pair discussion and going straight from individual posting to a brief full-group scan.

## Best Used When
- Opening a workshop or team session and wanting a low-risk, playful way to help people show up as humans first.
- Working with diverse or international groups where cultural references might not be shared.
- Running virtual sessions where you want to build energy and create a shared visual artifact early on.
- Time is limited but you still want a genuine connection moment (10 min version).

## Related Concepts
- **1-2-4-All** (Liberating Structures) — used here to scale the sharing conversation in larger groups without losing everyone's voice.
