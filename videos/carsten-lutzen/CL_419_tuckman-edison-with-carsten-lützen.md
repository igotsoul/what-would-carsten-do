# Tuckman-Edison with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten explains the Tuckman model of team development and its extension by Edison (2008), which adds phases beyond "High Performance" to describe how stable teams can degrade over time. The video covers all stages from Forming to Adjourning, with particular focus on the Conforming/Deforming trap and how coaches and Scrum Masters can help teams transform back to high performance.

## Methods & Techniques

### The Tuckman-Edison Model (ab 00:30)
- **What:** An extended version of Tuckman's classic team development model, augmented by Edison in 2008 with phases that describe what happens after a team reaches high performance — including the risk of conformity-driven decline and a path back through transformation.
- **When to use:** As a diagnostic and explanatory framework when working with teams at any stage — especially useful for coaching conversations about team health, stability, or stagnation.
- **Group size:** Applicable to any team
- **Duration:** Framework for ongoing team lifecycle, not a single session activity
- **How it works:**
  1. **Forming** — People are put together and begin to establish themselves as a team; performance starts neutral
  2. **Storming** — Conflicts arise as people figure out roles, communication, and working norms; performance often dips below starting point — this is *normal and functional*
  3. **Norming** — The team finds common ground and defines how they work together; performance recovers
  4. **Performing** — The team operates above initial performance levels; people know each other well
  5. **High Performing** — Peak performance; the team is deeply effective
  6. **Informing** *(Edison)* — High-performing teams share learnings and knowledge with the broader organization; key to sustaining high performance. Note: learnings should *inspire*, not be copied wholesale
  7. **Conforming** *(Edison)* — Risk zone: the team stops challenging the status quo, gets complacent, loses fresh perspective; performance begins to drop
  8. **Deforming** *(Edison)* — Continued decline; the team operates on autopilot with a false sense of competence
  9. **Adjourning** *(Tuckman)* — Team is disbanded if no intervention occurs
  10. **Transforming** *(Edison)* — If recognized early enough, a transformational event or intervention can move the team from Conforming back into a new Norming cycle, repeating the functional loop

### Recognizing and Intervening in the Conforming Phase (ab 04:30)
- **What:** A coaching practice of identifying when a high-performing team has entered the Conforming phase and facilitating a re-energizing event or conversation to trigger transformation.
- **When to use:** When a previously strong team seems stagnant, resistant to change, or has a "we know best" attitude
- **Group size:** Team-level
- **Duration:** Varies — could be a kickoff event, a facilitated discussion, or bringing in an external perspective
- **How it works:**
  1. Notice signs of conformity: no challenge of status quo, complacency, drop in energy or output
  2. Create space for reflection — facilitate discussions about what's working and what's not
  3. Consider introducing a transformational event (kickoff, external facilitator, team reset)
  4. Bring in a 3rd party if needed to provide fresh perspective
  5. Guide the team into a new Norming phase with updated agreements and shared ground

## Key Principles
- Stable teams perform better — disrupting team composition resets the cycle and costs performance (see also: Mythical Man Month)
- Storming is *functional*, not a sign of a broken team — it is a necessary phase that cannot and should not be rushed
- Not all teams reach Performing or High Performing — some never leave Norming
- The Conforming phase is a natural risk for long-lived teams; recognizing it early enables recovery
- Learnings from high-performing teams should *inspire* others, never be copied directly — context matters
- Functional phases: Forming → Storming → Norming → Performing → High Performing → Informing → Transforming (loop back)
- Dysfunctional phases: Conforming → Deforming → Adjourning

## Facilitation Tips
- Don't try to shortcut the Storming phase with a single afternoon of team-building activities — it takes time and that's OK
- When a team is in the Conforming phase, a facilitator's role is to help them *recognize* the stagnation and create conditions for challenge and renewal
- Kickoffs, retrospectives, and structured discussions can serve as entry points for the Transforming phase
- Bringing in a 3rd party (external coach or facilitator) is a valid and sometimes necessary intervention for breaking conformity
- As a Scrum Master or Agile Coach, stay aware of which phase a team is in — your intervention style should match the phase

## Best Used When
- Coaching a team that seems stuck, stagnant, or resistant to change
- Explaining to stakeholders *why* stable teams matter and why constant re-teaming is costly
- Helping a team understand that conflict (Storming) is normal and productive
- Designing a team reset or kickoff intervention for a long-running team in decline
- Onboarding new Scrum Masters or coaches to team development theory

## Related Concepts
- **Tuckman Model** (original, 1965) — Forming, Storming, Norming, Performing, Adjourning
- **Edison extension** (2008) — Informing, Conforming, Deforming, Transforming
- **Mythical Man Month** — Adding people to a team (or changing composition) resets the development cycle
- **Stable Teams** — Core agile principle; teams that stay together develop shared context and outperform reconfigured teams
- **Psychological Safety** — Underlying enabler for teams to move through Storming productively
