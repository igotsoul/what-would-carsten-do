# "Human Org Chart" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten introduces a short physical exercise called the "Human Org Chart" that makes formal reporting structures tangible by having people physically arrange themselves in a room to represent their org chart. The exercise helps teams sense-make their own organizational structure and surfaces insights about staffing, dependencies, and structural constraints that might otherwise remain invisible.

## Methods & Techniques

### Human Org Chart (ab 00:20)
- **What:** A physical exercise where participants arrange themselves in a room to represent the formal org chart, then reflect on what the structure reveals.
- **When to use:** When a team or cross-functional group needs to understand and discuss their formal organizational structure — particularly useful when structural constraints are causing friction or confusion.
- **Group size:** Whole-room groups, works best when all relevant people are physically present.
- **Duration:** Not specified; the setup is quick, reflection time is variable.
- **How it works:**
  1. Place the highest-ranking person in the center of the room (not on a stage or at one side — center is critical).
  2. All direct reports to that person stand one arm's length away, close enough to touch their shoulder.
  3. All direct reports to *those* people stand one arm's length away from them.
  4. Continue until everyone is standing in a physical tree/graph structure.
  5. Ask each branch to raise their hand so the room can sense the relative size of each part of the org.
  6. Reflect individually and in silence: "What stands out to you? What do you notice?"
  7. Share in pairs, then groups of four, then groups of five or six.
- **Facilitation tip:** Do NOT place the top-ranking person on a stage or at one side of the room — this creates spatial problems as the tree expands.

## Key Principles
- Most exercises address informal connections; this one intentionally targets the **formal** reporting structure.
- Making structure physical and spatial makes it **tangible in a way that org charts on paper or in portals do not**.
- The exercise can reveal systemic reasons for delivery problems — e.g., a team that "never delivers" may visibly show up as severely understaffed once people can see how few people are in that branch.
- Carsten uses a **1-2-4-All style reflection sequence** (individual → pair → group of four → larger group) to surface insights progressively.

## Facilitation Tips
- Position the top-ranking person in the **center** of the room — this is the most important logistical detail. A side or stage position breaks the spatial tree as it grows outward.
- After the physical structure is formed, use the **hand-raising per branch** step to make relative team sizes viscerally visible to everyone at once.
- Prompt reflection with open questions: "What stands out to you?" or "What became apparent when you saw this?"
- Use silence first — give individuals time to form their own observations before group discussion shapes perception.

## Best Used When
- A cross-functional group lacks awareness of how they are formally organized relative to each other.
- Teams are frustrated with dependencies or delivery bottlenecks that may have structural causes.
- During large-group workshops or org-design sessions where sensemaking about structure is needed.
- As an energizer or opening exercise that gets people moving while generating meaningful insight.

## Related Concepts
- **1-2-4-All** (Liberating Structures) — the reflection sequence Carsten uses mirrors this format.
- Informal network mapping exercises (Carsten notes this addresses the *formal* level, implying complementary informal exercises exist).
- Organizational design and systems thinking — the exercise surfaces structural constraints as a system property, not individual failure.
