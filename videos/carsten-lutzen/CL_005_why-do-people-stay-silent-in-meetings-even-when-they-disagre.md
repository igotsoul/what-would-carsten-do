# Why do people stay silent in meetings, even when they disagree?

## Summary
Carsten explains the bystander effect — the social phenomenon where people in groups default to inaction because responsibility feels shared and therefore diluted. He connects this directly to facilitation practice, showing why silence in meetings is often structural rather than personal, and offers concrete techniques to redistribute responsibility back to participants.

## Methods & Techniques

### 1-2-4-All (ab 01:45)
- **What:** A structured participation format where people first reflect individually, then discuss in pairs, then in groups of four, before sharing with the whole room.
- **When to use:** When a group is stuck in silence or passive observation — the bystander effect is active and no one is taking initiative.
- **Group size:** Any size; scales well.
- **Duration:** Flexible; typically 10–20 minutes depending on the question.
- **How it works:** Invite participants to engage in pairs first, removing the anonymity of the large group and lowering the threshold to contribute. Gradually escalate to the full group.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=U8ijE34YVsw&t=105

### Round Robin (ab 02:00)
- **What:** A facilitation technique where every participant is explicitly invited to speak, one by one, around the room.
- **When to use:** When you want to ensure all voices are heard and no one can default to silence by relying on others.
- **How it works:** Clearly explain the intention upfront — tell participants what you're asking them to contribute. Go around the group and give each person a turn.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=U8ijE34YVsw&t=120

### Role Assignment — Devil's Advocate (ab 02:10)
- **What:** Assigning a specific role to a participant (e.g., "you will look for problems with this") to give them permission and mandate to speak up.
- **When to use:** When the group dynamic suppresses dissent or critical thinking; when you need someone to voice concerns that others may hold but won't express.
- **How it works:** Before the discussion, explicitly assign the role and describe its purpose. The role externalizes the responsibility — it's not personal criticism, it's a job.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=U8ijE34YVsw&t=130

### Holding the Silence / Negative Capability (ab 02:20)
- **What:** Deliberately staying with silence in a meeting rather than rushing to fill it, trusting that someone will eventually speak.
- **When to use:** When the pause after a question feels uncomfortable but the group needs time to think; as a first response before more direct interventions.
- **How it works:** After posing a question, resist the urge to rephrase or prompt. Hold the silence. If no one speaks after a reasonable time, then invite a specific person.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=U8ijE34YVsw&t=140

## Key Principles
- The bystander effect is a **structural phenomenon**, not a character flaw. More people in a room leads to more diffused individual responsibility — each person feels less personally accountable.
- Because the cause is structural, the solution must also be **structural**: how you design participation into the meeting format.
- Responsibility in a group doesn't disappear — it just gets thinner. The facilitator's job is to **narrow the focus of responsibility** back onto individual participants.
- Carsten is cautious about directly calling out silent individuals ("I haven't heard from you yet") — this can push people out of their comfort zone in a way that feels coercive rather than inviting.

## Facilitation Tips
- Default to structural interventions (1-2-4-all, round robin) rather than putting individuals on the spot.
- Be explicit about what you're asking participants to do before you ask them to do it — clarity reduces hesitation.
- Silence is not failure. Practice sitting with it (negative capability) before escalating to a more direct prompt.
- If you must call on someone directly, frame it as an invitation, not a spotlight.

## Best Used When
- Preparing for or debriefing a meeting where participation was low or one-sided.
- Designing retrospectives, decision-making sessions, or any meeting where you need genuine input from all participants.
- Coaching scrum masters, agile coaches, or managers who report that "no one speaks up" in their team meetings.

## Related Concepts
- **Liberating Structures** — 1-2-4-All is a core Liberating Structure; this video provides accessible context for why it works psychologically.
- **Psychological safety** — the bystander effect compounds in low-safety environments; Carsten's structural solutions help create conditions for safety without requiring trust to already exist.
- **Diffusion of responsibility** — the social psychology concept (Darley & Latané) underlying the bystander effect, originally studied in emergency bystander contexts and applied here to group meetings.
