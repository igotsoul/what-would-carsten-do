# "Eisenhower Matrix" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten introduces the Eisenhower Matrix as a personal and team prioritization tool, inspired by a talk by Said Hubricks. He walks through all four quadrants and reflects on how Scrum Masters and agile coaches can use the framework — particularly the "important but not urgent" quadrant — to carve out deliberate time for process improvement rather than constantly firefighting.

## Methods & Techniques

### Eisenhower Matrix (ab 00:15)
- **What:** A 2x2 prioritization matrix with axes of Urgency (urgent / not urgent) and Importance (important / not important), used to decide what to do, delegate, schedule, or delete.
- **When to use:** When feeling overwhelmed, when everything feels urgent, or when trying to protect time for strategic and improvement work.
- **Group size:** Individual or team-level prioritization.
- **Duration:** Ongoing practice; no fixed session length required.
- **How it works:**
  1. **Urgent + Important → Do it yourself now.** These are the fires that need immediate attention.
  2. **Urgent + Not Important → Delegate.** It needs to happen soon, but someone else can handle it, freeing you for higher-value work.
  3. **Not Urgent + Important → Schedule it.** Block specific time in your calendar (e.g., "Friday 10–11") and protect that block. This is where process improvement, reflection, and strategic thinking live.
  4. **Not Urgent + Not Important → Delete it.** If it truly matters, it will resurface and become urgent later — then you can decide again.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=sy_Zq6vXlXg&t=15

### Scheduled Process Improvement Time (ab 02:30)
- **What:** Deliberately blocking calendar time for process improvement and reflection, rather than relying solely on the retrospective cadence.
- **When to use:** When the Scrum Master or agile coach notices they are only doing reactive work and never getting to systemic improvements.
- **Group size:** Individual practice (Scrum Master / coach).
- **Duration:** Carsten suggests a slot like "Friday 10–11" as a starting point.
- **How it works:** Book a recurring calendar slot dedicated to stepping back — reviewing the backlog, checking trend graphs, observing team dynamics — and protect it from being displaced by urgent items.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=sy_Zq6vXlXg&t=150

## Key Principles
- When everything feels urgent, the important-but-not-urgent quadrant gets permanently crowded out — leading to stress and eventual burnout.
- Delegation is not avoidance; it is the right action for tasks that are urgent but not the highest-value use of your personal attention.
- Deleting genuinely unimportant tasks is safe: if something truly matters, it will resurface.
- Carsten frames the "not urgent + important" quadrant as a real eye-opener — the key insight is that this work will never happen organically; it must be actively scheduled and defended.
- Retrospectives cover process improvement on a cadence, but daily or weekly micro-reflection time fills the gap between retros.

## Facilitation Tips
- Use the Eisenhower Matrix visually (draw or display the 2x2) so the quadrant logic is immediately tangible for the audience or team.
- When introducing the matrix to a team, ask them to place current backlog items or tasks into the quadrants — this surfaces hidden assumptions about urgency vs. importance.
- Carsten suggests modeling vulnerability: he admits he has not been protecting his own "important, not urgent" time, normalizing the struggle for the audience.

## Best Used When
- A Scrum Master or coach is feeling reactive and overwhelmed by operational work.
- A team discussion about workload, prioritization, or stress levels is needed.
- Onboarding new team members to how the team decides what to work on.
- A retrospective action is to improve how the team handles incoming requests and interruptions.
