# "Liberating Structures Micro-Design Elements" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten introduces the five micro-design elements that are the building blocks of every Liberating Structure. Understanding these elements helps facilitators analyze existing LS formats, modify them, and even design new ones. The core idea is that all Liberating Structures are composed of the same fundamental components configured in different ways.

## Methods & Techniques

### The Five Micro-Design Elements of Liberating Structures (ab 00:20)
- **What:** Every Liberating Structure is built from five structural building blocks — called micro-design elements — that together define how a structure works. By understanding these elements, facilitators can deconstruct any LS, adapt it, or create hybrid designs.
- **When to use:** When designing or customizing workshops using Liberating Structures; when learning to read and apply the LS menu more intentionally.
- **Group size:** Applicable across all group sizes
- **Duration:** Conceptual framework — no fixed time
- **How it works:**
  1. **Structuring Invitation** — The precise wording that invites participants into the activity. Carsten stresses that the invitation determines who and what comes in; a weak or vague invitation undermines the whole structure.
  2. **How Space is Arranged and Materials Needed** — Physical or virtual setup: room layout, floor space, whiteboard placement, materials. The space signals what kind of participation is expected and makes certain behaviors easy or hard.
  3. **How Participation is Distributed** — Who gets to speak, contribute, or act, and in what ratio. Carsten frames this as a key lever: distributing participation as broadly as possible is a core LS principle (everyone in, no one left out).
  4. **How Groups are Configured** — Size and composition of groups (individuals, pairs, triads, large group, etc.). Group configuration shapes the dynamics and the quality of output.
  5. **Sequence of Steps and Time Allocation** — The order of activities and the time assigned to each step. Carsten notes this is where the "string of structure" comes together — timing and sequencing make or break execution.

## Key Principles
- All 33+ Liberating Structures are variations of the same five micro-design elements configured differently — they are not magic, they are intentional design choices.
- The **invitation** is the most underestimated element: it directly determines who participates and how much energy they bring.
- Space and materials are not logistics — they are design decisions that enable or suppress certain behaviors.
- Distributing participation is a core value of LS, not just a technique; aim to include everyone as much as possible.
- Group configuration can be adjusted in real time — it is one of the levers a facilitator can pull during a session.
- Understanding the building blocks allows facilitators to **mix and match** elements across structures to create new hybrids.

## Facilitation Tips
- Before running any Liberating Structure, explicitly review all five micro-design elements in your preparation — don't just copy the default format.
- Pay close attention to crafting the invitation: it sets the entire frame for participation.
- When something feels off in a session, diagnose which micro-design element is causing the problem (wrong group size? unclear invitation? bad space setup?).
- Use the micro-design elements as a **checklist** when preparing a new or adapted LS format.

## Best Used When
- Introducing Liberating Structures to facilitators who are new to the menu and need a mental model for how the formats work.
- Deepening an existing LS practitioner's ability to adapt or create structures.
- Running a LS learning session or train-the-trainer event.
- Reflecting after a workshop on why a particular structure did or did not work.
