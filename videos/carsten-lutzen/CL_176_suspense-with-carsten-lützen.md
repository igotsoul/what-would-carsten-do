# "Suspense" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten shares a short, playful technique for ending a meeting with a joke that requires psychological safety. It is a simple humor-based closing move designed to leave participants with a laugh rather than a flat sign-off.

## Methods & Techniques

### Suspense Close (ab 00:20)
- **What:** A one-liner joke used to close a meeting — ask "How do you keep people in suspense?" and immediately log off without giving the punchline.
- **When to use:** At the end of an online meeting when you want to leave people with energy and a smile rather than a dry sign-off.
- **Group size:** Any, works best online where you can abruptly leave the call.
- **Duration:** A few seconds — it is the very last move before you exit.
- **How it works:**
  1. As the meeting is wrapping up, say: "How do you keep people in suspense?"
  2. Immediately log off — do not wait for a response.
  3. Optional escalation: replace "people" with "idiots" for a stronger comedic effect ("How do you keep idiots in suspense?" — then sign off).
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=25gnhhyjD_8&t=20

## Key Principles
- Humor at meeting close creates a memorable, positive ending rather than an awkward fade-out.
- The technique only lands well when there is genuine psychological safety in the team — people need to feel comfortable enough to laugh at mild irreverence.
- The escalated version ("idiots") is self-deprecating in its implication and tends to generate a beat of silence followed by genuine laughter, according to Carsten's experience.
- Courage is required from the facilitator — but the payoff in team energy and warmth is worth it.

## Facilitation Tips
- Carsten notes there is typically "one or two seconds of awkward quietness" before people laugh — do not be alarmed by that pause; it is part of the effect.
- The joke travels: Carsten reports that participants bring it back to their own team meetings, giving it a multiplier effect on team culture.
- Do not overuse it — it works as a surprise move.

## Best Used When
- Closing recurring online team meetings where the group already has rapport.
- You want to inject lightness into a meeting that has been heavy or routine.
- The team culture supports mild humor and the facilitator has built enough trust to take a small risk.
