# Focus on Outcomes with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten introduces a solution-focused mindset shift: instead of asking "what actions do I need to take to fix a problem?", ask "how would I recognize that the problem is solved?" This outcome-first approach — borrowed from Solution-Focused Coaching — ensures that actions are backtracked from actual desired value rather than performed for their own sake.

## Methods & Techniques

### Outcome-First Backtracking (ab 00:30)
- **What:** When facing an obstacle, define what "solved" looks like before identifying any actions. Then work backwards to find only the actions that actually lead to that outcome.
- **When to use:** When a team or individual is stuck on an impediment, conflict, or challenge and risks defaulting to busy-work or predefined solutions.
- **Group size:** Individual coaching, 1-on-1, or small team
- **Duration:** Can be applied as a framing question in minutes; suitable as a coaching prompt in any conversation
- **How it works:**
  1. Identify the obstacle or problem to solve.
  2. Instead of listing what to do, ask: "How would we know this has been resolved?"
  3. Compile a list of observable indicators that the problem is gone.
  4. Only then backtrack to identify the actions that would produce those indicators.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=lWOXmuDI0Ok&t=30

## Key Principles
- Focus on outcomes, not actions — actions only have value if they lead to the right outcome.
- Solution-Focused Coaching (referenced via Jesper and Roy) avoids prescribing steps; instead it clarifies what success looks like.
- Backtracking from outcomes gives more control over what you're actually solving and how.
- Outcome clarity filters out low-value actions automatically — you only do what moves toward the recognized end state.

## Facilitation Tips
- Use the trigger question: *"How would you know this obstacle has been removed?"* as a reframe whenever someone presents a problem with a pre-packaged solution.
- Compile a list of observable signals before generating any action items — resist jumping to solutions.

## Best Used When
- A team is spinning on how to resolve a conflict or impediment without clarity on what "resolved" actually means.
- A Scrum Master or coach notices the team is busy with actions that don't clearly connect to a desired outcome.
- In retrospectives when teams default to generic action items without defining the goal behind them.
