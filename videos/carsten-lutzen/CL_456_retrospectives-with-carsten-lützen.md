# Retrospectives with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten Lützen, a Scrum Master and Agile Coach, walks through a complete retrospective flow he has refined through experimentation. The video covers a four-part structure: a warm-up check-in, a review of previous focus points, the Starfish exercise with dot-voting, and a closing retro evaluation. Each technique is practical and immediately applicable.

## Methods & Techniques

### One-Word Sprint Summary (ab 00:30)
- **What:** Each team member summarizes the sprint in a single word, taken as a round-table turn with no discussion or commentary allowed.
- **When to use:** As an opening warm-up to get everyone engaged and surface the emotional tone of the sprint quickly.
- **Group size:** Any team size; works especially well for small-to-medium Scrum teams.
- **Duration:** ~2–3 minutes (1 minute to think, then one round of shares).
- **How it works:**
  1. Give participants ~1 minute to think of one word describing the sprint.
  2. Go around the table; each person says their word — no reactions, no discussion.
  3. After the round, optionally drill down: ask one or two people who said a particularly interesting or concerning word to elaborate.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=kcR0El8cCYs&t=30

### Review of Previous Focus Points (ab 01:20)
- **What:** A structured check-in on action items or improvement goals agreed upon in the previous retrospective.
- **When to use:** At the start of every retrospective (after the warm-up) to close the feedback loop and maintain accountability.
- **Duration:** Short — typically 5–10 minutes; discussion is welcome but not required.
- **How it works:**
  1. Present the list of focus points from the previous retro.
  2. For each item: did the team achieve it? If yes, close it. If not, keep it visible for later in the session.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=kcR0El8cCYs&t=80

### Starfish Exercise with Dot-Voting (ab 02:00)
- **What:** A five-category retrospective format where team members individually write post-its for Start, Stop, Keep, Less, and More — followed by dot-voting to prioritize.
- **When to use:** As the core retrospective exercise to surface diverse perspectives and democratically identify the most important improvements.
- **Group size:** Any Scrum team size.
- **Duration:** ~20–30 minutes (writing + sharing + voting + prioritization).
- **How it works:**
  1. Draw or display a starfish diagram with five sections: Start, Stop, Keep, Less, More.
  2. Distribute post-its and pens. Team writes in silence — nothing goes on the board until everyone is done (approximately 5 minutes).
  3. One by one, each person places their post-its on the board with a brief explanation. No debate — all input is valid.
  4. Run dot-voting: give everyone 3 dots (stickers or pen marks); they place dots on items they consider most important. Multiple dots on one item is allowed.
  5. Take the top 3 voted items, assign an owner to each (the owner ensures progress happens, not necessarily does the work themselves).
  6. Add the items to the sprint backlog for visibility and transparency.
  7. Move completed items to "done" as they are resolved.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=kcR0El8cCYs&t=120

### Retrospective Evaluation / Closing (ab 05:00)
- **What:** A quick closing activity where participants rate the quality of the retrospective itself, so the format can continuously improve.
- **When to use:** At the end of every retrospective.
- **Duration:** 3–5 minutes.
- **How it works (multiple options Carsten suggests):**
  - **Thumbs:** Thumbs up / neutral / thumbs down.
  - **Hand fingers (0–5):** 0 = complete waste of time; 5 = best retro ever. Ask a few people to explain their rating, and ask those who gave lower scores what would have made it better.
  - **Physical scale:** One end of the room = awesome, other end = tedious. People stand at their position; facilitator asks a few to explain.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=kcR0El8cCYs&t=300

## Key Principles
- The retrospective is arguably the most important Scrum ceremony — it is the team's primary mechanism to reflect and improve.
- All input posted during the Starfish exercise is valid. Carsten is explicit: "This is my perception of reality" — no one should dismiss or challenge another's contribution.
- Silence during individual writing protects psychological safety and prevents groupthink before ideas are on the board.
- Assigning an owner to improvement items (distinct from the person who does the work) is key to accountability without overburdening one individual.
- Adding retro action items to the sprint backlog creates transparency about process improvement work.
- Evaluating the retrospective itself is a meta-learning loop that keeps the format fresh and prevents participant disengagement over time.

## Facilitation Tips
- During the one-word warm-up, resist the urge to allow immediate commentary — the silence rule creates safety and keeps pace.
- When drilling down after the one-word round, be selective: only follow up on one or two words that warrant more context.
- During the Starfish sharing round, reinforce that no discussion is allowed when post-its go up — validate every contribution verbally if needed ("that's a valid point, thanks").
- When using the hand-finger rating at close, always ask both directions: "Why did you give it a 5?" and "What would have made it a 4 for you?" — the second question generates the most actionable feedback.
- Dot-voting allows all dots on a single item if someone feels strongly — this is intentional and should be communicated upfront.

## Best Used When
- Running a standard Scrum sprint retrospective and looking for a reliable, structured end-to-end flow.
- Teams new to retrospectives who need a clear, low-ambiguity format.
- Teams whose retrospectives have become stale and need a refreshed but proven structure.
- Facilitators who want a complete retro design they can run without extensive preparation.

## Related Concepts
- **Derby-Larson model** — Carsten references this as the structural backbone of his retrospective flow (Set the Stage → Gather Data → Generate Insights → Decide What to Do → Close).
- **Dot-voting** — a classic prioritization technique used here to surface team consensus on improvement areas.
- **Sprint backlog** — Carsten recommends adding retro action items here to keep process improvement work visible alongside delivery work.
- **Alternative retro formats** mentioned briefly: movie reviews, Trump tweets, train station info screens — suggesting variation as an antidote to retro fatigue.
