# "Elevenie" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten introduces the Elevenie — a simple 11-word poem format (originally a German poetry style) as a creative workshop exercise. Participants write a structured micro-poem to reflect on a sprint, project, or shared experience. It's lightweight, fun, and produces memorable, shareable artifacts.

## Methods & Techniques

### Elevenie Poem (ab 00:30)
- **What:** A structured 11-word poem in 5 lines, where each line follows a fixed word count and theme. Participants use it to articulate feelings or reflections about a sprint, product, or project.
- **When to use:** Retrospectives, sprint closings, gratitude rounds, team check-ins, or any moment where you want people to reflect creatively without the overhead of a complex exercise.
- **Group size:** Any; each person writes individually, then shares aloud.
- **Duration:** 5–10 minutes (writing) + sharing time.
- **How it works:**
  1. **Line 1 — 1 word:** Context. A single word capturing the theme or feeling (e.g., *happiness*).
  2. **Line 2 — 2 words:** What does this word do or describe? (e.g., *team happy*).
  3. **Line 3 — 3 words:** Where or how was this observed? (e.g., *home and office*).
  4. **Line 4 — 4 words:** What do you mean by it? (e.g., *I felt super happy*).
  5. **Line 5 — 1 word:** Conclusion or outcome (e.g., *motivation*).
  - Total: 1+2+3+4+1 = 11 words.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9qBofOBF7mE&t=30

## Key Principles
- Simple constraints unlock creativity — a fixed structure makes it easier to express something that might otherwise feel hard to articulate.
- Carsten deliberately uses exercises that "seem a bit weird" to help people see things from a different perspective.
- The poem becomes a physical artifact: print them, hang them in the team space, read them aloud — giving the team something tangible to remember the iteration by.
- Can serve multiple purposes: reflection, gratitude, acknowledgment of feelings, or celebration.

## Facilitation Tips
- Facilitate line by line — explain each line, give people time to write it, then move to the next. Don't front-load all instructions at once.
- Encourage reading poems aloud at the end; this is where the energy and fun emerges.
- Consider printing the finished poems and hanging them in the team space as a sprint memento.
- Frame it lightly — acknowledge it's a bit unusual, which lowers the pressure to be "good" at poetry.

## Best Used When
- You want a low-effort creative closing activity in a retrospective.
- The team needs a moment to acknowledge feelings or achievements without a heavy exercise.
- You want to generate a memorable, displayable artifact from a sprint or project milestone.
- Gratitude or appreciation rounds feel stale and you want a fresh format.
