# "Silence" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten explores three distinct types of silence in facilitation, building on work by Bastian Stokkebæk (referenced as "Bastian"). He argues that most facilitators default to only one type — reflective silence — while neglecting the more challenging but equally valuable restorative and relational forms. The video offers a practical framework for intentionally incorporating all three into meetings and workshops.

## Methods & Techniques

### Reflective Silence (ab 01:00)
- **What:** Giving participants a specific task to reflect upon individually and in silence — the most commonly used form of silence in facilitation.
- **When to use:** After introducing an activity, question, or prompt where individual thinking time adds depth before group discussion.
- **Group size:** Any
- **Duration:** Varies by activity
- **How it works:** Facilitator explains the task, then explicitly asks participants to reflect on it in silence before sharing. Common in Liberating Structures and similar formats.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=IgrsQCqyesE&t=60

### Restorative Silence (ab 01:45)
- **What:** Unstructured silence with no task — participants are simply invited to sit and be, without focusing on breath, mindfulness, or any prompt.
- **When to use:** To allow brains to reset and participants to return to a more natural, relaxed state — particularly useful at transitions in workshops or after intense segments.
- **Group size:** Any
- **Duration:** Not specified; Bastian recommends at least 10% of meeting time across all silence types combined.
- **How it works:**
  1. Explicitly tell participants there is no task.
  2. Explain the purpose: to let the brain relax and shift states.
  3. Invite them to simply sit and be.
  4. Be prepared for discomfort — Carsten notes this feels strange at first, including for himself.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=IgrsQCqyesE&t=105

### Relational Silence (ab 02:45)
- **What:** Silence with a loose relational lens — participants sit quietly while noticing observations about others or the group (e.g., shared backgrounds, things they'd like to learn from a colleague).
- **When to use:** To build connection and awareness of the group without requiring verbal interaction; useful for warming up relational attention.
- **Group size:** Any
- **Duration:** Not specified
- **How it works:** Ask participants to sit in silence and gently notice: interesting features about others in the room, things they'd like to learn from a specific person, or what binds the group together.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=IgrsQCqyesE&t=165

## Key Principles
- Most facilitators overuse reflective silence and neglect the other two types entirely.
- Bastian recommends spending at least **10% of meeting time in silence** as a rule of thumb — adjusting based on facilitation context.
- Restorative silence requires more explicit framing than the other types because it defies participants' expectations of purposeful activity.
- Silence is not passive — even in relational silence, meaningful cognitive and social processing is happening.
- Carsten openly names restorative and relational silence as personal growth areas, modelling honest self-assessment for coaches.

## Facilitation Tips
- When introducing restorative silence, be very explicit about *why* you're doing it — participants (and the facilitator themselves) may find it uncomfortable or awkward without context.
- Don't conflate restorative silence with mindfulness or breathwork — the distinction matters: no task, no focus, just being.
- Use the 10% rule as a practical starting point to audit your own facilitation habits around silence.
- Carsten recommends reading *Fri lils* by Bastian Stokkebæk (in Danish) for a deeper exploration of these concepts.

## Best Used When
- Designing workshops or meetings where cognitive load is high and participants need recovery moments.
- Facilitating groups that tend to rush from activity to activity without integration time.
- Working on team connection and noticing group dynamics without forcing verbal interaction.
- Seeking to move beyond standard "reflect individually" prompts toward a more intentional use of quiet.
