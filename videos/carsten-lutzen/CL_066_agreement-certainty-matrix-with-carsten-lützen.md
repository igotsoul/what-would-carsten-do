# "Agreement & Certainty Matrix" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten Lützen introduces the Agreement & Certainty Matrix, a Liberating Structure derived from the Stacey Matrix, for helping teams or organizations get an overview of their tasks and problems by classifying them according to certainty and agreement. The structure surfaces mismatches in how team members perceive their work — revealing where alignment is missing and where follow-up action is needed.

## Methods & Techniques

### Agreement & Certainty Matrix (ab 00:20)
- **What:** A facilitated exercise where individuals and small groups classify their tasks or challenges on a 2x2 matrix (certainty vs. agreement), then compare their classifications to surface alignment gaps and complexity patterns.
- **When to use:** When a team or department needs an overview of the tasks or problems they're facing and wants to identify where there is shared understanding versus where confusion or disagreement exists.
- **Group size:** Pairs, then groups of 4–6; scalable to larger organizations.
- **Duration:** ~35–40 minutes total (5 min individual + 5 min placing + 5–10 min pair discussion + 10 min group comparison + 5 min big board + 5 min reflection + 10 min next steps).
- **How it works:**
  1. Explain the matrix: one axis is **certainty** (high to low), the other is **agreement** (high to low). This creates four zones: Simple (high certainty, high agreement), Complicated (high agreement, lower certainty), Complex (lower agreement, lower certainty), Chaotic (low certainty, low agreement).
  2. Distribute individual handouts with the matrix. Display a large version on the wall for reference.
  3. **5 minutes:** Individuals silently generate tasks or problems they (or the team) are sitting with and place each on their personal matrix.
  4. **~5 minutes:** Each person places their items on the shared wall matrix.
  5. **Pair up:** Partners discuss what they placed and help each other with anything they're unsure about.
  6. **Form groups of 4–6:** Each pair joins another pair. Groups compare their matrices, paying special attention to agreements, differences, and mismatches — especially items placed in very different zones. Set aside ~10 minutes for this step.
  7. **5 minutes:** All groups add their items to the big shared board to enable collective sensemaking.
  8. **5 minutes:** Groups step back and look for patterns — what stands out across all placements?
  9. **~10 minutes:** Full group discussion on next steps: what could we do about the patterns and mismatches discovered?

## Key Principles
- The matrix is based on the Stacey Matrix — complexity is a function of both how certain people are about a solution and how much agreement exists around it.
- Tasks in the **complex** and **chaotic** zones warrant the most attention: they signal where teams lack shared understanding or direction.
- **Mismatches between individuals' placements** are the most valuable output — they reveal hidden disagreements about what kind of problem something actually is.
- The exercise is a sensemaking tool, not an end in itself. Carsten explicitly recommends following it with another Liberating Structure to ensure action is taken on discovered mismatches.

## Facilitation Tips
- Use the large wall matrix primarily for explanation at the start, then as a shared canvas for the group consolidation phase.
- Keep individual brainstorming time-boxed (5 minutes) — it gives enough time without overthinking.
- During group comparison, direct attention explicitly to mismatches and differences, not just similarities. These are where the real conversations need to happen.
- Always plan a follow-up activity after this exercise to move from sensemaking into action — otherwise it risks becoming "just another exercise where nothing changes," as Carsten warns.

## Best Used When
- A team or organization wants to take stock of their current workload or challenges and categorize them by complexity.
- There is a suspicion that team members have different mental models of what problems they face or how solvable those problems are.
- Preparing for a planning or prioritization conversation that benefits from shared context about complexity and alignment.
- Introducing complexity thinking (Cynefin/Stacey) to a team in a fast, accessible way.

## Related Concepts
- **Stacey Matrix** — the theoretical foundation for the Agreement & Certainty Matrix axes and zones.
- **Cynefin Framework** — related complexity model (Simple, Complicated, Complex, Chaotic domains).
- **Liberating Structures** — the broader collection of participatory facilitation structures this belongs to.
- Carsten recommends combining this with another Liberating Structure as a follow-up to move from insight to action.
