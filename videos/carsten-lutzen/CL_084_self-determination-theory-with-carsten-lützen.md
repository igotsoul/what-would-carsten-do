# "Self-Determination Theory" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten Lützen introduces Self-Determination Theory (SDT), a well-researched academic framework developed by Deci and Ryan in the 1970s–80s, and connects it to agile coaching practice. The video explains the three core human needs — competence, relatedness, and autonomy — and why they matter for understanding motivation in individuals and teams.

## Methods & Techniques

### Self-Determination Theory — Three Core Needs (ab 01:00)
- **What:** A framework identifying three fundamental psychological needs that must be met for individuals (and teams) to be intrinsically motivated: competence, relatedness, and autonomy.
- **When to use:** When diagnosing low motivation, designing team structures, or coaching individuals and teams toward self-organization and engagement.
- **Group size:** Applicable to individuals and teams alike.
- **Duration:** N/A — this is a diagnostic/design lens, not a timed activity.
- **How it works:**
  1. **Competence** — People need to feel a sense of mastery in what they do. They don't need to be experts, but they must feel effective and capable. Carsten notes this is about enjoying the work and feeling able to do it.
  2. **Relatedness** — People need a sense of belonging and connection to others. Carsten ties this directly to the agile principle of moving work to teams rather than individuals — teams satisfy this fundamental need.
  3. **Autonomy** — People need to feel they have choice and control over decisions. At the team level, this translates to feeling empowered to determine direction collectively.

## Key Principles
- SDT is grounded in decades of empirical research, giving it strong academic credibility — Carsten highlights this as valuable for practitioners who want evidence-based tools.
- The three needs (competence, relatedness, autonomy) apply at both the individual and team level — each has a team-scale equivalent.
- The agile focus on teams is not arbitrary: it directly addresses the human need for relatedness and belonging.
- Empowered teams that deliver value to stakeholders can satisfy all three needs simultaneously.
- SDT also covers a spectrum from amotivation to intrinsic motivation, which Carsten flags as content for a future video — this card covers only the three basic needs portion.

## Facilitation Tips
- Use the three needs as a diagnostic lens when a team feels disengaged: ask which of competence, relatedness, or autonomy is missing or undermined.
- When advocating for team empowerment or self-organization to stakeholders, Carsten suggests SDT provides solid academic backing for the argument.
- Frame team design decisions (e.g., stable teams, shared ownership, decision-making authority) in terms of meeting these fundamental needs.

## Best Used When
- Coaching a team or individual experiencing low motivation or disengagement.
- Making the case for self-organizing teams or reduced micromanagement.
- Designing team structures or working agreements that sustain intrinsic motivation.
- Facilitating a retrospective or team health check that surfaces underlying needs.
