# "How Might We's" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten introduces "How Might We" (HMW) as a reframing tool drawn from design thinking and design sprints, applicable far beyond those contexts. The technique helps teams transform challenges and impediments into open-ended questions that spark curiosity and prevent premature solution-jumping. It's especially useful after identifying problems in retrospectives or problem-framing sessions.

## Methods & Techniques

### How Might We (HMW) Reframing (ab 00:30)
- **What:** A technique for rephrasing challenges or impediments as open "How Might We...?" questions, keeping a team curious and solution-agnostic while still pointing in a meaningful direction.
- **When to use:** After collecting challenges or impediments (e.g., in a retrospective), when a group tends to jump to solutions too quickly, or when you want to reframe a problem before ideation.
- **Group size:** Works individually or in groups; Carsten suggests everyone in the group can each rephrase the same challenge independently.
- **Duration:** Not specified; used as a transitional step within a larger session.
- **How it works:**
  1. Collect challenges or impediments (e.g., through a retrospective or problem-collection exercise).
  2. Vote on which challenges are most impactful to address.
  3. Individually or as a group, rephrase each selected challenge into one or more "How might we...?" questions.
  4. Generate multiple competing HMWs for the same challenge — there is no single right answer.
  5. Select the HMW(s) that give the group energy and feel most useful to explore.
  6. Use the chosen HMW(s) to guide further ideation or problem-solving.

## Key Principles
- HMWs are a balancing act: too narrow and you've slipped into solution mode; too broad and they lose focus and usefulness.
- A good HMW points toward the problem space without prescribing the solution.
- Multiple competing HMWs on the same challenge are not only acceptable — they're encouraged. The group then selects the most energizing one.
- The goal is to maintain curiosity about the actual problem rather than anchoring on a specific fix.
- Example of too narrow (solution-embedded): "How might we make a user see his account details on a page that also shows his address?" — this has a solution baked in.
- Better: "How might we help users see their own data?" — direction without prescription.
- Example of too broad: "How might we fix world peace?" — useful as a warning sign to tighten scope.

## Facilitation Tips
- According to Carsten, after collecting impediments in a retro, don't immediately problem-solve — use HMWs as an intermediate step to reframe before diving in.
- Have participants generate HMWs individually first, then compare and select together.
- Use energy as a selection criterion: ask the group which HMW gives them the most energy or feels most workable.
- Watch for HMWs that are secretly solutions in disguise — a sign the group has slipped into solution mode prematurely.

## Best Used When
- A team has surfaced challenges or impediments and is ready to focus on one or two before ideating solutions.
- Group dynamics tend toward premature solution-jumping.
- You want to introduce structured curiosity into a retrospective, design sprint, or Lightning Decision Jam.
- Problem definition feels fuzzy or contested and needs a shared reframe before moving forward.
