# "Chair Game" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten presents the Chair Game, a silent icebreaker/energizer that surfaces competing goals and the value of transparency in teams. Participants are split into three groups, each given a secret mission involving chairs, and must figure out — without speaking — how to collaborate despite seemingly conflicting objectives. The debrief reveals a powerful parallel to organizational dynamics.

## Methods & Techniques

### The Chair Game (ab 00:30)
- **What:** A silent group exercise where three teams each receive a secret, seemingly conflicting mission involving chairs. Through the process, teams discover that all missions can be fulfilled simultaneously — but only through emergent collaboration.
- **When to use:** As an energizer or icebreaker to open a conversation about transparency, alignment, competing priorities, or cross-team collaboration.
- **Group size:** 12+ participants (split into three groups)
- **Duration:** 15–20 minutes (Carsten recommends 15–20 over 30)
- **How it works:**
  1. Split participants into three groups (self-organize or count off 1-2-3; have them say the number aloud so they remember it).
  2. Announce that silence is mandatory — only the facilitator may speak.
  3. Show each group their written mission card privately, ensuring no other group sees it:
     - Group 1: "Put all chairs in a circle."
     - Group 2: "All chairs must be upside down."
     - Group 3: "All chairs must be in pairs."
  4. Start the exercise. Groups will initially compete, each trying to fulfill their own mission while others undo their work.
  5. If frustration rises, the facilitator may reveal: "All missions can in fact succeed simultaneously."
  6. Observe as groups shift from competing to aligning — e.g., chairs arranged in a circle, in pairs, and upside down.
  7. Debrief once all missions are fulfilled.

## Key Principles
- Competing goals in teams often aren't actually incompatible — they just appear that way when objectives aren't shared openly.
- Without transparency, teams default to competition; with transparency, they move toward alignment faster.
- The exercise mirrors real organizational dynamics: silos, hidden agendas, and unclear priorities lead to wasted effort and friction.
- Carsten attributes the original concept to someone named "Burnt" (linked in the video description).

## Facilitation Tips
- Prepare three physical instruction cards in advance — one per group — so missions can be revealed privately and simultaneously.
- When counting groups, have participants say the number themselves (rather than the facilitator counting them) — they're more likely to remember which group they're in.
- Enforce silence strictly; the constraint is what makes the dynamic emerge authentically.
- If the group gets stuck or frustrated, offer the hint that all missions can succeed — this nudges them toward cooperation without giving away the solution.
- Lean toward 15–20 minutes rather than 30; the insight tends to arrive well before the longer time limit.

## Best Used When
- Opening a session on cross-team collaboration, organizational transparency, or silos.
- Working with teams that struggle with competing priorities or misaligned goals.
- Needing a physical, engaging energizer that also generates rich debrief material.
- Running workshops where the theme is communication, alignment, or organizational effectiveness.

## Related Concepts
- Transparency as a Scrum value — the exercise is a kinesthetic demonstration of why openness accelerates team performance.
- Competing commitments / organizational silos — the setup mirrors how teams often operate with hidden or misaligned objectives.
- Psychological safety — silence and uncertainty create mild stress; the debrief is a safe space to reflect on the reaction.
