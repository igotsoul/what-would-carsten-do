# Session Rules with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten explains how to stage-set a workshop room by establishing explicit session rules before the work begins. The focus is on building psychological safety so participants feel comfortable sharing openly. He covers three core rules he uses and how to handle the rules conversation itself.

## Methods & Techniques

### Establishing Session Rules (ab 00:20)
- **What:** Before a workshop or retrospective begins, the facilitator explicitly states the rules of engagement — what stays in the room, how questions are handled, and how opinions are treated.
- **When to use:** Any workshop, retrospective, or coaching session where psychological safety matters — especially with new teams or sensitive topics.
- **Group size:** Any size
- **Duration:** 2–5 minutes at session start
- **How it works:**
  1. Present a rules slide at the start of the session
  2. Walk through each rule explicitly so expectations are clear
  3. Ask participants: "Is there any rule that is missing?"
  4. Add any participant-suggested rules to the slide
  5. Print or hang the rules slide on the wall so it stays visible throughout the session
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=dO4iu-5krrs&t=20

### The Vegas Rule (ab 00:55)
- **What:** "What happens in Vegas, stays in Vegas" — adapted to mean that what is shared in the session stays within the room unless the group explicitly agrees otherwise.
- **When to use:** Retrospectives, coaching workshops, any session involving personal observations, team tensions, or sensitive feedback.
- **How it works:** State clearly at the start that nothing discussed will leave the room without explicit group consent. This enables participants to speak freely.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=dO4iu-5krrs&t=55

### Perception is Reality (ab 01:30)
- **What:** A ground rule that treats each person's perception as their valid reality — no one's experience is wrong, even if others experienced the same situation differently.
- **When to use:** Whenever teams discuss events, incidents, or interpersonal dynamics where people may have conflicting views.
- **How it works:** Explicitly name this principle at the start. When someone shares their experience, the group acknowledges it as their reality rather than debating whether it "really happened that way."
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=dO4iu-5krrs&t=90

### All Questions Are OK + Parking Lot (ab 02:00)
- **What:** Participants are encouraged to ask questions freely, but the facilitator manages the flow to prevent one or two people from derailing the session.
- **When to use:** Any facilitated session where discussion can go off-track.
- **How it works:**
  1. State that all questions are welcome
  2. Give space for reflection before expecting answers
  3. If one participant asks too many questions, acknowledge them and redirect: "I hear you have many questions — let's save some for the break"
  4. Use a parking lot (visible list on the wall) to capture deferred questions so nothing is lost
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=dO4iu-5krrs&t=120

## Key Principles
- Explicit rules create the conditions for trust and openness — don't assume participants know what's expected
- Psychological safety is a prerequisite for a team to improve, not a nice-to-have
- "Perception is reality" — what someone experienced is valid even if others experienced it differently
- The facilitator's perception of a session (e.g., "the room felt open") may differ from participants' perception; check in rather than assume
- Involving participants in co-creating the rules increases buy-in and surfaces needs the facilitator may have missed

## Facilitation Tips
- Hang the rules slide on the wall so it stays visible and can be referenced if the group drifts
- After presenting your rules, always ask "Is there any rule that is missing?" — this invites ownership and catches gaps
- When managing a question-heavy participant, validate their curiosity before redirecting: "I hear you have a lot of questions..." rather than shutting them down
- Answer the relevant questions in the moment; park the others rather than ignoring them
- Don't rush the rules-setting moment — this investment at the start pays off throughout the session

## Best Used When
- Starting a retrospective, coaching workshop, or any session involving personal or sensitive content
- Working with a team that lacks psychological safety or hasn't worked together before
- Facilitating a session where confidentiality expectations need to be explicit
- Onboarding participants who are unfamiliar with facilitated formats
