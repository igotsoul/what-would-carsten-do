# "Cognitive Load" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten Lützen explains cognitive load through the lens of facilitation — why it matters, how to spot it in the room, and what facilitators can do before, during, and after a meeting to reduce unnecessary mental burden. The video offers a practical three-phase framework for keeping participants able to absorb and act on information without overwhelm.

## Methods & Techniques

### Three-Phase Cognitive Load Management Framework (ab 03:30)
- **What:** A structured approach to reducing cognitive load split into three phases: before, during, and after the meeting.
- **When to use:** Any time you are designing or running a meeting, workshop, or collaborative session where participants need to process and act on information.
- **Group size:** Any
- **Duration:** Applicable to sessions of any length
- **How it works:**
  1. **Before the meeting:** Send a simple agenda with 3–5 headlines. Offer asynchronous prep work so people can contribute and reflect in advance. Set clear expectations: what decision will be made, what the goal is, and what is explicitly out of scope for this session.
  2. **During the meeting:** Chunk information into bite-sized pieces. Limit the number of choices presented at once. Use visual aids and handouts deliberately, not liberally. Build in pauses — Carsten recommends a rule of thumb of 10% silence per meeting (approximately 6 minutes per hour). Use that silence for open reflection, not structured exercises.
  3. **After the meeting:** Share a brief recap focused only on next steps — who does what and why. Keep it short; avoid multi-page summaries where only 5% of the content is relevant.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=rxhR64WzlmQ&t=210

### The 10% Silence Rule (ab 05:30)
- **What:** Reserving approximately 10% of meeting time for unstructured silence — no prompt, no task, just space to reflect or process.
- **When to use:** Any meeting or workshop where participants are processing complex or dense information.
- **Group size:** Any
- **Duration:** ~6 minutes per hour of meeting time
- **How it works:** Simply ask participants to sit in silence and notice what feels relevant to them. Do not assign a specific reflective task. Let the silence do the work.

## Key Principles
- Carsten uses the metaphor of a browser with too many open tabs: the more tabs open, the less we can focus — one may be playing music, another pops up a notification. Facilitators must help participants close unnecessary tabs.
- There are three types of cognitive load to manage: **intrinsic load** (complexity of the topic itself), **extraneous load** (unnecessary distractions, poor structure, phone checking), and **germane load** (the effort needed to build meaningful understanding from what is being discussed).
- Signs of cognitive load overload: zoning out, multitasking, checking phones, inability to make decisions due to information fatigue.
- Expert knowledge is not evenly distributed in a team. When facilitating, pace the flow of information for the least informed participant, not the most expert.
- Poor decisions in meetings are often a symptom of information fatigue, not disagreement or disengagement.

## Facilitation Tips
- Keep agenda items in the invite to 3–5 headlines at most. This leaves you room to adapt without setting rigid expectations.
- Use async prep to give reflective thinkers a chance to contribute meaningfully before the session starts.
- Explicitly name what is out of scope at the start of a meeting and offer to schedule a separate session for derailed topics — this respects divergent needs without letting the meeting lose focus.
- When you notice participants zoning out or multitasking, treat it as a signal that cognitive load is too high, not that people are disengaged.
- Recaps should be ruthlessly edited — Carsten notes that in many long recaps, only about 5% of the content is actually relevant.

## Best Used When
- Designing workshops or meetings where participants need to absorb complex or technical information
- Running sessions where decisions need to be made and information fatigue is a risk
- Coaching teams or facilitators on meeting quality and attention management
- Preparing for a meeting where participants have varying levels of expertise on the topic
