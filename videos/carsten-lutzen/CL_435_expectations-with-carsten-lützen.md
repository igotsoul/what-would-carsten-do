# Expectations with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten Lützen presents a structured paper-based exercise for aligning mutual expectations within a team. Participants write what the team can expect from them and what they expect from the team, then pass papers around so each person adds their own expectations of and for each colleague. The result is a tangible, co-created artifact that captures team agreements and can be referenced after the session.

## Methods & Techniques

### Expectations Exercise (ab 00:45)
- **What:** A structured worksheet exercise where each team member articulates their own commitments to the team and their expectations of the team, then receives peer input on both dimensions.
- **When to use:** Retrospectives, team kick-offs, or dedicated alignment sessions — especially when a team faces a challenging period and needs to strengthen cohesion.
- **Group size:** Small to medium teams (works well when everyone can sit around a table)
- **Duration:** ~30–60 min depending on team size and debrief depth
- **How it works:**
  1. Hand each participant a pen and a blank sheet of paper.
  2. Participants divide the paper: draw a horizontal line splitting it in half, then divide the upper half vertically — creating three zones (top-left, top-right, bottom).
  3. Each person writes their name on the paper.
  4. **Top-left:** "What the team can expect from me" — individual writes their own commitments and contributions.
  5. **Top-right:** "What I expect from the team" — individual writes their own expectations.
  6. Once everyone has finished writing, papers are passed around the circle. Each person reads the named individual's top sections and adds their comment in the **bottom zone**: what they personally expect from that person. They sign each comment.
  7. Papers keep rotating until everyone has added a comment and each person receives their own paper back.
  8. Individuals silently read and digest all comments. Facilitated debrief follows.

### Circle Icebreaker (ab 00:38)
- **What:** The whole team stands in a circle; each person says one positive thing to the person next to them, going all the way around.
- **When to use:** As a warm-up before the main expectations exercise to open the emotional space.
- **Group size:** Any team size
- **Duration:** 5–10 min
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=fgIxO1p6lko&t=38s

## Key Principles
- Expectations should flow in both directions: what you offer the team AND what you need from the team.
- Peer expectations (bottom section) surface what colleagues actually count on from each individual — this often differs from self-perception.
- Gaps are valuable: if many people list something as a team expectation but nobody claims to deliver it, that tension is worth discussing.
- The physical paper artifact serves as a durable team agreement that can be revisited after the session.
- Carsten notes the icebreaker can itself be "difficult enough for some teams" — acknowledging that positive affirmation is not always easy and should be taken seriously.

## Facilitation Tips
- Watch for the moment people lift their pen and look around — that's the signal everyone is done writing and you can move to the rotation phase.
- During debrief, ask: *Is there anything surprising here? Anything you'd like clarification on?*
- Surface systemic patterns: if a theme appears often in the "expect from team" column but no one owns it in "expect from me," name that gap explicitly.
- Allow time for quiet reading before opening discussion — people need a moment to digest peer feedback before reacting.
- The exercise works both as a retro activity and as a standalone alignment session; frame it based on what the team needs.

## Best Used When
- A team is newly formed or entering a challenging phase and needs shared clarity on roles and mutual accountability.
- There is implicit tension around unmet expectations that has not been surfaced explicitly.
- A team needs a concrete, take-away artifact from an alignment session rather than just a conversation.
- During a retrospective when the focus is on team norms and working agreements.
