# Achievement Posters with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten introduces Achievement Posters, a retrospective warm-up exercise designed to celebrate what the team did well during a sprint. By creating large visual posters organized around meaningful categories, teams build positive energy and create an ongoing archive of success they can revisit when confidence dips.

## Methods & Techniques

### Achievement Posters (ab 00:20)
- **What:** Teams create A3-sized posters listing sprint successes organized into categories — turning retrospective celebration into a visible, tangible artifact.
- **When to use:** At the start of a retrospective to build positive energy and shift the team's focus toward accomplishments before diving into improvements.
- **Group size:** Whole team
- **Duration:** Not specified; intended as a retro kick-off activity (est. 10–15 min)
- **How it works:**
  1. Prepare an A3 (or large) sheet of paper per poster.
  2. Define categories relevant to the team — examples: Product Metrics, Business Value, Social/Team, Knowledge Sharing, Other.
  3. Brainstorm and fill in concrete achievements under each category (e.g., "app reached 1M downloads", "0 critical bugs", "we had a Friday beer").
  4. Display the poster — ideally on a wall alongside posters from previous sprints.
  5. Over time, the collection becomes a visible history of team success.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aaELoO2twpo

## Key Principles
- Retrospectives should celebrate success, not only surface problems — positive energy matters.
- Categories should be defined per team context; there is no universal list.
- The social dimension of teamwork (how we did as people, not just deliverers) deserves explicit recognition.
- An ongoing wall of Achievement Posters serves as a confidence resource: when morale is low, teams can look back at concrete evidence of past success.
- Achievements don't have to be work-related — team moments and personal milestones count too.

## Facilitation Tips
- Use A3 paper — the larger format signals that achievements are worth displaying prominently.
- Include a social/team category explicitly; Carsten considers it especially important and it's easy to overlook.
- Add an "Other" category so nothing meaningful gets left out.
- Keep the posters and display them on a shared wall — the cumulative effect over multiple sprints is where the real value builds.
- If the team has known issues (e.g., siloed knowledge, quality problems), design categories that make progress in those areas visible and celebrated.

## Best Used When
- Kicking off a retrospective and the team needs an energy lift or positive framing.
- Team confidence is low and a reminder of past successes would help.
- The team has had a strong sprint and deserves structured recognition of it.
- You want to build a team habit of noticing and celebrating progress over time.
