# "Tiebreakers" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten introduces a small but effective facilitation technique for resolving the "who goes first?" awkwardness in pair exercises. By posing a quick, playful question (e.g., "who has owned the most fish?"), facilitators can eliminate social friction and get participants into the actual exercise faster — while keeping the space psychologically safe.

## Methods & Techniques

### Tiebreaker Questions (ab 00:30)
- **What:** A quick, lighthearted question posed to a pair to determine who speaks first, replacing the awkward negotiation of "you go first / no, you go first."
- **When to use:** At the start of pair exercises within Liberating Structures (e.g., 1-2-4-All, Impromptu Networking) or any format where one person in a pair needs to go first.
- **Group size:** Pairs
- **Duration:** < 1 minute — meant to be resolved instantly
- **How it works:**
  1. Before the pair exercise begins, pose a single comparison question to each pair.
  2. The person with the "most" (or "longest", "most unique", etc.) goes first.
  3. Example questions: "Who has owned the most fish?", "Who traveled the longest to get here today?", "Who has the most books?", "Who has the most different colors on their clothes?"

## Key Principles
- Social politeness around "who goes first" wastes energy and creates low-grade friction — removing it actively improves the participant experience.
- Tiebreaker questions should be harmless, slightly absurd, and fun — the goal is a quick laugh, not a deep answer.
- Psychological safety is the constraint: avoid questions touching on politics, weight, age, income, or any protected or sensitive attribute.
- The technique is a micro-intervention that speeds up the transition into the real exercise.

## Facilitation Tips
- Keep it fast — the tiebreaker is not a discussion, just a one-second comparison.
- Lean into the absurdity (e.g., "most fish", "longest hair") — the silliness itself relaxes the room.
- Hard avoids: voting history, body-related attributes (weight, age, height), income, or anything politically charged.
- Carsten suggests collecting and sharing your own tiebreaker questions with the community to build a repertoire.

## Best Used When
- Running pair-based Liberating Structures (Impromptu Networking, 1-2-4-All, Troika Consulting, etc.)
- Groups are new to each other and social awkwardness is likely
- You want to add a playful micro-moment without derailing pace
- You need a culturally neutral way to assign speaking order
