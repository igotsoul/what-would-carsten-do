# "Dialogic Orientation Quadrant" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten introduces the Dialogic Orientation Quadrant (DOQ), a reflective tool created by Hazen Moon, for analyzing how coaches "load" their questions. The quadrant maps questions across two axes — past/future and positive/negative — to help coaches become more intentional about which quadrant they operate in during coaching sessions. Carsten shares that many coaches believe they ask forward-looking, empowering questions, but self-analysis often reveals otherwise.

## Methods & Techniques

### Dialogic Orientation Quadrant (DOQ) (ab 00:30)
- **What:** A two-by-two framework for reflecting on and intentionally directing the questions you ask in coaching conversations, based on whether they are future/past-oriented and positive/negative in orientation.
- **When to use:** During coaching supervision, self-reflection on a recorded session, or when preparing to shift the energy of a coaching conversation toward more empowering territory.
- **Group size:** Individual coach reflection; applicable in coaching dyads or supervision groups.
- **Duration:** Can be applied as a brief post-session reflection or a structured analysis of a recorded session.
- **How it works:**
  1. Draw a 2x2 grid with one axis representing **Past ↔ Future** and the other representing **Negative ↔ Positive**.
  2. Label the four quadrants:
     - **Preferred Future** (future + positive): Where do we want to go? What does success look like?
     - **Resourceful Past** (past + positive): When has this worked before? What resources have we already used?
     - **Troubled Past** (past + negative): When did it not work? What went wrong?
     - **Dreaded Future** (future + negative): What are we afraid will happen? What feels hopeless?
  3. Listen back to a coaching session and place a mark for each question asked in the corresponding quadrant.
  4. Notice where you actually spend your time versus where you assumed you were operating.
  5. In future sessions, consciously load more questions toward the **Preferred Future** and **Resourceful Past** quadrants.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=PnGN9EewF5M&t=30

## Key Principles
- All questions are "loaded" with presuppositions — the coach's task is to load them with presuppositions that are useful to the client (attributed to Healing and Pavelas).
- Most coaches assume they ask primarily Preferred Future questions, but self-analysis typically reveals significant time spent in the Troubled Past and Dreaded Future quadrants.
- Carsten draws on the solution-focused approach: all solutions have likely been seen working in some form already, which makes the Resourceful Past a powerful and underutilized quadrant.
- Awareness of question orientation is the first step; intentional redirection is the second.

## Facilitation Tips
- Record or listen back to an actual coaching session and physically mark each question on the quadrant — the data is often surprising and humbling.
- Don't assume you are operating in the Preferred Future quadrant; verify it with evidence.
- When entering a new session with a coachee who is stuck or negative, deliberately front-load the conversation with Preferred Future and Resourceful Past questions to shift the energy.
- The DOQ works as both a diagnostic (after the fact) and a planning tool (before or during a session).

## Best Used When
- A coach or Scrum Master wants to reflect on the quality and direction of their questioning habits.
- A coachee seems stuck in problem-focus or hopelessness and needs to be reoriented toward possibility.
- Supervision or peer coaching sessions where question analysis is the focus.
- Preparing for a high-stakes coaching conversation where intentional framing matters.

## Related Concepts
- Solution-Focused Coaching — the philosophical backbone of the Resourceful Past quadrant
- Appreciative Inquiry — similar orientation toward strengths and the positive
- Powerful Questions — general coaching competency around question design
