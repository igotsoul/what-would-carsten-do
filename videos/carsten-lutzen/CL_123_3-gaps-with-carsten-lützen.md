# "3 Gaps" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten introduces the "Three Gaps" model — originally from Steven Bungay, referenced via Martin Delman's book on Sprint Goals — as a language framework for teams to surface and discuss hidden assumptions. The model identifies three recurring gaps between intention and reality that teams must name and address explicitly to drive meaningful change.

## Methods & Techniques

### The Three Gaps Model (ab 01:30)
- **What:** A diagnostic framework that identifies three types of gaps between what teams plan and what actually happens, giving teams a shared vocabulary to surface and address assumptions.
- **When to use:** When working with a team on change initiatives, retrospectives, sprint planning, or any situation where intended actions aren't producing expected results.
- **Group size:** Any team size
- **Duration:** Can be used as a brief framing tool (5–10 min) or as the basis for a deeper team conversation
- **How it works:**
  1. **Effects Gap** — The gap between what you *expect* your actions to achieve versus what they *actually* achieve. Use when actions produce unexpected outcomes. Address with solution-focused coaching: ask "How would we notice a positive change has happened?"
  2. **Knowledge Gap** — The gap between what you *think* you know versus what you *actually* know. Surfaces hidden assumptions and unconscious incompetence. Address by distinguishing facts from beliefs, and where uncertain, design experiments to learn.
  3. **Alignment Gap** — The gap between what you *want* people to do versus what they *actually* do. Address by clarifying intent, not just instructions — people may comply in form but miss the spirit of the action.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=w4Dmw8uQgZc

## Key Principles
- Teams spend significant time operating on assumptions without naming them — giving those assumptions a category (effects, knowledge, alignment) makes them discussable.
- Distinguish between "I know this for a fact" and "I feel/think this is true" — feelings are not evidence, and acting on them as facts creates knowledge gaps.
- When a knowledge gap exists, the appropriate response is a scientific approach: form a hypothesis, run an experiment, learn from the result.
- Alignment gaps often stem from unclear intent, not lack of willingness — people may comply superficially while missing the purpose behind an action.
- Carsten attributes the model to Steven Bungay, surfaced through Martin Delman's book on Sprint Goals.

## Facilitation Tips
- Use the three gap labels explicitly in team conversations: "I think this falls into the knowledge gap — what can we do to minimize it?"
- When addressing the effects gap, apply solution-focused framing: ask what a positive outcome would *look like* before designing actions.
- For the alignment gap, examine not just whether people are doing the action, but whether they understand the *intent* behind it.
- The model works well as a recurring check-in lens during retrospectives or after sprint reviews to categorize what went wrong and why.

## Best Used When
- A team is reflecting on why a planned change didn't produce the expected result
- During retrospectives where surface-level issues keep recurring
- Coaching a team through a change initiative where buy-in or understanding feels inconsistent
- When a team needs a shared, neutral language to discuss dysfunction without blame
