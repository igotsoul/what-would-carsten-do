# "Pyt-button" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten introduces the Danish concept of "pyt" (roughly "oh well") as a mental tool for facilitators and coaches to avoid overthinking when things don't go as planned. The video explores how to use self-forgiveness and positive reframing to move forward after setbacks — a mindset Carsten considers essential for anyone working in complex, unpredictable environments.

## Methods & Techniques

### The Pyt-Button (ab 01:30)
- **What:** A Danish concept — "pyt" means something like "oh well" or "that's life" — used as a mental reset button when something doesn't go as expected. Carsten frames it as a deliberate practice of self-compassion and forward movement.
- **When to use:** After a workshop exercise falls flat, a joke doesn't land, a facilitation choice backfires, or any moment where you catch yourself replaying what went wrong.
- **Group size:** Individual practice (personal resilience tool)
- **Duration:** Instantaneous — a single phrase or thought used in the moment
- **How it works:**
  1. Notice you are spiralling into "why did I do that?" thinking
  2. Say (aloud or internally) "pyt" or "oh well" — acknowledge that what happened, happened
  3. If that's enough, pick up any learnings and move on
  4. If you cannot let it go, reframe: identify what you learned specifically ("that exercise didn't work because of X")
  5. Express the learning positively and then release it
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=qIDAvICxi04&t=90

### Positive Reframing as a Second Step (ab 02:45)
- **What:** When the pyt-button alone isn't enough to break the overthinking loop, Carsten suggests consciously rephrasing the experience as a learning statement rather than a failure narrative.
- **When to use:** When the situation feels too significant to simply let go without processing.
- **How it works:** Replace "why did I do that?" with "that didn't work because X, and next time I'll try Y." Converts rumination into a concrete, forward-facing takeaway.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=qIDAvICxi04&t=165

## Key Principles
- Hindsight is always 20/20, but foresight is not — especially when working in the complex domain (e.g., with Kanban, agile, human systems). Accepting this asymmetry is foundational to reducing self-blame.
- The goal is not to become Teflon and stop reflecting — reflection matters. The goal is to prevent reflection from becoming rumination.
- Self-forgiveness is the first step to learning. You cannot extract a useful lesson while you are still punishing yourself.
- "Thinking traps" (looping on "why didn't I do X instead?") actively consume mental capacity that facilitators and coaches need for the next moment.

## Facilitation Tips
- Carsten normalises imperfection explicitly: not *if* something goes wrong, but *when*. Facilitators who accept this framing enter sessions with more psychological safety for themselves.
- Having a simple, repeatable phrase or ritual ("pyt", "oh well") lowers the activation energy needed to reset after a misstep mid-session or post-session.
- The two-step approach (pyt → reframe if needed) gives a usable protocol rather than vague advice to "just let it go."

## Best Used When
- Debriefing after a workshop where an exercise didn't land as expected
- Coaching facilitators or Scrum Masters who are prone to perfectionism or self-criticism
- Introducing the concept of psychological safety — starting with safety *for the facilitator*, not just the team
- Sharing in agile retrospectives as a prompt for how teams handle failure and learning

## Related Concepts
- Psychological safety (applying it to oneself, not only to teams)
- Growth mindset / learning from failure
- Kanban and complexity thinking (Cynefin complex domain — unpredictable outcomes are inherent)
- Retrospective formats focused on self-compassion and team resilience
