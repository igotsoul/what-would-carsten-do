# "Generative Relationships" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten introduces Generative Relationships (also known as STAR+STAR), a Liberating Structure designed as a quick conversation starter for teams and working groups. Using a four-axis star model, participants individually reflect on their team's current dynamics and then progressively share insights in pairs and groups of four. The exercise surfaces patterns and creates a shared foundation for improvement discussions in roughly 25 minutes.

## Methods & Techniques

### Generative Relationships STAR+STAR (ab 00:30)
- **What:** A structured reflection and conversation exercise using a four-axis star model to help teams assess their current relational dynamics across Separateness, Tuning, Action, and Reason.
- **When to use:** Ideal as a conversation starter with newly formed or existing teams, working groups, or any constellation of people that needs to examine how they work together. Works well at the start of a workshop or team development session.
- **Group size:** Works in pairs, then groups of four; scales to full room debrief. No explicit maximum stated.
- **Duration:** Approximately 25 minutes total.
- **How it works:**
  1. Introduce the four axes to participants so everyone has a shared understanding:
     - **Separateness** — diversity within the group (1 = low, 5 = high)
     - **Tuning** — how well the group listens to and internalizes each other
     - **Action** — how well the group acts as a team rather than as individuals
     - **Reason** — whether there is a genuine shared reason to work together
  2. Give each participant their own copy of the star model (printed or on a virtual whiteboard).
  3. Individuals reflect **silently** for ~5 minutes, marking where they see the team on each axis and making notes on their reasoning.
  4. Participants pair up and share their ratings and reasoning with each other.
  5. Pairs join another pair (groups of four) to look for patterns and discuss emerging themes.
  6. Full-room debrief: collect patterns on a shared large version of the model visible to everyone.
  7. Follow up with an action-oriented exercise such as 15% Solutions, 1-2-4-All, or 4-2-1 Snap to identify first steps.

## Key Principles
- Many Liberating Structures share common underlying mechanics — Generative Relationships follows the familiar pattern of individual reflection → pairs → small groups → whole room.
- Individual silent reflection before discussion prevents anchoring bias and ensures every voice is captured before group dynamics take over.
- The four axes are designed to be complementary tensions: too much "tuning" without "action" produces listening but no output; too little "reason" undermines the value of all other axes.
- The model provides a neutral, shared language that makes it easier for teams to discuss sensitive relational dynamics without it feeling personal.
- Carsten emphasizes that the exercise is a **conversation starter**, not a diagnostic endpoint — the real value comes from the patterns that emerge and the follow-up actions.

## Facilitation Tips
- Prepare one small copy of the star model per participant plus one large central copy for the group debrief.
- For virtual sessions, provide individual spaces on a shared whiteboard so everyone can work simultaneously without seeing each other's answers first.
- Rating scale can be numeric (1–5) or qualitative (low–high) — choose whichever feels right for the group.
- During the pair and group-of-four phases, prompt participants to share not just **what** they rated but **why** — the reasoning behind ratings generates the richest conversation.
- Follow up immediately with 15% Solutions or a similar action exercise to convert insights into concrete next steps while energy is high.
- The full sequence — including debrief — typically runs in ~25 minutes, making it practical even for shorter sessions.

## Best Used When
- A team has just been formed and needs to establish a shared baseline of how they want to work together.
- An existing team is stuck or underperforming and needs a structured, psychologically safe way to surface relational gaps.
- A workshop needs a fast, engaging opener that grounds participants in team dynamics before moving into problem-solving.
- A facilitator wants to create alignment around what "good collaboration" looks like before launching a longer intervention.
