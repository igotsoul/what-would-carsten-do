# "Conflict Staircase" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten introduces the Conflict Staircase — a model for understanding how conflicts escalate through distinct phases, from productive disagreement to open hostility. The model helps coaches and Scrum Masters assess where a conflict currently sits and choose appropriate interventions to de-escalate it.

## Methods & Techniques

### Conflict Staircase Model (ab 00:20)
- **What:** A staged escalation model that maps how conflicts move from healthy disagreement to destructive polarization, with each step representing a qualitative shift in dynamics.
- **When to use:** When assessing the severity of a team conflict or choosing an intervention strategy; useful as a shared diagnostic tool in coaching conversations.
- **Group size:** 1-on-1 coaching or team context
- **Duration:** Diagnostic tool — no fixed duration
- **How it works:**
  1. **Disagreement (green)** — Healthy difference of opinion; a learning opportunity where alignment creates mutual growth. This is the desired state.
  2. **Personification** — Focus shifts from the issue to the person. The other party becomes "the problem" rather than the disagreement itself.
  3. **Escalation** — The conflict broadens; the person is seen as the source of multiple problems, not just the original one.
  4. **Communication breakdown** — People stop talking to each other. Carsten flags this as a critical threshold: silent conflicts are far harder to resolve than vocal ones.
  5. **Open hostility / Polarization** — Full-blown conflict; people are entrenched and antagonistic. Requires significant effort to de-escalate.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=QEphcUSU644&t=20s

### Using the Model as a Shared Language (ab 02:30)
- **What:** Invite conflicting parties to locate themselves on the staircase, creating a common frame of reference for the conflict.
- **When to use:** Early in a conflict coaching conversation, before diving into solutions.
- **How it works:** Show the model, explain each step, and ask: "Where do you see yourselves right now?" The shared language alone can begin to de-escalate by making the conflict discussable.

## Key Principles
- Conflict is not inherently bad — disagreement is green because it signals a learning opportunity.
- The critical threshold in the staircase is **loss of communication**: once people stop talking, the coach must intervene more actively.
- Steps 1–3 (disagreement through escalation) can often be resolved through conversation, potentially without external facilitation.
- Steps 4–5 require deliberate coaching effort and de-escalation work before any content-level resolution is possible.
- A shared model and shared language is itself a de-escalation tool — naming the dynamic reduces its power.

## Facilitation Tips
- Carsten recommends **Solution-Focused Coaching** as a concrete approach to create actionable steps toward de-escalation, particularly at the mid-to-high escalation phases.
- Use the visual staircase as a prop in coaching conversations — asking parties to self-locate reduces defensiveness and opens dialogue.
- Aim to move people not just one step down the staircase, but ideally off it entirely back to productive disagreement.

## Best Used When
- A Scrum Master or coach senses tension in a team and wants to assess severity before choosing an intervention.
- Two team members have stopped collaborating or communicating effectively.
- You need a neutral, non-judgmental framework to open a coaching conversation about conflict.
- Preparing for a conflict mediation or team intervention and wanting to set the stage with a shared model.

## Related Concepts
- Solution-Focused Coaching — mentioned by Carsten as a recommended de-escalation tool
- Conflict models generally exist in many variations; Carsten notes this is one chosen flavor
- Psychological safety — foundational condition that keeps teams in the disagreement (green) zone
