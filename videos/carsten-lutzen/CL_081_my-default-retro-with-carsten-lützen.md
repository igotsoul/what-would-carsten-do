# "My Default Retro" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten Lützen, an agile coach and Scrum Master, walks through his default 8-step retrospective template that he relies on because it consistently produces results. The format balances psychological safety, positive reflection, structured inspection, and forward-looking improvement — all within a recommended 90-minute timebox.

## Methods & Techniques

### Vegas Rule (ab 00:45)
- **What:** An explicit agreement that what is shared in the retrospective stays in the retrospective, unless the team deliberately decides otherwise.
- **When to use:** At the start of every retrospective, especially with teams where trust or openness is not yet established.
- **Group size:** Any
- **Duration:** 1–2 minutes
- **How it works:** The facilitator states the rule out loud — do not assume it is understood. The only exception is when the team consciously votes to bring something out of the retro as a shared focus point.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=KJV_lGqsfkQ&t=45s

### Silent Art Energizer (ab 01:30)
- **What:** A silent drawing exercise where participants create a picture using only sticky notes, without talking, to warm up and signal their emotional state.
- **When to use:** At the start of a retrospective to transition people into the space and give the facilitator an early read on team mood.
- **Group size:** Any; works well remotely
- **Duration:** 3–5 minutes drawing, brief debrief after
- **How it works:**
  1. Prepare a virtual picture frame (e.g., in Miro or Mural).
  2. Ask participants to draw anything they want using only sticky notes — no talking allowed.
  3. After 3–4 minutes, participants add a few words, name themselves as the painter, and give the artwork a title.
  4. The facilitator observes: are the drawings cheerful or heavy? This gives early insight into team mood.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=KJV_lGqsfkQ&t=90s

### Achievement Posters (ab 02:30)
- **What:** A structured celebration of the team's wins across four categories: product achievements, knowledge sharing, social activities, and anything else.
- **When to use:** Early in the retrospective to anchor the team in a positive mindset before moving into inspection.
- **Group size:** Any
- **Duration:** 5–10 minutes
- **How it works:**
  1. Ask the team to contribute to four columns: product achievements, knowledge sharing, social activities, and "other" (personal milestones, deliveries, etc.).
  2. Capture these as a record — Carsten recommends archiving them so they can be revisited a year later to show team growth.
- **Facilitation note:** The archive becomes a powerful longitudinal artifact. A year from now, you can ask: "A year ago you were proud of this — what are you proud of now?"
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=KJV_lGqsfkQ&t=150s

### Follow-up Review (ab 03:15)
- **What:** A brief status check on action items or focus points from the previous retrospective.
- **When to use:** Every retrospective except the first one with a team.
- **Group size:** Any
- **Duration:** 5 minutes
- **How it works:**
  1. The driver of each previous follow-up item gives a quick status update.
  2. The team votes: continue focusing on this, or close it?
  3. Items kept open are carried forward; closed items are archived.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=KJV_lGqsfkQ&t=195s

### Retro Wheel (Modified Starfish) (ab 03:50)
- **What:** A modified starfish format with six dimensions: Start, Stop, Continue, More, Less — plus an appreciative feedback dimension directed at a specific person in the room.
- **When to use:** As the core inspection activity of the retrospective.
- **Group size:** Any
- **Duration:** 15–25 minutes including individual thinking, clustering, and dot voting
- **How it works:**
  1. Give participants 4–5 minutes to silently add sticky notes to each of the six dimensions.
  2. As a group, cluster the notes and give clusters a title.
  3. Run dot voting to surface the top priorities.
  4. Pick the top 2–3 items to take forward.
  5. For the appreciative feedback dimension: each person gives specific, non-sarcastic, non-ironic feedback to one named colleague. The facilitator is excluded as a recipient.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=KJV_lGqsfkQ&t=230s

### Signs of Improvement (ab 05:00)
- **What:** For each top-voted item, the team defines observable signs that would indicate the problem is improving or resolved — before jumping to solutions.
- **When to use:** Immediately after dot voting on the Retro Wheel, before assigning actions.
- **Group size:** Any
- **Duration:** 5–10 minutes per item
- **How it works:**
  1. Take the highest-voted item.
  2. Ask: "How would we — as a team member, developer, or manager — know that this has improved or is no longer a problem?"
  3. Capture 3–10 signs, whatever makes sense.
  4. Assign a volunteer "driver" — not responsible for fixing the issue, but responsible for giving a status update at the next retrospective.
  5. Deliberately do not lock in specific actions; the signs guide organic experimentation.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=KJV_lGqsfkQ&t=300s

### Session Evaluation (ab 07:00)
- **What:** A simple 1–5 ROI rating to evaluate the retrospective itself, plus an open sticky note area for reflections.
- **When to use:** At the close of every retrospective as a role-modeling habit.
- **Group size:** Any
- **Duration:** 2–3 minutes
- **How it works:** Ask participants to rate the session on a 1–5 scale (How well was time spent?). Optionally, leave space for sticky notes with takeaways or things to never repeat.
- **Facilitation note:** Carsten frames this as role modeling — the facilitator wants to improve too, so they invite feedback on their own session.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=KJV_lGqsfkQ&t=420s

## Key Principles
- The retrospective is the single most important meeting for a team — it is where teams improve both their process and their relationships.
- Psychological safety must be made explicit, not assumed. Saying the Vegas Rule out loud is a deliberate act.
- Start in a positive space before inspecting problems. Celebration and achievement-framing precede critique.
- Avoid premature solutioning. Signs of Improvement anchor the team in observable outcomes first, allowing solutions to emerge naturally.
- Limit focus points to 2–3 items maximum. More items means none get meaningful attention.
- The driver role is about accountability for reporting, not responsibility for solving. This reduces pressure and encourages volunteering.
- Longitudinal archives of achievement posters make team growth visible over time.
- Role modeling matters: when the facilitator asks for feedback on the session, it signals that improvement applies to everyone.

## Facilitation Tips
- Introduce yourself briefly if you are not the team's regular facilitator — one or two sentences is enough context.
- Do not assume the Vegas Rule is understood; state it explicitly every time.
- During the Silent Art Energizer, observe drawings in real time for mood signals — are they light and playful, or heavy and dark?
- Keep the Retro Wheel individual and silent during the writing phase (4–5 minutes) before any group discussion.
- Cap follow-up items at 2–3. If the team has historically failed to close items, fewer is better.
- The appreciative feedback dimension of the Retro Wheel requires firm framing: it must be specific, sincere, and directed at a named person — not the facilitator.
- 90 minutes is the recommended timebox. 60 minutes is possible but risks going too fast and too shallow.
- Keep the overall structure consistent across several sprints so the team builds familiarity with the format before experimenting with variations.

## Best Used When
- Running a full-team retrospective (any size, co-located or remote)
- Facilitating a team you are not deeply embedded with and need to establish trust quickly
- The team needs both a structured inspection process and space for relationship-building
- You want a repeatable, low-prep format that scales from new teams to mature ones
