# Figures with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten demonstrates two simple techniques for drawing human figures on flipcharts: a basic circle-body figure and "Star People." He shows how to scale both to represent groups and offers tips for adding color and grounding to give illustrations a polished finish.

## Methods & Techniques

### Simple Circle Figure (ab 00:20)
- **What:** A minimalist human figure built from a circle (head), vertical line (body), two lines for legs, and optional arms.
- **When to use:** Quick illustrations on flipcharts where recognizable humans are needed without artistic skill.
- **Group size:** N/A (drawing technique)
- **Duration:** Seconds per figure
- **How it works:**
  1. Draw a circle for the head
  2. Add a vertical line for the body
  3. Draw two eyes and a mouth
  4. Add two legs
  5. Optionally add arms, hands, colors, and speech bubbles
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=gAs1quOGDfU&t=20

### Star People (ab 01:20)
- **What:** A cross/star-shaped figure — one horizontal line for arms, one vertical line splitting into two legs — with a circle head added on top.
- **When to use:** A widely recognized alternative figure style; slightly faster to draw for groups.
- **Group size:** N/A
- **Duration:** Seconds per figure
- **How it works:**
  1. Draw a circle for the head
  2. Draw one long horizontal line for both arms
  3. Draw two diagonal lines downward for legs
  4. Add a face
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=gAs1quOGDfU&t=80

### Drawing Groups of People (ab 02:00)
- **What:** Technique for efficiently drawing crowds or teams on a flipchart using either figure style.
- **When to use:** When visualizing a team, audience, stakeholders, or any collective on a flipchart.
- **How it works (Circle style):**
  1. Draw all bodies first in a row
  2. Add heads on top of each body
  3. For larger groups, stack a second row of heads above
  4. Add faces to all
  5. Add legs and shoes last
- **How it works (Star People style):**
  1. Draw one long horizontal line
  2. Add arms branching off
  3. Add pairs of legs below (aim for an even number)
  4. Add corresponding heads above the line
  5. Fill in faces
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=gAs1quOGDfU&t=120

## Key Principles
- You don't need artistic skill — simple, recognizable figures are sufficient for facilitation purposes.
- Carsten recommends drawing heads first, then body, legs, and arms as a reliable sequence.
- For Circle figures, you can also start with the body and add the head second — both orders work.
- Adding a ground line so figures don't "float in midair" significantly improves the visual finish.
- Color (crayons, pencils, or markers) adds a finishing touch and increases perceived quality and buy-in.

## Facilitation Tips
- Carsten uses crayons for coloring but notes pencils or any available tool work fine — lower the barrier to starting.
- Adding a shirt color to figures gives them personality and makes flipcharts feel more complete.
- For large groups, batch-draw all bodies first, then all heads — it's faster and keeps proportions consistent.
- Carsten mentions Bikablo as an alternative figure style and encourages experimentation — there is no single right approach.

## Best Used When
- Preparing or live-drawing facilitation materials on flipcharts or whiteboards
- Visualizing team structures, user stories, personas, or stakeholder maps
- Running visual facilitation training or introducing drawing to reluctant participants
- Creating low-fi illustrations that need to be understood at a glance
