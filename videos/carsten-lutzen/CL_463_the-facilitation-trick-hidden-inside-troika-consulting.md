# The facilitation trick hidden inside Troika Consulting

## Summary
Carsten unpacks the underlying mechanic of Troika Consulting — one client, two consultants, with the client deliberately stepping out while the consultants discuss — and shows that this mechanic can be repurposed far beyond classic problem-solving. He walks through three concrete variants he has experimented with: communication-focused Troika (practising how a message lands), solution-focused Troika (surfacing existing resources rather than inventing solutions), and prioritization Troika (exposing hidden assumptions about what's important).

## Methods & Techniques

### Troika Fundamentals — quick recap (ab 00:30)
- **What:** The base Liberating Structure that all variants below build on.
- **When to use:** Any situation where one person needs structured outside input on a challenge.
- **Group size:** Triads (works in parallel across larger groups)
- **Duration:** 10–15 minutes per round
- **How it works:**
  1. Client pitches their problem or challenge to two consultants
  2. Consultants ask clarifying questions
  3. Client turns away (mutes/looks away) — listens but does not react
  4. Consultants discuss the challenge between themselves
  5. Client returns and plays back what they heard the consultants say
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zNQS4V94za4&t=30

### Communication-Focused Troika (ab 01:20)
- **What:** Variant where the client doesn't bring a problem — they try to convey a specific message they want to land.
- **When to use:** When a person wants to practise presenting, communicating a position, or testing how a message comes across before delivering it for real.
- **Group size:** Triads
- **Duration:** 10–15 minutes
- **How it works:**
  1. Client communicates a message they want to convey (presentation, pitch, position)
  2. Client steps out
  3. Consultants discuss: *What did we hear? What was the message? Were there blind spots? What was unclear or unintended?*
  4. Client returns and hears their own message reflected back through someone else's understanding
- **Facilitator note:** Frame it explicitly as a communication exercise so consultants don't slip into "fixing" the client — the goal is reflection on the message, not solutions to a problem.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zNQS4V94za4&t=80

### Solution-Focused Troika (ab 02:00)
- **What:** Variant where consultants are constrained to search only for what is already working and what resources the client already has, rather than proposing fresh solutions.
- **When to use:** When the client is stuck or feeling depleted — to reconnect them with their own existing capabilities and reinforce a positive frame.
- **Group size:** Triads
- **Duration:** 10–15 minutes
- **How it works:**
  1. Client pitches their challenge as usual
  2. Client steps out
  3. Consultants are instructed to *not* invent new solutions — only to surface what the client already mentioned that is working, resources they hinted at, and prior experience they can build on
  4. Client returns and discovers, "I already have insights and resources I wasn't seeing"
- **Why it works:** Reinforces positivity and self-efficacy; counters the tendency in problem-solving formats to ignore what the client is already doing well.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zNQS4V94za4&t=120

### Prioritization Troika (ab 03:02)
- **What:** Variant where the client pitches their current to-do list rather than a single challenge, and consultants prioritize it for them.
- **When to use:** When someone feels overwhelmed by their workload, or when implicit assumptions about what matters need to be made explicit.
- **Group size:** Triads
- **Duration:** 10–15 minutes
- **How it works:**
  1. Client describes the tasks currently on their plate and what they're working on
  2. Client steps out
  3. Consultants reflect on the list and propose a priority order, explaining their reasoning out loud
  4. Client returns and hears the proposed order — the gap between the consultants' order and what the client *would have done* surfaces hidden assumptions: "Why do I think A is more important than B?"
- **Why Carsten finds this most interesting:** It externalises the unspoken criteria the client is using, often for the first time.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zNQS4V94za4&t=182

## Key Principles
- The "trick" of Troika is not the consulting content — it's the **mechanic**: a person speaks, then steps out so others can react without them, then comes back and integrates.
- That mechanic can be repurposed for any goal where reflection-by-proxy is valuable: communication, solution-finding, prioritization, and likely more.
- Constraining what the consultants are allowed to do (e.g., "only surface existing resources") changes the entire character of the exercise.
- The client stepping out is not optional — it's what creates the space for unfiltered reflection and what lets the client hear themselves through someone else's interpretation.
- Naming the variant explicitly to participants matters: without a clear frame, consultants default to problem-solving mode.

## Facilitation Tips
- Always brief the consultants on the *specific constraint* of the variant (communication / solution-focused / prioritization) before the round starts — otherwise they'll fall back to generic advice-giving.
- Make the "client steps out" move physical and visible (turn camera off, look away, mute) so the rest of the group treats the discussion as if the client truly isn't there.
- For the communication variant: warn consultants explicitly *not* to react to the client's challenge — only to the message and how it landed. Otherwise the client comes back feeling misunderstood.
- For the solution-focused variant: have consultants name resources/strengths in concrete language ("you already mentioned X works") rather than abstractions.
- For the prioritization variant: ask consultants to verbalise *why* they ordered things that way — that's where the surfaced assumptions live.

## Best Used When
- A person wants to rehearse a difficult message before delivering it
- A team member is stuck and needs to reconnect with their own existing capabilities
- Someone is overwhelmed by a backlog or to-do list and unclear assumptions are driving the chaos
- Coaching contexts where you want the coachee to hear themselves reflected through others
- Workshop designs that already use Troika and you want a fresh variation
