# "My day" by Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten shares what a typical workday looks like for him as an agile coach and Scrum Master — from a morning walk and observing stand-ups to coaching sessions, preparing workshops, and deliberate learning in the open. The video offers a candid reflection on the often-invisible nature of the Scrum Master role and touches honestly on imposter syndrome.

## Methods & Techniques

### Morning Walk to Prepare (ab 00:30)
- **What:** Starting the day with a walk after the school drop-off to clear the head and mentally prepare for the day ahead.
- **When to use:** As a daily ritual to create intentional space before diving into work.
- **How it works:** Use the walk to think through what will happen during the day — what to pay attention to, what needs follow-up.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=g-OKIkp5lF8&t=30

### Observing the Daily Stand-Up (ab 00:50)
- **What:** Attending the daily stand-up either as a facilitator or silent observer, depending on team maturity.
- **When to use:** Always, but the role shifts — mature teams benefit from the coach stepping back and just observing dynamics.
- **How it works:** Watch for team dynamics that are slipping, blockers, or patterns worth coaching into. The goal is a read on what is happening, not control.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=g-OKIkp5lF8&t=50

### Sitting Next to Team Members (ab 01:20)
- **What:** Informal check-ins where Carsten sits beside individual team members to observe their work firsthand.
- **When to use:** When you want to build proximity, spot invisible blockers, or simply close the distance between coach and team.
- **How it works:** No agenda — just be present, watch what someone is working on, and ask occasional questions. Carsten acknowledges he often cannot help technically, but the relational signal matters.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=g-OKIkp5lF8&t=80

### Spreading Fun Intentionally (ab 01:50)
- **What:** Actively looking for moments to inject lightness — jokes, playful activities — without disrupting focus.
- **When to use:** Throughout the day, reading the room for when people are not in deep focus.
- **How it works:** Keep it lightweight and contextually appropriate. The intent is breaking monotony, not interrupting flow states.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=g-OKIkp5lF8&t=110

### Learning in Public (ab 02:30)
- **What:** Reading books or studying openly in the shared office space rather than hiding learning time away.
- **When to use:** Whenever doing personal development during work hours — make it visible rather than private.
- **How it works:** Sit with a book in plain sight. Carsten acknowledges it feels "daunting" but sees the role modeling as more important than any awkwardness. The goal is normalizing learning as part of the workday.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=g-OKIkp5lF8&t=150

## Key Principles
- The Scrum Master's daily work is largely invisible — check-ins, observations, and informal presence don't produce visible artifacts, which makes it easy to question your own value.
- Role modeling matters: doing learning openly signals to the team that self-improvement is legitimate and valued, not something to hide.
- Imposter syndrome is common in the Scrum Master and agile coach role — acknowledging it openly is itself an act of transparency and psychological courage.
- Proximity builds trust: sitting next to someone, even without adding technical value, creates relational closeness that enables coaching later.
- Fun is a professional tool, not a distraction — used thoughtfully it reduces monotony and maintains team energy.

## Facilitation Tips
- When teams are mature enough, shift from facilitating the stand-up to purely observing — you learn more and signal trust in the team.
- Prepare sessions (retros, planning, community of practice) as a visible, time-budgeted activity — it is real work, not overhead.
- Use the question "What does a typical day look like for you?" as a reflection prompt with Scrum Masters or coaches who may be struggling to articulate their value.

## Best Used When
- Onboarding new Scrum Masters who are unsure how to structure their time.
- Coaching someone experiencing imposter syndrome in an agile role.
- Making the case internally for what a Scrum Master actually does (the hidden work of the role).
- Facilitating a peer reflection session among agile coaches on daily habits and role clarity.
