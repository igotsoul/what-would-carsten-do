# 4L with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten introduces the 4L retrospective format as a simple, structured way to collect feedback after a sprint or workshop. The four Ls — Liked, Lacked, Longed For, and Learned — guide teams through a balanced reflection that builds on successes, surfaces gaps, and captures learning. Carsten recommends it both for retrospectives and meeting evaluations.

## Methods & Techniques

### 4L Retrospective (ab 00:20)
- **What:** A structured feedback format using four prompts — Liked, Lacked, Longed For, and Learned — to guide team reflection after a sprint, workshop, or meeting.
- **When to use:** End-of-sprint retrospectives or evaluation of any meeting or workshop session.
- **Group size:** Not specified; suitable for teams of typical Scrum size.
- **Duration:** Not specified; can be adapted to available time.
- **How it works:**
  1. Ask participants to reflect on four questions about the session:
     - **Liked** — What did you enjoy? What should we definitely do again?
     - **Lacked** — What should we have done more of? What was missing?
     - **Longed For** — What were you hoping would happen but didn't?
     - **Learned** — What did you actually learn during this session?
  2. Collect responses (typically on sticky notes or a shared board).
  3. Discuss patterns across the four categories as a team.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Jv6nepERSTA&t=20

## Key Principles
- Balancing positive and developmental feedback: focusing on "Liked" ensures the team builds on what works, not just fixing problems.
- "Learned" acts as a health check — if the board is empty, it signals the session format may need rethinking.
- "Longed For" is an alignment signal: if it's consistently populated, expectations weren't set clearly enough at the start.
- "Lacked" points to things that were underdone or skipped — useful for calibrating future session design.

## Facilitation Tips
- Carsten suggests treating an empty "Learned" column as a prompt to step back and question whether the current approach is working.
- If "Longed For" frequently appears, invest more time upfront in aligning expectations — give participants a chance to express what they're hoping to get out of the session before it begins.
- A good reference for the format is Retromat.org, which provides a solid description and variations.

## Best Used When
- Closing a sprint retrospective and wanting a structured, balanced reflection.
- Evaluating a workshop or training session.
- Teams new to structured feedback who need clear prompts to guide discussion.
- Facilitators who want to quickly surface alignment gaps and learning outcomes.
