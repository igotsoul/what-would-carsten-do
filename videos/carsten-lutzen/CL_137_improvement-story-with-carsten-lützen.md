# "Improvement Story" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten introduces Improvement Stories as a structured format for capturing current-state vs. desired-state goals in a measurable way. This video is part of a series comparing story formats (user stories, job stories, improvement stories), helping practitioners choose the right format based on what behavior or outcome they want to capture.

## Methods & Techniques

### Improvement Stories (ab 00:20)
- **What:** A story format that pairs a current measurable situation with a desired measurable outcome — without prescribing how to get there.
- **When to use:** Best for concrete, measurable improvements where the gap between current and desired state can be clearly defined (e.g., reducing checkout drop-off from 5% to 1%).
- **Group size:** Not specified — applicable in backlog refinement with any team size.
- **Duration:** Not specified.
- **How it works:**
  1. State the current situation with a measurable value (e.g., "We have a 5% drop-off in the checkout flow").
  2. State the desired situation with a target value (e.g., "We want a 1% drop-off").
  3. Leave the "how" open — use this as a starting point for Discovery work to identify causes and solutions.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=e-0CEd-pyrA&t=20

## Key Principles
- Choose your story format based on what behavior or outcome you are trying to capture — no single format fits all situations.
- Improvement Stories separate goal-setting from solution design, creating space for discovery before committing to an approach.
- The format works best when the improvement is concrete and measurable, not for open-ended exploration or "find the next big thing" discovery.
- A well-chosen story format leads to a tidier, more structured backlog.

## Facilitation Tips
- When coaching teams on backlog refinement, use the current/desired framing to help teams articulate goals before jumping to solutions.
- Carsten suggests using the format to start discussions and set measurable goals — it makes progress visible ("we can always see how far we have gotten").
- Encourage teams to follow up an Improvement Story with Discovery work to understand root causes once the target is clear.

## Best Used When
- A team needs to reduce or improve a measurable metric (conversion rates, performance, error rates, cycle times).
- You want to define "done" for an improvement effort before deciding how to achieve it.
- Backlog items feel vague or hard to measure — this format forces specificity.
- You are coaching teams to think in outcomes rather than outputs.
