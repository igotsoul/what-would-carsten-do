# Drawing a Wishlist with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten demonstrates how to draw a curled-paper wishlist template on a flip chart — a reusable visual aid for retrospectives and working agreement sessions. The video covers the full drawing process step by step, including shadow effects, color, and a decorative headline style that makes the template look like a taped note.

## Methods & Techniques

### Curled-Paper Wishlist Template (ab 00:45)
- **What:** A hand-drawn flip chart template shaped like an old curled scroll or wish list, used as a visual container for participant input via post-its.
- **When to use:** Retrospectives (e.g., "learning wishlist" — what do I wish my team knew?), working agreements, or any collection exercise where a themed visual adds engagement.
- **Group size:** Any — template is prepared by the facilitator before the session.
- **Duration:** ~5–10 min to draw; reusable across sessions.
- **How it works:**
  1. Draw a spiral shape at the bottom to create the curl of the paper.
  2. Extend lines upward to form the body of the scroll.
  3. Draw the top edge of the paper to close the shape.
  4. Add a grey marker shadow at the curl, edges, and top for depth.
  5. Color the entire template yellow (or another color) with crayons.
  6. Add the headline using a "taped note" style: draw four corner shapes, then connect them to simulate tape holding a label onto the page.
  7. Participants write on post-its and stick them inside the template; post-its can be removed and the template reused.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=nLjHxiF5MNo&t=45

### Taped-Note Headline Style (ab 03:30)
- **What:** A headline design that mimics a piece of paper taped onto the flip chart — four corner shapes connected to form a bordered label.
- **When to use:** Whenever the visual theme of the flip chart calls for a document or paper aesthetic; pairs naturally with the wishlist scroll template.
- **How it works:** Draw four small corner bracket shapes, then connect them with lines to create a rectangular border that looks like tape is holding it down.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=nLjHxiF5MNo&t=210

## Key Principles
- Breaking standard drawing rules (e.g., always framing with a black border) is appropriate when the visual metaphor of the content calls for it.
- A themed visual container (scroll, wishlist) reinforces the intent of the exercise and helps participants understand what kind of input is expected.
- Simple shadow effects with a grey marker add character and depth without requiring artistic skill — not strictly necessary, but worth the extra 30 seconds.
- Bright accent colors (e.g., colored "tape" corners) draw attention to the key element of the flip chart.
- Signing the template (e.g., initials in the corner) gives participants a point of contact and signals ownership of the design.

## Facilitation Tips
- Use crayons — Carsten notes these are easy to find wherever there are kids, making them accessible in most workplaces or training venues.
- Color the full template yellow before the session; this takes time but pays off visually.
- The template is reusable: peel off the post-its after the session and bring it to the next one.
- The "learning wishlist" framing — *what do I wish the rest of the team knew that they don't know yet?* — is a strong retrospective prompt for surfacing hidden knowledge gaps.

## Best Used When
- Preparing a retrospective focused on team learning or knowledge sharing.
- Facilitating a working agreements session and wanting a visual anchor that participants remember.
- Building a library of reusable flip chart templates to speed up session preparation.
