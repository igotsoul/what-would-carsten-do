# Panarchy with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten introduces Panarchy, a Liberating Structure that extends the Ecocycle into multiple layers — team, department, and company level. By running simultaneous Ecocycle maps across organizational levels, teams can surface systemic patterns and trigger deeper conversations about where energy and effort are well spent.

## Methods & Techniques

### Panarchy — Multi-Layer Ecocycle (ab 01:00)
- **What:** A multi-layered application of the Ecocycle Liberating Structure, mapping the same cycle (Gestation → Birth → Maturity → Creative Destruction) across three organizational levels simultaneously.
- **When to use:** When you want to understand systemic patterns across team, department, and company levels — not just local optimization. Ideal for cross-team gatherings or leadership workshops.
- **Group size:** Best with representatives from multiple teams; works on the floor with physical space.
- **Duration:** Not specified; likely 60–120 min given the depth of debrief suggested.
- **How it works:**
  1. Set up three Ecocycle maps — one for the team level, one for the department, one for the company.
  2. Have participants map initiatives, products, or practices onto each cycle: what's in Gestation, Birth, Maturity, Creative Destruction, Poverty Trap, or Rigidity Trap.
  3. Lay the maps out on the floor so people can walk between them and compare levels.
  4. Use 1-2-4-All (or a similar structure) to surface patterns across the layers.
  5. Facilitate a discussion: What patterns emerge? What is striking or surprising about the distribution?

### Ecocycle — Quick Recap (ab 00:30)
- **What:** A single-layer Liberating Structure for mapping where initiatives, practices, or products sit in a lifecycle: Gestation, Birth, Maturity, Creative Destruction — plus two traps: Poverty Trap (stuck before birth) and Rigidity Trap (stuck past maturity).
- **When to use:** As a standalone team exercise or as the building block before running Panarchy.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=V7lzRg4HbOA&t=30s

## Key Principles
- Panarchy makes the invisible visible: patterns that are hidden at one level become obvious when you compare across levels.
- Three layers is the practical sweet spot — the official Liberating Structures description allows up to eight, but Carsten recommends starting with three (team, department, company).
- The Poverty Trap and Rigidity Trap are diagnostic signals: Poverty Trap = insufficient investment to move forward; Rigidity Trap = continuing something past its usefulness out of habit.
- Laying maps physically on the floor creates spatial engagement and invites cross-level comparison in a way that slides cannot.

## Facilitation Tips
- Use physical floor space for the maps — walking between levels triggers different thinking than looking at a screen.
- Follow the mapping phase with 1-2-4-All to generate observations before opening a full group discussion.
- Guide participants to look for patterns, not just individual placements: Are all the soft-skill initiatives stuck in the Poverty Trap? Are hard deliverables the only things reaching Maturity?
- Prompt questions: "What's interesting or surprising about this distribution?" and "What does the pattern across levels tell us?"

## Best Used When
- You have representatives from multiple teams in the room (e.g., a PI Planning event, a department offsite, or a leadership retrospective).
- The organization is wrestling with where to invest energy — or what to stop doing.
- You want to move beyond a single team's perspective and surface systemic dynamics.

## Related Concepts
- **Ecocycle** (Liberating Structures) — the single-layer precursor; Carsten has a separate video on this.
- **1-2-4-All** (Liberating Structures) — recommended debrief structure after the mapping phase.
- **Creative Destruction** — the phase that feeds back into new seeds; understanding this prevents the Rigidity Trap.
