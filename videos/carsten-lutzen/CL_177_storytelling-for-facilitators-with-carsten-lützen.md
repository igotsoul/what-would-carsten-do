# "Storytelling for Facilitators" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten introduces storytelling as a core facilitation skill, explaining why stories outperform facts for engagement and retention. He shares a simple three-part model — Context, Conflict, Change — that facilitators can use to quickly craft and deliver compelling stories in workshops.

## Methods & Techniques

### Context–Conflict–Change Story Model (ab 02:10)
- **What:** A simplified storytelling framework with three steps: set the context (where/who), introduce the conflict (the tension or challenge), and describe the change (what happened and why it matters).
- **When to use:** Opening a workshop, framing a concept, or sharing learnings — any moment where you want to engage an audience instead of conveying dry facts.
- **Group size:** Any
- **Duration:** Can be very short — Carsten uses it to string together a quick anecdote in under a minute.
- **How it works:**
  1. **Context** — Establish the setting and who is involved. ("We are in a workshop, I'm teaching a group about storytelling.")
  2. **Conflict** — Name the tension, problem, or challenge. ("I can see people zoning out — they're not engaged.")
  3. **Change** — Describe what shifted and why it matters. ("Instead of talking about storytelling, I told a story — and people leaned in.")

## Key Principles
- Stories activate more — and different — parts of the brain than facts alone, leading to higher engagement and better retention.
- Research consistently shows that people remember stories significantly better than plain information (Carsten notes the exact multiplier varies across studies but the effect is large).
- Stories create emotional connection, which drives engagement and increases the likelihood that participants contribute ideas and retain workshop content.
- If you're teaching storytelling but only presenting facts about it, you're missing the point — model the skill you're advocating for.
- The Ladder of Inference is relevant here: always consider why the audience should care about your story.

## Facilitation Tips
- When introducing storytelling to a group, don't lead with research and facts — open with an actual story to demonstrate the concept immediately.
- Use the Context–Conflict–Change model as a quick mental template to compose a story on the fly, even mid-session.
- Watch for audience energy: if people are "slowly walking toward the exit" mentally, that's a signal to shift from information delivery to narrative.
- The simpler the model, the more likely you'll actually use it under pressure — Carsten favors this three-step version over more elaborate frameworks precisely because it's easy to recall.

## Best Used When
- Opening a workshop and wanting to create immediate engagement rather than starting with an agenda or objectives slide.
- Introducing a new concept or framework where participant buy-in matters.
- Sharing lessons learned or experiences from previous workshops or projects.
- Any moment where you sense the room losing attention and want to re-anchor people emotionally.

## Related Concepts
- **Hero's Journey** — Classic narrative framework; Context–Conflict–Change is presented as a simplified variant.
- **Ladder of Inference** — Referenced as a lens for checking whether your story will resonate with the audience ("why should they care?").
- **Stage Setting** — The workshop opening phase where storytelling can be most powerfully applied.
