# "Coat of Arms" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten Lützen shares a visual icebreaker called "Coat of Arms" where participants fill out a heraldic shield template by interviewing each other. It serves as a fun, structured way to help team members — especially those who don't know each other well — share personal context and working preferences. The exercise is a variation of the "User Manual to Me" format with a more playful, visual frame.

## Methods & Techniques

### Coat of Arms Exercise (ab 00:20)
- **What:** Participants fill in a heraldic shield template divided into sections, each containing a predefined question about themselves. They do this by interviewing each other in pairs, then present their partner's coat of arms to the group.
- **When to use:** Team kickoffs, onboarding new team members, building psychological safety, or any session where you want people to understand each other's working styles and personal context.
- **Group size:** Works in pairs; scales to any team size
- **Duration:** Not specified; likely 20–45 minutes depending on debrief format
- **How it works:**
  1. Prepare a template: a coat of arms shield divided into sections, each with a predefined question (see examples below).
  2. Pair people up — preferably with someone they don't know well.
  3. Partners interview each other and fill in each other's shields.
  4. Debrief options:
     - Post shields on the wall for a silent gallery walk and group reflection.
     - Run a plenary debrief: "What stood out? How did this make you feel?"
     - Most fun option: each person introduces their partner in character — "I am Carsten's squire, let me present Sir Carsten's coat of arms…" — reading out what they filled in.
- **Example questions Carsten suggests:**
  - Something about me that no one here knows (scalable to comfort level — doesn't need to be a deep secret)
  - What you need to know to get the best out of me
  - Two things I personally need more of to feel supported
  - What makes me most proud to be part of this organization
  - My greatest wish for us as a team
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=TkXyvhBxAF8&t=20

## Key Principles
- Psychological safety is built in by design: participants choose how deeply to share. The question "something no one knows about me" can be answered with something light (e.g., "my first programming language was Delphi").
- The heraldic frame adds playfulness and novelty — it shifts the social register away from a corporate intro and invites more authentic sharing.
- Having someone else present your coat of arms (rather than presenting your own) creates warmth, humor, and a moment of being seen by a colleague.
- The exercise functions as a "User Manual to Me" — surfacing working preferences and support needs — but in a more engaging visual format.

## Facilitation Tips
- Prepare the template in advance with the questions already placed inside the shield sections — don't ask participants to come up with their own structure.
- Pair people who don't know each other well to maximize the value of the introductions.
- The "squire introduction" format (presenting your partner's coat of arms as their herald) is Carsten's recommended debrief for maximum fun and engagement.
- Works well online — the template can be shared digitally and filled in on a virtual whiteboard.

## Best Used When
- Kicking off a new team or project
- Onboarding new team members into an existing group
- Running a team-building session where psychological safety is still developing
- You want a fresh alternative to standard "introduce yourself" rounds
