# "Naughty or Nice" Retrospective with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten shares a Christmas-themed retrospective exercise called "Naughty or Nice," inspired by the classic Stop/Start/Continue format. The exercise uses Santa's lists as a metaphor to help teams reflect on what didn't work as expected, what worked well, and unexpected positive surprises — without singling out individuals.

## Methods & Techniques

### Naughty or Nice Retrospective (ab 00:45)
- **What:** A Christmas-themed retrospective exercise using three categories — Naughty, Nice, and Gifts — to help teams reflect on the sprint or iteration.
- **When to use:** During sprint retrospectives, especially in December or when teams need a playful format to surface issues. Also works when the team needs a simple but effective exercise after more elaborate formats.
- **Group size:** Any team size (implied for Scrum teams)
- **Duration:** Not specified; suitable as a standard retrospective timebox
- **How it works:**
  1. **Naughty List** — What did not work out as expected? This is not about blaming people, but about things (tools, processes, dependencies) that fell short of expectations. Example: a build server that broke mid-sprint, or materials that didn't arrive on time.
  2. **Nice List** — What worked out as expected, or even better? These are things the team should continue doing and potentially do more of, since they clearly deliver value.
  3. **Gifts** — Unexpected goodness. Things the team didn't plan for but that turned out to be a positive surprise. This can be an unplanned discovery or a moment to recognize a teammate's contribution ("Peter was a gift to this team because...").
  4. After collecting items, the team selects what to focus on from the Naughty list (what to improve) and from the Nice/Gifts lists (what to amplify).

## Key Principles
- Simple exercises can be just as powerful as elaborate ones — don't overlook them.
- The Naughty list is about systemic or situational issues, never about shaming individuals.
- "Gifts" reframes the typical retrospective format to celebrate unexpected goodness, not just planned successes.
- Retrospective formats can and should be adapted seasonally or thematically to keep them fresh and engaging.
- The exercise is deliberately rooted in the classic Stop/Start/Continue format — the Christmas framing is a wrapper, not a replacement for substance.

## Facilitation Tips
- Carsten emphasizes framing the Naughty list carefully: it's about *what* didn't work, not *who* didn't behave as expected.
- Encourage the team to use the Gifts category to praise each other — it creates space for recognition that might not surface in standard formats.
- After collecting input, facilitate a prioritization step: which Naughty items deserve focus? Which Nice/Gift items should be amplified?
- Use this format as a lighter exercise when the team needs something simple and fun rather than a heavy, structured retrospective.

## Best Used When
- It's December or the holiday season and the team appreciates a thematic touch.
- The team is fatigued with standard retrospective formats and needs a fresh framing.
- You want a simple, low-barrier exercise that still produces actionable output.
- There's a need to create space for peer recognition alongside problem identification.
