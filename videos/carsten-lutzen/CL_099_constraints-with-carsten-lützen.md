# "Constraints" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten introduces the distinction between governing and enabling constraints, drawing on Dave Snowden's Cynefin framework. He argues that in complex product development environments, enabling constraints are more effective than governing ones because they create direction and space without stifling creativity.

## Methods & Techniques

### Enabling vs. Governing Constraints (ab 00:30)
- **What:** A framework for categorizing constraints by how much freedom they allow. Governing constraints set hard boundaries and direction but limit growth; enabling constraints provide direction and space while allowing the system to evolve within them.
- **When to use:** When working with teams operating in complex domains — particularly in product development — to design guardrails that foster creativity rather than suppress it.
- **Group size:** Not specified; applicable at team or organizational level.
- **Duration:** Not specified; this is a design principle, not a time-boxed activity.
- **How it works:**
  1. Identify what kind of system you are operating in (Cynefin's complex vs. complicated domain).
  2. Distinguish between governing constraints (hard limits, like legal rules — analogous to an exoskeleton that caps growth) and enabling constraints (directional boundaries that create a navigable space — analogous to an endoskeleton that guides without capping growth).
  3. When designing team or product boundaries, favor enabling constraints: define the direction and the space to operate in, then let the team navigate within it.
  4. Avoid over-constraining: limiting the solution space is useful, but locking it down too tightly kills the creativity that complexity requires.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=ZpRc1BoSKsk&t=30

## Key Principles
- In complex systems (Cynefin complex domain), enabling constraints outperform governing constraints because they preserve adaptability.
- The exoskeleton metaphor (insects) illustrates governing constraints: the system cannot grow beyond its hard boundary.
- The endoskeleton metaphor (human bones) illustrates enabling constraints: bones give direction and structure, but do not prevent growth.
- Constraining the solution space can *increase* creativity by giving teams a defined room to navigate rather than an overwhelming blank slate.
- Carsten acknowledges there is significant depth to this topic and encourages further exploration of Snowden's work on enabling and governing constraints.

## Facilitation Tips
- When framing team agreements or product boundaries, explicitly name whether a constraint is governing (hard limit) or enabling (directional space) — this helps teams understand what they can and cannot experiment with.
- Use the endoskeleton/exoskeleton metaphors to make abstract constraint concepts tangible for teams.
- Invite teams to surface and classify their own constraints: which ones are truly non-negotiable, and which ones are assumed limits that could be redesigned as enabling?

## Best Used When
- Coaching a team that feels overly restricted and is losing creative energy.
- Designing sprint goals, team charters, or product visions that need to guide without micromanaging.
- Facilitating discussions about organizational rules, policies, or ways of working where the team needs to distinguish between what is fixed and what is flexible.
- Working in product development or any complex adaptive environment where outcomes cannot be fully predicted upfront.

## Related Concepts
- **Cynefin framework** (Dave Snowden) — the broader sense-making model from which the enabling/governing constraint distinction originates.
- **Complex vs. complicated domains** — the Cynefin domains that determine which type of constraint is appropriate.
- **Psychological safety** — enabling constraints can support psychological safety by clarifying the space in which it is safe to experiment.
- **Team charters / working agreements** — practical artifacts where the enabling vs. governing distinction can be applied directly.
