# "Process Illustration" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten demonstrates how to use simple hand-drawn illustrations on a flip chart to make processes tangible, discussable, and easier to align on. Using a backlog refinement funnel as a live example, he shows that even rough sketches with basic shapes and arrows are enough to bring clarity and energy to otherwise dry process conversations.

## Methods & Techniques

### Live Process Illustration on a Flip Chart (ab 00:30)
- **What:** Drawing a process in real time using simple shapes, arrows, and metaphors — no artistic skill required. The goal is to make abstract processes visible and discussable.
- **When to use:** When discussing or aligning on how a process works — especially with teams, product managers, or stakeholders who might not share the same mental model.
- **Group size:** Works for 1-on-1 conversations up to larger team or stakeholder sessions.
- **Duration:** Can be done in a few minutes as part of a larger conversation; not a standalone activity.
- **How it works:**
  1. Identify the process you want to illustrate (e.g., how work items flow from request to sprint backlog).
  2. Start with the input — draw the different forms it can take (post-it, document, email).
  3. Draw a literal funnel to represent how inputs get filtered and shaped.
  4. Add checkpoints along the funnel (e.g., a product manager check-in step).
  5. Show the output — uniform backlog items landing in a pile.
  6. Use arrows to show branching paths: items going into the backlog, being discarded (trash bin), or looped back for further refinement.
  7. Label roles at each step using simple stick figures or text.

## Key Principles
- You don't need drawing skills — simple shapes, arrows, and recognizable metaphors (funnel, trash bin, pile) are enough to convey complex processes.
- A rough sketch on a flip chart is more useful for alignment than a perfectly worded document, because it invites discussion rather than passive reading.
- Visual metaphors (funnel, scroll, trash can) are universally understood and reduce the need for lengthy explanation.
- The act of drawing together — or drawing in front of others — adds energy and engagement to topics that are otherwise dry to discuss.
- The illustration can be reused across different audiences (team, product manager, stakeholders) without modification.

## Facilitation Tips
- Carsten explicitly reassures that imperfect drawings are fine — don't let perfectionism stop you from sketching.
- Use literal, everyday metaphors: a funnel for filtering, a pile for a backlog, a trash bin for discarded work. Audiences immediately grasp these without explanation.
- Draw as you talk — the live construction of the visual keeps attention and makes the logic easier to follow step by step.
- Arrows are your best friend: they show flow, branching, and feedback loops without needing extra words.
- Skip colors and shading entirely — they add complexity without proportional clarity benefit at this stage.

## Best Used When
- A team or group needs to align on how a process actually works before they can improve it.
- You're having a process design conversation and want to prevent people from talking past each other.
- A discussion feels abstract or stuck — sketching it out can unstick the conversation quickly.
- You want to make a refinement, intake, or workflow process concrete for stakeholders who aren't deeply familiar with it.
