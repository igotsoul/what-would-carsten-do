# Visual Agenda with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten Lützen shares a simple technique for creating a visual agenda using a physical Kanban board with To Do, In Progress, and Done columns. The approach gives meeting and workshop participants constant visibility into what has been covered, what is current, and what is coming next — while also enabling real-time reprioritization of the agenda.

## Methods & Techniques

### Visual Kanban Agenda (ab 00:30)
- **What:** A physical Kanban board (To Do / In Progress / Done) used as a live, visible agenda for meetings, workshops, retrospectives, or full-day sessions.
- **When to use:** Any meeting or workshop where participants benefit from knowing where they are, what comes next, and how much remains — from a one-hour retro to a full-day event.
- **Group size:** Any size; particularly valuable in larger or longer sessions where participants may lose track of the flow.
- **Duration:** Scales from a one-hour meeting to multi-day workshops.
- **How it works:**
  1. Write each agenda item on a card and place them all in the **To Do** column before the session starts.
  2. Move the current item to **In Progress** as you begin it.
  3. Move completed items to **Done** as the session progresses.
  4. Reprioritize cards in the To Do column at any time by physically moving them — with group agreement if needed.
  5. Add new items to To Do as they emerge during the session.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=68wDa4KwIWo&t=30

### Drawing vs. Text on Agenda Cards (ab 02:30)
- **What:** Using simple hand-drawn illustrations on agenda cards rather than (or in combination with) plain text labels.
- **When to use:** Especially effective for general facilitation, retrospective activities, and soft-skills topics. Carsten recommends defaulting to drawings even when imperfect.
- **How it works:** Draw a visual icon or sketch representing each agenda item. For highly technical or abstract computer-science topics, plain text may be more appropriate. For everything else, embrace the drawing — participants tend to warm up to it quickly even if initially puzzled.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=68wDa4KwIWo&t=150

## Key Principles
- **Transparency over perfection:** A visible, physical agenda — even a rough one — gives everyone a shared understanding of the session structure at all times.
- **Progress visibility matters:** Knowing "we've done X, we're on Y, Z is still ahead" creates a meaningful sense of forward momentum, similar to a slide counter but far more tangible.
- **Agendas should be flexible:** Treating the agenda as a backlog makes reprioritization natural and collaborative rather than disruptive.
- **Low overhead by design:** Kanban is already familiar to most participants, so there is almost no explanation required to introduce this format.
- **Drawings create engagement:** Imperfect drawings are often more memorable and playful than plain text, and participants tend to buy in once they see it in action.

## Facilitation Tips
- Place the board somewhere visible to all participants throughout the entire session — on a wall, not just at the front.
- Don't wait for full consensus to reprioritize; if someone thinks a topic is more urgent, surface the conversation and move the card if the group agrees.
- For retrospectives using the Derby-Larsen model, create one card per phase so participants always know which phase they are in.
- In classroom or training settings, reference the board actively to signal transitions: "we'll get to the feedback topic later, but right now we're focusing on X."
- Keep headlines brief — the card doesn't need to be a full description, just enough to orient participants.

## Best Used When
- Running retrospectives, workshops, or multi-hour training sessions where participants need orientation.
- Facilitating sessions where the agenda may need to flex based on emerging priorities or group energy.
- Working with groups who appreciate transparency and collaborative ownership of the session structure.
- You want to add a visual, low-tech element to a physical meeting room.

## Related Concepts
- **Kanban** — the underlying framework adapted here for agenda management rather than work tracking.
- **Derby-Larsen Retrospective Model** — mentioned as a specific use case where each phase maps to a card.
