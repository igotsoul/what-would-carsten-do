# "Workshop Alignment" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten walks through his informal but consistent pre-workshop alignment process — the questions he asks organizers before agreeing to facilitate a workshop. The core insight is that clarifying the desired outcomes upfront (not just logistics) is what separates a well-designed workshop from a scattered one. Inspired by a talk from Brittany Bowring on facilitation preparation.

## Methods & Techniques

### Workshop Alignment Conversation (ab 00:45)
- **What:** A structured set of questions Carsten asks the organizers before designing or facilitating a workshop. The goal is to surface logistical constraints and — most importantly — the desired outcomes before any design work begins.
- **When to use:** Any time you are approached to facilitate a workshop, especially when organizers already have strong opinions about format or content.
- **Group size:** N/A — this is a 1:1 or small group conversation with the organizers/sponsors.
- **Duration:** Not specified, but Carsten implies this is a focused conversation, not a long process.
- **How it works:**
  1. **Format:** Ask whether the event is in-person, online, or hybrid. Carsten flags hybrid as high-risk without a strong technical setup and a dedicated co-facilitator — he explicitly notes it can be a deal-breaker.
  2. **Number of participants:** Clarify headcount early. It affects complexity, co-facilitation needs, and which exercises are feasible (e.g., whether Troika Consulting from Liberating Structures is viable).
  3. **Best hopes question:** Ask organizers: *"Imagine we ask participants after the workshop: 'What made this the best use of your time?' — what would you want them to answer?"* This is Carsten's most important question. Their answers directly shape exercise choices, invitations to exercises, and overall workshop design.
  4. **Date, time, and prep window:** Confirm logistics and whether there is enough time to do proper preparation.
  5. **Anything else to know:** Surface sensitive context — recent restructures, interpersonal tensions, or anything that would affect how you facilitate.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=a_kKxzwDCOQ&t=45

### The "Best Hopes" Question (ab 02:10)
- **What:** A single powerful question posed to workshop organizers to surface their true desired outcomes: *"If we ask participants after the workshop what made this the best use of their time — what would you want them to say?"*
- **When to use:** Early in any pre-workshop alignment conversation, especially when organizers come in with a pre-formed agenda or lots of activity ideas.
- **Group size:** Works with one organizer or a small sponsor group.
- **Duration:** Carsten says he spends the majority of his alignment time on this question alone.
- **How it works:** Ask the question, then ask clarifying follow-ups to drill into the actual change or experience the organizers want participants to have. The goal is to move from vague intentions to specific desired outcomes that can guide design decisions.

## Key Principles
- Alignment before design: No workshop design should begin until the organizers' desired outcomes are clearly understood.
- Hybrid facilitation is a risk — treat it as a distinct challenge requiring exceptional technical setup and a dedicated co-facilitator, not just a variation of online.
- Participant count determines complexity: it shapes co-facilitation needs, group splitting strategies, and the feasibility of specific exercises.
- The "best hopes" framing shifts organizers from activity-thinking to outcome-thinking, which is the more useful lens for workshop design.
- Focus over breadth: Carsten emphasizes pinpointing specific desired outcomes rather than trying to tackle everything at once.

## Facilitation Tips
- When organizers arrive with lots of activity ideas, redirect them with the "best hopes" question before engaging with their suggestions — it reframes the conversation around outcomes.
- Ask clarifying questions after the best hopes answer to get to the *actual* change or experience they want, not just the surface-level answer.
- Be willing to push back on or decline hybrid events if the technical setup and co-facilitation support are not in place.
- Use the alignment conversation answers to guide not just exercise selection, but also how you frame and introduce exercises to participants during the workshop.

## Best Used When
- You are asked to facilitate a workshop and need to quickly scope and align with the organizers before designing anything.
- Organizers are unclear about what they want, or they are over-specified on format/activities but under-specified on outcomes.
- You need to assess feasibility (hybrid risk, group size, prep time) before committing to facilitating.
