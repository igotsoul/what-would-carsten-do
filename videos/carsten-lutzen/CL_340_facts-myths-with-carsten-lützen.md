# "Facts & Myths" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten introduces "Facts & Myths" (also known as Mythbusters or Factbuster), a group reflection exercise for checking comprehension of new or existing content. Teams write unlabeled fact and myth statements on identical stickies, circulate them between groups, and then collaboratively sort and discuss them — surfacing misunderstandings and sparking meaningful dialogue.

## Methods & Techniques

### Facts & Myths (ab 00:30)
- **What:** Groups write three facts and three myths about a topic on identical, unmarked stickies. The stickies are then circulated to another group, who must figure out which is which — without labels to guide them.
- **When to use:** After introducing new terminology, frameworks, or concepts; great for checking how well a group has internalized new knowledge. Also works as a reflection exercise on content participants already know.
- **Group size:** Groups of 3–6 people (Carsten suggests 2–4 per group as a sweet spot for discussion)
- **Duration:** ~20–30 minutes total (5–10 min writing, 5–10 min sorting, then presentation rounds)
- **How it works:**
  1. Introduce new content (a framework, concept, terminology).
  2. Split participants into groups of 3–6.
  3. Each group writes 3 facts and 3 myths on **identical, unmarked stickies** — no color-coding, no labels. The group keeps track internally.
  4. After 5–10 minutes, circulate the stickies: group 1 gets group 2's stickies, group 2 gets group 3's, and so on.
  5. Each group reads the stickies they received and sorts them into facts and myths. Unclear ones can go in the middle. Allow 5–10 minutes for discussion.
  6. Groups take turns presenting their sorted stickies on a shared flipchart or online whiteboard — explaining their reasoning ("We think this is a fact because…").
  7. After each presentation, the group that originally wrote the stickies responds: Do they agree? If not, why did they see it differently?
  8. The resulting discussions reveal where understanding diverged — and why.

## Key Principles
- The stickies must be **identical and unmarked** — no color coding, no labels. This is essential to create the sorting challenge and the discussion.
- Ambiguity is a feature, not a bug: stickies that land in the "unclear" middle are often the richest discussion points.
- The final reveal (original authors responding to the other group's interpretation) is where the real learning happens — mismatches surface assumptions and gaps.
- Works equally well in-person and on online whiteboards — the format transfers without modification.

## Facilitation Tips
- Use the sorting results as a real-time diagnostic: as a facilitator, you gain insight into how well each group understood the content you were trying to convey.
- Let groups place uncertain stickies in the middle rather than forcing a binary choice — it keeps the energy honest and opens better conversations.
- When groups present, prompt them to give reasoning, not just verdicts: "Why do you think this is a myth?"
- After the original authors respond, invite the room to weigh in if there's disagreement — this avoids the exercise becoming a "who's right" dynamic.

## Best Used When
- Introducing a new framework or concept and wanting to check comprehension before moving on.
- Running a workshop where participants have mixed prior knowledge — the exercise surfaces both gaps and confident misunderstandings.
- You want to generate discussion without leading it — the format lets the content drive the conversation.
- Working with groups online where engagement can be harder to maintain.
