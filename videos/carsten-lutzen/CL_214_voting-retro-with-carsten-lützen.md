# "Voting Retro" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten shares a retrospective technique he learned from a colleague named Benny, where the team votes on broad themes before the retro rather than having the facilitator pre-select the focus. This approach removes the assumption that the facilitator knows what the team needs to discuss, and lets the team's own priorities shape the retrospective agenda.

## Methods & Techniques

### Voting Retro / Theme-Based Retrospective (ab 00:30)
- **What:** Instead of the facilitator choosing the retro exercises and focus area, the team first brainstorms and votes on broad topics they want to reflect on. The winning theme(s) then become the headline(s) for the retrospective.
- **When to use:** When you're unsure what the team most needs to focus on, or when you want to increase team ownership of the retrospective agenda.
- **Group size:** Not specified; suitable for standard Scrum teams.
- **Duration:** Fits within a standard retrospective timebox (not specified precisely).
- **How it works:**
  1. Throughout the Sprint, note down topics or themes you observe as a facilitator.
  2. At the start of the retro, invite the team to brainstorm additional broad topics (e.g., communication, process, team culture, ways of working).
  3. Have the team vote on which themes matter most right now.
  4. Use the top-voted theme(s) as the focus for the retro — e.g., "What went well with communication?" and "What to improve on communication?"
  5. If two themes tie or come close, run parallel tracks: e.g., "What went well with communication" + "What went well with ways of working" + improvement columns for both.
  6. Optionally, add an "Other / things that fall outside" category so people can still vent on topics that don't fit the chosen theme.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=lrltlokoWZ8

## Key Principles
- The facilitator pre-selecting the retro focus assumes they know what the team needs — but they often don't. Letting the team vote transfers that knowledge back to the team.
- Theme examples Carsten suggests: process, communication, team culture, ways of working, definition of ready, working agreements.
- Keeping Sprints short helps ensure fast traction on whatever theme is chosen, avoiding being stuck on one issue for too long.
- Always make space for venting and topics that don't fit the theme — emotional safety matters even in a structured format.

## Facilitation Tips
- Collect potential topics yourself during the Sprint before bringing them to the retro as seeds for the brainstorm.
- Don't force everything into the chosen theme — add an "other" column as a pressure valve for things people need to express regardless.
- If two themes receive nearly the same number of votes, consider running a split retro covering both rather than forcing a single winner.

## Best Used When
- The facilitator is uncertain what the team's current biggest pain point or growth area is.
- The team has been doing retros for a while and needs more ownership and engagement in the process.
- You want to move away from generic retro formats toward something more focused and meaningful to the team right now.
