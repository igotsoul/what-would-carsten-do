# "Parkinson's Law" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten introduces Parkinson's Law — the principle that work expands to fill the time available for its completion — and explores its origins and practical relevance in agile contexts. He discusses how both too much and too little time creates problems, and encourages practitioners to find the sweet spot between effort and available time.

## Methods & Techniques

### Applying Parkinson's Law as a Timebox Calibration Tool (ab 01:30)
- **What:** Deliberately constraining the time given for a task to prevent unnecessary work from being invented to fill available space.
- **When to use:** Sprint planning, project scoping, meeting design, or any situation where time allocations are being set.
- **Group size:** Applicable at individual, team, and organizational level.
- **Duration:** Ongoing principle, not a single exercise.
- **How it works:**
  1. Identify a task or body of work.
  2. Estimate the minimum time needed to deliver meaningful value.
  3. Set the timebox close to that minimum — not generously padded.
  4. Reflect afterward: was the time too tight (stress, no value delivered), too loose (pseudo-work invented), or in the sweet spot?
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=XhfY4ummQXQ&t=90

### Effort-Time Calibration (the "Sweet Spot" Model) (ab 02:30)
- **What:** A mental model visualizing the relationship between effort and time, helping teams identify when they are burning out (high effort, too little time) vs. inventing unnecessary work (low effort, too much time).
- **When to use:** Retrospectives, capacity planning discussions, or coaching conversations around team pace and sustainability.
- **How it works:**
  1. Draw two axes: effort (vertical) and time available (horizontal).
  2. Mark the extremes: top-left = burnout zone (high effort, too little time); bottom-right = pseudo-work zone (low effort, too much time).
  3. Identify the sweet spot in the middle where meaningful work is delivered sustainably.
  4. Use this as a reflection tool: where is the team currently sitting?
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=XhfY4ummQXQ&t=150

## Key Principles
- Parkinson's Law: "Work expands to fill the time available for its completion."
- If given too much time, people don't finish early — they talk to more stakeholders than needed, gather more data than needed, and invent requirements to fill the space (the Danish concept of *pseudo-work*).
- If given too little time, people become stressed and feel unable to deliver meaningful value, leading to disillusionment.
- The same principle applies beyond tasks: storage space, budgets, and project scopes all tend to fill whatever container is available.
- Sustainable delivery requires balancing the timebox — tight enough to prevent waste, loose enough to prevent burnout.
- Carsten models the principle himself: he deliberately limits preparation and recording time for his videos to avoid over-engineering them.

## Facilitation Tips
- Use Parkinson's Law explicitly when setting Sprint length or meeting durations — name the principle so the team can reason about it together.
- In retrospectives, ask: "Did we invent work to fill the Sprint, or were we squeezed too tight?" — the Effort-Time model gives language for that conversation.
- When coaching on estimation, help teams distinguish between the time needed to deliver value and the time available — these are often not the same.
- Naming the *pseudo-work* concept (busy work invented to fill available time) can be a powerful moment of recognition for teams.

## Best Used When
- Scrum teams are discussing Sprint length or feeling like Sprints are either always chaotic or always padded with low-value work.
- Coaching conversations around sustainability, burnout, or low team velocity.
- Planning workshops where time allocations for tasks or projects are being set.
- Retrospectives focused on waste or focus.
