# "Flipchart Layout" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten shares his personal approach to structuring and laying out flipcharts for workshops and presentations. The video covers practical techniques for readability, space management, and preparation — emphasizing that good flipchart work comes from practice and intentional simplicity, not perfection.

## Methods & Techniques

### Pencil Prototyping (ab 04:45)
- **What:** Sketch the flipchart layout in pencil before the session — including expected text and structural elements — then erase unused parts afterward.
- **When to use:** When you want to ensure key points are captured and layout feels intentional without appearing over-prepared to participants.
- **Group size:** Any
- **Duration:** Done during preparation or during breaks
- **How it works:**
  1. Draw the full layout in soft pencil before the session
  2. Write expected key points in pencil so you can read them up close
  3. Fill in the flipchart live during the session or in a break
  4. Erase pencil marks that were not used
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=e0zqb83tGYc&t=285

### Text Boxing and Visual Grouping (ab 02:00)
- **What:** Enclosing related text in boxes, speech bubbles, or post-it style curly corners to signal that content belongs together and improve readability.
- **When to use:** Whenever a flipchart has multiple distinct ideas or sections that could be confused as one block of text.
- **How it works:** After writing a sentence or group of sentences, draw a surrounding shape — a simple rectangle, speech bubble, or curly-cornered post-it style box — to visually group the content.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=e0zqb83tGYc&t=120

### Color Accent Circle (ab 03:30)
- **What:** Using a second marker color to circle or frame the entire flipchart content at the end, adding visual cohesion.
- **When to use:** As a finishing touch to make the flipchart feel complete and visually polished.
- **How it works:** After filling in the content, switch to a contrasting color marker and draw a loose border or circle around the main content area.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=e0zqb83tGYc&t=210

### Headline + Initials Structure (ab 00:30)
- **What:** Start every flipchart with a clear headline and add your initials at the bottom so participants know who to follow up with.
- **When to use:** Any facilitated session where participants may want to reference the flipchart later or ask follow-up questions.
- **How it works:** Write the topic headline prominently at the top, and sign your initials at the bottom of the page.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=e0zqb83tGYc&t=30

## Key Principles
- Flipchart quality improves primarily through repetition — elements like banners and lettering become muscle memory over time.
- Short sentences on flipcharts are better than dense text: they reduce writing effort and give participants space to add their own interpretation to keywords.
- Leaving deliberate white space makes flipcharts easier to follow and gives participants room to add their own notes if they are sketching along.
- Flipcharts do not need to be perfect — "hand drawn is also okay" and imperfection is part of the medium's authenticity.
- Two-line text wrapping is acceptable; a small indent on line two can signal that it is a continuation of the same thought.
- A small decorative doodle in an unused corner adds personality and warmth to the flipchart without requiring artistic skill.

## Facilitation Tips
- Carsten intentionally uses flipcharts instead of PowerPoint because the format forces him toward shorter sentences and away from walls of text.
- Soft pencil pre-sketching is invisible to participants at a distance, so it functions as a hidden guide without affecting the session's live feel.
- Keep a spare marker or refill pens before sessions — running dry mid-session disrupts flow.
- Reserve empty space on the flipchart for spontaneous additions that arise during the conversation.

## Best Used When
- Preparing for workshops or training sessions where flipcharts are the primary visual medium
- Facilitators who are new to flipchart design and want a structured starting point
- Anyone looking to move away from slide-heavy presentations toward more participatory, low-tech visuals
