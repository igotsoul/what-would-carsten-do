# Troika Consulting with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten Lützen introduces Troika Consulting, a Liberating Structure designed to generate ideas and advice for real challenges people face. Working in groups of three, each person gets dedicated time as a "client" receiving structured peer consultation from two "consultants." The format is highly effective for surfacing actionable ideas and ensuring everyone both gives and receives meaningful input.

## Methods & Techniques

### Troika Consulting (ab 00:20)
- **What:** A Liberating Structure in which triads rotate through a client/consultant role structure, giving each person focused peer consultation on a real challenge they bring to the session.
- **When to use:** When people need fresh ideas or perspectives on a real problem; when you want everyone to both contribute and receive input; works well in retrospectives, coaching sessions, or any workshop where participants have individual challenges.
- **Group size:** Groups of exactly 3 (triads). Scale by running multiple triads in parallel.
- **Duration:** Approximately 30–45 minutes for a full round (all three members as client), depending on group pace.
- **How it works:**
  1. **Setup:** Arrange chairs in groups of three. Split participants into triads.
  2. **Reflect (1 min, silent):** Prompt the question: *"What is your challenge?"* or *"What do you need help with?"* Each person reflects individually on what they want input on.
  3. **Designate roles:** One person becomes the **client**; the other two are **consultants**.
  4. **Client shares (1 min):** The client presents their problem. Consultants listen without interrupting.
  5. **Q&A (1 min):** Consultants may ask clarifying questions to ensure they have what they need to help.
  6. **Client turns away (5 min):** The client physically turns their back to the group — still able to hear — and takes notes on what resonates. The two consultants brainstorm freely: ideas, possible solutions, what they would try.
  7. **Client returns (2 min):** The client turns back and shares key insights they captured. Brief clarifying dialogue follows.
  8. **Rotate:** A new person becomes the client. Repeat until all three have had their turn.

## Key Principles
- Everyone participates as both giver and receiver of advice — no passive observers.
- The "client turns away" step is the key design move: it removes social pressure, lets consultants speak freely, and puts the client in listening mode rather than defensive mode.
- Peer wisdom is often underutilized — this format surfaces it systematically.
- The structured timing prevents any one voice from dominating.

## Facilitation Tips
- Encourage clients to bring a real, current challenge — not a hypothetical. The more concrete, the more useful the consultant brainstorm.
- Coach consultants to brainstorm rather than interrogate during the 5-minute window — it's a dialogue between two, not a panel review.
- Remind clients during the "back-turned" phase to note what resonates, not to evaluate or rebut internally.
- The role of client can be self-selected or assigned — both work; Carsten suggests letting triads figure it out themselves.

## Best Used When
- Participants arrive with real individual challenges or blockers they want help thinking through.
- You want high engagement and peer learning without plenary discussion.
- In retrospectives, team coaching sessions, or leadership workshops where psychological safety is sufficient for people to share real problems.
- You need a format that scales easily — just add more triads.
