# "Exception Questions" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten introduces "Exception Questions," a technique rooted in solution-focused coaching. Rather than analyzing why something isn't working, you explore past moments when it did work — surfacing existing resources and partial solutions already present in the team or system.

## Methods & Techniques

### Exception Questions (ab 00:20)
- **What:** A solution-focused coaching technique where you ask about times when the current problem did NOT occur — i.e., the "exceptions" — to uncover already-existing resources and fragments of a solution.
- **When to use:** When a team or individual is stuck on a recurring problem (e.g., Sprint Goals not being met, stakeholder misalignment, process breakdowns). Especially useful when analytical problem-solving is going in circles.
- **Group size:** Works 1:1 or in team settings
- **Duration:** Can be used as a single powerful question or as a short structured conversation
- **How it works:**
  1. Identify the problem the team is struggling with (e.g., Sprint Goals not working)
  2. Ask about times when the problem was *absent* — "Tell me about times when the Sprint Goals did work"
  3. Explore those exceptions through the lens of **who, what, when, and where** (not *how*)
     - *Who* noticed it was working?
     - *What* did they see that told them it was working?
     - *When* did they see it working?
     - *Where* did they see it working?
  4. Use the answers to surface resources, behaviors, or conditions that can be intentionally recreated
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=LXJAwsLzCRY

## Key Principles
- Solution-focused coaching assumes that fragments of a solution already exist in the coachee's past experience — the coach's job is to surface them
- Asking "who, what, when, where" (not "how") keeps the conversation concrete and observational rather than theoretical
- Shifting from "why isn't it working?" to "when did it work?" reframes the conversation in an elegant, non-blaming way
- The technique works because it redirects attention from deficits to existing strengths and successful moments

## Facilitation Tips
- Resist the urge to ask *how* it worked — focus on the observable: who, what, when, where
- Use this when a team feels stuck or when retrospectives keep circling the same problem without progress
- The question "how did you notice it was working?" is a useful probe to make success concrete and visible
- Include multiple perspectives: how did the team notice? How did stakeholders notice? This broadens the picture

## Best Used When
- A team repeatedly struggles with the same issue and conventional problem analysis isn't generating new insight
- You want to shift a conversation from blame or frustration toward agency and possibility
- Coaching a Product Owner or team on Sprint Goal effectiveness or any recurring process challenge
- You want a lightweight but powerful reframe that takes under 5 minutes to introduce
