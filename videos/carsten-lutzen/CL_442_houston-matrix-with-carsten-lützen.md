# Houston Matrix with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten introduces the Houston Matrix, a framework from Todd Little's article "Context Adaptive Agility," which classifies projects along two axes — uncertainty and complexity — into four quadrants. The model helps teams and coaches have grounded conversations about which approach fits a given project, and why a process must evolve as the project moves between quadrants over time.

## Methods & Techniques

### Houston Matrix — Project Classification Framework (ab 00:30)
- **What:** A two-axis matrix that scores projects on uncertainty (Y-axis) and complexity (X-axis), yielding four quadrants, each with a distinct archetype and recommended approach.
- **When to use:** When a team or stakeholder group debates which methodology to use, or when you want to explain *why* Agile is or isn't the right fit for a specific project.
- **Group size:** Any — works well as a discussion tool in team settings or leadership conversations
- **Duration:** Can be run as a 15–30 min discussion exercise; here explained as a reference model
- **How it works:**
  1. Score the project on two dimensions: **uncertainty** (how unknown the outcomes/requirements are) and **complexity** (how many interdependencies, moving parts, specialist knowledge required).
  2. Place the project in one of four quadrants:
     - **Colts** (low complexity, high uncertainty): Fast-moving, uncertain projects — Agile shines here; you need to move fast and change direction without heavy process overhead.
     - **Skunks** (low complexity, low uncertainty): Routine "just get it done" work — minimal process needed, just execute.
     - **Dogs** (moderate-high complexity, low uncertainty): Steady, loyal projects with known scope — good rhythm, complexity under control, predictable delivery.
     - **Bulls** (high complexity, high uncertainty): The most demanding projects — off-the-shelf methodologies won't cut it; need tailored, adaptive approaches.
     - **Cash Cows** (high complexity, low uncertainty): Rewarding but complex — competitors unlikely to disrupt quickly; Agile still recommended to manage complexity even if uncertainty is low.
  3. Use the quadrant as a **discussion starter**, not a prescription.
  4. Track movement: projects often start as Colts, evolve into Bulls as complexity grows, then move to Cows as uncertainty is resolved, and finally to Dogs once both are managed.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=GO0Myc_xQ70

## Key Principles
- This is not an exact science — use the matrix as a **discussion tool**, not a definitive answer.
- No single methodology is a perfect fit for every project; context determines the right approach.
- Projects are not static — they migrate between quadrants as uncertainty is resolved and complexity becomes understood.
- The process must evolve with the project. Starting with one methodology and never revisiting it is a mistake.
- Retrospectives and other inspect-and-adapt mechanisms are essential precisely because projects move between quadrants.

## Facilitation Tips
- Carsten recommends framing the Houston Matrix explicitly as a **conversation starter** when presenting it to stakeholders — this reduces resistance to the "fuzzy" scoring.
- When a team is debating methodology choice, plotting their current project on the matrix can quickly surface different mental models and assumptions about complexity and uncertainty.
- Use quadrant movement over time to explain *why* processes should change mid-project — it reframes "changing our approach" from weakness to good practice.

## Best Used When
- Coaching a team or leadership group that is locked in debate about Agile vs. traditional project management
- Onboarding stakeholders to why Agile is (or isn't) appropriate for a specific initiative
- Running a process health check: "Has our project shifted quadrants — and has our process kept up?"
- Explaining the rationale behind retrospectives and continuous process adaptation
