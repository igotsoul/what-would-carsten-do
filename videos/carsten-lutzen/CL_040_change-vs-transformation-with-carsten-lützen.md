# "Change vs Transformation" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten Lützen draws a clear distinction between change and transformation — two terms often used interchangeably but with meaningfully different implications. Change is incremental and modifies how we work; transformation is a fundamental shift in identity, perspective, or system. The video argues that being precise about which one you're actually doing is critical to managing expectations and avoiding frustration.

## Methods & Techniques

### Change vs. Transformation Distinction (ab 00:45)
- **What:** A conceptual framework for differentiating between incremental improvements (change) and fundamental systemic shifts (transformation).
- **When to use:** When scoping organizational initiatives, setting expectations with stakeholders, or diagnosing why a change effort feels unsatisfying to teams.
- **Group size:** N/A — conceptual framework, applicable in any context
- **Duration:** N/A
- **How it works:**
  1. Ask: Are we modifying how we work in a small, gradual way? → That is **change**.
  2. Ask: Are we shifting identity, perspective, or the entire system? → That is **transformation**.
  3. Examples of change: breaking down work items into smaller pieces.
  4. Examples of transformation: moving from a project-based to a product-based organization.
  5. Assess whether leadership is selling transformation but delivering change — if so, name the gap explicitly.

## Key Principles
- **Change** = modification or gradual step in behavior or process. Small, manageable, lower risk.
- **Transformation** = shift in identity, perspective, or system. Requires letting go of control and certainty so something new can emerge.
- Using the two terms interchangeably creates frustration: people expecting transformation feel let down when they only receive change.
- Transformation is inherently riskier than change because it requires dismantling existing structures rather than adjusting them.
- For Scrum Masters and agile coaches, change is the everyday toolkit — the small notches that improve team performance. But some problems are too deeply rooted for change alone to fix.
- "Digital transformation" and "product transformation" are frequently used labels that often describe change initiatives in practice — worth scrutinizing which one is actually happening.

## Facilitation Tips
- When working with teams or leadership, explicitly name whether the initiative is a change or a transformation before diving into execution. This sets accurate expectations.
- If there is a mismatch between what is being sold (transformation) and what is being introduced (change), surface that gap as early as possible to prevent disillusionment.
- Carsten suggests sitting with the question: "Is what we're doing actually a change or actually a transformation?" as a useful reflective prompt before any major initiative.

## Best Used When
- Scoping a new organizational initiative and needing to calibrate stakeholder expectations
- Debriefing why a change effort felt disappointing despite good execution
- Coaching leadership on the difference between incremental improvement and systemic redesign
- Preparing a team or organization for a genuinely transformative shift (e.g., restructuring from projects to products)
