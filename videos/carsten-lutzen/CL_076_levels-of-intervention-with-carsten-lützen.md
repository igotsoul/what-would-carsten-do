# "Levels of Intervention" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten Lützen introduces the "Levels of Intervention" model — a framework for understanding how a facilitator or coach influences a group through every action, from complete absence to outright dominance. The model helps practitioners become more conscious of where they sit on the intervention spectrum at any given moment, and how different participants may perceive the same behavior very differently.

## Methods & Techniques

### Levels of Intervention Model (ab 00:30)
- **What:** A spectrum model describing the different degrees to which a facilitator or coach can influence a group — ranging from neglect and absence, through passive listening and enabling, to advice-giving and dominance.
- **When to use:** As a reflective framework for facilitators and coaches to calibrate their own behavior during sessions and coaching conversations.
- **Group size:** Not specified; applies to any group facilitation or 1:1 coaching context.
- **Duration:** Ongoing — a mental model to apply throughout any intervention.
- **How it works:**
  1. **Neglect / Absence** — The facilitator is physically or mentally absent. Considered a red zone; rarely desirable.
  2. **Passive / Listening** — The facilitator is present but not speaking. Three levels of listening apply here:
     - *Internal listening* (red zone): Listening to reply, not truly hearing the other person.
     - *Focused listening* (green zone): Attending to what the participant is actually saying.
     - *Universal listening* (green zone): Picking up all cues — words, body language, room dynamics.
  3. **Enabling through questions** (green/yellow boundary): Asking open-ended questions that center the participant, not the facilitator. Closed questions introduce bias and shift attention.
  4. **Advice / Suggestion / Convincing** (yellow zone): The facilitator begins speaking and risks centering themselves — suggesting solutions, selling tools or models.
  5. **Dominance** (red zone): Issuing directives, making plans unilaterally, imposing requirements — the facilitator fully controls the group's direction.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=tGxQeTdKY9s

### Three Levels of Listening (ab 01:50)
- **What:** A simplified listening typology used within the Levels of Intervention model to distinguish the quality of a facilitator's attention.
- **When to use:** When reflecting on your listening presence during coaching or facilitation.
- **How it works:**
  - *Internal listening*: Waiting for an opening to speak; not truly attending to the other person.
  - *Focused listening*: Actively hearing what the participant is saying and meaning.
  - *Universal listening*: Receiving all signals in the room — verbal, non-verbal, energetic.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=tGxQeTdKY9s&t=110

### Open vs. Closed Questions (ab 02:50)
- **What:** A coaching discipline of defaulting to open-ended questions to enable participants rather than leading them.
- **When to use:** Whenever a facilitator or coach is tempted to ask a yes/no or leading question.
- **How it works:** Replace closed, biased questions ("Do you want to try Y?") with open ones ("What would you like to try?" / "How would you like to do it?"). Closed questions are not forbidden but carry inherent bias and should be used consciously.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=tGxQeTdKY9s&t=170

## Key Principles
- Every action a facilitator takes — including silence and listening — influences the group. There is no neutral position.
- The goal as a facilitator is generally to stay in the enabling zone (focused/universal listening + open questions), keeping the participant at the center of attention.
- The same behavior can be perceived completely differently by different participants: silence may feel like neglect to one person and respectful space to another.
- Moving toward dominance (advice, convincing, ordering) pulls the facilitator into the center and reduces participant agency.
- Awareness of your audience — personality types, team culture, context — is essential to calibrating where on the spectrum to sit.
- Carsten credits this model to Krina Kelson, encountered in a Professional Agile Coaching course.

## Facilitation Tips
- Use the Levels of Intervention model as a real-time self-check: "Where am I on this spectrum right now, and is that where I want to be?"
- Be especially cautious when speaking up — as soon as you open your mouth, you shift the intervention level and risk being perceived as suggesting or directing.
- Recognize internal listening as a trap: if you are mentally composing your next point while someone is talking, you are in red zone listening.
- There are legitimate uses for closed questions and even directive interventions — the model is about conscious choice, not rigid rules.
- Remain humble about perception gaps: your self-assessed intervention level may not match how participants experience it.

## Best Used When
- Preparing for or reflecting on facilitation sessions and coaching conversations
- Developing facilitators or coaches who are learning to calibrate their presence
- Debriefing a session where the facilitator felt they over- or under-intervened
- Team or cohort discussions about coaching philosophy and stance
