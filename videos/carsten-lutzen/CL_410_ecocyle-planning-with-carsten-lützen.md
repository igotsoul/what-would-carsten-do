# Ecocyle Planning with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten introduces Ecocycle Planning, a Liberating Structure originally from The Liberators (Barry and Christiaan), that uses a nature-inspired lifecycle metaphor to help teams, departments, or individuals map where they are spending their time. The method surfaces hidden energy drains, stalled initiatives, and activities that have outlived their value — and creates a shared starting point for deciding what to do more or less of.

## Methods & Techniques

### Ecocycle Planning (ab 00:30)
- **What:** Participants map their current activities onto a lifecycle model shaped like an infinity symbol, with four phases — Gestation, Birth, Maturity, and Creative Destruction — plus two trap zones (Poverty Trap, Rigidity Trap). The visual reveals where energy is going and where change is needed.
- **When to use:** When a team or department wants to reflect on how they are spending their time, identify what to stop or start, or surface hidden blockers and stale commitments.
- **Group size:** Works for individuals, pairs, teams, or whole departments.
- **Duration:** Not specified precisely; the 1-2-4-All structure suggests ~15–30 minutes depending on group size.
- **How it works:**
  1. Introduce the ecocycle model using the nature metaphor (plants, lifecycle phases).
  2. Create a large infinity-symbol on the floor; give each participant a smaller version.
  3. **Individual (1 min):** Each person silently maps the activities they spend time on.
  4. **Pairs (1–2 min):** Partners compare their lists and start placing activities on the ecocycle.
  5. **Groups of four:** Groups consolidate findings and plot activities on the large floor version.
  6. **All:** Walk through the full ecocycle together — what is surprising? What is missing or misplaced?
  7. Focus on outcomes: for items in a trap, define telltale signs of movement and agree on actions.

### 1-2-4-All as scaffolding (ab 03:30)
- **What:** Carsten explicitly uses 1-2-4-All (another Liberating Structure) as the participation structure inside Ecocycle Planning to ensure individual reflection before group convergence.
- **When to use:** Whenever you need to prevent groupthink and ensure quieter voices contribute before the group converges.

## Key Principles

- **Nature as metaphor:** The ecocycle mirrors what we observe in ecosystems — things are born, mature, decline, and need to be destroyed to make room for renewal. Framing it this way makes the model feel natural and non-threatening.
- **Four lifecycle phases:**
  - **Gestation** — ideas that exist in the back of the mind but haven't materialized; consume energy without producing value.
  - **Birth** — early-stage initiatives that have had some investment and are ready to take off.
  - **Maturity** — where value is harvested; low energy input, steady returns.
  - **Creative Destruction** — intentionally burning down what no longer serves, to make space for something new.
- **Two traps to watch for:**
  - **Poverty Trap** — good ideas stuck between gestation and birth; not yet tested, consuming energy, causing frustration.
  - **Rigidity Trap** — mature activities that have stopped adding value but haven't been let go of; often held by habit or lack of courage.
- **Focus on movement, not just mapping:** The real value comes from identifying what needs to shift and defining the concrete signs that movement has happened.

## Facilitation Tips

- Build a large-scale infinity symbol on the floor — Carsten notes this works "really really well" for physicality and shared focus.
- Give individual reflection time before any group discussion to surface diverse perspectives.
- During the all-group debrief, explicitly ask: "What is surprising?" and "What did you expect to see somewhere else?"
- When working with a trap (Poverty or Rigidity), ask: "What are the telltale signs that this has moved?" before jumping to actions — this makes outcomes concrete and observable.
- The model is versatile: it works for team activities, department time allocation, individual workloads, or even personal relationships.

## Best Used When

- A team feels overwhelmed or spread too thin and needs to surface what is consuming energy without returning value.
- A retrospective or planning session needs a fresh format beyond standard formats.
- Leadership or a department wants a structured conversation about strategic priorities and what to stop doing.
- Introducing the concept of creative destruction to a group that tends to hold onto outdated processes or reports.

## Related Concepts

- **Liberating Structures** — Ecocycle Planning is one of the 33 Liberating Structures; created and documented by Keith McCandless and Henri Lipmanowicz; popularized in agile contexts by The Liberators (Barry and Christiaan).
- **1-2-4-All** — Liberating Structure used here as the participation format within Ecocycle Planning.
- **Creative Destruction** — concept from economics (Schumpeter) applied here to team and organizational renewal.
