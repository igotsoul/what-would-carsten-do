# "Improv Prototyping" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten introduces Improv Prototyping, a Liberating Structure that combines improvisation theater with iterative prototyping to help teams work through real situations they want to change. Participants enact a scene, debrief in small groups, build improved prototypes, and iterate — progressively converging on a solution that could address the problem.

## Methods & Techniques

### Improv Prototyping (ab 00:30)
- **What:** A Liberating Structure where a group enacts a real situation (e.g., a team conflict, a broken knowledge transfer), observers debrief and prototype improvements, and the loop iterates until a viable solution emerges.
- **When to use:** When a team has a recurring interpersonal or process situation they want to improve — conflicts, communication breakdowns, ineffective knowledge transfer, etc.
- **Group size:** Small group of players (volunteers) + observers split into smaller groups; also works with two groups enacting simultaneously depending on crowd size.
- **Duration:** Each enacted scene is 3–5 minutes (Carsten notes five minutes may already be too long); full session runs multiple iterative loops.
- **How it works:**
  1. Introduce the situation — set the stage and share the context or challenge to explore.
  2. Recruit volunteers to be **players**; the rest become **observers** split into small groups.
  3. Players **enact the scene** (3–5 minutes) — no scripting, improvised.
  4. Observer groups **debrief internally** using 1-2-4-All: identify what worked well and what didn't, then determine what could be done differently.
  5. Each observer group **acts out their own prototype** — keeping the good chunks, replacing what didn't work.
  6. One group (or two, depending on size) **presents their prototype** to the full crowd.
  7. **Repeat the loop** — enact, debrief, prototype — iterating until a believable, workable prototype emerges.

## Key Principles
- Improv Prototyping combines improvisation theater (unscripted enactment) with iterative prototyping — you build solutions by doing, not by discussing abstractly.
- The iterative loop (enact → debrief → prototype → repeat) mirrors agile thinking: small cycles of action and reflection converge on better outcomes.
- The use of 1-2-4-All within observer groups ensures everyone contributes to identifying what works and what doesn't before building the next prototype.
- Courage and vulnerability are implicit: one group shares their situation and prototype openly for the rest to reflect on.
- The goal is a prototype the group genuinely believes could fix the problem — not a perfect solution, but a believable next step.

## Facilitation Tips
- Keep enacted scenes short — 3 minutes is often better than 5. Carsten explicitly notes five minutes may already be too long.
- Use 1-2-4-All as the debrief structure within observer groups to surface individual observations before converging on group insights.
- Adjust how many groups present their prototype based on crowd size — one group is the default, two groups works for larger audiences.
- Frame the session clearly upfront: set the scene, explain roles (players vs. observers), and establish psychological safety before asking for volunteers.
- Let the iterations run until the group feels the prototype is genuinely usable — don't stop at the first pass.

## Best Used When
- A team is stuck in a recurring situation (conflict, miscommunication, broken process) and talking about it hasn't led to change.
- You want to generate multiple solution ideas quickly through embodied practice rather than abstract discussion.
- The team has enough psychological safety to enact real situations in front of peers.
- You need to move from problem identification to prototype solutions in a single session.
