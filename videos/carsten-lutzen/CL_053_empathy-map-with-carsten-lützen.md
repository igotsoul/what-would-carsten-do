# "Empathy Map" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten Lützen walks through the Empathy Map canvas — a tool originally created by Dave Gray — explaining each of its sections and how they help teams build genuine understanding of their users or stakeholders. The video is a concise practical guide to running or facilitating an empathy mapping session, covering both the observable outer-world stimuli and the harder-to-reach inner world of a person's thoughts, fears, and hopes.

## Methods & Techniques

### Empathy Map (ab 00:20)
- **What:** A structured canvas developed by Dave Gray that helps groups build empathy with a specific user, persona, or stakeholder by systematically mapping what they see, say, do, hear, think, and feel.
- **When to use:** When a team needs to deeply understand a user's perspective before building a product, designing a service, or coaching someone through a challenge. Especially useful early in design or discovery phases.
- **Group size:** Suitable for group work; Carsten frames it as a collaborative exercise ("slowly but surely in a group").
- **Duration:** Not explicitly stated; implied as a workshop-length activity.
- **How it works:**
  1. **Who** — Define who you are empathizing with: a persona, a concrete person, or a stakeholder type. Set the situation they are in.
  2. **Goals** — Capture what the user needs to do, wants to do differently, and what job they are trying to get done (links to Jobs-to-be-Done framework).
  3. **See** — What do they observe in their environment? What competitors, products, or situations are visible to them?
  4. **Say** — What do they actually verbalize about the problem or product? (e.g., "I'm really annoyed by having to flip my toast bread.")
  5. **Do** — What are they actually doing today? Carsten notes a gap often exists between what people say and what they do.
  6. **Hear** — What do friends, colleagues, or others say about the problem or product? What external voices influence them?
  7. **Think & Feel** (inner world) — What pains and fears do they have? What gains do they hope for? What are their innermost wants regarding this problem? Space is also left for other thoughts or behaviors.
  8. **Synthesis** — Once the canvas is filled, evaluate your solution or idea against it: Will they see it? Will they hear others talk about it? Does it deliver the gains they hope for, or does it tap into their fears?

## Key Principles
- The Empathy Map distinguishes between the **outer world** (See, Say, Do, Hear — observable and quantifiable) and the **inner world** (Think & Feel — harder to access but critical).
- There is often a gap between what people say they do and what they actually do — mapping both surfaces that tension.
- A completed Empathy Map becomes a validation lens: you can test any proposed solution against each quadrant to assess fit.
- Carsten connects the "Goals" section to the **Jobs-to-be-Done** framework, suggesting these two tools are complementary.
- The canvas works for any stakeholder, not just end users — Carsten explicitly mentions coaches thinking about the perspectives of the people they work with.

## Facilitation Tips
- Start by anchoring the group on **who** specifically they are empathizing with before filling in any other section — vague personas lead to vague outputs.
- Use the "Say vs. Do" tension deliberately: prompt the group to challenge whether stated behaviors match actual behaviors.
- The Think & Feel quadrant is the hardest to fill — Carsten suggests leaving space for additional thoughts and behaviors that surface naturally during the exercise.
- After completing the canvas, use it actively as a **design review tool** by asking: "Does our solution show up in their world? Does it meet their gains? Does it trigger their fears?"
- Dave Gray's original blog post and downloadable canvas are worth sharing with participants before or after the session (Carsten references providing a link).

## Best Used When
- A team is about to design, build, or iterate on a product or service and needs to ground decisions in user reality.
- A coaching conversation or intervention needs to be designed with the coachee's perspective at the center.
- A group has made assumptions about users and needs a structured way to surface and challenge those assumptions.
- Early in a discovery or design sprint, before solution ideas are formed.

## Related Concepts
- **Jobs-to-be-Done** — Referenced in the Goals section of the canvas; complements the empathy map by focusing on the functional and emotional job a user is trying to accomplish.
- **Dave Gray** — Original creator of the Empathy Map; Carsten attributes the tool to him and references his blog post.
- **Persona design** — The "Who" section links directly to persona-based design practices.
- **Design Thinking** — The Empathy Map is a core tool in the empathize phase of design thinking.
