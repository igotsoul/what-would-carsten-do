# Magic Estimation with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten Lützen introduces Magic Estimation, a silent, interactive technique for estimating backlog items using the Fibonacci sequence. It is designed to replace or complement Planning Poker, making refinement sessions more energetic and physically engaging while surfacing misalignments in team understanding.

## Methods & Techniques

### Magic Estimation (ab 00:30)
- **What:** A silent, timeboxed estimation technique where team members take turns placing or moving backlog items on a Fibonacci scale — no discussion allowed during the activity.
- **When to use:** During backlog refinement when you want to spice up estimation, get people moving, and surface misalignments quickly. Works as an alternative to Planning Poker.
- **Group size:** Whole Scrum team
- **Duration:** Multiple 2-minute timeboxes until all items are estimated
- **How it works:**
  1. Prepare the backlog with items sorted roughly from most to least important.
  2. Display the Fibonacci scale (1, 2, 3, 5, 8, 13, 20) on a physical or virtual board. Carsten deliberately uses 20 instead of 21 to signal that anything that large is too uncertain to estimate precisely.
  3. Team members take turns — each person can either place a new story on the scale or move an existing story to a different position. No talking allowed.
  4. Run the round for a 2-minute timebox.
  5. After the timebox, pause: identify any stories that were moved back and forth repeatedly — these signal misalignment and need a discussion.
  6. Discuss only those contested stories: do team members share the same understanding of scope? Add detail or split stories as needed.
  7. Repeat timeboxes until all items are estimated, then write story points on the post-its.

## Key Principles
- Estimation is optional and context-dependent — Carsten uses it when it helps the team align on what a story actually contains, not as a mandatory ritual.
- Silence during placement forces individual judgment before group influence kicks in, making divergence visible rather than suppressed.
- Stories that get juggled back and forth are diagnostic: they reveal gaps in shared understanding, not just different gut-feel estimates.
- Using 20 instead of 21 is intentional — it signals that very large stories are inherently too imprecise to estimate meaningfully, and should be split.
- The physical movement (getting people up and placing items) adds energy compared to sitting at a table doing Planning Poker.

## Facilitation Tips
- Strictly enforce the silence rule — the value of the technique comes from seeing where disagreement emerges without social pressure influencing individual moves.
- Keep timeboxes short (2 minutes) to maintain energy and urgency.
- Use the "juggled stories" as your discussion agenda rather than opening up general debate after every timebox.
- The discussion after contested stories may generate new backlog items or simply add acceptance criteria — both are valid outcomes.
- Alternate between Magic Estimation and Planning Poker across sessions to keep refinement fresh.

## Best Used When
- The team has fallen into a routine with Planning Poker and engagement is dropping.
- You want to quickly surface stories with unclear or divergent scope.
- The team benefits from physical movement and standing up during a session.
- You have a reasonably sized backlog to work through and want to estimate many items efficiently.

## Related Concepts
- **Planning Poker** — the more common alternative; Magic Estimation is positioned as a complement or replacement for variety.
- **Fibonacci Sequence** — the estimation scale used; Carsten's deliberate use of 20 (not 21) reflects the "cone of uncertainty" thinking for large stories.
- **Backlog Refinement** — the ceremony this technique is designed to enhance.
- **Timeboxing** — core mechanic keeping the activity focused and energetic.
