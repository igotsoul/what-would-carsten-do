# "Celebrity Interview" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten introduces the Liberating Structure "Celebrity Interview," a structured format for extracting knowledge and insights from a specific individual (the "celebrity") in front of a group. The format combines a prepared interview with an audience-generated question round, using 1-2-4-All to ensure broad participation before the second interview round.

## Methods & Techniques

### Celebrity Interview (ab 00:30)
- **What:** A structured interview format where a designated "celebrity" (expert, executive, or person of interest) is interviewed in front of an audience, with a two-round structure that blends prepared questions with audience-generated questions.
- **When to use:** When you want to surface knowledge from a specific individual and make it accessible to a group — useful for town halls, knowledge transfers, onboarding sessions, or stakeholder engagement.
- **Group size:** Any audience size; note that the "All" step of 1-2-4-All may be excessive for very large groups.
- **Duration:** 35 minutes to 1 hour depending on crowd size and depth desired.
- **How it works:**
  1. **Introduction / stage setting** — Briefly introduce the celebrity and frame why they are there and what theme will be explored.
  2. **Round 1 — Prepared interview** (~15 min, up to 30 min) — The interviewer asks pre-prepared questions, covering what the audience would likely want to know. Carsten recommends keeping this part short to leave room for interaction.
  3. **1-2-4 question generation** — Based on what they heard, audience members individually reflect (1), then discuss in pairs (2), then in groups of four (4) to generate new questions. The "All" step is optional and may be skipped depending on group size, since the full-group discussion is covered by the interview itself.
  4. **Collect questions** — Questions from the small groups are collated and handed to the interviewer. In more mature groups, individuals from each group of four may volunteer to ask their question directly to the celebrity.
  5. **Round 2 — Audience-driven interview** (~10–15 min) — The interviewer (or volunteers) asks the audience-generated questions to the celebrity. Carsten recommends investing more time here than in Round 1.
  6. **Closing reflection** — Ask the audience for takeaways: *What was your biggest insight? What surprised you most?* Thank the celebrity explicitly for their time.

## Key Principles
- The real value is in Round 2: Carsten deliberately keeps Round 1 short to maximize time for audience-generated questions — that's where the deeper engagement happens.
- The 1-2-4 step serves a dual purpose: it generates questions *and* gives introverts and slower thinkers time to formulate their thoughts before speaking up.
- Stage setting matters — briefly framing *why* this person is the celebrity and what theme is being explored creates alignment before the interview begins.
- Thanking the celebrity explicitly at the end is part of the format, not a nicety — it closes the container and acknowledges the vulnerability of being interviewed in public.

## Facilitation Tips
- Prepare your Round 1 questions in advance, anticipating what the audience would want to know — this primes the audience and sets a baseline.
- Skip the "All" step of 1-2-4-All if the group is large; the full-group plenary is already covered by the interview format itself.
- If the group is experienced or psychologically safe, invite volunteers from the groups of four to ask their questions directly to the celebrity rather than routing through the interviewer.
- Keep Round 1 to 15 minutes — resist the urge to go longer even if the conversation is rich. The audience-driven round is where the magic happens.

## Best Used When
- You have access to a specific person whose knowledge, experience, or perspective is valuable to a group.
- You want to make a town hall, AMA, or expert session more interactive and structured than a pure Q&A.
- You need to surface questions from an audience that may be hesitant to speak up directly (the 1-2-4 step lowers that barrier).
- Onboarding, leadership engagement sessions, retrospective guests, or community knowledge-sharing events.
