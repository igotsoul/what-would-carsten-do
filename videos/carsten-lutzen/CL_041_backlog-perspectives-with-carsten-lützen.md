# "Backlog Perspectives" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten shares how he combines a personal Kanban board with Ecocycle Planning from Liberating Structures to gain perspective on where he is actually investing his energy versus where he should be. The video is a short personal reflection on using a structured framework to counter the natural tendency to chase new ideas at the expense of maturing existing work.

## Methods & Techniques

### Personal Kanban for Work Prioritization (ab 00:20)
- **What:** A Kanban-style board with columns representing time horizons — Later, This Month, This Week, Now, In Progress, Done — used to organize and focus personal work.
- **When to use:** Ongoing personal work management, especially for coaches and facilitators juggling multiple concurrent commitments.
- **Group size:** Individual
- **Duration:** Ongoing system, reviewed regularly
- **How it works:** Tasks and ideas are placed in columns according to when they are relevant. The board provides a visual overview of focus and priorities across different time horizons.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Wtslyxr9Hxw&t=20

### Ecocycle Planning as a Backlog Temperature Check (ab 01:30)
- **What:** Carsten periodically maps items from his personal Kanban backlog onto the Ecocycle (a Liberating Structures format) to assess the maturity and energy distribution of his work.
- **When to use:** When you want a periodic reality check on whether your effort allocation matches your intentions — especially useful if you tend to start many things but struggle to mature or complete them.
- **Group size:** Individual (described here), though Ecocycle Planning also works with teams
- **Duration:** Periodic review — not a daily practice but a recurring check-in
- **How it works:**
  1. Pull items from your backlog.
  2. Map each item onto the four phases of the Ecocycle: Gestation (ideas not yet started), Birth (experiments and one-offs), Maturity (things delivering value with reasonable effort), Creative Destruction (things to stop or let go of).
  3. Observe where your items cluster — this reveals where you are putting energy and where gaps exist.
  4. Use the map to make conscious choices about what to nurture, harvest, or stop.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Wtslyxr9Hxw&t=90

## Key Principles
- Where you put your effort and where you *should* put your effort are often not the same — making this visible is the first step to changing it.
- Chasing new and shiny ideas is a natural tendency; having a structure that surfaces mature, high-value work counterbalances this bias.
- Creative destruction — consciously deciding to stop things you no longer believe in — is a discipline worth practicing deliberately.
- The Ecocycle is not just a team tool; it has genuine value as a personal reflection instrument.

## Facilitation Tips
- Carsten uses Ecocycle Planning "from time to time" rather than continuously — framing it as a periodic temperature check rather than a rigid process makes it sustainable.
- When applying Ecocycle to personal backlogs, the Gestation and Birth phases tend to fill up naturally; the Maturity and Creative Destruction phases are where honest self-reflection is most needed.

## Best Used When
- A coach, Scrum Master, or facilitator wants to reflect on their own professional development and workload.
- Someone is aware they start many things but rarely finish or harvest value from them.
- Teams or individuals want to complement their Kanban or backlog practice with a periodic strategic lens.
