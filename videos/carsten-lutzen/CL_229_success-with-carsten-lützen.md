# "Success?!?" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten shares a simple but powerful exercise (credited to his colleague Fleming) for helping teams, leaders, or departments articulate what success actually means to them. By examining combinations of three parameters — time, scope, and business outcome — groups surface hidden assumptions and misalignments about what they are truly optimizing for.

## Methods & Techniques

### Success Definition Exercise (ab 00:30)
- **What:** A structured discussion exercise that breaks "success" into three dimensions — time, scope, and business outcome — and has participants evaluate different combinations of those dimensions.
- **When to use:** When a team or group needs to align on what success looks like before or during a project; also effective with leadership teams or entire departments. Especially valuable when implicit assumptions about success may differ across the group.
- **Group size:** Works for teams, leadership groups, or larger departments; small-group breakouts recommended
- **Duration:** Not specified; small-group discussion format suggests 20–45 minutes depending on group size
- **How it works:**
  1. Introduce the three parameters: **Time** (did we meet the deadline?), **Scope** (did we deliver what was agreed, with the resources agreed?), and **Business Outcome** (did we solve the underlying need?).
  2. Walk through the combinations — there are only a few meaningful permutations (e.g., all met, only time+scope met, only outcome met, none met, etc.).
  3. Split into smaller groups and have each group discuss: "Is this a success?" for each combination.
  4. Use a **1-2-4-All** structure: individual reflection → pairs → groups of four → whole group discussion.
  5. Optionally, have groups fill out a table rating each combination.
  6. Surface and discuss where people disagree — these disagreements are the real value of the exercise.

## Key Principles
- Many people have a gut feeling about success but struggle to articulate it — making it explicit creates alignment.
- The three parameters (time, scope, business outcome) are not equally weighted in most organizations, and that imbalance often goes unexamined.
- Some combinations produce surprising disagreement: delivering on time and scope but *not* solving the business outcome is often still celebrated as success — which may not hold up under scrutiny.
- Conversely, solving the business outcome through a different scope than agreed is often *not* counted as success, even though it arguably should be.
- The exercise doesn't just define success — it reveals what the team or organization is actually *rewarding*, which may differ from what they claim to value.

## Facilitation Tips
- Use **1-2-4-All** to structure the small-group discussions and ensure everyone's voice is heard before converging on a shared view.
- Provide a simple table for groups to fill out — one row per combination, one column per judgment (success / not success / depends).
- Start with the obvious cases (all met = success; none met = not success) to build momentum and trust before moving to the ambiguous ones.
- The most valuable discussions happen around the edge cases — don't rush past them.
- Attribute the exercise to Fleming (Carsten's co-worker) if sourcing matters in your context.

## Best Used When
- Kicking off a new project or initiative to align the team on what "done well" looks like
- Running a retrospective focused on outcomes rather than process
- Working with leadership teams who need to examine what metrics and behaviors they are implicitly incentivizing
- Any situation where a team has delivered "on time and on budget" but something still feels off

## Related Concepts
- **1-2-4-All** (Liberating Structures) — recommended as the discussion format for this exercise
- **OKRs / KPIs** — the exercise surfaces whether teams are measuring outcomes or just outputs
- **Iron Triangle / Triple Constraint** — the time/scope/cost framework from project management; this exercise reframes the third leg as *business outcome* rather than cost
