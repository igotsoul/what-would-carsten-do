# "Solution Focused Stand-ups" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten Lützen proposes a solution-focused reframing of the classic three Daily Scrum questions. By shifting the language from task-reporting to achievement, user value, and best hopes, teams become more forward-looking, user-centric, and collaborative in their daily stand-ups.

## Methods & Techniques

### Solution-Focused Stand-up Questions (ab 00:30)
- **What:** A direct replacement of the classic three stand-up questions with solution-focused, user-centric alternatives that emphasise achievements, intentions, and openly asking for help.
- **When to use:** In any Daily Scrum or stand-up where the team has fallen into task-listing mode or where collaboration and user focus feel low.
- **Group size:** Any Scrum or agile team
- **Duration:** Same as a regular stand-up (typically 15 min or less)
- **How it works:**
  1. Replace "What did I do yesterday?" with **"What did I achieve yesterday?"** — ideally framed from the user's perspective: *"Yesterday, our users can now do X, which gives them Y value."*
  2. Replace "What will I do today?" with **"Today has been good if…" / "My best hopes for today are…"** — focusing on the desired outcome rather than the technical path, e.g. *"At the end of the day I hope the user is able to create and send an invoice."*
  3. Keep "Is there anything holding me back?" but reframe it as openly asking for help: **"I need help with X"** — treating this as a normal, trust-based act rather than a sign of weakness.

## Key Principles
- **Achievements over activity:** Asking "what did I achieve?" invites pride and reflection on impact, not just task completion.
- **User-centric language:** Framing answers around what users can now do shifts the team's awareness toward value delivery without extra ceremony.
- **Outcome framing unlocks collaboration:** Stating what you *hope to reach* (rather than *how you plan to get there*) creates natural space for teammates to offer ideas without triggering irrelevant technical deep-dives.
- **Asking for help is a team strength:** Normalising "I need help" reduces the hero-programmer culture and reinforces collective ownership — but requires a foundation of trust.
- **Progress orientation:** The reframed questions collectively ask "how can we move the needle further in a positive direction?" rather than reporting status.

## Facilitation Tips
- Frame the question change as a small experiment — just three new phrasings — to lower resistance.
- Coach team members to include the user in their language: *"the user can now…"* or *"the user is now able to…"*.
- If the team is not yet comfortable openly asking for help, Carsten explicitly recommends investing in trust-building first before the third question can work as intended.
- Avoid letting the second question drift into technical how-to discussions; redirect to the outcome if it does.

## Best Used When
- Stand-ups have become dry status-reporting sessions with low energy or engagement.
- The team lacks a sense of user connection or value awareness day-to-day.
- You want to introduce solution-focused thinking gradually, without a full retro or workshop intervention.
- Trust within the team is sufficient for vulnerability (or you are actively working on building it).
