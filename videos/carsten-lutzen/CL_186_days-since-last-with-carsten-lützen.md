# "Days Since Last" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten introduces "Days Since Last" — a deceptively simple visual metric that counts how many days have elapsed since a meaningful event occurred. It works both as a team transparency tool and a personal accountability nudge, and integrates naturally into standups and retrospectives.

## Methods & Techniques

### Days Since Last (ab 00:30)
- **What:** A counter — physical sticky note or digital display — that tracks how many days have passed since a specific event. Some metrics you want to keep near zero; others you want to see climb as high as possible.
- **When to use:** In team spaces (physical or digital boards) as a continuous, low-effort visibility tool; in standups for a quick daily check-in; in retrospectives as a conversation starter when a counter has reset.
- **Group size:** Any team size; also works for individual use.
- **Duration:** Setup takes minutes; maintenance is seconds per day during standup.
- **How it works:**
  1. Identify the event you want to track (e.g., "last board update", "last coaching conversation", "last release to production", "last crash", "last voided sprint").
  2. Create a sticky note or card with the metric name and the current count.
  3. Optionally write the date of the last update in a corner so you can reconstruct the count if you forget to update it.
  4. Each day — ideally during standup — update the number.
  5. In retrospectives, use resets (back to zero) as a trigger for reflection.

## Key Principles
- Some counters you want low (days since last board update, days since last release) — they signal freshness and flow.
- Some counters you want high (days since last crash, days since last stress sick leave, days since last voided sprint) — they signal stability and health.
- The streak psychology is a feature: as the number climbs, teams become more motivated to protect it, which can change behavior (e.g., finding alternatives to killing a sprint rather than just cancelling it).
- This is not an omnipotent metric — it is one input into your process, most valuable when combined with conversation.
- Carsten uses a personal version to track coaching conversations, keeping himself accountable without aiming for zero.

## Facilitation Tips
- The facilitator can own the daily update — it takes only seconds during standup to run through the counters.
- Write the date of the last update in the corner of the sticky note so you can reconstruct the count if you miss a day.
- Alternatively, write it on the back of the sticky note if you find the date visually distracting.
- Make updating the counter a daily habit — consistency is what gives the metric its signal.
- In retrospectives, any counter that has recently reset to zero is an automatic agenda item: what happened, and what can we change?

## Best Used When
- A team wants lightweight, always-visible process health indicators without complex tooling.
- You are coaching a team on continuous delivery and want to surface how often they actually release.
- A team's board hygiene or work-in-progress is hard to discuss directly — a "days since last update" counter makes the issue visible without blame.
- A Scrum Master or coach wants a personal accountability tool for habits like coaching conversations or 1-on-1s.
- A retrospective needs a concrete data point to ground the discussion.
