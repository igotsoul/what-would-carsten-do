# "Nine Whys" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten introduces Nine Whys, a Liberating Structure designed to help people uncover and articulate their deeper purpose and motivation. By repeatedly asking "Why is that important to you?", the format creates a structured space for meaningful reflection — both individually and as a group. It's a compact exercise that builds deep listening skills and surfaces shared values across a team.

## Methods & Techniques

### Nine Whys (ab 00:30)
- **What:** A paired interview structure where one person repeatedly asks "Why is that important to you?" to help the other person dig beneath the surface of their stated work or goal — down to their core motivation.
- **When to use:** When a team needs to reconnect with their shared purpose, before starting a new project or initiative, or when surface-level alignment is hiding deeper divergence in values.
- **Group size:** Pairs within a larger group; works with any group size as long as you can form pairs.
- **Duration:** ~15–20 minutes total (5 minutes per role in each pair, plus reflection and group sharing).
- **How it works:**
  1. Give everyone a starting prompt along the lines of: "I'm working on [specific project or initiative]..."
  2. Break the group into pairs. One person is the **subject**, the other is the **interviewer**.
  3. The interviewer asks only one question, repeatedly: **"Why is that important to you?"** — up to nine times.
  4. The subject tells their story and reflects aloud; the interviewer listens without judgment.
  5. After 5 minutes, switch roles. Repeat.
  6. After both roles are complete, individuals take a few minutes to reflect silently — Carsten suggests writing key insights on a sticky note or piece of paper.
  7. Pairs or small groups then share their reflections, looking for **commonalities** across stories.
  8. Facilitate a whole-group discussion: What do we have in common? What does this tell us about how we want to shape our future actions?

## Key Principles
- The repeated "why" question is intentionally simple — the constraint forces the subject to go deeper rather than deflect into explanation.
- The goal is not to reach a "correct" answer after nine iterations but to facilitate progressive reflection; depth emerges from the repetition.
- Commonalities discovered in the debrief are more powerful than individual insights — they reveal shared purpose that often goes unspoken.
- According to Carsten, Nine Whys "doesn't take that much time" but reliably helps people get comfortable with deep listening and self-reflection as a team practice.

## Facilitation Tips
- Keep the interviewer's role strictly limited to the one question — no paraphrasing, no follow-up probes beyond "Why is that important to you?" Carsten emphasizes this constraint is what gives the structure its power.
- Use a neutral, curious prompt to get started (e.g., "I'm working on [project name]") — specific enough to anchor the conversation, open enough to allow movement toward deeper meaning.
- During individual reflection after the paired interviews, give people something concrete to do: write one key insight on a post-it or a page before sharing.
- In the group debrief, actively name the commonalities you hear — surfacing them explicitly helps the group feel a sense of shared identity and purpose.

## Best Used When
- A team is starting a new project and needs to build genuine alignment, not just task-level coordination.
- You sense that people are going through the motions without connection to deeper purpose.
- You want to build psychological safety and listening culture without a lengthy intervention.
- As an opener for a strategy workshop, team launch, or values conversation.
