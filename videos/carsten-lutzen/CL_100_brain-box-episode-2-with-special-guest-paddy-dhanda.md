# Brain Box - Episode 2 with Special guest: Paddy Dhanda

## Summary
A short live-drawing session where Paddy Dhanda (co-founder of Visual Jam) and collaborators each have 7 minutes to visually illustrate the same random prompt — in this episode, "a team goal." The video demonstrates a practical visual thinking warm-up format done asynchronously, with Paddy narrating his sketching process and decision-making in real time. Viewers are invited to try the same exercise themselves.

## Methods & Techniques

### Brain Box — Random Prompt Visual Sprint (ab 00:30)
- **What:** Participants spin a randomizer to receive a prompt, then independently sketch it within a 7-minute time box. Sketches are compared afterward without prior coordination.
- **When to use:** As a warm-up for visual thinking practice, team meetings, or facilitation workshops. Also useful for building visual vocabulary and creative confidence.
- **Group size:** Works individually or in small groups (2–4 shown here)
- **Duration:** 7 minutes per round
- **How it works:**
  1. Use a spinner or randomizer to select a prompt.
  2. Each participant sets a 7-minute timer independently.
  3. Everyone draws the same prompt asynchronously — no peeking at others' work.
  4. Reconvene to compare and discuss the different visual interpretations.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=XYtPgC06wYM&t=30

### Brainstorm-Before-You-Draw (ab 01:30)
- **What:** Before committing to a sketch, Paddy deliberately pauses to brainstorm the conceptual associations of the prompt — identifying symbols, metaphors, and emotions it evokes — before picking up the pen.
- **When to use:** Whenever a visual prompt is abstract or conceptual (e.g., "resilience," "team goal," "innovation"). Prevents defaulting to the first obvious image.
- **How it works:**
  1. Write down the prompt.
  2. Spend 30–60 seconds listing associated concepts, emotions, and symbols (e.g., for "team goal": celebration, striving, sport, journey, milestone).
  3. Pick the most expressive or interesting combination to draw.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=XYtPgC06wYM&t=90

### Three-People Rule for Representing a Team (ab 06:30)
- **What:** Paddy shares a visual shorthand: always draw at least three people to convey "team." Two feels like a pair; three or more reads as a group.
- **When to use:** Any time you need to sketch a team, community, or group in a visual deliverable, facilitation artifact, or slide.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=XYtPgC06wYM&t=390

## Key Principles
- Imperfection is intentional: Paddy repeatedly emphasizes "it doesn't need to be perfect — just illustrate what you're trying to say." The goal is communication, not artistry.
- Time constraints unlock creativity: The 7-minute time box forces decisions and prevents over-thinking. Paddy notes the constraint "can really do something creative-wise."
- Visual metaphors carry meaning: Paddy moves through multiple metaphors (star, basketball hoop, mountain with flag) and lands on the one that best tells a story — a team helping each other climb toward a goal. Choosing a metaphor is itself a design decision.
- Asynchronous comparison surfaces diversity of interpretation: Because participants draw independently, reconvening reveals how differently the same concept can be represented — a useful insight for teams about shared understanding.

## Facilitation Tips
- Narrate your thinking out loud while drawing — this models the brainstorming process and makes it accessible to people who believe they "can't draw."
- Color doesn't need to be elaborate; a quick layer at the end adds energy and life to a sketch without requiring extra time.
- Invite participants to share their own attempts and suggest future prompts — this builds community ownership of the practice.
- When stuck on how to represent a concept, ask: "What story could I tell with this image?" A narrative frame (people on a journey, reaching a peak) is often more engaging than a static symbol.

## Best Used When
- Running visual thinking warm-ups at the start of workshops or team meetings.
- Coaching teams to develop a shared visual language for abstract concepts like goals, values, or strategy.
- Building psychological safety around drawing — especially with people who resist "non-artistic" visual work.
- Practicing rapid ideation and decision-making under time pressure.
