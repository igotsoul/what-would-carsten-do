# "Nonviolent Communication" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten introduces Nonviolent Communication (NVC), a four-step framework developed by Marshall Rosenberg for navigating tense or conflicting situations. The model helps speakers express what they observe, feel, and need — and make a clear request — without projecting blame or threatening the other party. It is especially powerful when taught to teams before conflict arises.

## Methods & Techniques

### Nonviolent Communication (NVC) — Four Steps (ab 00:30)
- **What:** A structured communication framework that keeps the focus on your own observations, feelings, and needs rather than projecting onto the other person, ending with a concrete, respectful request.
- **When to use:** In tense or conflicting situations; also proactively to equip teams with a shared language before conflict occurs.
- **Group size:** One-on-one or small groups; teachable to whole teams.
- **Duration:** A single NVC exchange can take minutes; teaching the model takes roughly 10–20 minutes.
- **How it works:**
  1. **Observation** — State what you perceive without judgment. *"What I hear you say is that you don't like X."* You are sharing your perception, not a universal truth.
  2. **Feeling** — Name the feeling that observation triggers in you. *"When I hear that, it makes me feel undervalued."* Do not project feelings onto the other person.
  3. **Need** — Express the underlying need behind that feeling. *"Because my need to feel seen is strong."* Own the need; do not impose it on the other person.
  4. **Request** — Make a specific, actionable request. *"Are you willing to try going to a coaching session with me?"* Serve it calmly and clearly.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=c_EUuhr5C-k&t=30s

## Key Principles
- Keep the ball in your own court — every step stays anchored in your own perception, feelings, and needs, never in accusations or assumptions about the other person.
- Reality is perception, not universal truth; NVC honours this by framing everything as "what I observe" rather than "what is."
- Separating observation, feeling, need, and request prevents emotional escalation and keeps conversation productive.
- NVC is also known as "giraffe language" — a metaphor Rosenberg used for speaking from the heart with a long-range perspective.
- The model is most effective when people have learned it *before* they find themselves in conflict, so the pattern is already familiar under stress.

## Facilitation Tips
- Carsten suggests introducing NVC to teams proactively, not just as a crisis tool — so you can later remind someone: *"Do you remember NVC? This might be a place to start."*
- In the heat of a conflict, it helps to mentally pause and recall the four steps rather than reacting instinctively.
- When coaching individuals through conflict, walk them through the four steps as a scripted structure first, then encourage internalisation over time.
- Point participants to Marshall Rosenberg's original material for deeper study — Carsten treats this video as a brief entry point only.

## Best Used When
- A team or pair is experiencing interpersonal tension or recurring conflict.
- A coach wants to give team members a shared communication protocol before tensions escalate.
- Preparing for a difficult conversation — with a manager, a colleague, or a stakeholder.
- Running conflict-resolution workshops or 1-on-1 coaching sessions.
