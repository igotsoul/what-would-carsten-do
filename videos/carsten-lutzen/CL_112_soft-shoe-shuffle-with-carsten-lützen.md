# "Soft Shoe Shuffle" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten Lützen introduces the Soft Shoe Shuffle, a physical group discussion technique for eight or more participants that uses spatial positioning to visualize agreement and disagreement with statements. Originally attributed to M. Lewis, it's a dynamic alternative to facilitator-predefined polling — letting the group's own perspectives emerge organically through movement and dialogue.

## Methods & Techniques

### Soft Shoe Shuffle (ab 00:20)
- **What:** A large-group technique where participants physically position themselves along a spectrum to show agreement or disagreement with spoken statements, then the most dissenting voice speaks next.
- **When to use:** When you want to explore a topic dynamically without pre-defining answer options; useful for retrospectives, product reviews, open space events, or any setting where surfacing diverse opinions matters.
- **Group size:** Minimum 8 participants; designed for larger crowds.
- **Duration:** Flexible — run 4–5 rounds or until perspectives feel exhausted; suitable for 20–45 minute segments.
- **How it works:**
  1. Set up an open space with enough floor room for people to spread out.
  2. The facilitator poses an open invitation or theme (e.g., "How do we think the product launch went?").
  3. Participants mill around until someone volunteers a statement or opinion.
  4. Everyone physically moves: those who agree stand close behind the speaker; those who disagree move to the opposite end of the room, creating a visible spectrum.
  5. The facilitator identifies the person standing furthest from the original speaker (most disagreement).
  6. That person speaks their perspective; the group realigns their positions accordingly.
  7. Repeat for 4–5 rounds or as long as fresh viewpoints emerge.
  8. Debrief the exercise at the end.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=o5r6vFbAXyA&t=20

### What? So What? Now What? Debrief (ab 02:35)
- **What:** A three-question debrief structure from Liberating Structures used to close the Soft Shoe Shuffle.
- **When to use:** After any experiential or observational group activity to draw out meaning and action.
- **How it works:**
  1. **What?** — What did you observe? What just happened?
  2. **So What?** — Why does that matter to you?
  3. **Now What?** — What can we do with this insight going forward?
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=o5r6vFbAXyA&t=155

## Key Principles
- Opinions should emerge from the group, not be predefined by the facilitator — the Soft Shoe Shuffle avoids anchoring participants to pre-set answer options.
- Physical movement makes agreement and disagreement visible at a glance, creating a shared picture without requiring everyone to speak.
- Amplifying the most dissenting voice (furthest-away person) keeps the conversation dynamic and ensures minority perspectives are heard.
- Participation is inclusive by design: those who don't want to speak can still express a position through where they stand.

## Facilitation Tips
- You need genuine floor space — don't attempt this in a cramped room or with chairs in the way.
- Enforce the "furthest away person speaks next" rule consistently; this is what keeps the dialogue from collapsing into groupthink.
- Carsten suggests running 4–5 rounds as a rough guide, stopping when no genuinely new perspectives are emerging.
- Close with What? So What? Now What? to translate the spatial, emotional experience into concrete insights and actions.

## Best Used When
- You're facilitating a retrospective, post-launch review, or any session where you want honest, distributed opinion.
- The group is large enough (8+) that a simple round-robin would be slow or dominated by a few voices.
- You want to avoid the facilitator inadvertently shaping outcomes by pre-defining response categories.
- Energy or engagement is low and you want to get people physically moving.

## Related Concepts
- **Liberating Structures** — the What? So What? Now What? debrief is a core Liberating Structure.
- **Open Space Technology** — the technique fits naturally within open space formats.
- **Spectrum / Line exercises** — similar in spirit to "take a stand" or "fist-to-five" voting, but fully emergent rather than response-to-prompt.
- **1-2-4-All** — another Liberating Structure for surfacing distributed perspectives in large groups.
