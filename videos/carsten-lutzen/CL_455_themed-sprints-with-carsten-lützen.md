# Themed Sprints with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten Lützen makes the case for treating the sprint goal as the primary guiding star of a sprint rather than obsessing over completing the entire sprint backlog. He explains how a clear, concise sprint goal helps teams prioritize when unexpected work appears, structure sprint reviews more effectively, and avoid the trap of team members picking up easy tasks instead of value-delivering ones.

## Methods & Techniques

### Sprint Goal as Guiding Star (ab 00:30)
- **What:** Instead of treating the sprint backlog as the primary commitment, define a clear sprint goal (2–3 short sentences) that captures what the team actually wants to achieve. This goal becomes the decision filter for the entire sprint.
- **When to use:** Every sprint, especially in teams that regularly have spillover or struggle with focus when unexpected work arrives.
- **Group size:** Full Scrum team
- **Duration:** Defined during sprint planning; active throughout the sprint
- **How it works:**
  1. During sprint planning, the PO and development team negotiate both the sprint backlog and a clear sprint goal.
  2. When team members are unsure what to pick up next, they look at the sprint goal and prioritize tasks that deliver toward it.
  3. When a disturbance (bug, urgent request) hits, the team fixes it and returns to sprint goal delivery rather than drifting to easy backlog items.
  4. During sprint review/demo, walk through the sprint goal first — demo and discuss everything related to each part of it before moving to remaining backlog items.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=G1We6HubVE8&t=30

### Themed Sprint Review Structure (ab 02:10)
- **What:** Structure the sprint demo/review around the sprint goal rather than a flat walkthrough of completed items. Go goal-part by goal-part, then handle remaining items afterward.
- **When to use:** Sprint reviews where the team struggles to tell a coherent story about what was accomplished.
- **Group size:** Full Scrum team + stakeholders
- **Duration:** Standard sprint review timebox
- **How it works:**
  1. Open the review by reading the sprint goal aloud.
  2. Demo and discuss all work related to the first part of the goal; confirm whether it was achieved.
  3. Repeat for each subsequent part of the goal.
  4. After the goal walk-through, cover any remaining backlog items that were completed.

## Key Principles
- A sprint goal gives the team a "guiding star" — a single answer to "what is the most important thing we deliver this sprint?"
- Teams are inherently optimistic when planning; a sprint goal helps them stay functional even when not everything gets done.
- Prioritization inside a sprint tends to slip because teams feel committed to the entire backlog — the sprint goal counteracts this drift.
- Unplanned work (bugs, dodgeballs) is inevitable; the sprint goal tells the team what to return to once the interruption is handled.

## Facilitation Tips
- Carsten recommends keeping the sprint goal to 2–3 short sentences — concise enough to be memorable and usable as a quick decision filter.
- Use the sprint goal actively during the sprint, not just at planning and review — post it visibly so team members consult it when choosing their next task.
- During the sprint review, lead with the sprint goal rather than the backlog; this reframes success around intent rather than throughput.

## Best Used When
- Teams frequently experience spillover and struggle to explain why
- Team members default to picking up easy or comfortable tasks rather than high-value ones
- Sprint reviews feel like a flat list of completed items rather than a coherent story
- Unexpected bugs or requests regularly derail focus mid-sprint
