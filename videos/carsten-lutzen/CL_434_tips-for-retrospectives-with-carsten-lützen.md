# Tips for Retrospectives with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten shares three practical tips for making retrospectives more solution-focused and engaging. The core problem he addresses is the antipattern of retros devolving into problem-pointing sessions without producing actionable solutions. Each tip is a small facilitation tweak that shifts energy and perspective without requiring a format change.

## Methods & Techniques

### Two Post-its at a Time + BINGO Clustering (ab 00:45)
- **What:** Instead of one person presenting all their post-its sequentially, each participant presents only their two most relevant post-its per round. When someone's unshared post-it matches one already presented, they shout "BINGO!" and place it on top, adding just one or two sentences.
- **When to use:** Any retro format with post-its (Starfish, Learning Matrix, etc.) where clustering and equal voice are needed.
- **Group size:** Works for typical Scrum team sizes (5–10 people)
- **Duration:** Replaces the normal readout phase; no significant time added
- **How it works:**
  1. Run the post-it writing phase as normal
  2. Go around the group — each person picks their two most fitting post-its and presents them
  3. Anyone with a similar post-it shouts "BINGO!", places theirs on top, and adds a sentence
  4. Repeat rounds until all post-its are presented
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=HYLgewB1Dkc&t=45s

### Write Your Neighbor's Thoughts (ab 01:50)
- **What:** Instead of writing your own reflections on the retro board, each participant writes what they think their neighbor would say — capturing the neighbor's perspective on what to keep, stop, or start.
- **When to use:** When a team tends to stay in their own silo or when you want to build empathy and cross-team awareness. Good as an occasional variation once the basic retro format is familiar.
- **Group size:** Any team size; works best when people know each other well enough to attempt the perspective-shift
- **Duration:** Similar to standard post-it writing phase; may need a minute or two extra
- **How it works:**
  1. Before writing, instruct everyone to write for their neighbor instead of themselves
  2. Allow space for participants to also add their own thoughts if there's something they strongly want to voice
  3. During readout, present as "Peter's thoughts" — then Peter responds, confirms, or adds nuance
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=HYLgewB1Dkc&t=110s

### External Viewpoint Prompt — Undercover Boss (ab 02:50)
- **What:** Ask the team to imagine an "undercover boss" or Product Owner walking in and observing the iteration. What would they see? This reframes reflections through an external stakeholder lens.
- **When to use:** When teams are too internally focused or stuck on the same recurring topics. Useful for shifting from blame/problem mode to outcome/solution mode.
- **Group size:** Any
- **Duration:** Can be used as a prompt within an existing format
- **How it works:** Pose the question as a framing before the post-it writing phase: "If our Product Owner walked in as undercover boss, what would they observe?"
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=HYLgewB1Dkc&t=170s

## Key Principles
- Retros that only surface problems without focusing on solutions lose their value — the antipattern to avoid is purely diagnostic retros
- Equal voice matters: rotating two-at-a-time presentation prevents one or two people from dominating the readout
- Perspective-taking exercises (neighbor's thoughts, stakeholder view) break habitual self-focused framing
- Small facilitation tweaks applied to familiar formats can significantly shift the energy without requiring a full format change

## Facilitation Tips
- When using BINGO clustering, explicitly coach the team that shouting "BINGO!" is encouraged — it signals alignment and speeds up clustering naturally
- For the neighbor exercise, remind participants they can still add their own post-it if something is important to them personally — this reduces the risk of important concerns being lost
- The neighbor exercise is "quite difficult" per Carsten — frame it as a fun challenge, not a test, to keep it psychologically safe

## Best Used When
- A team's retros feel repetitive or problem-focused without producing action
- You want to energize the readout phase and make it more interactive
- The team needs a perspective shift to see their work through a stakeholder's eyes
- You're running a Starfish, Learning Matrix, or similar post-it-heavy format
