# "The Bouncer" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten shares an insight from a fellow agile coach who previously worked as a bouncer: bring in a third party much sooner in conflicts. This reframes the common coaching tendency to wait until conflict is severe, and connects early intervention to solution-focused coaching techniques like the miracle question.

## Methods & Techniques

### Early Third-Party Intervention (ab 00:45)
- **What:** Introduce a neutral third party into a conflict earlier than feels natural — before the conflict has escalated to silence, hostility, or entrenched positions.
- **When to use:** As soon as early signals of interpersonal friction or non-constructive conflict appear within a team.
- **Group size:** Typically 2+ people in conflict
- **Duration:** Varies — the point is to intervene early, not late
- **How it works:**
  1. Notice early warning signs that something is starting to go wrong.
  2. Rather than waiting for the conflict to grow, proactively offer to step in as a facilitator or coach.
  3. Use the lower steps of the conflict staircase as the entry point — when people are still talking and open.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=KiD9c2NbIM4&t=45

### Solution-Focused Coaching for Early Conflict (ab 01:45)
- **What:** Apply solution-focused coaching questions — such as the miracle question — when conflict is still small, to help people recognize the issue and co-create a path forward.
- **When to use:** Early-stage conflict where people haven't yet hardened into positions.
- **Group size:** 1-on-1 or small group
- **Duration:** One focused coaching conversation
- **How it works:**
  1. Catch the conflict early, before it becomes a "big monster."
  2. Use coaching questions to help parties realize something is off.
  3. Ask questions like the miracle question to help them envision what a good resolution looks like.
  4. Guide them to define what change they want and how they'd recognize improvement.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=KiD9c2NbIM4&t=105

## Key Principles
- Bouncers are trained to de-escalate conflicts — their instinct to bring in support early is a professional skill worth borrowing.
- The conflict staircase describes how conflicts escalate in stages; most coaches intervene too late, high up on the staircase.
- Early intervention keeps the conflict workable — easier to dissolve before it becomes entrenched.
- Past experiences from unrelated jobs often carry underappreciated coaching wisdom.
- Proactivity is key: don't wait for the conflict to become undeniable — act on early signals.

## Facilitation Tips
- Ask yourself: "Am I waiting too long before stepping in?" Use the conflict staircase as a mental model to assess where a team is.
- When coaching teams, watch for subtle friction — withdrawal, passive language, avoidance — as early-stage conflict signals.
- Solution-focused questions work best before people have fully entrenched positions.
- Reflecting on your own non-coaching background may surface useful instincts or frameworks you've been underusing.

## Best Used When
- A team shows early signs of interpersonal conflict or tension that hasn't yet become explicit.
- You're coaching or facilitating and notice something starting to feel off between team members.
- You want a rationale for proactive conflict intervention rather than waiting to be asked.
