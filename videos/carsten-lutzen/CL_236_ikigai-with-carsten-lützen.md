# "IKIGAI" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten introduces the westernized version of Ikigai — a Japanese-origin concept adapted in the West as a four-circle Venn diagram for finding personal purpose. He walks through the four dimensions (what you love, what the world needs, what you can be paid for, and what you're good at) and the intersections between them. The video is intended to help individuals — and those coaching them — reflect on where sustainable fulfillment lies.

## Methods & Techniques

### Westernized Ikigai Reflection (ab 00:30)
- **What:** A four-circle Venn diagram framework that maps the intersection of what you love, what you're good at, what the world needs, and what you can be paid for — with the overlap of all four representing your Ikigai (purpose/sweet spot).
- **When to use:** In 1-on-1 coaching conversations, career reflection sessions, or team workshops focused on role alignment, motivation, and sustainable engagement.
- **Group size:** Works individually or in small groups; well-suited for 1-on-1 coaching.
- **Duration:** Not specified; works as a reflective exercise or extended conversation piece.
- **How it works:**
  1. Draw or present four overlapping circles labeled: *What you love*, *What the world needs*, *What you can be paid for*, *What you're good at*.
  2. Invite the person to place examples from their own life into each circle.
  3. Explore the intersections — each pairing has a named quality:
     - Love + Good at = **Passion**
     - Love + Paid for = **Hobby** (satisfaction, but possible feeling of uselessness if not world-needed)
     - Good at + Paid for + World needs (without love) = **Vocation** (excitement, but sense of uncertainty)
     - Love + World needs = **Mission**
     - All four = **Ikigai**
  4. Reflect on where the person currently sits — and where they want to be.

## Key Principles
- "All models are wrong, but some are useful" — Carsten explicitly frames Ikigai as an imperfect but practical tool, not a definitive truth.
- The westernized Ikigai is a simplification; the original Japanese concept is richer and worth exploring separately.
- Ikigai is not a fixed destination — it shifts as you develop, as your skills evolve, and as the world changes.
- "World needs" can be interpreted on any scale — global, community, family, or team. Carsten deliberately broadens the definition to make it more accessible and personally meaningful.
- Monetization alone doesn't equal purpose: pet projects that feel exciting may land in the "satisfaction but feeling of uselessness" zone if the world doesn't need them.
- Being good at something you don't love can create a sense of emptiness — skill without passion is not sustainable.

## Facilitation Tips
- Use personal examples to ground abstract circles — Carsten uses his own hobbies (Lego, drawing, watching movies) to make the intersections concrete and relatable.
- Don't let people rush to "find their Ikigai" — the value is in exploring each intersection, not just naming the final overlap.
- Normalize that most people are *not* fully in their Ikigai — the framework is diagnostic, not prescriptive.
- Invite reflection on *change over time* — help coachees see that a previous Ikigai may have shifted, and that's okay.
- If using with a team, the "what the world needs" circle can be reframed as "what our organization or customers need" to keep it professionally grounded.

## Best Used When
- A coachee is questioning career direction, job satisfaction, or sense of purpose.
- Someone feels stuck or burned out and needs a structured way to reflect on what energizes them.
- Onboarding new team members or revisiting role fit in a team context.
- As a conversation starter in a coaching session rather than a standalone exercise.
