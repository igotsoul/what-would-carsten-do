# "The Curse of Knowledge" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten introduces the Curse of Knowledge — a cognitive bias where experts unconsciously assume others share their level of understanding. He explains how this plays out in agile coaching contexts and offers practical strategies to counter it, primarily by staying curious, meeting people where they are, and building knowledge incrementally.

## Methods & Techniques

### Acknowledging the Curse Out Loud (ab 02:10)
- **What:** When you notice a communication breakdown, name the bias explicitly — say "Is this actually a case of the Curse of Knowledge?" — to create shared awareness and reset the conversation.
- **When to use:** Whenever communication feels ineffective, when you're coaching a new team, or when you sense a gap in understanding between yourself and others.
- **Group size:** Any (1-on-1 to team level)
- **Duration:** A moment of reflection; not a formal exercise
- **How it works:** Pause the conversation, name the bias, and use it as an invitation to recalibrate — check your assumptions, simplify your language, or change your approach entirely.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=UofeMYHah3g&t=130s

### Starting with the Basics (ab 02:25)
- **What:** Rather than unpacking your full toolkit at once, Carsten recommends starting from fundamentals and building the team's knowledge incrementally — for example using the Heart of Agile or a single retrospective as a first step.
- **When to use:** When introducing agile to a team with little or no prior experience; when a team feels overwhelmed or disengaged.
- **Group size:** Team-level
- **Duration:** Ongoing — a phased onboarding approach over multiple sprints or weeks
- **How it works:** 1. Resist the urge to introduce everything you know at once. 2. Start with a simple anchor (e.g., Heart of Agile's four words, or one retrospective). 3. Let the team experience and reflect. 4. Layer in additional practices as understanding grows. 5. Only introduce fine-tuning once a solid foundation is in place.

## Key Principles
- The Curse of Knowledge was first described in 1989 by Camerer, Loewenstein, and Weber in the field of economics, and has since been applied to education, programming, and coaching.
- Experts naturally — but unhelpfully — assume shared context that doesn't exist in the other person yet.
- "Steamrolling" a team with prescriptive process (even when well-intentioned) is a symptom of this bias; it leads to compliance rather than genuine adoption.
- Carsten frames it simply: leaving your ego at the door is the precondition for effective coaching.
- A team needs to make its own learnings along the way — the coach's job is to create the conditions for that, not to shortcut it.
- Having a name for the bias is itself powerful: it makes the invisible visible and creates a shared vocabulary for the coaching relationship.

## Facilitation Tips
- Before working with a new team, actively check your assumptions about what they already know.
- Use the Heart of Agile (Collaborate, Deliver, Reflect, Improve) as a minimal, accessible entry point for teams new to agile.
- A single retrospective can serve as the entire first intervention — it introduces the habit of reflection without requiring buy-in to a full framework.
- When you sense a knowledge mismatch — in either direction — name it. This normalizes the gap and opens a more honest conversation.
- Watch for the "wrong tools" signal: if something isn't landing, the issue may not be resistance but misalignment in assumed knowledge level.

## Best Used When
- Coaching or onboarding a team that is new to agile or to a specific practice
- Preparing to facilitate a group where you hold significantly more domain expertise than participants
- Debriefing a workshop or coaching session that felt ineffective — the Curse of Knowledge may explain why
- Training or mentoring junior coaches who tend to over-explain or over-prescribe

## Related Concepts
- **Heart of Agile** — Alistair Cockburn's distillation of agile into four words: Collaborate, Deliver, Reflect, Improve; referenced by Carsten as a beginner-friendly entry point
- **Beginner's Mind (Shoshin)** — Zen concept closely related; approaching familiar topics with openness and lack of preconception
- **Shu-Ha-Ri** — Agile learning model that maps well to the incremental knowledge-building approach Carsten advocates
- **Ego at the Door** — Recurring theme in Carsten's facilitation philosophy; prerequisite for effective coaching presence
