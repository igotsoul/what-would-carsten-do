# "The 90-9-1 Rule" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten Lützen introduces the 90-9-1 Rule — originally observed in online communities — and explores how it manifests in facilitated group settings. The video explains why most participants stay silent and offers concrete facilitation strategies to shift lurkers into active contributors. Carsten frames this as a core challenge and responsibility for facilitators.

## Methods & Techniques

### 1-2-4-All (ab 04:30)
- **What:** A Liberating Structures format where participants first reflect individually (1), then pair up (2), then work in groups of four (4), before sharing with the whole room (All).
- **When to use:** Any meeting or workshop where you want to dramatically increase participation and reduce the dominance of a vocal few.
- **Group size:** Any size
- **Duration:** Typically 12–15 minutes
- **How it works:** Each stage builds on the previous — solo thinking reduces groupthink, pairing creates psychological safety to test ideas, the group of four refines input, and the full group harvests the best insights.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=ldWqoYiPYBg&t=270

### Inviting Diverse Perspectives (ab 03:00)
- **What:** As facilitator, explicitly ask for different or contrasting views rather than seeking consensus or "correct" answers.
- **When to use:** Whenever a group appears to be converging too quickly or when quieter participants are not contributing.
- **How it works:** Frame questions as "Do we have any diverse perspectives?" or "Are there other views in the group?" to signal that disagreement and difference are welcome. Avoid asking for "right" answers.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=ldWqoYiPYBg&t=180

### Appreciating Half-Baked Ideas (ab 03:30)
- **What:** Actively encourage and visibly appreciate contributions that are incomplete, uncertain, or off-topic — not just polished input.
- **When to use:** When participants are hesitant to speak up, especially in environments with strong hierarchy or past experiences of being dismissed.
- **How it works:** Acknowledge every contribution positively. If an idea is off-topic, thank the person warmly and redirect: "That's a really interesting insight — can I ask you to hold that and share it again when we reach that topic?"
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=ldWqoYiPYBg&t=210

## Key Principles
- The 90-9-1 Rule originates from online community research: 90% lurk, 9% occasionally participate, 1% drive most of the activity. Carsten observes a similar — though less extreme — pattern in facilitated meetings.
- The facilitator's job is to actively move lurkers toward participation, not to accept silence as a given.
- Four root causes of non-participation: **fear of judgment**, **hierarchy**, **cognitive effort**, and **past experiences** of being ignored or dismissed.
- Liberating Structures address the 90-9-1 problem structurally by distributing control — one of their five design principles — which reduces both hierarchical pressure and fear of judgment.
- Pair and small-group work allows participants to validate their ideas before exposing them to the full room, lowering the perceived risk of speaking up.
- A facilitator's tone and reaction to contributions shapes whether others will risk sharing. Consistently positive, curious acknowledgment builds a participation culture over time.

## Facilitation Tips
- Never ask for "right" answers — ask for "diverse" perspectives. The framing signals psychological safety.
- When someone shares something off-topic or incorrect, thank them genuinely before redirecting. Don't let any contribution feel like a mistake.
- Use structured formats (like 1-2-4-All) rather than open discussion to give quieter participants a protected on-ramp into conversation.
- Be conscious of who is in the room — the presence of senior leaders (VP, director) raises the psychological stakes for everyone else. Design accordingly.
- Encourage explicitly: tell the group that half-baked ideas are welcome. Say it out loud.

## Best Used When
- Designing meetings or workshops where participation is historically low or uneven
- Working with groups that have strong hierarchical culture
- Diagnosing why a team or community seems disengaged
- Choosing between facilitation formats and wanting to understand the underlying rationale for more structured approaches
- Coaching other facilitators on how to increase engagement
