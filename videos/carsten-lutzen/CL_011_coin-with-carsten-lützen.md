# "COIN" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten Lützen introduces the COIN model — a four-step framework for giving safer, more constructive feedback. Originally introduced by Anna Carroll, COIN helps structure feedback so it is grounded in observable facts rather than feelings, making it easier to deliver tough messages without triggering defensiveness. Carsten also covers how to use the model when *receiving* feedback.

## Methods & Techniques

### COIN Feedback Model (ab 01:00)
- **What:** A four-step acronym framework for giving structured, constructive feedback: **C**ontext, **O**bservation, **I**mpact, **N**ext steps.
- **When to use:** Any time you need to deliver feedback — especially difficult or challenging feedback — to an individual or group in a way that feels safer and more credible.
- **Group size:** Any — works one-on-one or with a team.
- **Duration:** A single feedback exchange can take under a minute if kept concise.
- **How it works:**
  1. **Context** — Frame the specific situation the feedback refers to. Make it time-bounded and clear (e.g., "In the last 10 minutes..."). Avoids vague praise or criticism that can't be acted on.
  2. **Observation** — State only what you *observed* — what you saw or heard, not what you felt or interpreted. Others could in principle have noticed the same thing (e.g., "I noticed only two people spoke").
  3. **Impact** — Share the potential impact of that observation without asserting it as absolute truth (e.g., "That may mean we are missing perspectives"). Own the impact statement; don't project it onto others.
  4. **Next steps** — Invite rather than command. Pose a question or suggestion that opens collaboration (e.g., "Would someone else like to pitch in?").
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=94FCMrsxwH8&t=60

### Using COIN When Receiving Feedback (ab 04:45)
- **What:** Actively listen for the four COIN elements when receiving feedback, and ask for missing pieces to make the feedback actionable.
- **When to use:** When feedback you receive feels vague, generic, or hard to act on.
- **How it works:** If context, observation, impact, or next steps are missing, ask specifically for them (e.g., "You said I was super awesome — where did you see that?"). This turns vague feedback into something concrete and usable.

## Key Principles
- The goal of COIN is not to make feedback *nicer*, but to make it *safer* — including for tough messages.
- Observations differ from feelings: an observation is something others could also have noticed; a feeling is subjective and harder to act on. Keeping feedback in the observation register reduces defensiveness.
- Don't follow the four steps mechanically — robotic delivery undermines authenticity. Use COIN as a mental model, not a script.
- Keeping the impact statement on "your half of the court" (owning it as your interpretation rather than a fact) makes it less likely to trigger pushback.
- Cultural differences affect how feedback is received; a structured model like COIN provides a safer shared container across different backgrounds.
- Credit: the COIN model was introduced by **Anna Carroll**.

## Facilitation Tips
- When using COIN in a group setting, frame the next-steps element as an open invitation to participate, not a directive — this preserves psychological safety.
- If a feedback exchange feels stuck, diagnose which COIN element is missing and ask about it explicitly.
- Remind participants not to over-engineer the model in live conversations — internalise the structure, then let it flow naturally.

## Best Used When
- Delivering critical or corrective feedback to a team member or group where directness risks defensiveness.
- Facilitating team retrospectives where observations about group behavior need to be raised constructively.
- Coaching someone who is struggling to give or receive feedback effectively.
- Onboarding teams to a shared feedback language.
