# "To Estimate or not to Estimate" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten Lützen, agile coach and Scrum Master, shares his practical view on when estimation adds value and when it doesn't. He argues that using estimation for velocity or planning is counterproductive and that statistical flow metrics are a far better alternative. The only place Carsten sees genuine value in estimation is as a conversation starter for team alignment.

## Methods & Techniques

### Flow Metrics & Statistical Analysis as a Planning Tool (ab 01:30)
- **What:** Instead of estimating to derive velocity, Carsten recommends using historical flow data — average lead time, standard deviation, and bottleneck analysis — as the basis for planning and forecasting.
- **When to use:** When teams need a reliable input for capacity planning, sprint forecasting, or release planning without the distortions that come from point-based estimation.
- **Group size:** Team level
- **Duration:** Ongoing; data is collected continuously from the board
- **How it works:**
  1. Record when each work item moves into "To Do"
  2. Record when it moves to "Done"
  3. Use any digital tool (or pen-and-paper) to derive average lead time and standard deviation
  4. Identify patterns and bottlenecks in the flow
  5. Use these statistics — not a velocity number — as the input for planning conversations
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=olKxIFRLm_c&t=90

### Estimation as a Discussion and Alignment Tool (ab 03:00)
- **What:** Using estimation not to produce a number, but to surface differing assumptions within the team about complexity and scope — then using that conversation to reach shared understanding.
- **When to use:** When a team needs to check that everyone has the same mental model of a task before committing to it. Useful in backlog refinement or sprint planning.
- **Group size:** Team level (typically 3–9 people)
- **Duration:** Short; the estimate itself is not the goal — the discussion that follows is
- **How it works:**
  1. Present a backlog item
  2. Ask team members to estimate (e.g., with planning poker or similar)
  3. Use diverging estimates as a trigger for discussion: "You see this as trivial — why? You see this as huge — what are you seeing that others aren't?"
  4. Aim for alignment on what needs to be done and what can be skipped or simplified
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=olKxIFRLm_c&t=180

## Key Principles
- Estimating for velocity is a trap: team members will — consciously or unconsciously — game the system, inflating estimates to appear more productive, which defeats the purpose entirely.
- Velocity is not the goal; understanding flow and predictability is. Statistics give you that without the gaming risk.
- The control chart is Carsten's recommended tool for visualising flow data and supporting planning (he references a separate dedicated video on this).
- Estimation has one legitimate use: as a catalyst for conversation and alignment, not as a measurement artifact in itself.
- "Don't make it about velocity — that's not really interesting." The real interest lies in lead time patterns, standard deviation, and bottlenecks.

## Facilitation Tips
- When coaching teams who want to introduce estimation for planning purposes, redirect them toward flow metrics first and explain the gaming risk explicitly.
- If a team insists on estimating, reframe the exercise: make it clear the number is disposable and the discussion is the output.
- Carsten suggests that even a simple pen-and-paper log of "when did we start / when did we finish" is sufficient to generate meaningful planning statistics — no special tooling required.

## Best Used When
- A Scrum Master or agile coach is being asked "should we estimate?" and needs a clear, practical framework for answering.
- A team is debating the value of story points or planning poker and needs to distinguish between estimation-for-planning and estimation-for-alignment.
- Preparing a coaching conversation or team session about flow, predictability, or sprint planning approaches.
