# "Statues" by Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten introduces "Statues," a physical energizer adapted from improv theater, originally learned from Andy Nelson. In this activity, participants collaboratively build living sculptures by striking poses together, while a third person names the resulting tableau. It is a quick, playful warm-up designed to energize a group and encourage creativity.

## Methods & Techniques

### Statues Energizer (ab 00:10)
- **What:** A collaborative improv exercise where participants take turns striking poses to form a group sculpture, which another participant names before swapping someone out and repeating.
- **When to use:** As an energizer or warm-up at the start of a workshop, retrospective, or team session — especially when energy is low or the group needs to loosen up.
- **Group size:** Up to 8–10 people actively participating; for larger groups, split into smaller groups or have one group perform while others watch as an audience.
- **Duration:** Short — suitable as a 5–10 minute energizer.
- **How it works:**
  1. One person strikes a pose.
  2. A second person looks at the pose, decides how to complement it, and joins by striking a related pose.
  3. A third person observes the two-person sculpture, gives it a creative name (e.g., "boxing in a nightclub").
  4. The third person taps out the person who has been posing the longest.
  5. The tapped-out person leaves, and the third person now strikes a new pose alongside the remaining person.
  6. A new third person observes, names the new sculpture, taps out the longest-standing poser, and the cycle continues.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=p0qznLcKtU0

## Key Principles
- The more playful and "wacky" participants allow themselves to be, the more fun and creative the names become — Carsten explicitly encourages going all over the place.
- Watching the exercise as an audience is also enjoyable, making it work well even for larger groups where not everyone can actively participate simultaneously.
- The exercise builds on improv theater principles: "yes, and" thinking, physical presence, and spontaneous creativity.

## Facilitation Tips
- Encourage participants to let go of self-consciousness and embrace silly or exaggerated poses — the energy of the room depends on it.
- For groups larger than 8–10, split into sub-groups or designate one group as performers and the rest as audience; both roles are engaging.
- Keep the pace moving — the tap-out mechanic ensures no one is stuck in a pose too long and maintains the flow.

## Best Used When
- Opening a workshop or retrospective and the group needs a physical, low-stakes warm-up.
- Working with a team that is overly serious or reserved and needs permission to be playful.
- You want an energizer that requires no materials, no prep, and no prior explanation beyond a quick demonstration.
