# "Birthday Song" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten shares a simple, low-prep energizer that emerged organically during a multi-day workshop: singing Happy Birthday to the group member with the next upcoming birthday. The activity doubles as a re-entry ritual after breaks and a natural catalyst for name-learning and personal connection within the group.

## Methods & Techniques

### Birthday Song Energizer (ab 00:30)
- **What:** Each time the group returns from a break, they sing Happy Birthday to the participant whose birthday is coming up soonest — working through the group in birthday order across the day's breaks.
- **When to use:** As a re-entry activity after breaks in multi-day workshops, especially early in a program when participants don't yet know each other well.
- **Group size:** Works for small to medium workshop groups.
- **Duration:** 1–2 minutes per break re-entry.
- **How it works:**
  1. Before or at the start of the workshop, find out participants' upcoming birthdays.
  2. After the first break, the group sings Happy Birthday to the person whose birthday falls soonest.
  3. After each subsequent break, sing to the person with the next nearest birthday.
  4. Continue through the day's breaks, working through the birthday order.

## Key Principles
- Simple, even trivial-seeming activities can create genuine connection if they give people something personal to engage with.
- Re-entry rituals after breaks help groups transition back into focus together rather than drifting back individually.
- Name-learning happens naturally when names are used in a meaningful, emotionally warm context (singing someone's name sticks better than an introduction round).
- Shared personal trivia (birthdays, proximity to family birthdays) opens low-stakes conversation and builds familiarity.

## Facilitation Tips
- Carsten notes this activity emerged spontaneously after the group realised they had missed a participant's birthday on day one — don't be afraid to improvise and turn a small mishap into an activity.
- The activity works best when the facilitator frames it lightly and playfully — it should feel like a moment of fun, not a forced ritual.
- Knowing who is "up next" gives the group a small ongoing thread of anticipation across breaks, which subtly builds group cohesion.

## Best Used When
- Running multi-day workshops where participants need to learn each other's names.
- You want a warm, low-effort re-entry activity that doesn't eat into content time.
- The group energy dips after breaks and needs a light, human moment to reconnect.
- You've discovered mid-workshop that someone had a birthday and want to acknowledge it retroactively.
