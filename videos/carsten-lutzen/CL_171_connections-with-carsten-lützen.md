# "Connections" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten introduces a simple icebreaker exercise called "Connections," originally shared with him by colleagues Laura and David. The exercise uses a ball of yarn (or a virtual whiteboard) to visually map what participants have in common around a shared theme, helping groups build rapport before diving into deeper workshop content.

## Methods & Techniques

### Connections (ab 00:30)
- **What:** A yarn-based icebreaker where participants discover and visualize what they have in common, building a physical or virtual "web of connections" across the group.
- **When to use:** At the start of a workshop or team session to warm up the group, build rapport, and create a sense of shared identity before moving into heavier content.
- **Group size:** Works best in smaller groups of 4–6; larger groups become more chaotic but still functional.
- **Duration:** Open-ended — run until everyone is connected or until the timer runs out.
- **How it works:**
  1. Choose a light, accessible theme (e.g., favorite movie, childhood toy, hobbies, current work tasks).
  2. **In person:** Have participants stand in a circle. One person holds the ball of yarn and shares something related to the theme. Anyone who feels a connection (to the topic, a detail, or something mentioned) shouts "Connection!" The first speaker holds their end of the yarn and throws the ball to the connected person.
  3. The next person shares, the process repeats, and the yarn gradually forms a web across the group.
  4. **Virtually:** Replicate on a virtual whiteboard with participant names; draw lines between names when a connection is called.
  5. Continue until everyone is connected or time runs out.
- **Facilitation note:** Carsten recommends making it *mandatory* to call out "Connection!" — otherwise quieter or more reserved participants may wait passively and never get included.

## Key Principles
- Connections form naturally when people talk about personal, low-stakes topics — the facilitator's job is just to create the conditions.
- Making participation mandatory (shouting "Connection!") ensures no one is accidentally left out of the web.
- The visual artifact (the yarn web or drawn lines) makes the group's interconnectedness tangible and memorable.
- Choosing a theme that fits the rest of the workshop creates a natural bridge from warm-up into deeper work.

## Facilitation Tips
- Pick a theme that is easy and inclusive — favorite toys, movies, or music lower the barrier to participation.
- Explicitly instruct participants that they are *obliged* to call "Connection!" when they feel one, to prevent passive observers from staying unconnected.
- Let the exercise run until everyone has been connected at least once, unless time pressure requires cutting it short.
- In larger groups, embrace the chaos — the messy web is part of the point.

## Best Used When
- Opening a workshop or team event and wanting to quickly build psychological safety.
- Working with a group that doesn't know each other well (new teams, cross-functional groups, external participants).
- Bridging a warm-up into a workshop topic by choosing a theme connected to the day's content.
- Running both in-person and remote/virtual sessions.
