# "Purpose to Practice" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten introduces the Liberating Structure "Purpose to Practice," a five-step framework for kicking off new initiatives. The structure helps teams align upfront on the essential questions before starting work, saving time and friction later. It is designed to be lightweight — typically one to two hours — and iterative.

## Methods & Techniques

### Purpose to Practice (ab 00:20)
- **What:** A Liberating Structure that guides a group through five sequential alignment steps when starting a new initiative, project, or long-running effort.
- **When to use:** At the kickoff of any new initiative, project, or team — especially when there is risk of misalignment on goals, roles, or ways of working.
- **Group size:** Flexible — can work with a small core team or a broader audience; the "Participants" step itself helps clarify who should be involved.
- **Duration:** One to two hours for an in-depth run-through.
- **How it works:**
  1. **Purpose** — Define why you are doing this. What rallies the group together?
  2. **Principles** — Agree on how you will work together. What values or norms guide the effort?
  3. **Participants** — Clarify who contributes, who is consulted, and who is responsible. Core team vs. broader audience?
  4. **Structure** — Design how the work is set up and how progress will happen.
  5. **Practices** — Define what specific practices will be applied (e.g., how written artifacts are produced, what rituals are used).

## Key Principles
- Alignment upfront is easier than resolving misalignment mid-flight — visiting these questions once at the start pays dividends throughout the initiative.
- Outputs do not need to be comprehensive documents; even brief, shared written agreements are sufficient to create transparency.
- The framework is iterative — if the initial answers turn out to be wrong (e.g., wrong participants), you revisit and adjust with full transparency.
- Making implicit assumptions explicit (who is doing what, why, how) is the core value of the structure.

## Facilitation Tips
- Carsten emphasizes keeping outputs lightweight — not a "10-page report," but something written down that everyone agrees upon.
- Frame the session as the beginning of an iterative process, not a one-time definitive plan — this reduces pressure and improves participation.
- The structure works well as a standalone kickoff workshop or as an opening module within a longer session.

## Best Used When
- Starting a new team, project, or initiative that will run for an extended period.
- There is uncertainty or diversity of expectations among participants about purpose, roles, or working norms.
- A team has started work without explicit alignment and needs to pause and establish shared foundations.
