# "Role Model" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten explores the principle of role modeling as a core facilitation skill — the idea that facilitators must embody what they ask of participants rather than just instructing them. Drawing on examples from a recent co-facilitated workshop, he shows how "show, don't tell" builds trust and psychological safety, making it easier for groups to engage with unconventional or playful activities.

## Methods & Techniques

### Role Modeling Playful Energizers (ab 00:40)
- **What:** The facilitator actively participates in — and often leads by example — any energizer or unusual activity they introduce, rather than just explaining it from the sidelines.
- **When to use:** Whenever introducing an activity that feels unusual, physical, or potentially awkward for participants.
- **Group size:** Any
- **Duration:** Varies by activity
- **How it works:** Step into the activity yourself first. For example, Carsten demonstrates a singing energizer where participants sit/stand depending on a trigger word (e.g., "bunny"). By doing it himself, he lowers the threshold for others to join. The invitation is explicit: "I won't force, I won't demand — I will invite."
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=03RWve8Qhts&t=40s

### Plank or Physical Constraint During Sharing (ab 01:45)
- **What:** Participants (and facilitators) must hold a plank position or stand on one leg while sharing back, limiting lengthy contributions through mild physical discomfort and creating a light, fun atmosphere.
- **When to use:** When share-backs or standups tend to run long; when the group needs energy after a sedentary working session.
- **Group size:** Any
- **Duration:** Only for the duration of the sharing segment
- **How it works:** Announce the rule before share-back begins. Critically, the facilitator also holds the plank — modeling that the constraint applies to everyone equally. This signals time-consciousness and shared accountability without a timer or explicit pressure.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=03RWve8Qhts&t=105s

### Device Policy as Role Model (ab 02:50)
- **What:** If a workshop is designed to be analog (no phones, no laptops), the facilitator visibly limits their own device use — not just announces a rule.
- **When to use:** In workshops where full presence and analog work is the intent.
- **Group size:** Any
- **Duration:** Entire workshop
- **How it works:** Set the expectation explicitly, then visibly put your own phone away. If you need notes, minimize device use and acknowledge it transparently. The act of visibly choosing analog signals the same commitment you're asking of participants.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=03RWve8Qhts&t=170s

### Whispering at the Top of Your Lungs (ab 03:15)
- **What:** Facilitators openly voice their own uncertainty or dilemmas in the room — saying out loud "I'm not sure what to do next, what do you think?" — turning transparency into a trust-building act.
- **When to use:** When the facilitator faces a genuine in-the-moment decision; when the group is ready for a more participatory facilitation style.
- **Group size:** Any
- **Duration:** Momentary, as needed
- **How it works:** Instead of projecting false confidence when uncertain, name it publicly and invite the group's input. Carsten credits Barry and Christian from The Liberators for introducing him to this practice. The effect on participants: seeing facilitators be vulnerable models the same transparency the facilitator hopes to elicit from the group.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=03RWve8Qhts&t=195s

## Key Principles
- **Show, don't tell** is the central principle: if you ask it of others, do it yourself first.
- **Psychological safety is a prerequisite for playfulness** — you need to have built trust before inviting unusual or physical activities.
- **Invitation over instruction:** Frame activities as invitations ("I won't force, I won't demand"), which gives participants agency and reduces resistance.
- **Transparency begets transparency:** If you want participants to be open, model openness — including about your own uncertainty.
- **Punctuality as role modeling:** If you expect participants to be on time and ready, you must be on time and ready. Lateness as a facilitator undermines the expectation.
- **Don't abuse trust:** Once psychological safety is established, don't use it to run arbitrary or pointless activities. Every unusual element needs a genuine reason behind it.

## Facilitation Tips
- Before introducing any unconventional activity, ask yourself: can I do this myself, visibly, in the room?
- Co-facilitators should also model the behaviors — Carsten and his co-facilitator both held planks during the exercise, reinforcing the shared expectation.
- The act of physically demonstrating an energizer (rather than just explaining it) is often the difference between buy-in and reluctant compliance.
- Naming your own uncertainty out loud ("I'm not sure what to do here") can be more trust-building than performing confidence you don't have.
- Keep playful constraints purpose-driven: the plank isn't just fun — it's a gentle time-keeper and a shared physical experience.

## Best Used When
- A facilitator is working with a new or mixed group and needs to build trust quickly.
- Introducing energizers or physical activities that might feel silly to participants.
- Share-backs or standups are consistently running over time.
- Running analog workshops where device distraction is a risk.
- Co-facilitating — having two facilitators model behaviors together amplifies the signal.
