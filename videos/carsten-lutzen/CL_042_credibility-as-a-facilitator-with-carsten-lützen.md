# "Credibility as a Facilitator" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten Lützen outlines how facilitators can build credibility with new groups by grounding their practice in three pillars: competence, trust, and neutrality. He shares concrete behavioural tips — from vocal clarity and body language to radical transparency — that help participants feel safe, engaged, and guided rather than led.

## Methods & Techniques

### Three-Pillar Credibility Framework (ab 00:30)
- **What:** A mental model for building facilitator credibility based on competence, trust, and neutrality working together.
- **When to use:** Whenever working with a new or unfamiliar group; useful as a self-assessment lens before and after sessions.
- **Group size:** Any
- **Duration:** Ongoing practice, not a one-time technique
- **How it works:**
  1. **Competence** — demonstrate you have a plan and know how to pivot when needed.
  2. **Trust** — build it through transparency, follow-through, and consistency.
  3. **Neutrality** — actively resist pushing your own agenda; your role is to guide the process, not shape the outcome.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=r_hM1bNeJIs&t=30

### Best Hopes Opening (ab 01:30)
- **What:** Starting a session by asking participants what their best hopes are for the day, before diving into content.
- **When to use:** At the opening of any workshop or facilitated session, especially with groups that don't know the facilitator.
- **Group size:** Any
- **Duration:** 5–10 minutes
- **How it works:** Simply ask the room: "What are your best hopes for today?" Use responses to anchor the day's direction and signal that the session is participant-centred.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=r_hM1bNeJIs&t=90

### Primary + Alternative Structure Strings (ab 01:50)
- **What:** Planning not just one sequence of activities (primary string) but two or more alternative strings you can pivot to depending on how the conversation unfolds.
- **When to use:** When facilitating with Liberating Structures or any modular activity set; critical for sessions with uncertain or emergent content.
- **Group size:** Any
- **Duration:** Preparation technique, not a session activity
- **How it works:**
  1. Design your primary string of structures for the expected flow.
  2. Prepare one or two alternative strings for likely divergent directions.
  3. During the session, read the room and pivot without hesitation when the anticipated discussion shifts.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=r_hM1bNeJIs&t=110

### Progressive Energizer Warm-Up (ab 04:30)
- **What:** Getting people talking as quickly as possible using low-stakes prompts that gradually increase in relevance to the session topic.
- **When to use:** At the start of any workshop, especially in-person networking or mixed groups; effective for breaking social inhibition early.
- **Group size:** Any; works well in larger rooms
- **Duration:** 5–10 minutes
- **How it works:**
  1. Begin with silly, non-threatening prompts to get voices in the room.
  2. Progressively shift prompts toward session-relevant questions (e.g., "What do you expect from today?").
  3. The goal is not deep thinking — it is normalising speaking up in this particular group.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=r_hM1bNeJIs&t=270

### Transparent In-Room Navigation (ab 06:30)
- **What:** Making visible to participants the facilitator's real-time decision-making process — including uncertainty — rather than hiding it backstage.
- **When to use:** Whenever you are uncertain how to proceed, or when a fork in the road appears mid-session.
- **Group size:** Any
- **Duration:** As needed, moments within a session
- **How it works:**
  1. Name the situation openly: "I can see two potential paths forward here."
  2. Describe both options briefly.
  3. Use a lightweight check (e.g., energy sensing, dot voting, show of hands) to let the room steer.
  4. Carsten also models the practice from Liberating Structures facilitators Keith McCandless and Henri Lipmanowicz of "whispering at the top of their lungs" — having planning conversations in full view and earshot of participants.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=r_hM1bNeJIs&t=390

## Key Principles

- A facilitator is a guide, not an expert. The group owns the content; the facilitator owns the process.
- Confidence is visible and contagious — participants trust facilitators who appear to have a plan, even when improvising.
- Speaking slowly serves dual purposes: it signals calm authority and buys the facilitator processing time to formulate precise communication.
- Pausing after instructions is not wasted time — it gives introverts and extroverts alike space to process before responding.
- Getting people talking early is a strategic investment; the more quickly participants hear their own voice in the room, the more willing they are to contribute later.
- Transparency about your own uncertainty humanises you and builds trust, provided you still demonstrate agency and direction.
- Timekeeping is a competence signal — consistently running out of time erodes confidence in the facilitator.

## Facilitation Tips

- Speak slowly, especially when nervous. Speed is the enemy of clarity and perceived confidence.
- Use deliberate pauses — after questions, after instructions, after transitions.
- Monitor body language: avoid hiding or collapsing posture; aim for confident and open, not arrogant.
- Own your mistakes and uncertainty out loud rather than masking them; the room respects honesty.
- When the session plan needs to change, present options to the group and let them co-decide direction rather than unilaterally pivoting.
- Keep notes on your phone if needed — but tell participants upfront so checking your phone does not read as disengagement.
- After an activity or segment, periodically check: "Is this adding value for you?" Normalises feedback and signals participant-centredness.

## Best Used When

- Onboarding into a new team or client group where you have no established credibility yet.
- Designing a workshop opening and wanting to quickly establish psychological safety.
- Reflecting on your own facilitation practice and looking for a simple framework to self-assess.
- Training or mentoring new facilitators who feel intimidated by their first sessions.
- Running sessions with high ambiguity where the path forward cannot be fully pre-planned.

## Related Concepts

- **Best Hopes** (Solution-Focused practice) — the opening question technique draws from solution-focused coaching.
- **Energizers** — acknowledged as a contested topic in facilitation circles, but defended here as a tool for voice activation rather than entertainment.
- **Psychological safety** — the underlying goal of most tips described: creating conditions where participants feel safe to speak and contribute.
