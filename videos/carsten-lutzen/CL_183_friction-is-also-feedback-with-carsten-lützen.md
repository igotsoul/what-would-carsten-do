# "Friction is also Feedback" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten introduces the idea that friction — tension, disagreement, obstacles, and pushback — should not be dismissed as people being difficult, but treated as a valuable source of feedback and organizational signal. In this short video, he reframes resistance as information and offers a mindset shift for change agents: surface the friction, listen for what lies beneath it, and use it to better understand the reality of the people you are working with.

## Methods & Techniques

### Surfacing the Signal Beneath Friction (ab 00:45)
- **What:** A mindset and inquiry approach for treating resistance or pushback as meaningful feedback rather than noise to be suppressed or ignored.
- **When to use:** During change initiatives, process redesigns, or any situation where you encounter pushback, tension, or lack of alignment.
- **Group size:** Applicable in 1-on-1 conversations or team settings.
- **Duration:** Ongoing practice; not a time-boxed exercise.
- **How it works:**
  1. Notice friction — tension, disagreement, obstacles, or resistance — without immediately labeling it as negativity.
  2. Pause and get curious: ask why the person is pushing back.
  3. Ask open questions such as "How do you see this change initiative from your seat?"
  4. Reframe what you are hearing: what is their reality? What are they actually experiencing?
  5. Put yourself in their shoes — "perception is reality" — and ask how you can help them from where they stand.
  6. Use the insight as feedback to inform your approach.

## Key Principles
- Friction is one of the best sources of information for agents of change — it signals where real misalignment, fear, or unmet needs exist.
- The first layer of friction (people being "difficult") is rarely the full picture; the signal lies underneath it.
- Perception is reality: understanding someone's experienced reality is a prerequisite to helping them move.
- Curiosity, not judgment, is the right initial response to resistance.
- Lack of alignment is a common and legitimate root cause of friction — do not assume alignment was achieved just because it was communicated.

## Facilitation Tips
- When you sense tension in a room or a meeting, name it rather than push through it: "I notice there is some tension here — what can we do about it?"
- Resist the urge to write off resistance early; at minimum, remove the first layer before concluding someone is being obstructive.
- Use perspective-taking actively: once you understand their frame, you can adapt your message or approach rather than repeating the same argument louder.

## Best Used When
- You are leading or supporting an organizational change initiative and encountering pushback.
- A retrospective or workshop surfaces repeated disagreement or low engagement.
- As a coach or Scrum Master, you notice a team member or stakeholder resisting a process change.
- You want to build a habit of listening for organizational signals rather than managing friction away.
