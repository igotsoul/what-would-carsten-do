# Daily Scrum with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten shares his experiences with Daily Scrum anti-patterns and how to move toward a team-driven, goal-focused standup. He covers common dysfunctions — from standups that never happen to status-report standups and unfocused discussions — and offers concrete facilitation nudges to shift the team toward genuine synchronization and planning.

## Methods & Techniques

### Goal-Focused Standup Questions (ab 03:10)
- **What:** Replace the classic three standup questions with sprint-goal-oriented versions to keep the team focused on what actually matters for the current sprint.
- **When to use:** When a team runs standups that feel disconnected from the sprint or when individuals work on tasks outside the sprint goal without awareness.
- **Group size:** Scrum team (typically 3–9 people)
- **Duration:** Within the 15-minute timebox
- **How it works:**
  1. Replace "What did I do yesterday?" → "What did I do yesterday that helped us achieve our sprint goal?"
  2. Replace "What will I do today?" → "What will I do today that helps us achieve our sprint goal?"
  3. Keep "Is there anything blocking me?" → "Is there anything blocking us from achieving the sprint goal?"
  - If a team member has nothing that connects to the sprint goal, that gap becomes visible and worth addressing.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Tdg6MyDUmwo&t=190

### Subtle Redirect for Status-Report Standups (ab 01:55)
- **What:** A non-verbal facilitation nudge to break the pattern of team members reporting to the Scrum Master instead of talking to each other.
- **When to use:** When the team directs eye contact and speech at the Scrum Master rather than each other, treating the standup as an upward status report.
- **Group size:** Any Scrum team
- **Duration:** Immediate — applied in the moment
- **How it works:**
  1. Make eye contact with another team member to signal where attention should go.
  2. Physically move so the speaker can no longer easily look at you.
  3. Step behind someone else to break line of sight entirely.
  - The goal is that standups continue unchanged even if the Scrum Master walks out.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Tdg6MyDUmwo&t=115

### Parking-Lot for Side Discussions (ab 04:20)
- **What:** A technique to prevent the standup from drifting into bilateral deep-dives while still honouring the value of those conversations.
- **When to use:** When two or more team members start a detailed discussion during the standup that isn't relevant to the whole group.
- **Group size:** Any team
- **Duration:** Seconds to apply; deferred conversations happen after standup
- **How it works:**
  1. Note down the topic (whiteboard, sticky, or mental note) when a side conversation starts.
  2. Optionally, agree on a team rule: if two people raise their hand, the current topic is parked.
  3. After everyone has planned their day, call out the parked items explicitly: "Carsten, you need to check in with Preben about X; Peter, you need to sync with John about Y."
  4. The relevant people then have those conversations immediately after standup.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Tdg6MyDUmwo&t=260

### Rotating Standup Questions (ab 03:45)
- **What:** Periodically changing the standup questions to prevent staleness and re-engage the team.
- **When to use:** When the standup feels rote, energy is low, or the questions no longer generate useful insight.
- **Group size:** Any Scrum team
- **Duration:** Ongoing practice, not a one-time change
- **How it works:** Experiment with different questions that address the team's current needs. Let the team co-create the new questions so they feel ownership.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Tdg6MyDUmwo&t=230

## Key Principles
- The Daily Scrum is for the **team** to plan and synchronize — not a status report to the Scrum Master or stakeholders.
- If people stop attending, it's a signal the standup isn't delivering value — diagnose the experience, not the attendance.
- A healthy standup should be self-sustaining: Carsten's test is whether it continues unchanged if he walks away.
- Linking the three questions to the Sprint Goal makes individual work visible in relation to collective outcomes and surfaces misalignment early.
- Deep discussions during standup aren't inherently bad — they just don't belong *in* the standup. Park them, then make them happen *after*.

## Facilitation Tips
- Use physical positioning as a facilitation tool: move your body to redirect team attention without saying a word.
- Capture parked topics visibly (whiteboard) so people trust they won't be lost — this removes the urge to resolve them on the spot.
- Introduce a talking-stick rule for teams prone to cross-talk: only the person holding the stick may speak.
- Consider a team-agreed signal (e.g., two raised hands) that allows anyone — not just the facilitator — to park a drifting conversation.
- Change questions collaboratively with the team; frame it as "what questions would add the most value right now?" rather than imposing new ones.

## Best Used When
- Onboarding a new team to Daily Scrum or resetting a team with a dysfunctional standup habit.
- Coaching a Scrum Master who is inadvertently becoming the focal point of the standup.
- A team's standup has become a status ritual disconnected from the sprint goal.
- Sprint reviews or retrospectives reveal that the team lacks shared daily awareness of progress toward the sprint goal.
