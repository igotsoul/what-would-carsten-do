# Space with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten introduces the importance of intentional physical space setup for workshops and retrospectives. He argues that the right room, layout, and environment can actively support — or undermine — the exercises you have planned. The video offers a short but practical framework for thinking about venue and setup as a core facilitation decision.

## Methods & Techniques

### Agenda-First Space Planning (ab 00:10)
- **What:** Start with your desired outcome and agenda, then work backwards to select a room and layout that supports the exercises — not the other way around.
- **When to use:** During preparation for any workshop, retrospective, or meeting that involves interactive exercises.
- **Group size:** Any
- **Duration:** Preparation step, not a timed activity
- **How it works:**
  1. Define the outcome and choose exercises that support it.
  2. Identify what the exercises require physically (walking room, open floor, circle formation, etc.).
  3. Select a venue that matches those physical requirements.
  4. Plan the chair/table layout — or whether furniture is needed at all.
  5. Arrive early to set up and verify everything is in place.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=x9KNIZFNDgo&t=10

## Key Principles
- Physical space is a facilitation variable — it can support or work against the session.
- Exercise selection and venue selection are linked decisions, not independent ones.
- Small rooms with fixed large tables actively constrain interactive formats requiring movement.
- Some formats (Eye Contact exercise, Chaos Cocktail Party) specifically require open floor and standing-circle arrangements.
- Pre-meeting setup time is non-negotiable — arriving early to configure the room is part of facilitation.
- Environmental details like temperature and refreshments contribute to psychological safety and a sense of welcome.

## Facilitation Tips
- Carsten explicitly asks: "Do I even need tables and chairs?" — questioning default room configurations is a useful habit.
- Consider whether participants need two chairs per person (e.g., for paired exercises).
- For movement-heavy exercises, over-estimate the space you need rather than under-estimate.
- Treat room setup as part of the facilitation design, not an afterthought.

## Best Used When
- Planning any session that includes physical movement, pairing, or circle-based exercises.
- Facilitating in corporate environments where default room configurations (boardroom tables) conflict with participatory formats.
- Onboarding new facilitators who may not yet think about the physical dimension of meeting design.
