# "Best Hopes for Our Family - Part 2" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten Lützen shares a follow-up on applying the "Best Hopes" exercise with his own family at the start of the year. The video demonstrates the real-world result of the exercise — a visible wall of wishes, many completed — and makes the case for using facilitation techniques in personal life. It reinforces the value of visual tracking and ongoing conversation as a complement to the initial goal-setting session.

## Methods & Techniques

### Best Hopes Exercise — Family Edition (ab 00:10)
- **What:** A facilitated exercise where family members (adults and children) each articulate their wishes and hopes for the coming year, collected visually on a wall or board.
- **When to use:** Start of a new year or any natural transition point where a family (or team) wants to align on shared and individual aspirations.
- **Group size:** Family unit — works with mixed ages including children.
- **Duration:** Not specified for the session itself; the follow-up spans the full year.
- **How it works:**
  1. Run the Best Hopes exercise (covered in Part 1) to collect wishes from all family members.
  2. Place wishes visibly on a wall — uncompleted items remain posted.
  3. Throughout the year, revisit the wall regularly and move completed wishes to a "done" area.
  4. Celebrate visibly when items move from wishes to done.
  5. At year end, review what was achieved and re-run the exercise for the next year.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=ezIAdZzsDik

## Key Principles
- **Visibility drives focus:** Keeping wishes on a physical wall ensures the family continuously sees and talks about what they want to achieve — not just at the moment of setting goals.
- **Conversation is the outcome:** Carsten notes that one of the most valuable results was the amount of time the family spent simply talking about their hopes throughout the year.
- **Goals are not pass/fail:** Expecting that some wishes won't be met is healthy and realistic. The value is in the direction and focus, not 100% completion.
- **Pride as a motivator:** Moving items from "wish" to "done" creates a visible moment of achievement that Carsten describes as genuinely motivating — even for children.
- **Eat your own cooking:** Carsten explicitly frames this as a "take your own dog food" practice — facilitators and coaches should use the methods they advocate professionally in their own lives.

## Facilitation Tips
- Involve children as full participants, not just observers — their wishes belong on the wall alongside adults'.
- Make the wall a living artifact, not a one-time output. Schedule lightweight check-ins across the year to ask: "Are we getting closer to what we want?"
- Don't take down the wall at year end immediately — use it as input for the next round. The unfinished items are useful data.
- The ritual of physically moving a card from "wish" to "done" carries emotional weight — preserve that moment rather than just updating a digital tracker.

## Best Used When
- Facilitators or coaches want to practice goal-setting and visual management methods in a personal context before using them professionally.
- A family or close-knit group wants a lightweight shared planning ritual that stays alive beyond a single conversation.
- You want to make individual and collective hopes visible and revisitable throughout the year.
