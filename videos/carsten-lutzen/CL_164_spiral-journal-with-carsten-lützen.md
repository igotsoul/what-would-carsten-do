# "Spiral Journal" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten introduces Spiral Journal, an unofficial Liberating Structure originally shared by The Liberators (Barry and Christian), designed to create quiet, individual reflection time during longer workshops. The exercise combines a meditative spiral-drawing activity with structured journaling prompts, helping participants — especially those who need internal processing time — consolidate what they have experienced before sharing with the group.

## Methods & Techniques

### Spiral Journal (ab 00:45)
- **What:** A quiet reflection exercise where participants first draw a slow, deliberate spiral, then respond to four written prompts in silence before optional group sharing.
- **When to use:** Mid- or end-of-workshop when participants need a break from external stimuli and time to process internally; especially valuable after intensive Liberating Structures sequences where talking has dominated.
- **Group size:** Any size — individual activity done in parallel.
- **Duration:** Approximately 10–12 minutes total (1 min setup, 2 min spiral, 5 min writing, 5 min sharing).
- **How it works:**
  1. **Setup (~1 min):** Each participant takes a sheet of paper and divides it into four quadrants.
  2. **Spiral drawing (~2 min):** In silence, participants draw a single spiral starting from the center of the page, making as many consistent loops as possible within the two minutes. The goal is slow focus, not speed or perfection.
  3. **Reflection prompts (~5 min):** Participants write individually and silently in each quadrant:
     - Quadrant 1: *What have I noticed so far in this workshop/meeting?*
     - Quadrant 2: *What have I felt in this session?*
     - Quadrant 3: *What question is emerging for me?*
     - Quadrant 4: *One bold thing I can do based on what I have experienced.*
  4. **Sharing (~5 min):** Popcorn-style voluntary sharing — no one is forced to share. Invite people to speak to what they noticed, felt, or want to do.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=o5VrLwm_Zos&t=45

## Key Principles
- The spiral-drawing step is not decorative — it serves as a focusing ritual that transitions participants from external activity to internal attention.
- Liberating Structures aim to maximize participant talking time, which means introverts and internal processors are easily overlooked; Spiral Journal deliberately addresses this gap.
- Role modeling matters: Carsten emphasizes that the facilitator should draw the spiral alongside participants, as people will look up to see if they are doing it "wrong."
- The questions are invitations, not interrogations — sharing is always voluntary.
- Sets of prompts are interchangeable; facilitators are encouraged to develop their own question sets suited to their context.

## Facilitation Tips
- Do the spiral yourself. Participants will glance around for reassurance; seeing the facilitator engaged normalizes the exercise.
- Remind participants explicitly that this is not a race — the spiral is about sustained focus, not coverage.
- Allow slightly more than one minute per quadrant during the writing phase (five minutes across four questions).
- Keep the sharing phase popcorn-style and unhurried. Do not cold-call participants.
- Consider using The Liberators' facilitation board for additional question sets and a fuller description of the method.

## Best Used When
- A longer workshop has been running and participants need to step back from group activity and process individually.
- The group includes people who process better in quiet and writing than in verbal discussion.
- You want to surface individual observations, emotions, and intentions before a closing or decision-making moment.
- Transitions between major workshop segments where reflection would add value.
