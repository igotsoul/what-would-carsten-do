# Mix & Match with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten argues against adopting large enterprise Agile frameworks as a silver bullet and instead advocates for a "mix & match" approach: teams and organizations should survey available frameworks (SAFe, LeSS, Scrum@Scale, Scrum of Scrums), pick elements that fit their specific context, and validate each element through deliberate experimentation. The core insight is that the Agile Manifesto's emphasis on people and interactions over processes and tools means your process should be tailored — not off-the-shelf.

## Methods & Techniques

### Mix & Match Process Design (ab 01:00)
- **What:** Instead of wholesale adopting a large framework, survey multiple frameworks and deliberately select elements that fit your specific context, then assemble your own hybrid process.
- **When to use:** When an organization is starting or scaling an Agile transformation and feels pressure to adopt SAFe, LeSS, or another enterprise framework wholesale.
- **Group size:** Organizational / team leadership level
- **Duration:** Ongoing — treated as a continuous improvement cycle, not a one-time decision
- **How it works:**
  1. Survey available frameworks: Scrum@Scale, LeSS, SAFe, Scrum of Scrums, etc.
  2. Identify elements that seem promising for your specific context.
  3. For each element, form a hypothesis: "Why would this give us value?"
  4. Design a small experiment to test the hypothesis.
  5. If the element delivers value, keep it. If not, replace it with something that achieves the same goal differently.
  6. Repeat — treat your process as a living system.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=J2b-a-oHDtI&t=60

### De-Scaling (ab 00:45)
- **What:** Rather than adding process complexity to handle growth, actively simplify — ask "how can we make our process simpler and more effective?"
- **When to use:** When a growing organization's instinct is to add more structure, governance, or ceremony. Counter-pressure to the pull toward big frameworks.
- **Group size:** Organizational / leadership
- **Duration:** Ongoing mindset, not a single exercise
- **How it works:** Regularly challenge each process element: does this add value? If not, remove or replace it before adding anything new.

## Key Principles
- **Silver bullets don't exist.** Large enterprise frameworks marketed as universal solutions rarely match the reality of a specific organization's context.
- **The Agile Manifesto is the anchor.** "People and interactions over processes and tools" means any framework should serve the people, not the other way around. If a process element isn't giving value, don't do it.
- **Hypothesis-driven process design.** Every adopted practice should have an explicit reason ("why do we believe this will help us?") and a way to verify or falsify that belief.
- **Failure is data, not defeat.** Carsten frames experimentation failures as opportunities to model organizational learning — showing others "we dare to fail and learn from our mistakes."
- **Even Scrum is a starting point, not a constraint.** Carsten acknowledges the Scrum Guide's strict definition exists to prevent Scrum-in-name-only, but still encourages teams to be playful within Scrum and drop elements that don't deliver value.
- **Learning is the key differentiator.** A learning-oriented approach to process design will outperform fixed framework adoption in the long run.

## Facilitation Tips
- When coaching organizations toward Agile, Carsten suggests reframing the question from "which framework should we adopt?" to "what elements do we want to experiment with and why?"
- Normalize failure explicitly: tell teams upfront that most experiments won't work the first time, and that this is expected and acceptable.
- Encourage teams to document their hypotheses before starting an experiment — this makes retrospective evaluation of whether it "worked" much cleaner.
- Don't let perfect be the enemy of good: staying vigilant and iterating beats waiting for the right framework.

## Best Used When
- An organization is under pressure to "go Agile" and defaulting toward expensive, certification-heavy framework adoption.
- A Scrum Master or Agile Coach is pushing back on top-down framework mandates and needs a principled alternative narrative.
- A team feels stuck in ceremonies that don't add value but fears abandoning them because "that's what the framework says."
- Preparing a coaching conversation or workshop about Agile transformation strategy with leadership.
