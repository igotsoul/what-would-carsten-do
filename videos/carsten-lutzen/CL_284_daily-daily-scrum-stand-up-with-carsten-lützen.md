# "Daily / Daily Scrum / Stand-up / ..." with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten challenges the assumption that the Daily Scrum must follow the three classic questions (yesterday / today / blockers), pointing to the Scrum Guide's actual wording which allows any format. He shares two alternative structures for larger teams where the standard format breaks down within the 15-minute timebox.

## Methods & Techniques

### Flag & Discuss (Board-First Daily) (ab 01:30)
- **What:** Before the stand-up begins, team members mark or flag the items on the board they want to discuss. The team then talks through the flagged (high-priority) items first, and only after those are covered does anyone who wants to share something additional get the floor.
- **When to use:** When the team is too large for everyone to give a two-minute update within 15 minutes, and when there are clear hot topics that need group attention.
- **Group size:** Larger Scrum teams
- **Duration:** Within the standard 15-minute timebox
- **How it works:**
  1. At the start of the daily, team members flag or mark items on the board (physical or digital) they want to raise.
  2. Work through the flagged items as a group.
  3. Once high-priority items are covered, open the floor — randomised order or raise-a-hand — for anything else anyone wants to share.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=z5ZG8EuU95w&t=90

### 1-2-All Daily Format (ab 02:15)
- **What:** A Daily Scrum structured using the 1-2-All (or 1-2-4-All) pattern: individuals first reflect on their own plan, then share in pairs or small groups, then the small groups report to the whole team.
- **When to use:** Larger teams where one-at-a-time updates are slow and passive; when you want more simultaneous engagement with the board and content.
- **Group size:** Larger teams where sequential updates become a bottleneck
- **Duration:** Within the 15-minute timebox
- **How it works:**
  1. **Individual (1):** Each person silently considers their plan for the day.
  2. **Pairs (2):** Share and align in pairs.
  3. **Small groups (4):** Pairs join into groups of four and consolidate.
  4. **All:** Groups share key points with the whole team.
  5. Result: a collective plan of who does what, arrived at in parallel rather than sequentially.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=z5ZG8EuU95w&t=135

## Key Principles
- The Scrum Guide does not mandate the three classic questions — it says developers can use whatever structure and techniques they want, as long as they produce an actionable plan for the next day's work.
- The Daily is best thought of as a **mini planning session** for the work until the next stand-up, not a status report.
- Carsten strongly prefers keeping teams together as one unit rather than splitting into artificial sub-teams when the format gets difficult.
- Parallelising participation (e.g. via 1-2-All) means more people interact with the board and content simultaneously, reducing the passive "status report" feel.

## Facilitation Tips
- End the Daily with a dad joke. Carsten's reasoning: people leave either groaning or laughing — either way, it's a better ending than a flat close.
- Avoid the trap of the daily becoming a status report; the goal is a shared, actionable plan.
- Experiment with format when the standard approach stops working — the Scrum Guide gives explicit permission to do so.

## Best Used When
- A Scrum team is struggling to fit everyone's updates into 15 minutes.
- The Daily has drifted into passive status reporting rather than collaborative planning.
- A team or coach wants to revisit Daily Scrum format with a mandate from the Scrum Guide itself.
