# "Arrows" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten walks through a practical recipe for drawing clean, consistent arrows on flipcharts and whiteboards. He covers several arrow variants — straight, curved feedback loops, directional turns, multi-entry, and branching — and shows how to add 3D depth using a single color or a gray accent marker. The techniques are simple enough to follow live during a workshop.

## Methods & Techniques

### Simple Straight Arrow (ab 00:45)
- **What:** A step-by-step recipe for drawing a clean, proportional single-direction arrow.
- **When to use:** Any time you need to show movement from A to B, e.g., from idea to backlog.
- **How it works:**
  1. If the arrow needs a label, write the text inside first.
  2. Draw the body (shaft) of the arrow.
  3. Draw the two sides of the arrowhead — keep them roughly equal in length.
  4. Mark the center point of the arrowhead base with a small dot.
  5. Mirror the shape to close the arrowhead symmetrically.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9T_RhkGIT1o&t=45

### Curved Feedback Loop Arrow (ab 02:00)
- **What:** An arrow whose tail curves back and merges with its own body — useful for showing iterative or cyclical flows like the Scrum feedback loop.
- **When to use:** Visualizing retrospectives, sprint cycles, continuous improvement loops.
- **How it works:**
  1. Draw the body of the arrow.
  2. Create the curved loop returning back on itself.
  3. Draw the bottom of the loop and continue it downward.
  4. Align the two parallel lines of the arrowhead to the same length.
  5. Place a dot at the midpoint and close the arrowhead.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9T_RhkGIT1o&t=120

### Turning Arrow (Left/Right) (ab 02:45)
- **What:** An arrow that bends 90 degrees to indicate a change of direction.
- **When to use:** Process flows, decision paths, phase transitions.
- **How it works:**
  1. Draw the shaft.
  2. Ensure the two arrowhead sides are the same length.
  3. Place a dot at the midpoint and complete the arrowhead.
  4. Add shading or shadow for visual weight if desired.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9T_RhkGIT1o&t=165

### Merging Arrow (Two Inputs, One Output) (ab 03:10)
- **What:** Two entry lines that converge into a single arrow — useful for showing synthesis or consolidation.
- **When to use:** Showing inputs feeding into one outcome, e.g., two team streams merging into a release.
- **How it works:**
  1. Draw the two entry lines as a U-shape.
  2. Continue with the single body/shaft.
  3. Apply the standard arrowhead recipe — equal sides, dot at midpoint, close symmetrically.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9T_RhkGIT1o&t=190

### Branching Arrow (Main with Sub-Arrow) (ab 03:35)
- **What:** A primary arrow with a smaller secondary arrow breaking off from it.
- **When to use:** Showing a main path with an exception, sub-process, or side effect.
- **How it works:**
  1. Draw the main body first.
  2. Add the small branch arrow off the side.
  3. Close both arrowheads and join them to the main shaft.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9T_RhkGIT1o&t=215

### 3D Shading Technique (ab 04:00)
- **What:** A simple coloring approach to give flat arrows a three-dimensional appearance without needing many markers.
- **When to use:** Whenever you want visual arrows to pop on a flipchart or whiteboard.
- **How it works:**
  1. Color the front face of the arrow with your main color (e.g., green).
  2. Color the side faces with either a deeper shade of the same color or a gray marker.
  3. Two shades of one color are sufficient — no full color set required.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9T_RhkGIT1o&t=240

## Key Principles
- **Body first, always:** Carsten's consistent rule is to draw the shaft before the arrowhead — this keeps proportions correct.
- **Symmetry through the dot:** Placing a small dot at the midpoint of the arrowhead base before closing it is the key trick for keeping arrowheads balanced.
- **Equal-length sides:** Both sides of the arrowhead should be roughly the same length — eyeballing this is enough.
- **Text before shape:** If an arrow has a label inside, write the text first so the arrow can be sized around it.
- **Own the freestyle mistakes:** Carsten demonstrates live that even experienced facilitators drift from their own rules. Naming it ("I freestyled, you saw how that went") keeps the room at ease.

## Facilitation Tips
- Practice the "dot + mirror" arrowhead method until it becomes automatic — it's the single biggest differentiator between clean and messy arrows.
- Two shades of one color (or one color + gray) are enough to create 3D depth; you don't need a full marker set.
- Keep gray markers available specifically for shading and shadow accents.

## Best Used When
- Preparing for a workshop where you'll be drawing live on a flipchart or whiteboard.
- Onboarding new Scrum Masters or facilitators who want to build basic visual facilitation confidence.
- Creating visual artifacts (process maps, flow diagrams, retrospective boards) that need clear directional indicators.
