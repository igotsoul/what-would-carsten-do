# "Minimum Viable Preparation" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten Lützen introduces the concept of "Minimum Viable Preparation" — a lightweight facilitation approach that prioritizes a clear goal and adaptable exercise sequences over rigid, detailed plans. Rather than over-preparing, Carsten argues that experienced facilitators benefit more from having alternative routes and staying responsive to the room. The video covers three core elements: defining a clear goal, building a flexible string of exercises, and pairing up with a co-facilitator.

## Methods & Techniques

### Minimum Viable Preparation (ab 00:30)
- **What:** A facilitation preparation approach that focuses only on the essentials — a clear goal, a primary sequence of exercises, and a set of alternatives — rather than a detailed script.
- **When to use:** When facilitating workshops or sessions where participant needs may shift and pivoting is likely. Especially useful for experienced facilitators who can read the room.
- **Group size:** Not specified; implies any workshop size.
- **Duration:** Applicable to any session length.
- **How it works:**
  1. Establish a clear, concise goal agreed upon with the session sponsors and audience.
  2. Design a "sunny day" string of exercises that you believe will meet the goal.
  3. Prepare alternative exercises or paths to pivot to if the primary sequence isn't working.
  4. Check in with participants during the session to validate whether they're getting value.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=lvZg9r7qE9E&t=30

### String of Exercises (ab 01:20)
- **What:** A planned sequence of facilitation exercises (using the Liberating Structures concept of a "string") representing the primary path through a session.
- **When to use:** As the backbone of any workshop plan — the default route if nothing unexpected occurs.
- **How it works:** Map out the exercises in order that, together, address the session goal. Hold this loosely, knowing you may deviate.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=lvZg9r7qE9E&t=80

### Alternative Routes (ab 02:00)
- **What:** Pre-prepared backup exercises or paths the facilitator can switch to when the primary string isn't the right fit in the moment.
- **When to use:** When an exercise doesn't land, energy shifts, or a new thread emerges that's more valuable to follow.
- **How it works:** Before the session, identify 2–3 alternative exercises for key decision points. During the session, pivot to these when needed rather than forcing the original plan.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=lvZg9r7qE9E&t=120

### Checking In with the Audience (ab 02:20)
- **What:** A mid-session practice of explicitly asking participants whether they are getting the value they came for and whether anything is missing.
- **When to use:** At natural transition points during a workshop, or whenever energy or engagement seems to shift.
- **How it works:** Pause and invite honest feedback: "Are you getting the value from this time that you hoped for? Anything missing?" Use responses to adjust the remaining session.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=lvZg9r7qE9E&t=140

### Whisper Out Loud (ab 02:45)
- **What:** A Liberating Structures technique where facilitators transparently verbalize their in-the-moment decision-making so participants can hear the considerations being made.
- **When to use:** During transitions between exercises, when pivoting, or when the facilitation team is deciding what to do next.
- **How it works:** The facilitator(s) speak their deliberation aloud — "Should we do this, or we could also do that?" — making the process visible to participants, who may then contribute their own input.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=lvZg9r7qE9E&t=165

### Co-Facilitation (ab 03:15)
- **What:** Pairing two facilitators to run a session together, with one facilitating actively and the other observing and supporting.
- **When to use:** Whenever possible — Carsten calls this his single best facilitation tip.
- **How it works:**
  1. One facilitator leads; the other watches the room.
  2. The observer notices who is zoning out, what's unclear, or where energy is dropping.
  3. The observer can step in to clarify, re-engage participants, or suggest a pivot.
  4. Roles can switch naturally during the session.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=lvZg9r7qE9E&t=195

## Key Principles
- A clear, concise goal is the non-negotiable foundation of any session — without it, there is no way to meet expectations.
- Over-preparation can be counterproductive; real sessions require pivoting, and a rigid plan can get in the way.
- Experience builds a repertoire of exercises, making it easier to improvise and adapt in the moment.
- Facilitators exist to serve the audience, not their own ego or plan.
- Transparency in facilitation (whisper out loud) builds trust and invites participant ownership.
- Co-facilitation reduces cognitive load, enabling both facilitators to be more present and relaxed.

## Facilitation Tips
- Know your goal before designing anything else — confirm it with session sponsors.
- Build your plan as a flexible string, not a script. Know where you might branch.
- Always have alternative exercises ready before you walk into the room.
- Check in verbally with participants mid-session — don't assume it's going well.
- Use "whisper out loud" during transitions so participants understand the reasoning and can influence direction.
- Pair up whenever you can. A co-facilitator reads the room differently than you do, and that difference is valuable.
- When co-facilitating, the non-active facilitator's primary job is watching the room, not watching the lead facilitator.

## Best Used When
- Facilitating workshops where participant needs or energy may shift unexpectedly.
- Designing sessions where the outcome matters more than following a prescribed process.
- Working with an experienced facilitation partner who can share observation duties.
- Introducing more transparency and participant agency into workshop facilitation.
- Coaching newer facilitators on how to let go of over-preparation.
