# "Tip for KR validation" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten shares a quick validation technique for checking whether Key Results (KRs) in OKR frameworks are genuinely tied to their Objectives. Attributed to Anna Sofia, the method adapts the classic "not test" used to evaluate political statements into a practical self-check for OKR teams — particularly useful for newcomers to OKRs who struggle to assess whether their Key Results actually deliver the intended outcome.

## Methods & Techniques

### The "Will Have Met" KR Validation Test (ab 00:45)
- **What:** A simple sentence-completion check to verify that a Key Result genuinely supports its Objective. Carsten attributes this technique to Anna Sofia.
- **When to use:** When defining or reviewing Key Results in OKR workshops or planning sessions, especially with teams new to OKRs.
- **Group size:** Any — works individually or in a group review.
- **Duration:** A few minutes per Key Result.
- **How it works:**
  1. Complete the sentence: "If we achieve [Key Result], then we will have met [Objective]."
  2. Evaluate whether the logic holds. Does achieving the KR actually deliver the Objective?
  3. If the connection feels weak or the KR is only loosely related (e.g., "more downloads" does not necessarily mean "more users"), revise the KR.
- **Example from Carsten:** "If we get an NPS score over X, then we will have delighted our customers" — this holds. "If we get one million downloads, then we will have more users" — this does not reliably hold, since downloads and active users are not the same thing.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9tJeSBK6weU&t=45

## Key Principles
- A Key Result that does not clearly and directly support its Objective is a weak KR — even if it sounds measurable.
- The "will have met" framing forces teams to make the causal link between a metric and the desired outcome explicit.
- Inspired by the political "not test": if negating a statement produces something no reasonable person would support, the original statement is trivially one-sided. Similarly, a KR that does not survive the "will have met" check is not pulling its weight.
- Proxy metrics (e.g., downloads as a stand-in for users) are a common OKR pitfall; this test helps surface them early.

## Facilitation Tips
- Use this test during KR review moments in OKR kickoffs, check-ins, or retrospectives on the OKR cycle.
- Read the sentence aloud as a team — weak links often become obvious when spoken out loud.
- When the logic breaks down, use it as a coaching question: "What would we need to measure instead to know we've truly met the Objective?"

## Best Used When
- Onboarding teams to OKRs for the first time.
- Running OKR workshops where Key Results are being drafted or refined.
- Coaching sessions where a team's KRs feel disconnected from their stated Objectives.
- Quick health checks on existing OKRs before a new quarter begins.
