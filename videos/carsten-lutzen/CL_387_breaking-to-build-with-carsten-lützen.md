# Breaking to Build with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten reflects on a conversation with a colleague about how to help a high-performing team escape a local optimum and grow further. He shares three concrete techniques the team used to break out of stagnation and move toward a higher level of performance — while acknowledging that the transition requires a temporary dip in velocity.

## Methods & Techniques

### Revisiting Ways of Working & Working Agreements (ab 01:45)
- **What:** The team collectively revisits and rewrites their explicit agreements about what "done" means at each stage of the workflow — e.g., what needs to be true for an item to enter a sprint, reach "Ready for Review", or be accepted by the Product Owner.
- **When to use:** When a team is stuck in a local optimum and retrospectives keep surfacing the same minor issues without meaningful progress.
- **Group size:** Whole team
- **Duration:** Not specified; treated as a dedicated working session
- **How it works:**
  1. Acknowledge that the team has grown and past agreements may no longer reflect current standards.
  2. Start with a clean slate — don't assume old agreements still apply.
  3. Make new agreements explicit and visible (e.g., on a board or wall).
  4. Map out what is now expected at each stage of the workflow.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=g7fnfe8AK7c&t=105

### Playing with the Product / Becoming Superusers (ab 02:45)
- **What:** Ensure that the development team regularly uses the product they are building, exploring it hands-on so they develop a strong sense of ownership and can identify real pain points.
- **When to use:** When the team has become disconnected from the end-user experience or lacks intrinsic motivation around quality.
- **Group size:** Development team
- **Duration:** Ongoing practice, not a one-off
- **How it works:**
  1. Give developers structured time to explore and use the live product or system.
  2. Encourage them to approach it as a power user — knowing what works and what doesn't.
  3. Let the experience inform prioritization and quality conversations.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=g7fnfe8AK7c&t=165

### Observing End-User Testing (ab 03:30)
- **What:** Have developers watch real users interact with the product — experiencing first-hand what customers see, feel, and struggle with.
- **When to use:** When the team needs a renewed sense of purpose or a reality check on assumptions about how the product is used.
- **Group size:** Ideally whole development team, or representatives
- **Duration:** Not specified; tied to existing user testing sessions
- **How it works:**
  1. Arrange for developers to attend or observe user testing sessions.
  2. Let them witness the actual behavior of customers without filtering through PO or UX summaries.
  3. Use the shared experience as input for retrospectives and prioritization.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=g7fnfe8AK7c&t=210

## Key Principles
- A team can reach a **local optimum** — a state of stable, good performance — that masks the existence of a higher potential state. Getting there requires deliberate disruption.
- The **Tuckman model** framing: high-performing teams can plateau and drift downward unless they introduce intentional transformation.
- Moving to a higher optimum requires a **temporary performance dip** — velocity will drop while the team restructures its ways of working. Carsten argues this tradeoff is worth it.
- Team transformation only works when **the team co-owns the solution** — they must be the ones identifying what needs to change and how.
- When retrospectives keep surfacing the same minor issues without real movement, it is a signal that the team is **stuck at the wrong level** of problem-solving.
- **Experimentation** is the underlying discipline: small tweaks, regularly, to avoid calcifying on any single hill.

## Facilitation Tips
- When retro themes repeat without meaningful improvement, treat this as a diagnostic signal rather than a process failure — the team may need a structural intervention, not just better action items.
- Revisiting working agreements works best when framed as "starting fresh" rather than "fixing what's broken" — it reduces defensiveness and opens space for higher standards.
- Developer exposure to real users should not be filtered or summarized by others; the direct emotional experience is what creates ownership and motivation.

## Best Used When
- A mature team is delivering consistently but feels stale or stuck in a plateau.
- Retrospectives have become repetitive and are generating diminishing returns.
- There is a sense that "there must be a better way" but no clear path forward.
- A Scrum Master or Agile Coach is looking for structural interventions beyond ceremony tweaks.
