# "Futures Wheel" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten introduces the Futures Wheel, a consequence-mapping technique created by Jerome C. Glenn. Starting from a central "what if" question, participants brainstorm first-, second-, and third-order consequences, building a spider-web of connected impacts. The method helps teams explore the ripple effects of decisions and identify where to focus energy — amplifying positives or addressing risks.

## Methods & Techniques

### Futures Wheel (ab 00:45)
- **What:** A visual brainstorming tool where a central "what if" question radiates outward through multiple layers of consequences, revealing how one change connects to many outcomes.
- **When to use:** When a team wants to explore the implications of a potential decision, change, or scenario before committing to a direction. Useful for strategic planning, change management, or any situation where consequences are hard to see upfront.
- **Group size:** Works individually or in groups; Carsten suggests using 1-2-4-All for the brainstorming phase.
- **Duration:** Not specified; can range from a short exercise to an elaborate session depending on depth.
- **How it works:**
  1. Place a "what if" question in the center — keep it open and curious, not solution-specific.
  2. Brainstorm **first-order consequences** — what directly follows from this scenario? Place these in a ring around the center.
  3. For each first-order consequence, brainstorm **second-order consequences** — what happens next as a result of those?
  4. Repeat for **third-order consequences**, building a spider-web of interconnected impacts.
  5. Note that consequences can be positive or negative — identify which to amplify and which to address.
  6. Optionally, **group and categorize** consequences by theme (e.g., people, organization, tech stack) using spatial clustering or colored sticky notes.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=pQCVvD722oM&t=45

## Key Principles
- The "what if" prompt should be open and curious — not tied to a specific solution, but not so broad it boils the ocean.
- Consequences are not inherently negative — the exercise surfaces both risks to mitigate and opportunities to pursue.
- The value of the method increases as layers deepen: second- and third-order effects reveal non-obvious connections.
- Categorizing consequences (by theme or color) during the exercise helps make sense of complexity and supports follow-up work.

## Facilitation Tips
- Use **1-2-4-All** for generating consequences: individual silent reflection first, then pairs, then group sharing. This avoids groupthink and draws out quieter voices.
- Add categorization as a layer — either through spatial grouping on the canvas or color-coded sticky notes — so the web remains readable and actionable.
- Remind participants that some items may belong to more than one category; find a visual way to represent those overlaps.
- The central "what if" framing is key: Carsten notes his own example was slightly too solution-specific — keep it genuinely exploratory.

## Best Used When
- A team is considering a significant change and wants to stress-test its second- and third-order effects.
- You need a structured way to move from "what could happen?" to "where should we focus?"
- You want to surface both risks and opportunities from a single scenario.
- Groups tend to think only one step ahead and need a method to slow down and think systemically.

## Related Concepts
- **1-2-4-All** (Liberating Structures) — suggested as the brainstorming protocol within the exercise.
- **Min-maxing** (game theory/strategy) — mentioned as a more complex alternative this method replaces for simpler contexts.
- **Systems thinking** — the spider-web of consequences mirrors systems-thinking principles around interconnectedness and feedback loops.
- Created by **Jerome C. Glenn** — founder of the Millennium Project; the Futures Wheel has been in use for decades as a foresight tool.
