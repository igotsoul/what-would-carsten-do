# "Lost Agile Coach?" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten reflects openly on how the agile coaching role has lost its clarity over time — driven by market forces, certification inflation, and blurred boundaries between roles. He questions whether "agile coach" is still a useful label and argues that practitioners should focus more on their actual craft (coaching, facilitation, training) than on titles. This is a candid personal brain dump, not a how-to — but it surfaces important questions for anyone in the agile/coaching/facilitation space.

## Methods & Techniques
*This video does not present discrete methods or techniques. It is a reflective piece on role identity in the agile coaching field.*

## Key Principles
- **Title inflation erodes trust.** When "agile coach" can mean facilitator, consultant, Jira admin, or coach depending on who you ask, teams don't know what they're getting — and that erodes the value of the role.
- **Certification inflation is real.** Certifications that require only payment and a multiple-choice test — and never expire — have questionable value. Carsten uses his own PSM1 as an example.
- **Blurred boundaries are partly self-inflicted.** Carsten acknowledges falling for vanity, misguided ambition, and tool-focus (e.g., Jira) rather than staying grounded in mindset and people.
- **The agile coaching community is fractured.** There is no shared definition of the role, and some former agile coaches are now rebranding as "product" focused because that label carries clearer meaning.
- **Titles vs. craft.** Carsten argues the field should emphasize craft — professional coaching, facilitation, Liberating Structures — rather than chasing hierarchical titles like "Enterprise Agile Coach."
- **Double down on professional coaching.** Regardless of label, Carsten believes knowing how to ask powerful questions, set a coaching contract, and hold proper coaching stances is foundational for anyone helping teams.
- **Purpose over identity.** Carsten's personal reframe: "For me it's not about agile — it's about helping people and teams succeed." That clarity of purpose matters more than the title on a business card.
