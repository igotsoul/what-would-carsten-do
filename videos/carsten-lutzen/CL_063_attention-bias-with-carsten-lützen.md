# "Attention Bias" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten introduces two cognitive phenomena — attention bias and inattentional blindness — and explains how they create blind spots in agile teams, retrospectives, and product development. He offers three practical countermeasures: using Liberating Structures to diversify inputs, creating psychological safety for unexpected observations, and building in silence and reflection time.

## Methods & Techniques

### 1-2-4-All for Diverse Sense-Making (ab 03:10)
- **What:** A Liberating Structure format that fans out input-gathering across individuals, pairs, small groups, and the whole room to maximize the diversity of observations.
- **When to use:** During retrospectives, data analysis sessions, or any moment of collective sense-making where a group might converge too quickly on familiar patterns.
- **Group size:** Any size; scales well
- **Duration:** Typically 15–30 minutes depending on topic depth
- **How it works:** Participants first reflect alone (1), then in pairs (2), then in groups of four (4), then share with all. Each stage surfaces observations the previous stage may have missed.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=VPDkRTLC6Lg&t=190

### Silence as a Facilitation Tool (ab 03:30)
- **What:** Deliberately building in quiet reflection time before and after decisions so participants can re-examine data and notice unexpected signals.
- **When to use:** When a group is rushing to conclusions, when analyzing complex data, or when you suspect important signals are being overlooked.
- **Group size:** Any
- **Duration:** Even a few extra minutes of silence can shift what gets noticed
- **How it works:** Resist the urge to fill space. Give people time to look at the data once, then again, and again — the unexpected often surfaces on the second or third pass.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=VPDkRTLC6Lg&t=210

## Key Principles
- Attention bias means that when we focus on something specific, we develop a bias toward seeing only that — and miss equally important signals elsewhere.
- Inattentional blindness means we only see what we expect to see; the unexpected is invisible until conditions change.
- In retrospectives, narrowing focus to the same recurring topics can cause teams to miss systemic issues hiding in plain sight.
- In product development, a feature-focused mindset can blind teams to valuable side effects or unrelated opportunities.
- People who do spot the unexpected can be socially pressured into doubting themselves — making psychological safety a prerequisite for overcoming these biases.
- These biases are not a sign of carelessness; they are how the brain is wired. Awareness is the first step, structural countermeasures are the second.

## Facilitation Tips
- Use formats like 1-2-4-All to "fan out" sense-making and reduce the chance that the group locks onto a single narrative.
- Actively protect dissenting voices — if someone notices something unexpected, create space for them to keep arguing for it rather than being talked out of it.
- Build in deliberate silence and reflection cycles, especially when reviewing data or sprint outcomes.
- Prioritize group diversity; the more varied the perspectives in the room, the higher the likelihood that blind spots get covered.
- Name these phenomena explicitly with teams — awareness that the brain plays these tricks is itself a useful inoculation.

## Best Used When
- Preparing for or facilitating retrospectives where the same topics keep recurring and the team feels stuck.
- Running sense-making sessions on complex data, metrics, or product feedback.
- Coaching a team that is heavily feature-focused and may be missing systemic signals.
- Designing workshops where convergence happens too fast and diverse input is critical.
