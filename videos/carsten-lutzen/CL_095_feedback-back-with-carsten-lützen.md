# "Feedback-Back" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten introduces "Feedback-Back," a short, playful team activity where participants write appreciative feedback on sticky notes and physically attach them to each other's backs. The exercise creates a memorable, light-hearted experience of giving and receiving positive feedback while preserving a degree of anonymity about who wrote what.

## Methods & Techniques

### Feedback-Back (ab 00:05)
- **What:** Team members write appreciative feedback on sticky notes addressed to specific colleagues, then physically stick them onto each other's backs — creating a walking, wearable feedback board.
- **When to use:** End of a sprint, team event, or workshop where you want to close on a positive, energizing note. Works well as a low-effort appreciation ritual.
- **Group size:** Small to medium teams where members know each other well enough to write personal feedback.
- **Duration:** 10–20 minutes depending on team size and debrief format.
- **How it works:**
  1. Give everyone sticky notes and ask them to write appreciative feedback for their teammates — specific and personal (e.g., "I love that you bring dad jokes to the office").
  2. Set a minimum: at least one sticky per team member.
  3. Optional deluxe version: provide a team t-shirt for each person; stickies go on the t-shirt rather than directly on clothing.
  4. Everyone walks around and places their stickies on the backs of the people they are meant for.
  5. As people circulate, they will also feel stickies being placed on their own back.
  6. Once done, participants either peel off the t-shirt to collect their stickies, or pair up to help each other retrieve them.
  7. Participants read their feedback privately and reflect. Carsten suggests letting it sit rather than immediately debriefing.
  8. Optional closing round: go around the table and let people say a simple "thank you."

## Key Principles
- Appreciative feedback only — this is explicitly a positive exercise, not a critique format. Carsten flags the "kick me" risk and addresses it upfront by setting the tone clearly.
- Specificity makes feedback land better — a sticky about a particular behavior ("bringing dad jokes") is more meaningful than a generic compliment.
- Anonymous authorship adds comfort — because stickies go on the back and people are moving around, recipients don't know who wrote what, which can make the feedback feel more genuine.
- The physical mechanic is the magic — placing something on someone's back, and feeling it happen to you simultaneously, creates a memorable embodied experience that pure verbal feedback lacks.
- The facilitator should know their team — Carsten explicitly defers on the minimum sticky count, recognizing that team norms vary.

## Facilitation Tips
- Set a minimum of one sticky per team member to ensure no one is left with sparse feedback.
- The t-shirt variant is the "deluxe version": each person wears or holds a t-shirt, stickies go on it, and they can take the t-shirt home as a keepsake or later use it as a team t-shirt with a slogan.
- If no t-shirts are available, participants may need to pair up to retrieve stickies from their own backs — plan for this logistically.
- Carsten recommends not over-debriefing: let people read and sit with the feedback rather than immediately processing it aloud. Only add a round-the-table debrief if the goal of the session calls for it.
- Frame the exercise clearly as appreciative feedback before starting to prevent confusion or misuse.

## Best Used When
- Closing a team day, offsite, or sprint review on a high note.
- The team needs a boost of appreciation and recognition.
- You want a light, playful activity with minimal prep and materials.
- You want to give feedback a physical, memorable quality rather than a purely conversational one.
