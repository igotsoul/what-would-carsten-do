# "Critical Thinking" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten Lützen, agile coach and facilitator, revisits critical thinking as a foundational skill that underpins effective agile practice. He breaks down its core components — analysis, observation, inference, communication, and problem solving — and shows how these map directly onto everyday agile ceremonies like retrospectives, dailies, and sprint planning. The video is a reminder to practice these fundamentals deliberately rather than unconsciously.

## Methods & Techniques

### Deliberate Question Asking (ab 03:30)
- **What:** Consciously training how you ask questions — specifically open-ended, non-judgmental questions — to gather data and challenge your own perception.
- **When to use:** In daily standups, retrospectives, or any team conversation where you want to surface diverse perspectives rather than confirm existing assumptions.
- **Group size:** Any
- **Duration:** Ongoing practice, not a one-time exercise
- **How it works:** Notice when you are about to ask a leading or closed question. Reframe it as open and curiosity-driven. The goal is to observe and collect data before drawing conclusions.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=IztR9Xghmso&t=210

### Critical Thinking in Retrospectives via the Derby-Lassen Model (ab 03:45)
- **What:** Using retrospective structure as a vehicle for practicing critical thinking steps: gather data → analyze → decide → act.
- **When to use:** Every sprint retrospective; especially useful when teams jump to solutions without grounding in observed data.
- **Group size:** Scrum team (5–9 typical)
- **Duration:** Standard retrospective timebox
- **How it works:**
  1. What did we observe in the last sprint? (Observation)
  2. How do we quantify or make sense of this data? (Analysis)
  3. What conclusions can we draw? (Inference)
  4. What are our next steps? (Decision / Problem solving)
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=IztR9Xghmso&t=225

### Critical Thinking in Daily Standups (ab 04:15)
- **What:** Framing the daily as a micro-application of the scientific method — analyze what happened, decide what needs to happen today to reach the sprint goal.
- **When to use:** Every daily standup, particularly when teams fall into rote status reporting.
- **Group size:** Scrum team
- **Duration:** 15 minutes (standard daily timebox)
- **How it works:** Shift the framing from "what did I do / what will I do" to "what does the data tell us, and what is the most informed decision we can make today to move toward the sprint goal?"
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=IztR9Xghmso&t=255

## Key Principles
- Critical thinking is defined as the ability to objectively analyze and evaluate an issue in order to form a judgment — a concept rooted in ancient Greece but central to modern agile practice.
- The five core skills Carsten identifies: **observation**, **analysis**, **inference**, **communication**, and **problem solving**.
- Most agile practitioners already use critical thinking but don't label it as such — making it explicit helps teams apply it more deliberately.
- Diverse perspectives in a team are a data asset: more viewpoints mean more data to analyze and more options to evaluate.
- Agile sprints involve high uncertainty; critical thinking is the tool for navigating that uncertainty through informed, iterative decisions.
- The scientific method is an analogy Carsten returns to: revisiting fundamentals prevents teams from drifting in the wrong direction.

## Facilitation Tips
- When facilitating teams, Carsten actively tracks whether critical thinking steps are being applied — not just whether conclusions are being reached.
- Encourage teams to separate observation from interpretation: "What did we actually see?" before "What does it mean?"
- Use retrospective structures explicitly as a critical thinking exercise, not just a feelings check-in.
- Model open-ended, non-judgmental questioning yourself to create the norm for the team.

## Best Used When
- A team is making decisions based on gut feel or habit rather than observed data.
- Retrospectives feel superficial — symptoms are named but root causes aren't explored.
- Daily standups have become mechanical status updates disconnected from sprint goals.
- You want to help a team develop a shared, more rigorous approach to problem solving without introducing a heavy new framework.
