# "Cynefin Exercise" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten shares a hands-on group exercise (originally from Dandy People) for introducing the Cynefin Framework experientially. Rather than explaining the domains abstractly, participants physically move through activities that embody the Obvious, Complicated, and Complex domains — making the framework tangible and memorable. Works best with larger groups.

## Methods & Techniques

### Cynefin Physical Introduction Exercise (ab 00:40)
- **What:** A three-round physical activity where participants organize themselves in space, each round mirroring a different Cynefin domain — culminating in a reveal and debrief using a Cynefin flipchart.
- **When to use:** When introducing the Cynefin Framework to people unfamiliar with it; great as a workshop opener to get people out of their chairs and into a shared experience before diving into theory.
- **Group size:** Works better for bigger groups (more chaos and emergent behavior to observe)
- **Duration:** Approx. 15–25 minutes including debrief (estimated based on content)
- **How it works:**
  1. **Round 1 — Obvious/Simple domain (ab 01:10):** Ask participants to line up along one axis by height. This is easy to verify visually, so one or two people will naturally start directing others. Debrief: "Was it difficult? What worked? Could we do it better?"
  2. **Round 2 — Complicated domain (ab 02:00):** Keeping the height order, participants now also sort themselves on a second axis by commute time (or mileage) to the venue. Coordination becomes harder — you cannot tell from a distance who commuted longer. Someone may still try to control it, but less effectively. Debrief: "Was this easier or harder than round one?"
  3. **Round 3 — Complex domain (ab 02:40):** Each participant secretly picks one other person, estimates the number of heel-to-toe steps to reach them, then raises their hand when ready. On "Go!", everyone moves simultaneously — but their target is also moving, so all estimates are immediately invalid. Have participants do it anyway. Debrief: Connect the experience to emergence and unpredictability.
  4. **Reveal (ab 03:15):** Bring out a Cynefin flipchart. Explain that round 1 was Obvious, round 2 was Complicated, round 3 was Complex. Discuss the differences. If someone asks about Chaotic: "Imagine I just let a tiger into the room — we have no idea what happens next. That's Chaotic."

## Key Principles
- The Cynefin Framework is easier to internalize through embodied experience than through lecture — let people *feel* the domain before naming it.
- In the Obvious domain, visual information makes coordination easy and natural leaders emerge quickly.
- In the Complicated domain, expertise is needed because not all relevant information is immediately visible.
- In the Complex domain, interdependence invalidates upfront estimates — adaptation and emergence replace planning.
- The Chaotic domain is best conveyed through a visceral analogy (the tiger) rather than a structured activity — because there is no structure to simulate.
- Physical exercises break monotony and improve retention during full-day workshops.

## Facilitation Tips
- Carsten credits this exercise to **Dandy People** — check their website for additional materials.
- He recommends linking to Dave Snowden's own introduction to Cynefin (available on YouTube) as pre-reading or supplemental material.
- Let the "natural directors" emerge organically in rounds 1 and 2 — don't suppress them; their behavior is part of the debrief material.
- In round 3, even if participants have already realized their target will move, have them do it anyway — the lived experience of a broken estimate is more powerful than the insight alone.
- Use a prepared Cynefin flipchart ready to reveal after round 3 — the visual anchor helps connect the kinesthetic experience to the framework.

## Best Used When
- Kicking off a workshop or training where Cynefin will be applied (e.g., product strategy, prioritization, organizational design)
- The group has little or no prior exposure to the framework
- You have enough space for people to move around
- Group size is medium to large (the emergent coordination dynamics need enough people to be visible)
