# "Altitude" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten Lützen introduces "Altitude," an exercise from the Gamestorming toolbox (created by Dave Gray) that helps groups align on the appropriate level of conversation for a meeting or workshop. Using paper planes as a playful prop, participants collectively define and agree on whether their discussions should happen at "satellite" (strategic), "plane" (tactical), or "ground" (operational) level — and then use the planes as a physical signal when the conversation drifts off-altitude.

## Methods & Techniques

### Altitude Exercise (ab 00:30)
- **What:** A structured, playful technique for aligning a group on the right conversational level for a meeting, using paper planes as props and the metaphor of altitude (satellite, plane, ground) to represent strategic, tactical, and operational discussions.
- **When to use:** At the start of a meeting or workshop where there is risk of conversations veering too abstract (strategy talk in an operational session) or too detailed (code-level specifics in a strategy session). Particularly useful when groups regularly go off-topic or talk past each other.
- **Group size:** Not explicitly stated; works for small to medium groups based on the format described.
- **Duration:** Not explicitly stated; likely 15–30 minutes including facilitation and dot voting.
- **How it works:**
  1. Ask participants to each make a paper plane — optionally decorate it. Do this before revealing the flip chart with altitude levels, to keep energy light and curious.
  2. Introduce the three altitude levels on a flip chart:
     - **Satellite:** Strategic, long-horizon discussions (e.g., three-year strategy)
     - **Plane:** Mid-level, tactical discussions relevant to the current work
     - **Ground:** Operational, detail-level discussions (e.g., specific code modules or tasks)
  3. Using 1-2-4-All (Liberating Structures), ask participants to define what each altitude looks like *for this specific meeting*: first individually in silence, then in pairs, then in groups of four, then in plenary.
  4. Capture definitions on a flip chart or sticky notes for each altitude level.
  5. Ask participants where they see the most value — use dot voting to identify the preferred altitude for this session.
  6. Agree collectively on the altitude for the meeting.
  7. Throughout the meeting, use the paper plane as a signal: if a conversation drifts to the wrong altitude, someone throws (or raises) their plane. The group then decides: move on, or rephrase the discussion to bring it back to the agreed altitude.

## Key Principles
- Conversational alignment before a meeting reduces drift and frustration — agreeing on *what level* to talk at is as important as agreeing on the agenda.
- Physical props (paper planes) lower the social cost of interrupting; a thrown plane is playful rather than confrontational.
- Letting the group define each altitude level themselves (rather than the facilitator imposing definitions) creates shared ownership and more precise, context-specific meaning.
- This is a more structured evolution of the simple "raise your hand if this isn't relevant to you" norm — it gives the group a shared language and a lightweight protocol.
- Carsten credits Dave Gray (Gamestorming) as the original creator of this exercise.

## Facilitation Tips
- Don't show the flip chart with altitude levels until after participants have made their paper planes — preserving the surprise keeps energy up.
- Combine with **1-2-4-All** (Liberating Structures) when defining what each altitude means for the group; this surfaces diverse perspectives before converging.
- Use **dot voting** to align on the target altitude; don't impose it top-down.
- Keep the plane-throwing signal light and fun — frame it explicitly as a playful check-in, not a critique of whoever is speaking.
- Offering a brief paper plane tutorial at the start is itself a small energizer and equalizer.

## Best Used When
- Opening a meeting or workshop where participants come with different expectations about the level of conversation.
- Teams that frequently get stuck in either too-abstract (vision/strategy) or too-detailed (implementation specifics) discussions during sessions meant for a different level.
- Any group that would benefit from a shared, low-friction signal to redirect off-track conversations.
- As a light energizer combined with a meta-conversation about how the group wants to work together.
