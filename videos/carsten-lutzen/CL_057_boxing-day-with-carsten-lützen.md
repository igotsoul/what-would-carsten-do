# "Boxing Day" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten Lützen introduces "Boxing Day," a festive peer feedback exercise where each participant decorates a personal box and receives written feedback from teammates. The exercise blends a social, creative activity with structured feedback practice, and the boxes can remain in the office afterward as a vehicle for ongoing, continuous feedback.

## Methods & Techniques

### Boxing Day Feedback Exercise (ab 00:20)
- **What:** A peer feedback activity where participants decorate personal boxes, then write feedback to each other using guided prompts. The box stays in the office afterward as a physical letterbox for continuous feedback.
- **When to use:** Team celebrations, end-of-year or holiday gatherings, team offsites, or any moment where the team has a short break together. Also effective as a way to introduce or reinforce a feedback culture.
- **Group size:** Small to medium teams where everyone knows each other well enough to write meaningful feedback
- **Duration:** Not specified precisely, but framed as something doable in a single team session
- **How it works:**
  1. Provide each participant with one box, stickies, colored paper, and arts & crafts supplies
  2. Each person decorates their own box — name it, draw on it, make it personal and self-expressive
  3. Prepare feedback prompt templates for participants to complete, e.g.:
     - "I see you at your best when…"
     - "This year I really appreciate that I saw you…"
     - "I see that you can improve on…"
  4. Participants write feedback for each other on paper or stickies and place them in the respective boxes
  5. Facilitate inclusion: set a rule that everyone must write at least one piece of feedback for every other person
  6. Unwrapping: participants open their boxes. Options include silent individual reading followed by open questions, or a group reflection ("How did it feel to receive this feedback? Can you use it?")
  7. Leave the boxes in the office as permanent letterboxes for ongoing peer feedback

## Key Principles
- Feedback is a gift — a phrase Carsten attributes to a colleague: "Feedback is a gift and we have to give it to each other"
- Balancing appreciation and growth: prompts should cover both praise for contributions and areas for improvement
- Equity in feedback: the facilitator should monitor that everyone receives a meaningful amount — avoid situations where one person gets dozens of notes while others receive none
- Making feedback a habit: the physical artifact (the box) serves as a visible, ongoing reminder and tool for continuous feedback beyond the initial exercise
- Wrapping serious practice in a social, creative frame lowers the barrier to participation and makes the experience memorable

## Facilitation Tips
- Prepare prompt starters in advance to help participants who struggle to formulate feedback — fill-in-the-blank formats reduce friction
- Scan the room during the writing phase to ensure equitable distribution; consider a minimum rule (at least one note per person per participant)
- Give participants a choice in the reflection format: silent individual reading suits introverted teams, while group reflection suits teams comfortable with open discussion
- Use the unwrapping moment to discuss the purpose of feedback: "What's in it for us? Why do we give it?"
- Leave the boxes visible in the workspace after the session to signal that feedback is a continuous cultural practice, not a one-time event

## Best Used When
- A team needs a low-threat entry point into peer feedback culture
- During festive or celebratory team moments (end of year, team off-sites) where a lighter format fits the mood
- A team wants a tangible, ongoing feedback mechanism without heavy tooling
- The facilitator wants to combine a social/creative activity with a developmental purpose
