# "Draw-along" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten demonstrates that everyone can draw by walking viewers through a simple face-drawing exercise step by step. The video is both a confidence-builder and a practical intro to graphical facilitation — specifically how simple character drawings can be used in workshops for self-introductions and role/accountability posters.

## Methods & Techniques

### Draw-Along: Simple Cartoon Face (ab 00:30)
- **What:** A guided, step-by-step method for drawing a simple human face using familiar letter shapes (e.g., the letter G for ears) to make drawing feel accessible.
- **When to use:** As a warm-up activity to introduce visual facilitation, or as a self-practice exercise before using drawings in workshops.
- **Group size:** Individual, but works in group settings where everyone draws simultaneously.
- **Duration:** ~4–5 minutes to complete the basic face.
- **How it works:**
  1. Draw two dots as eyes.
  2. Add a simple nose below.
  3. Draw a mouth — either a plain arc or more detailed with cheeks, open mouth, and tongue.
  4. Draw ears using the shape of a capital G and its mirror image, aligned with the top of the eyes.
  5. Add a small inner arc to each ear to disguise the G-shape.
  6. Draw the jawline — avoid making it too wide/round; a more angular line looks less cartoonish.
  7. Add hair (or leave bald with a half-circle).
  8. Optionally add personal characteristics (e.g., freckles) to make it recognizable.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=cVw92SoAvNc&t=30

### Self-Introduction Portrait (ab 04:30)
- **What:** Using the simple face drawing as the basis for a personal introduction card — adding a neck, body, and written name/role next to the drawing.
- **When to use:** At the start of workshops or team events where participants introduce themselves; can be prepared in advance or done live.
- **Group size:** Individual activity, works in groups of any size.
- **Duration:** 5–10 minutes depending on detail level.
- **How it works:**
  1. Draw the face using the method above.
  2. Extend it with a neck and simple body outline.
  3. Write your name and a short description next to or below the figure.
  4. Post on the wall for others to see.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=cVw92SoAvNc&t=270

### Role & Accountability Posters (ab 05:00)
- **What:** Pre-prepared large-format posters featuring drawn characters representing specific roles, with responsibilities written around or below them — a visual alternative to plain text role descriptions.
- **When to use:** In workshops focused on roles, accountabilities, or team structure (e.g., defining a Scrum team's responsibilities).
- **Group size:** Prepared by facilitator in advance; used with groups of any size.
- **Duration:** Preparation time varies; used as a facilitation prop during the session.
- **How it works:**
  1. Draw a character for each role on a large poster sheet.
  2. Scale the drawing to fit the paper.
  3. Write responsibilities at the bottom or around the figure.
  4. Use during the workshop as a visual anchor for discussion.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=cVw92SoAvNc&t=300

## Key Principles
- Everyone can draw — it is a skill that most people had as children and can be re-learned with practice.
- Using familiar shapes (letters, simple arcs) as building blocks removes the intimidation of "not being able to draw."
- Adding personal characteristics (like freckles) makes visual representations more recognizable and human.
- Simple drawings are more engaging than generic stick figures or clip-art icons in workshop materials.

## Facilitation Tips
- Carsten suggests starting with the eyes as anchor points and building all other features relative to them (ears align with eye height).
- Avoid overly round jawlines — a slightly angular jaw looks more natural and less cartoonish.
- Encourage participants to add one personal characteristic to their drawing to make it distinctly theirs.
- Scale drawings to fit the medium — a small self-portrait card vs. a large wall poster require different sizing.

## Best Used When
- You want to introduce graphical facilitation to a team or group that believes they "can't draw."
- Preparing workshop materials that go beyond plain text or generic visuals.
- Running a team event that starts with personal introductions and you want something more engaging than a name tag.
- Facilitating a workshop on roles and accountabilities where visual anchors help the conversation.
