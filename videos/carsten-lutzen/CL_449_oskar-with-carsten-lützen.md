# OSKAR with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten introduces OSKAR, a solution-focused coaching model credited to Roy Marriott, as a structured approach to 1-on-1 coaching conversations. The model builds on the classic 1–10 scaling question but adds a full arc from current state to desired outcome to concrete small actions. It keeps coachees focused on what is already working rather than dwelling on problems.

## Methods & Techniques

### OSKAR Coaching Model (ab 00:30)
- **What:** A five-step solution-focused coaching framework — Outcome, Scaling, Know-how, Affirm & Action, Review — that guides a coachee from current situation to small actionable next steps.
- **When to use:** 1-on-1 coaching sessions where someone is stuck, in conflict, or wants to improve a situation. Especially useful when the coach has limited information and the coachee is the subject matter expert.
- **Group size:** 1-on-1
- **Duration:** Not specified; fits a standard coaching conversation (30–60 min implied)
- **How it works:**
  1. **O — Outcome:** Establish the desired future state. "What outcome do you want to achieve?"
  2. **S — Scaling:** Introduce a 1–10 scale. Anchor 1 as completely unrealistic (e.g., "the world is in flames") and 10 as an ideal zen-like perfection. Ask where the coachee is now. This deliberately avoids the KPI trap of treating the scale as a hard metric.
  3. **K — Know-how:** Surface what is already working. Ask: "You said it was a 4 — can you give me 10 reasons why it is not a 3?" This shifts focus toward existing strengths and progress.
  4. **A — Affirm & Action:** Acknowledge the current position, then generate small next steps. Ask: "What would make this a 4.1?" Keep actions minimal and doable — baby steps only.
  5. **R — Review:** Step back to the meta level. Revisit the actions and desired outcome to close the loop.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=fRG4PVFVVsM&t=30

## Key Principles
- Rooted in **Solution-Focused Coaching**: focus on what is working and build on it, rather than analyzing what is broken.
- The extreme anchors on the scale (1 = absurd worst case, 10 = unreachable perfection) are intentional — they reduce pressure and prevent the scale from being treated as a KPI.
- The "10 reasons why it's not lower" question is a deliberate reframe: it produces a list of positives the coachee already owns.
- The coachee is always the subject matter expert — the coach's role is to ask, not prescribe.
- You can only change yourself, not others. Actions must be things the coachee can do unilaterally.
- Small increments (4 → 4.1, not 4 → 10) make follow-through realistic and measurable.

## Facilitation Tips
- Let the coachee give a brief explanation of their current score, but keep it short — then redirect immediately to the "why not lower?" question.
- When defining actions, explicitly ask what it would take to move just one tenth of a point, not a full point. This prevents overwhelm and produces concrete behavior.
- In conflict situations, remind the coachee that only their own behavior is in scope for action planning.

## Best Used When
- A coachee feels stuck or is focused on what is going wrong rather than what could improve.
- Navigating interpersonal conflict where only one party is in the room.
- You want a lightweight, repeatable coaching structure that does not require deep diagnosis of root causes.
- As an alternative to deficit-focused problem analysis in team or individual coaching.

## Related Concepts
- **Solution-Focused Coaching / Solution-Focused Brief Therapy (SFBT)** — the broader school of thought this model belongs to.
- Classic 1-to-10 scaling questions — OSKAR is presented as a structured extension of this common coaching move.
- Roy Marriott — credited by Carsten as the person who introduced him to this model.
