# "Three Word Stories" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten Lützen introduces "Three Word Stories," a creativity workout energizer originally shared by Vlad Glavu and James. The exercise challenges participants to describe a movie in exactly three words, stimulating creative thinking and group energy. It's a simple, flexible activity that gets people both generating ideas and riffing off each other.

## Methods & Techniques

### Three Word Stories (ab 00:45)
- **What:** Participants describe a well-known movie using exactly three words (e.g., "Fish Finding Family" for Finding Nemo, "Wizard In School Shenanigans" for Harry Potter).
- **When to use:** As an energizer or creativity warm-up before sessions requiring creative or innovative thinking; works equally well in-person and remote.
- **Group size:** Any — scales easily in a chat-based format for large groups.
- **Duration:** 5–10 minutes depending on format.
- **How it works:**
  1. Ask participants to think of a movie and describe it in exactly three words.
  2. Have them share in chat or say it out loud.
  3. Other participants try to guess the movie.
  4. Let the group riff — people build on each other's ideas and invent variations.
  5. Optional: run a stricter quiz format by preparing examples in advance.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=uAVtF7qTytc&t=45

## Key Principles
- Energizers should do more than raise energy — they can also stimulate creative thinking and get blood flowing to prime the brain for innovation work.
- Low-threshold activities that everyone can participate in generate broad engagement and spontaneous collaboration.
- Allowing participants to riff on each other's answers creates emergent creativity, not just individual output.
- The loose, open format (rather than a strict quiz) is recommended because it unlocks more creativity and interaction.

## Facilitation Tips
- Carsten recommends the open/freeform version over the quiz format — letting everyone generate their own three-word stories produces more energy and engagement than passively guessing prepared examples.
- Use the chat feature in remote sessions to let multiple people share simultaneously, then surface interesting ones for group discussion.
- Let the group naturally start guessing and building on each other's answers — don't over-facilitate it.

## Best Used When
- Opening a session that requires creative or innovative thinking.
- Re-energizing a group mid-session after a heavy analytical segment.
- Remote or hybrid meetings where you need a low-barrier activity that works well in chat.
- Teams working on product innovation or creative problem-solving who benefit from regular "creativity workouts."
