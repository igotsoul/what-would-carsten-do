# The Retro Wheel with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten introduces "The Retro Wheel" (also called "The Modified Starfish") — a retrospective format that extends the classic Starfish exercise by adding an Appreciations category. The format prompts teams to reflect across five standard dimensions plus structured peer appreciation, with deliberate rules to keep the appreciation genuine and face-to-face.

## Methods & Techniques

### The Retro Wheel / Modified Starfish (ab 00:20)
- **What:** An extended version of the Starfish retrospective that adds an "Appreciations" segment for positive, named feedback to team members.
- **When to use:** Any sprint or iteration retrospective, especially useful when teams are new to giving and receiving structured feedback.
- **Group size:** Small to mid-size Scrum teams
- **Duration:** Not specified; typical retro slot (45–90 min implied)
- **How it works:**
  1. Set up a board (physical or digital) with six sections: *Continue*, *More of*, *Start*, *Less of*, *Stop*, and *Appreciations*
  2. Ask participants to silently write post-its — or use 1-2-4-All (Liberating Structures) as the divergence format
  3. Each section has a clear meaning:
     - **Continue:** Things being done well at the current level
     - **More of:** Things already happening that should increase
     - **Start:** Things not yet being done (e.g. code reviews)
     - **Less of:** Things being done that should be minimized
     - **Stop:** Things that should be dropped entirely
     - **Appreciations:** Positive, sincere feedback directed at a named team member present in the meeting
  4. For Appreciations, Carsten recommends stating explicit rules upfront: *"Only non-sarcastic, non-ironic comments, to a person in this meeting, and it cannot be me (the facilitator)."*
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=SVuIugNXN6c

## Key Principles
- Combining a familiar format (Starfish) with appreciation creates a retro that is both structured and emotionally meaningful
- Appreciations work best when they are face-to-face and happen in the moment — absent team members should not be the target
- Without explicit rules, appreciation segments tend to attract jokes, ironic comments, or easy compliments directed at the facilitator rather than the teammates who actually deserve recognition
- Knowing your team well allows you to anticipate these dynamics and address them proactively with clear framing

## Facilitation Tips
- State the Appreciations rules explicitly before the exercise starts: positive, non-sarcastic, non-ironic, directed at someone present, and not the facilitator
- This prevents the facilitator becoming the default target of compliments — which sidesteps the real purpose of peer recognition
- Silent writing or 1-2-4-All works well as the divergence method; choose based on team comfort level with reflection
- The name ("Retro Wheel", "Modified Starfish") is secondary — Carsten encourages making up your own name

## Best Used When
- Teams are learning to give and receive feedback and need scaffolding
- You want to close a retrospective on a positive, connective note
- Team members have done strong individual work that deserves explicit recognition
- The team is comfortable enough for peer appreciation not to feel forced

## Related Concepts
- **Starfish Retrospective** — the base format this builds on (Continue, More of, Start, Less of, Stop)
- **1-2-4-All** (Liberating Structures) — suggested as an alternative divergence method for generating post-its
- **Appreciations** (from Liberating Structures) — the standalone appreciation practice that is embedded here as one segment
