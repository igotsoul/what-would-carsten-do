# "5 Characteristics of Play... ful work" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten introduces the LEGO Foundation's five-ingredient model of play and applies it to the design of playful work. He explains each characteristic and connects the model to practical workshop design — particularly Liberating Structures — as a mental checklist for facilitators who want to make work more engaging and meaningful.

## Methods & Techniques

### The 5 Characteristics of Playful Work (ab 00:40)
- **What:** A model from the LEGO Foundation, research-based, identifying five ingredients that make work — or play — genuinely playful. Not all five need to be active simultaneously, but they should all appear at some point.
- **When to use:** When designing workshops, team sessions, or any collaborative work experience where engagement and retention matter.
- **Group size:** Any
- **Duration:** Applies as a design lens, not a timed activity
- **How it works:**
  1. **Joyful** — The experience must have a joyful element. Carsten emphasizes there is no universal formula for joy; facilitators must read their audience and choose accordingly (e.g., dress-up might delight one group and alienate another).
  2. **Socially interactive** — Humans are social beings. The design must create moments of social interaction and a sense of belonging and doing things together. Balance interaction with reflection time.
  3. **Actively engaging** — Playful work is not a lecture. Participants must *do* something — take turns, build on each other's contributions, interact. Control must be distributed, not held by the facilitator.
  4. **Meaningful** — If participants can't see the relevance or meaning, they won't engage fully and won't experience it as playful. Only design activities that matter to the context.
  5. **Iterative** — Returning to ideas, repeating with variation, or refining what was already explored deepens learning and activates different cognitive processes. Iteration makes things stick.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=xc40BxvSLjA&t=40

## Key Principles
- All five characteristics don't need to be present at every moment — they need to be present across the overall experience.
- Joy is audience-dependent. What is joyful for one group may be off-putting for another. Know your participants.
- Active engagement means distributed control — getting the crowd involved quickly, not keeping the facilitator at the center.
- Meaning is non-negotiable. Carsten is explicit: if an activity isn't important, don't do it.
- Iteration is a learning multiplier — it "unblocks parts of the brain" and makes content stick in ways single-pass approaches cannot.
- Liberating Structures naturally cover several of these characteristics: iterative design, active engagement, and social interaction are baked into many LS formats. Meaningfulness depends on the problem domain.

## Facilitation Tips
- Use this five-characteristic model as a mental checklist when designing any workshop or work session.
- When checking for "joyful," don't default to a generic icebreaker — ask what *this specific group* would find fun or energizing.
- When checking for "actively engaging," ask: am I distributing control fast enough, or am I holding the floor too long?
- Liberating Structures are a practical toolkit that already embodies several of these principles — lean on them when designing for playfulness.

## Best Used When
- Designing a workshop, team session, or collaborative meeting from scratch and wanting a quality check.
- Reflecting on why a past session felt flat or disengaging — use the five characteristics as a diagnostic lens.
- Advocating for more playful work culture within a team or organization.
