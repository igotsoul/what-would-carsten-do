# "Positive Feedback" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten shares his personal philosophy and three concrete rules for giving positive feedback effectively. He argues that well-meant positive feedback is one of the most energizing forces in the workplace and challenges viewers to try giving only positive feedback for two days to observe its effect on their environment.

## Methods & Techniques

### Three Rules for Positive Feedback (ab 01:30)
- **What:** A simple, memorable framework for giving positive feedback that is genuine, timely, and clean — free of disguised critique.
- **When to use:** Any time you want to appreciate a colleague, team member, or peer in a way that actually lands and builds energy rather than creating confusion or distrust.
- **Group size:** Individual (1-on-1), but applicable in team settings with sensitivity to context.
- **Duration:** Spontaneous, in-the-moment — no formal setup required.
- **How it works:**
  1. **Never joke about it** — Positive feedback must always be sincere. Sarcasm or humor undermines the message and makes people unsure whether you mean it. If you tend to joke a lot (like Carsten), be especially conscious of this.
  2. **Do it in the moment** — When you see or hear something awesome, reach out immediately. Be sensitive to context: not everyone wants public praise, so ask for permission if unsure ("Is it okay if I share some positive feedback with you?").
  3. **Never disguise critique as positive feedback** — Avoid the feedback sandwich ("This was great, but..."). If you have constructive feedback, ask for separate permission to share it. Before doing so, ask yourself: am I sharing this to help them, or to sound clever?
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=MZuzW_62Hjw&t=90

### Two-Day Positive Feedback Challenge (ab 04:00)
- **What:** A personal experiment where you commit to giving only positive feedback for two days and observe what changes in your environment.
- **When to use:** As a reset or awareness exercise for yourself or a team that tends to default to critique.
- **Group size:** Individual practice, team-wide impact.
- **Duration:** 2 days.
- **How it works:** Intentionally withhold constructive or corrective feedback for 48 hours. Focus only on noticing and naming what is working well. Observe how people react and how the team atmosphere shifts.

## Key Principles
- Positive feedback is one of the most energizing forces in a workplace — it fuels people's motivation to continue doing good work.
- Sincerity is the foundation: feedback that might be a joke is not positive feedback, it is noise at best and undermining at worst.
- Timeliness matters — feedback given in the moment is more powerful than delayed appreciation.
- The feedback sandwich is not a compromise between positive and constructive feedback; it is a communication failure. Keep them separate.
- Asking for permission before giving constructive feedback respects autonomy and makes the feedback more likely to be received.
- According to Carsten, even if someone declines to receive constructive feedback, that is a valid outcome — not every critique needs to be delivered.

## Facilitation Tips
- When giving positive feedback in group settings, be aware that not everyone is comfortable receiving appreciation publicly. Check in before praising someone in front of others.
- Use a simple permission-asking phrase: "Is it okay if I share some positive feedback with you?" — low friction, high respect.
- In retrospectives, Carsten connects this to the appreciative feedback column in the Starfish format (referenced in his "Retro Wheel" video) — building positive feedback into structured formats helps normalize it.

## Best Used When
- Onboarding teams to a culture of appreciation and psychological safety.
- Coaching individuals who struggle to give or receive positive feedback authentically.
- Running retrospectives where the team tends to focus only on problems.
- Personal development work around communication habits.
