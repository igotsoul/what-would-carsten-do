# "Metaphors" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten Lützen explores the foundational role of metaphors in facilitation and coaching. He explains how metaphors create distance from difficult problems, enabling teams to surface insights that direct conversation might not. Carsten shares practical guidance on when to use predefined metaphors versus inviting teams to create their own.

## Methods & Techniques

### Predefined Metaphors in Retrospectives (ab 01:00)
- **What:** Using a pre-selected metaphor (e.g., sailboat, treasure map) to represent the team and its way of working, allowing participants to discuss challenges indirectly.
- **When to use:** When a team needs structure to discuss process and dynamics, especially when direct conversation feels charged or abstract.
- **Group size:** Any team size
- **Duration:** Embedded within the retrospective format
- **How it works:**
  1. Select a metaphor that maps well to the team's context (e.g., sailboat: wind = what drives us forward, anchor = what holds us back).
  2. Introduce the metaphor as the frame for the session.
  3. Invite participants to populate it with their observations about the team or process.
  4. Debrief what the metaphor reveals about real dynamics.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=WdOGvR3t4zc&t=60

### Team-Generated Metaphors (ab 02:15)
- **What:** Rather than providing a metaphor, the facilitator invites the team or client to define their own metaphor for how they work together.
- **When to use:** When you want deeper ownership and resonance, or when exploring identity and culture. Rooted in the Clean Language approach.
- **Group size:** Any
- **Duration:** Can be a standalone exercise or opening to a longer session
- **How it works:**
  1. Ask the team: "What metaphor captures how we work as a team?"
  2. Hold back your own suggestions — keep language "clean" of your predefined frames.
  3. Let the team surface and discuss their own image or analogy.
  4. Use the metaphor they chose as a shared reference point throughout the session.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=WdOGvR3t4zc&t=135

### Discussing Why a Metaphor Works (or Doesn't) (ab 03:10)
- **What:** Turning the imperfection of a metaphor into a discussion prompt — asking where it fits and where it breaks down — as a way to surface difficult team conversations.
- **When to use:** When a proposed metaphor sparks tension or when direct discussion of team health feels blocked.
- **How it works:**
  1. After a metaphor is proposed, ask: "What makes this a good metaphor for us?"
  2. Follow up: "Where does this metaphor fall short?"
  3. Use the gap between the metaphor and reality to explore what's not ideal in the team's environment.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=WdOGvR3t4zc&t=190

## Key Principles
- Metaphors create productive distance: they allow teams to discuss difficult realities without the defensiveness that direct questions can trigger.
- Team-generated metaphors produce more ownership and engagement than facilitator-imposed ones.
- An imperfect metaphor is not a failure — the discussion about *why* it doesn't quite fit is often where the real insight lives.
- Clean Language practice encourages keeping facilitator language free of embedded metaphors so the client's own framing can emerge.

## Facilitation Tips
- Prioritize inviting the team to define their own metaphor rather than defaulting to your prepared one.
- Once a metaphor is established (especially a team-generated one), use it consistently throughout the session and beyond.
- Explicitly discuss the metaphor together: what makes it apt, and where does it not quite hold? This kickstarts conversations that are otherwise hard to have.
- If you as facilitator don't understand a metaphor someone has offered, say so. Carsten notes that if the facilitator is confused, others likely are too — asking for clarification models psychological safety and deepens shared meaning.

## Best Used When
- Running retrospectives and wanting to go deeper than a surface-level format.
- Coaching a team or individual through a challenge that's hard to name directly.
- The team is stuck or conversations feel circular — a new metaphorical frame can unlock fresh angles.
- Building shared language and identity within a team.

## Related Concepts
- **Sailboat Retrospective** — a widely used predefined metaphor format for team retrospectives.
- **Treasure Map Retrospective** — another predefined metaphor format Carsten references.
- **Clean Language** — a coaching methodology (developed by David Grove) that avoids introducing the facilitator's own metaphors, instead drawing out the client's natural imagery.
