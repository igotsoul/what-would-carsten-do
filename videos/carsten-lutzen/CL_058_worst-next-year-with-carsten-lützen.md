# "Worst Next Year" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten Lützen shares a short, fun end-of-year facilitation exercise called "Worst Next Year," inspired by the Liberating Structures pattern TRIZ. By asking teams to brainstorm how to make next year fail spectacularly, then reflecting on what they are already doing (even unintentionally) that mirrors those failure modes, the exercise surfaces honest self-reflection and generates meaningful, team-owned improvement goals for the coming year.

## Methods & Techniques

### Worst Next Year (ab 00:30)
- **What:** An end-of-year reflection and goal-setting exercise based on the TRIZ Liberating Structure. Teams first generate creative ideas for how to guarantee failure next year, then identify which of those failure behaviors they are already exhibiting, and finally determine first steps to stop or prevent them.
- **When to use:** End of year (or end of quarter/cycle) when a team wants to close out the period and set meaningful intentions for the next one — especially as an alternative to top-down goal-setting.
- **Group size:** Works for teams; Carsten references pairs and groups of four, suggesting it scales well from small teams upward.
- **Duration:** Not specified explicitly, but the structure suggests 30–60 minutes depending on team size and depth of discussion.
- **How it works:**
  1. Frame the session as "new year resolution setting" or equivalent future-looking meeting.
  2. Ask: *"What can we do to make sure that next year fails most spectacularly — or just fails?"*
  3. Individual silent reflection and note-taking.
  4. Pair up and compare notes; write ideas on sticky notes.
  5. Share in groups of four and build a collective list of failure modes (encourage creativity).
  6. Ask: *"What are we already doing — even unintentionally — from this list that would make next year fail?"*
  7. Individual silent reflection on current behaviors.
  8. Share in pairs, then in groups of four.
  9. Ask: *"What would a good first step be to stop or avoid these behaviors?"* — turn insights into concrete commitments.

## Key Principles
- The inversion technique (asking "how to fail") lowers defensiveness and makes it easier for people to name uncomfortable truths about current team behavior.
- The progression from imaginative failure brainstorming → honest self-audit → concrete first steps mirrors TRIZ's core logic: destroy the system on paper first, then examine what already exists that does the same damage.
- Carsten emphasizes that most teams will enter the new year loaded with new goals and tools; end-of-year housekeeping should also include *removing* things — creating cognitive space, not just adding commitments.
- The 1-2-4-All structure (individual → pairs → fours → whole group) ensures psychological safety and equal contribution before ideas are shared broadly.

## Facilitation Tips
- Cover up the follow-up questions when introducing the exercise — reveal them one at a time so teams don't jump ahead and self-censor during the failure brainstorming phase.
- Encourage teams to be as creative and exaggerated as they want during the failure brainstorming; the more playful this phase, the more honest the reflection tends to be.
- The second question ("what are we already doing from this list?") is where real introspection happens — give people enough silent time here, as it requires looking inward at both team patterns and personal behaviors.
- Use sticky notes throughout to make the list tangible and sortable.

## Best Used When
- Closing out a year, quarter, or major delivery cycle with a team.
- A team needs to set its own goals rather than receiving them top-down.
- Psychological safety is moderate — the humor of "how to fail" gives cover for honest admissions.
- The team is at risk of taking on too many new initiatives and would benefit from also discussing what to *stop* doing.

## Related Concepts
- **TRIZ** (Liberating Structures) — the direct inspiration; a technique for surfacing and eliminating contradictions by first imagining how to guarantee the unwanted outcome.
- **1-2-4-All** (Liberating Structures) — the structural pattern used for idea generation and sharing (individual → pairs → fours → plenary).
- **Health checks / team radars** — mentioned as an example failure mode (ignoring code health checks), suggesting this exercise pairs well with technical or team health diagnostics.
- **Retrospectives** — this exercise functions as a forward-looking retrospective hybrid, combining reflection on the past with intention-setting for the future.
