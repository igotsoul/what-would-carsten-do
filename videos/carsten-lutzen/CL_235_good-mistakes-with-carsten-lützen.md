# "Good Mistakes" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten introduces the concept of "Good Mistakes" — inspired by Jeff Watts — as a reframe of the common "fail fast" mentality. A good mistake is one made in good faith that generates energy and learning rather than demotivation. The video explores what makes a mistake "good" and how teams can build a culture that embraces them.

## Methods & Techniques

### Good Mistakes Wall (ab 03:20)
- **What:** A visible board or wall where the team logs their good mistakes and the learnings derived from them, keeping them in plain sight.
- **When to use:** In teams trying to normalise failure and accelerate learning; especially useful in early-stage teams or psychological safety initiatives.
- **Group size:** Team level
- **Duration:** Ongoing practice, not a one-off exercise
- **How it works:**
  1. When a mistake is made in good faith, the team names it openly.
  2. The learning is captured and posted on a visible wall or board.
  3. Leaders role-model by adding their own mistakes first.
  4. The wall becomes a shared artifact that normalises learning from failure.

## Key Principles
- A **good mistake** is one that generates energy and learning, not demotivation — distinguishing it from demoralising failure.
- Good mistakes are always made **in good faith**: given the information, resources, and team available at the time, it was the best possible decision.
- When acting in good faith, **blame is inappropriate** — neither self-blame nor blaming others is justified.
- Good faith always means acting **in pursuit of something better or right**, not for selfish or careless reasons.
- Good mistakes create **short feedback loops** — they help teams learn what works and what doesn't, quickly and cheaply.
- Short feedback loops lead to **early risk reduction** and keep options open, mirroring the logic of iterative development.
- The more openly a team talks about good mistakes, the easier it becomes to **admit and share** future mistakes.

## Facilitation Tips
- Carsten emphasises the importance of **role modeling**: leaders and coaches should visibly share their own good mistakes first to make it safe for others.
- Framing matters — use the language "good mistake" rather than "failure" to shift the emotional charge and invite energy instead of shame.
- Create a **physical or visible artifact** (a wall, board, or shared space) to make the practice tangible and persistent.

## Best Used When
- A team is struggling with blame culture or fear of failure.
- You want to establish psychological safety as a foundation for retrospectives or coaching.
- Preparing a team for iterative, experimental ways of working where fast learning is essential.
- Onboarding a new team to agile or Scrum practices where a learning mindset is needed.

## Related Concepts
- **Fail fast** mentality — Carsten positions "good mistakes" as a more energising reframe of this common agile principle.
- **Jeff Watts** — the originator of the "good mistakes" framing; Carsten recommends his book on teamwork.
- **Psychological safety** — underpins the entire concept; without it, good mistakes cannot be shared openly.
- **Iterative development** — shares the same logic of short cycles and early risk reduction.
- **Celebrate failures / celebrate learnings** — a related cultural practice that good mistakes connect to.
