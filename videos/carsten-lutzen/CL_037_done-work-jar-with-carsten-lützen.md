# "Done Work Jar" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten Lützen shares a simple, playful technique called the "Done Work Jar" for making completed work visible in a lightweight, low-friction way. By placing a piece of candy in a jar every time a piece of work is finished, teams generate weekly throughput data while building a fun team ritual. The method is designed as an accessible first step toward transparency and continuous improvement.

## Methods & Techniques

### Done Work Jar (ab 00:30)
- **What:** A physical jar where team members drop one piece of candy each time they complete a piece of work. At the end of the week, the team counts and records the total, then eats the candy together.
- **When to use:** When a team wants to start tracking throughput without heavy tooling or process overhead; great as a first step toward data-driven improvement.
- **Group size:** Works individually or as a team.
- **Duration:** Ongoing weekly ritual; end-of-week review takes a few minutes.
- **How it works:**
  1. Place an empty jar somewhere visible (on the team's desk or in the team space).
  2. Every time anyone completes a piece of work, drop one candy into the jar.
  3. At the end of the week, count the candies and record the number (e.g., "March 18–24: 15 pieces").
  4. Eat the candy together as a small celebration.
  5. Start the next week with an empty jar.
  6. Over time, compare weekly counts: look for high and low weeks, identify patterns, and ask what drove the differences.

## Key Principles
- Transparency doesn't have to start with complex tools — a jar and candy is enough to generate meaningful data.
- Playfulness lowers resistance to change; tying a habit to something enjoyable (candy) makes adoption easier.
- Aggregate tracking is sufficient to start — you don't need to break work down to the sentence level to gain insight.
- Data collected over weeks enables pattern recognition: why were some weeks high and others low?
- The ritual doubles as a lightweight end-of-week retrospective trigger.

## Facilitation Tips
- Keep the granularity coarse — one candy per meaningful completed piece of work, not micro-tasks. Avoid over-counting.
- Store the weekly numbers somewhere visible (a sticky note, a simple spreadsheet) so trends become apparent over time.
- Use the candy-eating moment as a natural prompt for a brief retrospective conversation: "What made this week different from last week?"
- Works for remote teams too if each member maintains their own jar and shares counts in a weekly check-in.

## Best Used When
- A team is new to transparency practices and needs a low-barrier entry point.
- There is little existing data on team throughput or velocity.
- The team culture benefits from playful, low-stakes rituals.
- You want to build a habit of reflection without adding formal process.
