# "My Intervention Checklist" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten Lützen shares a three-step mental checklist he uses to decide whether — and how — to intervene during facilitation sessions. The core tension he explores is between a facilitator's impulse to act and the risk of that action reducing group ownership or learning. The checklist helps facilitators distinguish between groups that need help and groups that simply need time.

## Methods & Techniques

### The Intervention Checklist (ab 01:30)
- **What:** A three-question mental filter Carsten runs before making any intervention during a workshop or coaching session, designed to prevent ego-driven or unhelpful facilitation moves.
- **When to use:** Any moment during a session when the facilitator feels the urge to step in — especially during silence, confusion, or off-track group work.
- **Group size:** Any
- **Duration:** Takes seconds — it's an internal check, not a visible exercise
- **How it works:**
  1. **Does the group actually need help, or do they just need time?** Carsten's default assumption is that it's time. If the setup was solid, participants will speak up when they genuinely need help.
  2. **Will my intervention decrease or increase ownership?** Stepping in and directing can strip ownership from the group by pushing them down a path they didn't choose. If the intervention opens a new pathway, do it. If it narrows options, hold back.
  3. **Am I doing this for the group or for myself?** If the impulse comes from wanting to share a clever observation or a fun anecdote, save it for the break — write it on a sticky note if needed so it isn't lost.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=iY60kh44x3w&t=90

### The "Genius Thought" Sticky Note (ab 04:30)
- **What:** A personal habit where Carsten writes a keyword on a sticky note to capture a joke, anecdote, or observation that arose in the moment but isn't appropriate to share with the group right now.
- **When to use:** When the facilitator has an impulse that passes the self-awareness check (it's for me, not them) but feels too good to fully discard.
- **How it works:** Jot a keyword on a sticky note during the session; share the thought with a participant, sponsor, or colleague during a break or after the workshop.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=iY60kh44x3w&t=270

## Key Principles
- **Leave your ego at the door.** Carsten attributes this framing to a participant at a coaching conference. Facilitators who take up too much space risk making themselves more memorable than the insights the group generated — which is the wrong outcome.
- **Silence is usually okay.** The reflex to fill quiet with re-explanation is often misplaced. Silence frequently means the group is thinking, not stuck.
- **Not intervening is also an intervention.** Carsten references a prior framework ("everything is an intervention") to make the point that deliberate restraint is an active, conscious choice — not passivity.
- **More often than not, it's time.** The group's most common need is simply enough time to work through something, not facilitation input.
- **Ownership is the metric.** The central question for any intervention isn't whether it's helpful in the abstract, but whether it increases or decreases the group's sense of ownership over the outcome.

## Facilitation Tips
- Before intervening, run the three-question checklist internally — it takes only seconds and prevents reactive, ego-driven moves.
- When in doubt about silence or apparent confusion, wait. Trust that the group will signal when they genuinely need help.
- Use sticky notes to "park" your own clever thoughts so they don't get suppressed entirely — just redirected to an appropriate moment.
- Measure facilitation success by what the group remembers, not by how much the facilitator contributed. If participants remember the facilitator more than their own insights, the facilitator took up too much space.

## Best Used When
- Preparing for or reflecting on facilitated workshops, retrospectives, or group coaching sessions
- Developing self-awareness as a facilitator, particularly around the impulse to over-intervene
- Coaching or training new facilitators on when restraint is more valuable than action
- Debriefing sessions where the facilitator felt they either over- or under-intervened
