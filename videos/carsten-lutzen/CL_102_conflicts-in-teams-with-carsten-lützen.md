# "Conflicts in Teams" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten Lützen presents a framework for understanding conflict in teams across three categories: no conflict (superficial harmony), destructive conflict, and constructive conflict. He argues that the absence of conflict is not healthy — it signals low psychological safety — and that the goal for coaches and facilitators is to help teams reach constructive conflict, where difficult things can be said without judgment. He offers practical coaching interventions for each category.

## Methods & Techniques

### Three-Category Conflict Diagnostic (ab 00:45)
- **What:** A simple diagnostic lens for assessing team conflict culture across three states: no conflict (superficial harmony), destructive conflict, and constructive conflict.
- **When to use:** When entering a new team or assessing team health; helps prioritize coaching interventions.
- **Group size:** Any team size.
- **Duration:** Observation-based; no fixed duration.
- **How it works:**
  1. Observe whether conflict is absent, destructive, or constructive.
  2. Absent conflict → signals superficial harmony and low psychological safety.
  3. Destructive conflict → often personal, one-sided, finger-pointing; may be driven by one or two individuals.
  4. Constructive conflict → professional disagreement, candid feedback, psychological safety to challenge.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=6SSmrJaQiy0&t=45

### Interventions for Low/No Conflict (ab 02:30)
- **What:** Techniques to move a team out of superficial harmony and build trust and psychological safety.
- **When to use:** When a team shows no conflict, avoids disagreement, and defaults to compliance.
- **How it works:** Carsten suggests exercises to help people get to know and trust each other at a deeper level:
  - **Market of Skills** — surface individual expertise and strengths across the team.
  - **Appreciative Interviews** — structured peer conversations that build connection and mutual respect.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=6SSmrJaQiy0&t=150

### Interventions for Destructive Conflict (ab 03:15)
- **What:** Coaching approaches to de-escalate and reframe destructive, personal conflict.
- **When to use:** When team conflict has become personal, one-sided, or blame-driven.
- **How it works:**
  1. Use the **Conflict Staircase** model to help team members locate where they are in the escalation cycle and create shared awareness.
  2. Apply **Solution-Focused Coaching** — shift perspective by asking: "How could you look at this from the other side?" or "How could we scale this down a bit?"
  3. If one or two individuals are driving destructive behavior, build a personal coaching relationship with them and help them see how the behavior is hurting both themselves and the team.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=6SSmrJaQiy0&t=195

### Sustaining Constructive Conflict Culture (ab 05:00)
- **What:** Coaching approach to maintain a healthy conflict culture as team membership changes.
- **When to use:** When a team already has constructive conflict and you want to make it durable.
- **How it works:**
  1. Identify telltale signs of the healthy culture — what specific behaviors signal it is working?
  2. Use Solution-Focused Coaching questions: "What is working right now that we should keep doing?" and "How would we know if we've improved further?"
  3. Create concrete tasks or agreements from these insights to preserve the culture through onboarding and offboarding.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=6SSmrJaQiy0&t=300

## Key Principles
- The absence of conflict is not a good sign — it typically signals superficial harmony and low psychological safety, not a healthy team.
- Constructive conflict requires psychological safety: people must be able to say difficult things without fear of judgment.
- Destructive conflict often becomes personal when individuals cannot detach themselves from their opinions.
- Diversity of views only emerges when constructive conflict is possible — without it, innovation and challenge of the status quo are suppressed.
- As a coach, your role shifts depending on where the team sits: activating conflict in low-conflict teams, de-escalating in high-conflict teams, and sustaining culture in healthy teams.
- Radical Candor is a useful framework in the constructive conflict zone — being honest and caring simultaneously.

## Facilitation Tips
- When observing a new team, hold your diagnosis loosely — one category is usually predominant, but multiple dynamics can coexist.
- For teams in destructive conflict, build a personal relationship with the individuals driving the behavior before attempting to change it — trust is a prerequisite for influence.
- Use the Conflict Staircase as a reflective tool with the team, not just as a coach's diagnostic — asking "Where do you see us on this staircase?" surfaces different perceptions and opens dialogue.
- In constructive conflict cultures, focus coaching energy on making the culture explicit and codified so it survives membership changes.
- Solution-Focused Coaching works across all three categories: what is working, what would improvement look like, what can we do to get there.

## Best Used When
- Entering a new team as a coach or facilitator and needing to quickly assess team health.
- A team appears "too harmonious" and you suspect people are holding back.
- Conflict has become personal and is disrupting team performance.
- A healthy team is growing or changing and you want to protect its culture.
- Designing team coaching interventions and needing a simple framework to explain the goal.
