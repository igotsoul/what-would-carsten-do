# "Agile in a Nutshell Poster" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten walks through the "Agile in a Nutshell" poster by Dandy People (designed by Mia), a single-page visual that consolidates a wide range of agile models and frameworks. The poster is presented as a practical facilitation artifact — useful for introducing agile to newcomers, sparking discussion about where a team stands, or doing a temperature check on which topics resonate most. Carsten highlights how each section of the poster tells a connected story from "why agile" to "how agile" to "being agile."

## Methods & Techniques

### Using the Poster as an Agile Introduction Tool (ab 00:45)
- **What:** A single-page visual artifact that covers the agile manifesto, waterfall vs. agile comparison, ways of working, teamwork models, and the agile mindset — used as a structured conversation starter.
- **When to use:** When onboarding new team members or stakeholders to agile, when facilitating a team discussion on agile maturity, or when doing a temperature check on which agile topics people want to explore further.
- **Group size:** Works for individuals through to larger groups; best in small to medium groups for discussion.
- **Duration:** Flexible — can be a quick overview (10–15 min) or a deeper dive by section.
- **How it works:**
  1. Use the left column ("Why agile") to stage-set the problem: what we know vs. don't know, and how waterfall stacks up risk vs. how agile delivers incrementally.
  2. Move to the middle column ("Ways of working") to contrast the waterfall handover chain with cross-functional, iterative agile teams — introducing concepts like build-measure-learn and T-shaped profiles.
  3. Use the right column ("To be agile") to explore the Agile Onion (visible vs. invisible layers of agility) and Cynefin (where complexity lives and why agility thrives there).
  4. Let participants point to sections they find most interesting — use that as input for deeper dives in follow-up sessions.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=TJUV1SyR69c

### Temperature Check via Poster (ab 01:10)
- **What:** Ask participants to indicate which section or model on the poster they find most interesting or relevant — use that signal to prioritize what to teach next.
- **When to use:** At the start of an agile introduction session or training, when calibrating where to spend facilitation time.
- **Group size:** Any.
- **Duration:** 5–10 min.
- **How it works:** Display or distribute the poster; ask "What on here stands out or intrigues you?" Use the answers to sequence deeper exploration.

## Key Principles

- **Waterfall vs. Agile contrast:** Waterfall stacks all decisions and risk upfront, then delivers at the end. Agile takes decisions incrementally, delivers small value slices along the way, and mitigates risk continuously — often reaching the actual goal sooner.
- **Fail faster to succeed sooner:** By iterating and measuring, teams can cut off branches that lead nowhere, reducing wasted effort. Carsten frames this positively: failing fast is a feature, not a failure.
- **Cross-functional over specialist silos:** Waterfall relies on handovers between specialists; agile teams revisit everything needed at every iteration, reducing knowledge loss at handovers.
- **T-shaped profiles:** Agile working encourages individuals to develop breadth beyond their specialty (a developer who can also test or do requirements work), enabling a team to respond flexibly.
- **The Agile Onion:** Visible practices (ceremonies, tools) are the least powerful layer. The deeper, less visible layers — mindset, culture, values — are where real agility lives.
- **Cynefin and complexity:** Agility thrives in the complex domain. Scrum specifically helps teams pull work from complex toward complicated, making it more manageable.
- **Modern Agile principles** (Joshua Kerievsky): Make people awesome, make safety a prerequisite, deliver value continuously, experiment and learn rapidly.

## Facilitation Tips

- Use the poster as a "one picture = 1000 words" tool — it lets facilitators point at a shared visual rather than abstractly explaining agile concepts, which makes the conversation more concrete.
- Don't try to cover everything in one session. Let participant interest (temperature check) drive which sections to go deep on in follow-up work.
- The three-column structure (Why / How / To Be) maps naturally to a learning journey — use it as a sequencing guide across multiple sessions.
- The "No more HiPPO decisions" note (Highest Paid Person's Opinion) on the poster is a useful, light-hearted provocation to open a conversation about decision-making culture.

## Best Used When

- Introducing agile concepts to teams or stakeholders who are new to agile or moving from waterfall
- Starting an agile coaching engagement and wanting a shared visual reference point
- Running a workshop on agile maturity or team development
- Facilitating a discussion about which agile topics or models to prioritize learning

## Related Concepts

- **Agile Manifesto** — Four values and twelve principles underlying the agile movement
- **Cynefin Framework** (Dave Snowden) — Sense-making model with domains: simple, complicated, complex, chaotic; agility is most relevant in the complex domain
- **Agile Onion** (Simon Cade) — Layered model of agile adoption from visible practices (outer) to values and mindset (inner)
- **Modern Agile** (Joshua Kerievsky) — Four principles replacing ceremony-heavy frameworks with outcome-focused values
- **Discover & Deliver Model** — Distinguishes product discovery (what to build) from delivery (building it); mentioned as part of the poster's sprint heartbeat section
- **Build-Measure-Learn loop** — Core iterative cycle underpinning agile delivery
- **T-shaped professionals** — People with deep expertise in one area and working knowledge across others; a goal of cross-functional agile teams
- **Dandy People** — Swedish agile company that created the poster; the designer is credited as Mia
