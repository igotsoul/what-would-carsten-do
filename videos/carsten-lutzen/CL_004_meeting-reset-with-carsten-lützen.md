# "Meeting Reset" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary

Carsten Lützen shares a quick 30-second check-in technique called "Meeting Reset" that can be dropped into any meeting or workshop to create shared focus and alignment. Participants individually and silently define what they want to achieve in the next short timebox, then share in a round-robin — making both the goal and the out-of-scope equally explicit.

## Methods & Techniques

### Meeting Reset Check-in (ab 00:18)
- **What:** A 30-second individual, silent reflection where each participant writes down what they want to achieve in the next defined time period, followed by a round-robin share-out.
- **When to use:** At the start of a meeting or workshop, or when a session needs to be refocused mid-way. Particularly useful before a condensed, time-boxed conversation.
- **Group size:** Any; works for both small and larger groups since reflection is individual and silent.
- **Duration:** ~30 seconds for the silent reflection; the round-robin adds a few minutes depending on group size. The target conversation timebox is short (e.g., 20 minutes).
- **How it works:**
  1. Pose the question: "What would you like to achieve in the next [timebox]?"
  2. Participants reflect individually and in silence, then write down their answer.
  3. Go around the room (round-robin) — each person shares their answer.
  4. The group is now explicitly aligned on both what is in scope and what is out of scope for the timebox.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=UOIKi5tC9Wg&t=18

## Key Principles

- Individual silent reflection before sharing prevents anchoring and groupthink — everyone commits to their own answer first.
- Making desired outcomes explicit also makes out-of-scope topics explicit, which helps steer the conversation and avoid rabbit holes.
- Keeping the timebox short (e.g., 20 minutes) creates productive constraint and focus.
- Alignment on purpose at the start of a meeting significantly increases the chance of a useful outcome.

## Facilitation Tips

- According to Carsten, keeping the timebox short is key — it creates a "good and condensed" conversation rather than an open-ended drift.
- Frame the round-robin as a brief share, not a discussion — this keeps the reset itself fast and doesn't consume the meeting time it is meant to protect.
- The question "what is it that you would like to achieve?" is intentionally personal — it invites ownership rather than just restating the agenda.

## Best Used When

- A meeting is at risk of losing focus or going off-topic.
- Participants may have different expectations of what the session will cover.
- You want to quickly surface hidden agendas or misaligned priorities before diving in.
- Running a short, timeboxed working session within a larger event.
