# "Hybrid Meetings" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten shares practical, low-budget approaches to running hybrid meetings where some participants are in the room and others are online. He walks through common challenges — latency, visibility, equal participation — and offers concrete gear and facilitation tactics to address them without defaulting to the lowest common denominator of putting everyone online.

## Methods & Techniques

### Level the Playing Field — Move Everyone Online (ab 00:45)
- **What:** When a true hybrid setup is too complex, have all participants join the meeting online — even those physically present — to give everyone the same experience and equal ability to contribute.
- **When to use:** When the group is small, the work is collaboration-heavy, or the tech setup is minimal.
- **Group size:** Any
- **Duration:** Any
- **How it works:** All participants open the video call on their own device, regardless of physical location. No one gets a "room advantage."
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=UPTZ09iH7mA&t=45

### Phone as Roving Camera (ab 02:10)
- **What:** Join the meeting on a second device (phone) to serve as a mobile camera — especially useful when a fixed screen-mounted camera shows only the backs of presenters.
- **When to use:** Any hybrid session where people will move around, present at flip charts, or do gallery walks.
- **Group size:** Any
- **Duration:** Any
- **How it works:** Keep the phone in the meeting as a second video feed. Use a gimbal for smooth movement. During a gallery walk, physically walk the phone to each poster and hold it steady so online participants can read the content.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=UPTZ09iH7mA&t=130

### Gimbal for Gallery Walks (ab 02:30)
- **What:** Attach a phone to a gimbal to create a stable, smooth roving camera for in-room activities.
- **When to use:** Gallery walks, poster reviews, physical artefact sharing with online participants.
- **Group size:** Any
- **Duration:** Any
- **How it works:** Mount phone on gimbal with its built-in stand. Walk slowly to each flipchart or poster, hold still so online participants can read, then move to the next.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=UPTZ09iH7mA&t=150

### Audio Mixer + PA System for Larger Sessions (ab 03:00)
- **What:** Use an external microphone fed through a mixer and loudspeaker to amplify in-room voices for both the room and the online call.
- **When to use:** Larger hybrid sessions where the laptop mic cannot capture the room well enough.
- **Group size:** 15+
- **Duration:** Any
- **How it works:** Hook a microphone into a mixer that feeds both the loudspeaker (so the room hears everything) and the online meeting (so remote participants hear the room). Online participants speak back through the loudspeakers.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=UPTZ09iH7mA&t=180

### Split Instructions with Muted Rooms (ab 03:30)
- **What:** When running parallel exercises (in-room on paper, online on a virtual whiteboard), mute one channel while giving instructions to the other group simultaneously with a co-facilitator.
- **When to use:** Larger hybrid workshops with divergent activities for in-room vs. online groups.
- **Group size:** 10+
- **Duration:** Any
- **How it works:** Two facilitators work in tandem. Mute the online meeting feed to the room. In-room facilitator gives instructions to physical participants while the other facilitator addresses online participants — both groups receive instructions at the same time. At sharing time, online chat is monitored and read aloud.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=UPTZ09iH7mA&t=210

### Phone as Impromptu Networking Bridge (ab 04:15)
- **What:** Hand a phone showing an online participant to someone in the room so the two can have a side conversation during networking or pair activities.
- **When to use:** Icebreakers, impromptu networking, or paired interview exercises in hybrid sessions.
- **Group size:** Any
- **Duration:** 5–15 min
- **How it works:** Online participant is visible on a phone screen. An in-room participant takes the phone (or puts on a headset), steps to a quiet corner, and holds a 1-on-1 conversation mimicking what in-room pairs are doing.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=UPTZ09iH7mA&t=255

## Key Principles

- **Expect something to go wrong.** Hybrid sessions always have technical hiccups — the mindset shift is to plan ahead enough to overcome most of them rather than be surprised.
- **Latency is invisible to the room.** In-room participants can naturally interrupt each other; online participants experience a delay that makes this very hard. Facilitators must actively compensate.
- **The facilitator is the equity bridge.** Carsten frames the facilitator's core hybrid role as actively surfacing and including online voices — not just managing the agenda.
- **Low-cost gear can go a long way.** A phone, a gimbal, and a 20-year-old PA system can create a solid hybrid experience without enterprise tech investment.
- **Read the chat out loud.** Online contributions in the chat are invisible to the room unless someone actively voices them. This is a simple habit with outsized impact.
- **Document and share in real time.** Photograph physical artefacts and post them to the chat or virtual whiteboard so online participants stay connected to what is being produced.

## Facilitation Tips

- Actively remember the online participants — call on them by name, build check-ins for online voices into your facilitation rhythm.
- Assign a dedicated "hybrid handler" role in larger sessions: one person whose only job is to monitor the online channel, read the chat, and make sure remote participants can follow and contribute.
- When presenting at a flip chart or screen, don't stand in front of it — the fixed room camera will obscure online participants' view. Use the phone camera instead.
- Move the roving camera slowly and hold it steady at each artefact; don't rush the gallery walk for the sake of the room.
- Give instructions to in-room and online groups simultaneously using the muted-room technique rather than sequentially, to keep the session pace equal.

## Best Used When

- Planning or preparing to run a hybrid workshop or meeting with a mixed in-room/online group
- Troubleshooting an existing hybrid setup that is leaving online participants behind
- Working with limited budget and standard video conferencing tools (Teams, Zoom, Webex)
- Designing activities (gallery walks, networking, paired exercises) that need to include online participants meaningfully
