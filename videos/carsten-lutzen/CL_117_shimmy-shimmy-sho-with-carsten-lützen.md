# "Shimmy Shimmy Sho" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten Lützen introduces Shimmy Shimmy Sho, a playful improv-based energizer he discovered at Innovation Round Table 23 via Improv Consulting. The exercise gets participants moving, laughing, and alert through a simple gesture-matching game played in a circle. A short debrief makes it a useful segue into the rest of a session.

## Methods & Techniques

### Shimmy Shimmy Sho (ab 00:30)
- **What:** A circle-based energizer where participants challenge each other with simultaneous silly gestures, repeating until they match, then swap places.
- **When to use:** As a warm-up or mid-session energizer to re-engage a group, lift energy, and create a playful atmosphere before deeper work.
- **Group size:** Any group that can stand in a circle; works well with multiple simultaneous pairs once energy builds.
- **Duration:** Approximately 10 minutes including debrief.
- **How it works:**
  1. Facilitator picks three silly gestures in advance and demonstrates them to the group.
  2. All participants stand in a circle.
  3. One person (starting with the facilitator) steps into the middle, faces another participant across the circle.
  4. Both say "Shimmy Shimmy Sho" together and on "Sho" each simultaneously performs one of the three gestures.
  5. They repeat until both show the same gesture — then they swap places in the circle.
  6. The new person in the middle finds someone else and the game continues.
  7. As momentum builds, the facilitator can seed multiple inner-circle challengers so several pairs are active at once.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Wblb764PhVY

## Key Principles
- Silliness is a feature, not a bug — doing something "fun and super weird and stupid" lowers social barriers and primes openness.
- Simultaneous action (both players reveal at the same time) removes hierarchy and keeps the game fair and surprising.
- Layering multiple active pairs scales energy across the whole group rather than letting people watch from the sidelines.
- Alertness is naturally triggered because anyone in the circle can be challenged at any moment.

## Facilitation Tips
- Prepare your three gestures before the session so you can demonstrate them confidently and consistently.
- Start the game yourself to model the energy level and silliness you want from the group.
- Seed three or four simultaneous challengers once the group understands the mechanics, so there is always activity happening.
- Debrief with open questions: Did people notice any patterns? Did anyone experiment with tactics (e.g., always picking the same gesture)? How was the overall experience? This turns a pure energizer into a brief reflection moment.

## Best Used When
- A group needs to shift out of passive listening mode.
- Energy is dipping mid-session or the group feels stiff at the start.
- You want to introduce a playful, improv-style mindset before creative or collaborative work.
- No physical exertion is possible or desired — the exercise is low-impact but still gets people on their feet and moving.
