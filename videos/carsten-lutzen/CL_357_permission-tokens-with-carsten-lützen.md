# "Permission Tokens" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Permission Tokens is a simple, tactile visualization experiment from the book *The Zombie Scrum Survival Guide* that helps Scrum teams see how much external permission they need to get work done. By dropping a token into a jar every time someone must ask for approval outside the team, teams build a concrete dataset to identify bottlenecks and measure progress toward greater autonomy over multiple sprints.

## Methods & Techniques

### Permission Tokens (ab 00:35)
- **What:** A physical visualization exercise where team members add a token to a shared jar each time they need to ask for permission outside the team — to change something, adopt a technology, or take an action that's not within their control.
- **When to use:** When a Scrum team suspects it is bottlenecked by external dependencies or low autonomy, and wants to surface and measure that problem concretely.
- **Group size:** One team (works at individual contributor level within the team)
- **Duration:** Run continuously over 2+ sprints to generate meaningful trend data
- **How it works:**
  1. Place a jar (or similar container) somewhere visible to the team.
  2. Every time a team member needs to ask for permission outside the team, they drop a token in the jar.
  3. **Optional — color-code by person:** Each team member gets a distinct token color, revealing who is bottlenecked most.
  4. **Optional — color-code by category:** Use colors to classify permission types (e.g., green = infrastructure, blue = legal), making it easy to see which domains restrict the team most.
  5. Run this for several sprints and compare the distributions over time.
  6. Use the data to drive targeted improvements and measure whether those improvements reduce the permission load.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=I6NArTLq9gs

## Key Principles
- Teams that frequently need external permission are teams with constrained autonomy — making this invisible cost visible is the first step to changing it.
- Color-coding adds a diagnostic layer: you can distinguish *who* is bottlenecked (person-based) from *what* is bottlenecked (category-based), enabling more targeted action.
- Running the experiment across multiple sprints turns an anecdote into a dataset — giving the team evidence to advocate for structural change.
- Pairs well with other dependency visualizations (Carsten also references the Painsnake for sprint-external work visibility).

## Facilitation Tips
- Keep the jar in a visible, central location so it stays top of mind without needing a formal process.
- Decide upfront whether you're color-coding by person, by category, or both — switching mid-experiment muddies the data.
- Revisit the jar during the retrospective: count tokens, discuss patterns, and pick one dependency to address next sprint.
- Even a single sprint of data can spark a useful conversation; multiple sprints reveal whether interventions are working.

## Best Used When
- The team feels slowed down by approvals, sign-offs, or external gatekeepers but can't easily articulate the scale of the problem.
- Leadership or stakeholders need concrete data before granting a team more autonomy.
- A team wants a low-overhead, ongoing measurement for self-organization health.
- Retrospectives feel repetitive around "we need more autonomy" without measurable progress.
