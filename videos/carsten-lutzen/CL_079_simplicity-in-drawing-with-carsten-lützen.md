# "Simplicity in Drawing" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten shares a key insight from Scott McCloud's *Understanding Comics: The Invisible Art*: simpler, more iconic drawings are more universally relatable and inclusive than detailed, realistic ones. For facilitators who draw during sessions, this reframes the goal — not artistic quality, but symbolic clarity that allows participants to project themselves into the image.

## Methods & Techniques

### Iconic Simplicity in Visual Facilitation (ab 00:45)
- **What:** Using minimal, symbolic drawings (icons) rather than detailed illustrations to represent people, places, things, or ideas during facilitation.
- **When to use:** Any time you are drawing during a workshop, retrospective, or facilitation session — especially when conveying emotions or representing participants.
- **Group size:** Any
- **Duration:** Ongoing practice, not a timed exercise
- **How it works:**
  1. Resist the urge to make drawings more detailed or "better."
  2. Use the simplest possible line representation — e.g., a basic circle-and-lines face instead of a realistic portrait.
  3. Keep icons generic (a person, not a specific person) so participants can project themselves into the image.
  4. Reference visual dictionaries like Bikablo for ready-made simple icons.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=WgLQqb7drx8&t=45

## Key Principles
- **Simplicity enables universality:** The more abstract and simple an image, the more people can see themselves in it. A detailed face represents one person; a simple face represents everyone.
- **Over-engineering is a facilitation trap:** Carsten draws a direct parallel to over-engineering in software development — adding complexity that reduces, rather than increases, value.
- **Icons are functional, not decorative:** In a facilitation context, drawing serves communication and participant engagement, not artistic expression.
- **Projection matters:** When facilitating emotions or states, simple smiley/sad faces invite participants to inhabit the image; realistic faces exclude.

## Facilitation Tips
- Use visual dictionaries like **Bikablo** as a reference for simple, functional icons rather than trying to invent or improve your own drawings.
- When drawing faces to represent feelings, use the most minimal version — a circle with two dots and a curve communicates more inclusively than an expressive portrait.
- Actively resist the impulse to "improve" your drawings during sessions. Simpler is more effective for the audience.

## Best Used When
- Facilitators want to improve their visual facilitation practice without formal art training.
- You are drawing emotions, personas, or situations on a whiteboard or flip chart and want all participants to feel represented.
- Preparing visual materials for workshops where inclusion and participant projection are important.
