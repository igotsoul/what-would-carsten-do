# "What Should I.." with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten shares a short energizer technique called "What Should I Look Forward To From [Name]?" — a simple round-the-table prompt that surfaces positive expectations about each participant. Ideal for situations where the facilitator doesn't know the group, it warms up the room and affirms individuals before a workshop or meeting begins.

## Methods & Techniques

### "What Should I Look Forward To From [Name]?" (ab 00:15)
- **What:** A round-the-table energizer where the facilitator asks the group what positive things they should look forward to seeing from each named participant, going around one by one.
- **When to use:** At the start of a workshop or meeting, especially when the facilitator is new to the group or participants don't know each other well.
- **Group size:** Small groups (exact number not specified, but Carsten emphasizes "smaller group of participants")
- **Duration:** A few minutes — the technique itself is quick
- **How it works:**
  1. Facilitator identifies a participant (e.g., "Yen")
  2. Asks the rest of the group: "What should I look forward to from Yen?"
  3. Group members share positive things they expect or appreciate from that person
  4. Repeat for each participant around the table
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=4oNy9Yogk8k&t=15s

## Key Principles
- Unlocking and surfacing positive resources within the group before work begins creates a constructive atmosphere
- Affirming individuals publicly reinforces their sense of value and belonging on the team
- A facilitator entering an unfamiliar group benefits from both the warm-up and the intelligence gathered about participants
- Credit: technique originates from Carsten's "coach buddy" Yas Kessen from Solutions for Stenmark

## Facilitation Tips
- Use this when you've been "thrown into" a team or environment where you don't know participants — it serves as both an icebreaker and a quick read on group dynamics
- Frame the question positively and keep the energy light — this is meant to be a "great happy way" to open
- Going around the table creates structure and ensures everyone is seen

## Best Used When
- Opening a workshop or meeting with an unfamiliar group
- Facilitating a small team that benefits from explicit positive framing before diving into work
- You want to quickly learn who the participants are and how they're perceived by peers
