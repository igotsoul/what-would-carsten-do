# Chaos Cocktail Party with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten introduces the Chaos Cocktail Party, a retrospective exercise from Retromat.org designed to inject fresh energy into routine retros. Instead of the usual Post-It wall and dot voting, participants physically mingle and competitively prioritize impediments in one-on-one exchanges. The result is high energy and broad participation across the team.

## Methods & Techniques

### Chaos Cocktail Party (ab 00:40)
- **What:** A retrospective prioritization exercise where team members mingle and pair up to compare and score their impediments head-to-head, building a ranked list organically through repeated one-on-one exchanges.
- **When to use:** When retrospectives have become routine and predictable; when the team needs more energy and movement; as an alternative to classic Post-It + dot voting formats.
- **Group size:** Any team size that allows mingling; works best when there are enough people to have 3–4 rounds of different pairings.
- **Duration:** Flexible — 3–4 rounds of timeboxed exchanges plus scoring; roughly 20–30 minutes total depending on round count and discussion length.
- **How it works:**
  1. Each person writes their single biggest impediment on one Post-It.
  2. Participants mingle and find a partner.
  3. Both share their Post-Its. Together they split 5 points between the two (e.g., 4/1, 3/2) based on perceived importance.
  4. Participants move on and find a new partner. Repeat 3–4 times.
  5. Optional: swap Post-Its before rounds start to reduce competitiveness about "winning."
  6. Tally all scores, rank impediments, identify the top 3, and define concrete outcomes for each focus point.
- **Facilitation tip:** Timebox each pairing exchange — without a timebox, conversations can drift off-topic.

## Key Principles
- One impediment per person forces prioritization before the exercise even starts — you can't hedge.
- Head-to-head pairwise comparison is a more engaging prioritization mechanism than passive dot voting.
- Physical movement and mingling raise energy and create more conversations than a static format.
- Randomizing whose Post-It someone advocates for reduces ego investment and competitive dynamics.

## Facilitation Tips
- Strictly timebox each exchange; pairs can easily go deep on tangents.
- If the group is competitive, pre-swap Post-Its so people advocate for someone else's impediment — Carsten notes this is suggested on Retromat.org and reduces "winning" behavior.
- Adjust the number of rounds (3 or 4) based on available time and energy in the room.
- After scoring, focus the group on defining a concrete outcome for each top impediment — don't let the ranking be the end result.

## Best Used When
- The team's retros have become formulaic or low-energy.
- You want higher physical engagement and cross-team conversations.
- The team has many competing impediments and needs a fun way to surface the most pressing ones.
- You want a change of pace from Starfish, Positive Retrospective, or similar static formats.
