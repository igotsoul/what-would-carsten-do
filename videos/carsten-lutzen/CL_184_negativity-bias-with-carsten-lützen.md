# "Negativity Bias" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten explores the negativity bias — our tendency to absorb and internalize negative experiences far more deeply than positive ones. He shares a personal aha moment connecting this cognitive bias to role modeling: negative behavior by leaders or facilitators is absorbed and replicated much faster than positive behavior, making awareness of our "worst moments" critically important for anyone in a leadership or facilitation role.

## Methods & Techniques

### Awareness of the 4:1 Ratio (ab 01:30)
- **What:** A rule of thumb that one negative event requires approximately four positive events to emotionally "even out" for most people.
- **When to use:** When designing feedback conversations, planning recognition efforts, or setting expectations about how much positive reinforcement is needed.
- **Group size:** Individual or team contexts
- **Duration:** N/A — a mental model, not an exercise
- **How it works:**
  1. Recognize that negative feedback or events carry disproportionate emotional weight.
  2. Apply a 4:1 ratio as a starting baseline — four positive events per negative one.
  3. Adjust for context: some people may need a 16:1 ratio or higher, others less, depending on their sensitivity and the domain of feedback.
  4. Consider the topic area: feedback on something someone is passionate about lands harder and may require more positive counterbalance.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=a-GFwNnZ6g8&t=90

### Negativity Bias in Role Modeling (ab 03:30)
- **What:** A single negative behavior from a leader or facilitator can undo the impression built by many positive instances, because observers absorb negative role modeling faster and more durably than positive.
- **When to use:** When coaching leaders, preparing facilitators, or discussing psychological safety and culture change.
- **Group size:** N/A — conceptual framework applicable to any team or organization
- **Duration:** N/A
- **How it works:**
  1. Understand that role modeling positive behavior requires consistent repetition — often four or more instances before it registers with observers.
  2. Recognize that one lapse (cutting someone off, skipping a commitment, behaving poorly) can reset that impression immediately.
  3. Be especially vigilant at your worst moments — exhaustion, stress, and distraction are when negative role modeling is most likely to occur.
  4. Use this awareness to design culture change efforts that account for the asymmetry: you need far more positive signals than you might expect.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=a-GFwNnZ6g8&t=210

## Key Principles
- The negativity bias is not universal — individuals differ significantly in how strongly they experience it, and context matters (a criticism in an area of personal pride hits harder than in a neutral area).
- Constructive feedback exists precisely because of the negativity bias: the framing and delivery of critical messages must account for how much harder negative input lands.
- Role modeling and negativity bias interact directly: leaders and facilitators are judged most harshly on their worst behavior, not their average behavior.
- Culture change requires asymmetric effort — you must generate significantly more positive signals than negative ones just to break even.
- One negative observation (e.g., a senior leader skipping an event) overrides many positive ones in the minds of observers, reinforcing cynicism and permission for others to do the same.

## Facilitation Tips
- When facilitating, be especially careful about moments when you are tired, rushed, or stressed — a single instance of cutting someone off or behaving dismissively can undermine your credibility and give others implicit permission to do the same.
- When coaching someone on giving feedback, anchor them to the 4:1 ratio as a starting point and invite them to reflect on whether the recipient might need more positive reinforcement than average.
- When working on psychological safety with teams, name the negativity bias explicitly — it normalizes why culture change feels slow and why positive efforts sometimes seem invisible.
- Carsten suggests adjusting the "scale" of how feedback is delivered based on how passionately the recipient identifies with the topic being critiqued.

## Best Used When
- Coaching leaders or senior stakeholders on the impact of their behavior and presence
- Designing feedback frameworks or preparing teams for feedback conversations
- Explaining why culture and psychological safety initiatives feel slow to take hold
- Debriefing with facilitators or Scrum Masters after sessions where something went wrong
- Preparing off-sites or leadership events where role modeling is especially visible
