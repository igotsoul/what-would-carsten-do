# "Best Hopes" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten introduces "Best Hopes," a solution-focused opening exercise for aligning expectations at the start of meetings, workshops, or coaching sessions. By asking participants what would make the session awesome, facilitators prime people to actively look for success signals throughout the session and create shared clarity on why everyone is gathered.

## Methods & Techniques

### Best Hopes (ab 00:30)
- **What:** A brief opening exercise where participants reflect on and share what a truly successful session would look like for them — creating alignment before the work begins.
- **When to use:** At the start of any meeting, workshop, or coaching session where shared purpose and expectation alignment matter.
- **Group size:** Works for individuals (1-on-1 coaching) and groups of any size.
- **Duration:** ~5 minutes (2 min silent reflection + time for sharing).
- **How it works:**
  1. Open the session by asking: *"If I ask you in 30 minutes what made this session awesome — what would you answer?"*
  2. Give participants 2 minutes of silent, individual reflection.
  3. Have them write their response on post-its or a virtual whiteboard, keeping it to themselves initially.
  4. Invite everyone to share their "best hope" so the whole group hears each other's expectations.
  5. Keep the post-its or notes visible throughout the session.
  6. At the end of the session, revisit them: check off what was covered, acknowledge what wasn't, and decide whether a follow-up is needed.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=mmEnPBtlrYw&t=30

## Key Principles
- The question is framed in a solution-focused, future-positive way — it primes participants to imagine success rather than problems.
- Making expectations explicit and visible creates psychological safety around "did we actually achieve what we came for?"
- Revisiting the hopes at the end turns the exercise into a transparent accountability tool, not just a warm-up.
- Carsten draws the technique from the solution-focused practice community.

## Facilitation Tips
- Keep the initial reflection silent and individual — don't let early voices anchor the group before everyone has thought for themselves.
- Post the responses somewhere visible so they can be referenced during and after the session.
- At closing, do a quick check: what got covered? What didn't? If something is missing, explicitly book it for a follow-up rather than leaving it hanging.
- Works equally well in-person and online — especially valuable online where focus and respect for time are harder to maintain.

## Best Used When
- Starting a workshop or meeting where participants have different agendas or unclear shared purpose.
- Opening a coaching session to quickly surface what the coachee needs most.
- Any context where you want people invested in the session outcome rather than passively present.
