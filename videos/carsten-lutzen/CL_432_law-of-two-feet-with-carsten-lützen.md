# Law of Two Feet with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten introduces the Law of Two Feet, a ground rule essential for Open Space, Unconferences, and multi-station workshop formats. The principle empowers participants to self-manage their time and attention — moving on whenever they are neither learning nor contributing. The video covers both the rule itself and the facilitator's role in making it visible and culturally safe.

## Methods & Techniques

### Law of Two Feet (ab 00:20)
- **What:** A ground rule that gives participants explicit permission — and responsibility — to move to a different group whenever they are neither learning nor contributing.
- **When to use:** Open Space events, Unconferences, World Cafés, or any workshop with multiple parallel stations where participants choose where to invest their attention.
- **Group size:** Whole-room; applies to any number of participants across multiple groups or stations.
- **Duration:** Applied throughout the entire event, not a timed exercise.
- **How it works:**
  1. Before the session begins, introduce the rule to all participants.
  2. Carsten's formulation: *"If you are neither learning nor contributing, use your two feet to respectfully move on to another group."*
  3. Print or write the rule on a flipchart and post it visibly — Carsten suggests hanging it in multiple places around the venue.
  4. Remind participants that leaving a group is not rude; the remaining group should not pause or react negatively when someone leaves or joins mid-discussion.

## Key Principles
- Participants staying in groups where they add no value drags down overall productivity in open formats — the Law of Two Feet is the antidote.
- The rule has many variations; what matters is choosing one formulation and communicating it clearly upfront.
- Psychological safety around movement is bidirectional: the person leaving must feel safe to go, and the remaining group must not treat departures as interruptions.
- Making the rule physically visible (posted flipchart) reduces the cognitive load of remembering it mid-session.

## Facilitation Tips
- Post the rule on a flipchart in multiple spots around the venue so it stays top of mind without the facilitator having to repeat it verbally.
- Frame the rule as empowering, not dismissive — leaving is an act of contribution to the overall event, not a rejection of a group.
- Explicitly coach the *receiving* side: groups should not stop their discussion or make a fuss when people arrive or leave.
- Decide on your preferred formulation before the event and stick to it consistently.

## Best Used When
- Running Open Space Technology or Unconference formats with emergent agendas.
- Facilitating World Café or multi-station workshop designs where participants circulate freely.
- Any large-group format where self-organization and participant autonomy are core design principles.
