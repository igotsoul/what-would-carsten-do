# "Unknown Knows" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten introduces the Known-Unknowns matrix — a four-quadrant model for classifying knowledge, risks, and gaps in a project or team context. The model helps practitioners become aware of what they know and don't know, enabling better strategies for risk management and discovery. Short and accessible, this video is a practical entry point into a powerful mental model.

## Methods & Techniques

### Known-Unknowns Matrix (ab 00:20)
- **What:** A four-quadrant framework that classifies knowledge into Known-Knowns, Known-Unknowns, Unknown-Knowns, and Unknown-Unknowns — helping teams surface hidden risks and gaps.
- **When to use:** Project kickoffs, risk workshops, product discovery sessions, or retrospectives where blind spots may be slowing progress.
- **Group size:** Applicable to individuals or teams of any size.
- **Duration:** The mapping exercise itself can be done in 15–30 minutes depending on depth.
- **How it works:**
  1. **Known-Knowns** — Things we know we know. Facts, confirmed requirements, risks with known mitigation. These are your solid ground.
  2. **Known-Unknowns** — Gaps we're aware of. We don't have the answer yet, but we know who to ask. Carsten notes: asking the right question instantly moves this into Known-Known territory.
  3. **Unknown-Knowns** — Things we handle through intuition without being consciously aware of why. Carsten recommends surfacing these so they can be examined, articulated, and optimized.
  4. **Unknown-Unknowns** — Risks and questions we haven't even thought to ask yet. These require exploratory work: product discovery, research, or structured facilitation to uncover.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=NckjX9NDxJ8&t=20

## Key Principles
- Moving knowledge from one quadrant to another is the goal — especially pulling Unknown-Unknowns into awareness.
- A Known-Unknown is never stuck: asking a single question can resolve it into a Known-Known immediately.
- Unknown-Knowns (intuition-driven responses) are powerful but fragile — making them explicit allows teams to optimize and share that knowledge.
- Unknown-Unknowns demand active exploration; they won't surface on their own.
- Not everything needs to be a Known-Known, but knowing *who to ask* is always valuable.

## Facilitation Tips
- Use this model as a framing tool at the start of a risk or discovery session to help participants classify what they're working with.
- When a team is stuck, ask: "Is this a Known-Unknown or an Unknown-Unknown?" — the distinction drives very different next actions.
- To surface Unknown-Knowns, try asking team members to explain *why* they made a particular decision instinctively — this externalizes tacit knowledge.
- For Unknown-Unknowns, Carsten recommends pairing this model with product discovery techniques or structured research methods.

## Best Used When
- Starting a new project and mapping out risks and knowledge gaps.
- A team is moving on assumptions and hasn't questioned what they don't know.
- Preparing for a product discovery or exploration sprint.
- Coaching someone on why intuition alone isn't a sufficient risk strategy.
