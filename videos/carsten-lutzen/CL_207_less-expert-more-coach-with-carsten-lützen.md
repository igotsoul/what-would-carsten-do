# "Less Expert, More Coach" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten reflects on his personal journey from expert-minded Scrum Master to coaching-oriented agile coach. He explores how the impulse to jump to advice-giving, appear competent, and "fix" problems can actually get in the way of helping teams grow. The core insight: being helpful means creating space for answers to emerge, not providing them yourself.

## Methods & Techniques

### Shift from Advice-Giving to Space-Creating (ab 01:30)
- **What:** Rather than immediately offering solutions, the coach holds back and instead designs conditions where the team can discover their own answers. Carsten describes this as moving from "I know what we're going to do" to "I don't know the answer, but I can create the space where it can emerge."
- **When to use:** Any time a coach or Scrum Master is tempted to jump in with a ready-made solution, especially when working with a team they are less deeply embedded in.
- **Group size:** Any team size
- **Duration:** Ongoing shift in practice, not a single technique
- **How it works:**
  1. Catch the impulse to give advice or fix the problem
  2. Ask instead: "How can I create a space where they figure this out themselves?"
  3. Use tools like Liberating Structures or playful formats to make experimentation feel safe
  4. Let the team define the problem, feel its impact, and generate their own solutions
  5. Resist the urge to fill silence — let it serve as reflection time
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=-jqbiCXw1j4&t=90

### Using Silence as a Coaching Tool (ab 05:00)
- **What:** Carsten reframes silence from something uncomfortable that needs to be filled to an active ingredient in coaching. Silence gives participants, teams, and coaches space to reflect and grow.
- **When to use:** Whenever a question has been posed and the coach feels the urge to jump in and answer it themselves.
- **How it works:** Notice the discomfort of silence. Resist it. Let it sit. Trust that the space is doing work.

## Key Principles
- **The expert backpack is heavy.** Carrying a history of knowing the answers makes it hard to step into genuine curiosity and coaching presence.
- **Appearing competent is a trap.** The desire to look capable — to team, managers, and organization — can drive premature advice-giving that undercuts the team's own development.
- **People-pleasing and fixing are related.** Carsten connects his tendency to fix problems to a deeper need to meet perceived expectations. Awareness of this pattern is the first step to changing it.
- **Helpful has to be redefined.** Helpful does not mean fixing. It means creating the conditions for discovery.
- **Talking crowds out thinking.** The more the coach talks, the less space the team has to discover their own answers. Less input from the coach often means more output from the team.
- **Working on what adds the most value ≠ being the hero.** High-value coaching work is often invisible — holding space, fostering safety, asking good questions.
- **This shift is gradual, not overnight.** Carsten is explicit that he is still on this journey, which normalizes the difficulty of changing deeply ingrained habits.

## Facilitation Tips
- When standups or other ceremonies are struggling, resist prescribing a fix. Instead, create a safe space for the team to experiment with how they run it themselves.
- Use Liberating Structures and playful formats to lower the stakes of experimentation and make it easier for teams to try new things.
- Before speaking in any team situation, ask yourself: "Will this add more than if I stay quiet?"
- Create a friendly atmosphere where being different from each other is accepted — this is a precondition for genuine problem-solving to emerge.

## Best Used When
- A Scrum Master or agile coach is transitioning from a technical expert background into a more facilitative role
- A coach notices they are talking more than listening in team sessions
- A coach is working with a team they don't know deeply and cannot rely on prior domain expertise
- Reflective practice or peer coaching conversations about coaching identity and style
