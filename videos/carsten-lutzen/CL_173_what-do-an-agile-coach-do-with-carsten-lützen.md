# "What do an Agile Coach do?" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten Lützen reflects on the challenge of explaining what an Agile Coach actually does, offering two memorable metaphors — the janitor and the gardener — to capture the role. The video is a short, honest exploration of identity and communication challenges for Agile Coaches, inviting the community to share how they describe their work.

## Methods & Techniques

### The Janitor Metaphor (ab 00:40)
- **What:** Describing the Agile Coach role as a janitor who maintains the environment — sweeping corners, ensuring equipment works, and keeping conditions as good as possible for the team.
- **When to use:** When explaining the Agile Coach role to people unfamiliar with it; useful for grounding the role in something tangible and service-oriented.
- **Group size:** N/A — this is a communication/framing technique
- **Duration:** N/A
- **How it works:** Frame the Agile Coach as someone who removes obstacles, ensures the team has what it needs, and keeps the working environment healthy — not someone who does the work themselves.

### The Gardener Metaphor (ab 01:10)
- **What:** Describing the Agile Coach role as a gardener who tends to conditions, helps different plants (people/teams) coexist, removes weeds (bad habits), and plants new seeds (new habits).
- **When to use:** When the janitor metaphor feels too passive — the gardener adds the dimension of active nurturing, helping growth, and deliberate removal of what hinders the team.
- **Group size:** N/A — this is a communication/framing technique
- **Duration:** N/A
- **How it works:** The Agile Coach tends the "garden" — nurturing conditions for growth, preventing one element (e.g., a dominant person or bad habit) from taking over, helping teams identify where to collaborate and where to set limits, rooting out bad habits and planting new ones.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=DK1aZEvi8Oo&t=70

## Key Principles
- Carsten acknowledges openly that explaining the Agile Coach role is genuinely difficult — normalizing this ambiguity for practitioners.
- The Agile Coach's primary job is to optimize the environment and conditions for the team, not to do the team's work.
- Removing bad habits ("weeds") is as important as introducing new practices ("planting seeds").
- Helping teams understand where to collaborate and where to draw limits is a core coaching responsibility.
- The role is fundamentally service-oriented and environmental — the coach enables others to thrive.

## Facilitation Tips
- When introducing yourself as an Agile Coach to stakeholders or new teams, having a ready metaphor (janitor or gardener) makes the abstract role tangible and relatable.
- Carsten invites coaches to reflect on and share how they personally communicate their role — suggesting this is an ongoing, community-level conversation worth having.

## Best Used When
- Onboarding into a new team or organization and needing to explain your role quickly.
- Coaching other Agile Coaches or Scrum Masters on how to articulate their value.
- Discussing Agile Coach identity and role clarity in communities of practice.
