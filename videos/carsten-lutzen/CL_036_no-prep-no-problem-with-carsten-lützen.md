# "No Prep, No Problem" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten Lützen presents his five go-to Liberating Structures that require little to no preparation, making them ideal for facilitators who need flexibility. The video is a concise overview of each structure with brief guidance on when and how to use them, serving as a practical starter kit for facilitators building their toolkit.

## Methods & Techniques

### 1-2-4-All (ab 01:05)
- **What:** A structured conversation format that moves from individual reflection to pairs, then groups of four, then the full room — ensuring broad participation before any single voice dominates.
- **When to use:** Any time you have a question to explore with a group, in meetings or workshops. Carsten calls it "the Swiss army knife of facilitation."
- **Group size:** Any size
- **Duration:** ~8 minutes (1 min silent, 2 min pairs, 4 min quads, ~5 min plenary)
- **How it works:**
  1. 1 minute of silent individual reflection
  2. 2 minutes in pairs
  3. 4 minutes in groups of four
  4. ~5 minutes debrief in plenary
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=VZdFL0F0GaM&t=65

### Impromptu Networking (ab 01:45)
- **What:** A structured mingling exercise where participants pair up with someone new and discuss a prompt, then rotate and repeat with a new partner and new prompt.
- **When to use:** As an energizer at the start of a workshop, to warm participants into a theme, or mid-day to re-energize and spark new conversations.
- **Group size:** Any size
- **Duration:** Flexible — typically 2 minutes per round, multiple rounds
- **How it works:**
  1. Ask everyone to find someone they haven't spoken to yet
  2. Give the pair a prompt (e.g., "What do you look forward to in today's workshop?")
  3. Designate who speaks first (e.g., "the person with the most colorful clothes")
  4. Each person shares for roughly 2 minutes
  5. When time is up, find a new partner and receive a new prompt
- **Facilitation note:** Carsten notes prompts can be prepared in advance or improvised based on what you read in the room.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=VZdFL0F0GaM&t=105

### What? So What? Now What? (ab 02:45)
- **What:** A three-stage debrief structure that guides a group from facts → meaning → action, using 1-2-4-All as the underlying mechanism at each stage.
- **When to use:** As a full retrospective or debrief sequence after any experience, event, or sprint.
- **Group size:** Any size
- **Duration:** ~45 minutes (three rounds of 1-2-4-All)
- **How it works:**
  1. **What?** — Collect the facts (1-2-4-All)
  2. **So What?** — Why does it matter? What's the significance? (1-2-4-All)
  3. **Now What?** — What actions can we take based on the above? (1-2-4-All)
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=VZdFL0F0GaM&t=165

### Triz (ab 03:30)
- **What:** An inversion technique where groups first brainstorm how to make things as bad as possible, then reflect on which of those behaviors they're already exhibiting.
- **When to use:** When exploring a challenge (e.g., building trust) and you want to unlock honest, creative thinking by lowering defensiveness through humor and inversion.
- **Group size:** Any size
- **Duration:** Not specified; flexible
- **How it works:**
  1. Pose an inverted question (e.g., "How could we completely destroy trust in this team?")
  2. Let participants brainstorm freely and creatively
  3. Flip the list: "Looking at this honestly — which of these are we actually doing?"
  4. Work with the results
- **Facilitation note:** Carsten notes this can spark "sometimes brutal reflections" or quieter "huh, wait a minute" moments — both valuable.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=VZdFL0F0GaM&t=210

### 15% Solutions (ab 04:20)
- **What:** A prompt that asks participants to identify actions they can take immediately, without additional budget, time, or resources.
- **When to use:** In the "Now What?" step of a debrief, or any time a group risks getting stuck on large, resource-heavy solutions. Focuses energy on agency and immediate action.
- **Group size:** Any size
- **Duration:** Flexible; can be embedded within other structures
- **How it works:**
  1. Ask: "What can you go out and do tomorrow — without extra funding, extra time, or extra manpower?"
  2. Participants identify their personal 15% — small, actionable steps within their control
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=VZdFL0F0GaM&t=260

## Key Principles
- The best facilitation toolkit contains structures that are versatile enough to use across contexts without heavy preparation.
- Starting with individual reflection before group discussion (as in 1-2-4-All) prevents the first vocal person from anchoring the group's thinking.
- Liberating Structures are designed to activate the full room — not just the loudest participants.
- Structures can be combined and nested: Carsten demonstrates using 1-2-4-All three times as the engine inside What-So-What-Now-What, and plugging 15% Solutions into the Now What step.
- Inversion (Triz) is a powerful way to surface honest truths that direct questioning might not reach.

## Facilitation Tips
- Keep these five structures "in your backpack" — they're lightweight enough to deploy on the fly without slides or complex setup.
- For Impromptu Networking, prompts can be improvised based on what you read in the room — preparation is optional, not required.
- Use a playful tie-breaker for who speaks first in pairs (e.g., "most colorful clothes") to add lightness and avoid awkward silence.
- When combining structures (e.g., What-So-What-Now What + 15% Solutions), you can run a rich 45-minute session from just two core tools.

## Best Used When
- You need to facilitate a session with little or no preparation time
- You want to ensure broad participation rather than domination by a few voices
- You're running a retrospective, debrief, or sense-making session
- You want to energize a group at the start of or during a workshop
- You need to surface honest team behaviors without triggering defensiveness
