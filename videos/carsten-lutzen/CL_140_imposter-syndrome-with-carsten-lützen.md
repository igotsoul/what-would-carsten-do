# "Imposter Syndrome" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten shares a personal and practical perspective on imposter syndrome — the feeling of being a fraud despite external recognition of your performance. He explains why agile coaches are particularly susceptible, and offers a concrete, facilitation-rooted antidote: building appreciative feedback into everyday work to slowly dismantle the feeling.

## Methods & Techniques

### Appreciative Feedback as an Antidote (ab 03:00)
- **What:** Deliberately building positive, specific feedback moments into facilitated sessions to help people recognize the value they add — countering the internal narrative of imposter syndrome.
- **When to use:** Any facilitated session, team interaction, or coaching context where participants (or the facilitator themselves) may struggle with self-worth or feelings of inadequacy.
- **Group size:** Any
- **Duration:** Ongoing practice, not a one-time intervention
- **How it works:**
  1. As a facilitator, consciously integrate moments of appreciative, specific feedback into your sessions.
  2. Name what someone did well and why it mattered ("That was really good — it added real value here").
  3. Over time, this trains people to recognize their own contributions and slowly erodes the fraud-feeling.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=jKPb4vBMMCw&t=180

## Key Principles
- Imposter syndrome is not the same as false modesty or fishing for compliments — it is a genuine disconnect between internal experience (feeling worthless) and external reality (being seen as high-performing).
- Suffering from imposter syndrome can be highly stressful; people feel they add no value and that their absence would go unnoticed.
- Agile coaches are especially vulnerable because their impact is indirect and hard to measure — nudging behavior change is difficult to attribute clearly.
- Certain personality types, particularly perfectionists and "lone wolves" who prefer doing everything themselves, are more prone to imposter syndrome.
- Positive reinforcement does not eliminate imposter syndrome but creates counter-evidence that chips away at it over time.
- Carsten openly acknowledges experiencing imposter syndrome himself, normalizing it as a common experience rather than a personal failing.

## Facilitation Tips
- Actively build appreciative feedback mechanisms into your facilitation design — don't leave positive recognition to chance.
- Be specific when giving positive feedback: name what the person did and why it was valuable, not just generic praise.
- Model the behavior yourself: if you want teams to give each other positive feedback, demonstrate it visibly during sessions.
- Normalize the conversation around imposter syndrome — naming it openly reduces its power.

## Best Used When
- Coaching or supporting agile coaches, Scrum Masters, or other roles with intangible or hard-to-measure impact.
- Designing retrospectives or team sessions where psychological safety and self-worth are relevant.
- Working with high-performing teams or individuals who may outwardly succeed but inwardly struggle with self-doubt.
- Onboarding or supporting new team members who may feel they haven't yet "earned their place."

## Related Concepts
- Appreciative Inquiry — the broader philosophy of focusing on strengths and positive contributions rather than gaps
- Psychological safety — creating environments where people feel safe to contribute without fear of judgment
- Feedback culture — embedding regular, specific, positive feedback as a team habit
- Perfectionism as a personality driver of imposter syndrome
