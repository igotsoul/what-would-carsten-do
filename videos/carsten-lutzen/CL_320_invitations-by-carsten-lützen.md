# "Invitations" by Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten explains the concept of "invitations" as used in Liberating Structures and facilitation broadly — how you frame participation requests shapes the entire direction of a group's thinking. A well-crafted invitation is neutral, specific about the problem, but open about the solution path.

## Methods & Techniques

### Crafting a Neutral Invitation (ab 00:30)
- **What:** A deliberate, carefully worded prompt that invites participants to engage without biasing them toward a predetermined direction or solution.
- **When to use:** At the start of any exercise, workshop segment, or Liberating Structure where you want genuine participant ownership of the outcome.
- **Group size:** Any
- **Duration:** Preparation step — not a timed activity itself
- **How it works:**
  1. Avoid judgmental or leading language that frames the problem in a specific way.
  2. If you catch yourself biasing the invitation mid-sentence, name it openly: "What I just said wasn't what I meant — please disregard that." Then rephrase.
  3. Keep the invitation specific about *what problem* is being addressed, but ambiguous about *how* to solve it.
  4. Explicitly open the solution space — leave room for unconventional or "quirky" perspectives.
  5. Clarify what "solved" looks like (success criteria), without prescribing the path.

## Key Principles
- The facilitator should not have an opinion about the direction the group should go — and must actively keep that out of the invitation.
- Pre-conceived ideas are natural, but even subtle framing in an invitation can bias participants before they start thinking.
- Invitations should open the *problem space* and *solution space* (using Double Diamond language), not close them down.
- Divergent thinking needs space — fresh, strange, or unconventional perspectives are often where the most value lives.
- Participants almost always know better than the facilitator what the right solution looks like. The invitation's job is to get out of their way.
- "Slightly abstract" is a useful heuristic: specific enough to give direction, open enough to allow genuine exploration.

## Facilitation Tips
- Catch yourself mid-invitation if you realize you're framing it directionally — name the mistake out loud and rephrase. Don't just quietly correct; acknowledge it so participants can mentally reset.
- Pause before delivering an invitation. Carsten notes he takes a few seconds to check: *Is this neutral? Does this open rather than close?*
- Distinguish between being specific about the *problem* (good) and specific about the *solution path* (bad). The invitation should do the former, never the latter.
- Use success criteria language: "We'll know this is no longer an issue when X, Y, Z has happened" — this grounds the invitation without directing the approach.

## Best Used When
- Designing Liberating Structures sessions or any participatory workshop.
- Preparing exercise prompts where you want genuine co-creation rather than validation of a pre-decided answer.
- Coaching yourself as a facilitator to check for hidden bias in how you phrase things.
- Retrospectives, problem-solving workshops, or any session where participant ownership matters.

## Related Concepts
- **Liberating Structures** — the broader framework where "invitation" is one of the five structural elements.
- **Double Diamond model** — referenced to distinguish problem space (diverge) from solution space (converge).
- **Facilitator neutrality** — the underlying principle that a facilitator's role is to hold the space, not steer the content.
