# "I am..., but I am not ..." with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten Lützen introduces a short energizer/icebreaker called "I am..., but I am not..." designed to surface and challenge stereotypes in a group setting. Participants complete sentence pairs that name a common identifier or stereotype they hold, then immediately counter it with a misconception that doesn't apply to them. The exercise opens conversations about diversity, inclusion, and the gap between labels and actual identity.

## Methods & Techniques

### "I am..., but I am not..." Energizer (ab 00:10)
- **What:** Participants complete the sentence "I am [common identifier/stereotype], but I am not [misconception about that identifier]" — revealing something true about themselves while challenging an assumption others might make.
- **When to use:** As an icebreaker or energizer at the start of a session; particularly valuable in contexts where inclusion, diversity, or psychological safety are themes.
- **Group size:** Scales to any group size; works well in plenary debrief.
- **Duration:** ~10–15 minutes total (1 minute per round, 2–3 rounds, plus debrief).
- **How it works:**
  1. Optionally prime participants in advance so they can prepare their pairs before the session.
  2. Introduce the sentence structure: "I am [identifier], but I am not [misconception]."
  3. Give participants 1 minute to complete one pair — on sticky notes, a folded paper with four fields, or any writing surface.
  4. Repeat for 2–3 rounds.
  5. Share in plenary and debrief: what stereotypes came up? What did people learn?

## Key Principles
- Stereotypes and identifiers often carry misconceptions that people carry silently — naming them out loud reduces their power.
- The exercise works because it is personal but structured: the sentence frame lowers the barrier to self-disclosure.
- Priming participants before the session (giving them thinking time) produces richer, more considered examples.
- Multiple rounds (2–3) allow participants to explore different facets of their identity rather than defaulting to the first thing that comes to mind.
- Carsten emphasizes the value of the debrief — the conversation *about* the stereotypes is where the real learning happens.

## Facilitation Tips
- Pre-prime participants if possible — send the prompt before the session so people arrive with examples ready.
- Use a folded paper divided into four fields as a simple physical artifact, or sticky notes if you want to collect and cluster responses.
- Model the exercise yourself with genuine personal examples to set the tone of openness (Carsten shares: "I am a certified dance teacher, but I am not a social dancer").
- Keep rounds short (1 minute each) to maintain energy and prevent overthinking.
- The debrief is essential — don't skip it. Use it to surface what assumptions the group holds and how identity is more complex than labels suggest.

## Best Used When
- Opening a workshop or team session where inclusion, diversity, or psychological safety is a focus.
- Working with a new team or group that doesn't know each other well.
- Preparing a group to have honest conversations about identity, assumptions, or bias.
- You need a low-prep, time-efficient energizer that still generates meaningful content.

## Related Concepts

- Identity iceberg / Diversity & Inclusion frameworks — visible vs. invisible aspects of identity.
- Check-in rounds — similar warm-up function but less structured around identity.
