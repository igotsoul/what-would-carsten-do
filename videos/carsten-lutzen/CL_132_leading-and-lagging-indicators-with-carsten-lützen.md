# "Leading and Lagging Indicators" with Carsten Lützen.

## Summary
Carsten introduces the concepts of leading and lagging indicators as a framework for helping teams understand whether they are on track. He explains the difference between measurements that reflect what has already happened (lagging) versus signals that can help predict future outcomes (leading), and argues that effective teams need both.

## Methods & Techniques

### Leading and Lagging Indicators Framework (ab 00:30)
- **What:** A dual-measurement approach where lagging indicators measure past outcomes and leading indicators attempt to predict future results.
- **When to use:** When helping teams set goals, track progress, or make data-driven decisions — particularly useful for defining meaningful metrics in agile or Scrum contexts.
- **Group size:** Not specified; applicable at team or organisational level.
- **Duration:** Not specified; ongoing as part of a metrics or goal-tracking practice.
- **How it works:**
  1. Identify your lagging indicators — outcomes that have already occurred and can be measured after the fact (e.g., orders placed and paid, velocity achieved).
  2. Identify your leading indicators — upstream signals in the process that correlate with and can predict those outcomes (e.g., quotes sent, work in progress, customer inquiries).
  3. Use lagging indicators to understand the current foundation and historical performance.
  4. Use leading indicators to navigate toward desired future outcomes and course-correct in time.
  5. Combine both: don't rely solely on one type.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=LDMsgaD_HTY&t=30

## Key Principles
- Lagging indicators are "after the fact" — they tell you what happened but can't change the outcome. Carsten compares this to experiencing lag in a video game: by the time you see the result, it has already occurred.
- Leading indicators are more difficult to define and use, but more powerful — they offer the ability to influence outcomes before they happen.
- Carsten frames the combination as a navigation metaphor: lagging indicators are the rear-view mirror, leading indicators are the windscreen. You need both to drive safely.
- In a sales funnel example, the lagging indicator is orders placed and paid; the leading indicator is quotes or requests for quotes sent — the pipeline activity that precedes the outcome.

## Facilitation Tips
- When working with teams on metrics, help them distinguish which of their current measurements are lagging (historical) versus leading (predictive) — many teams only track lagging indicators without realising it.
- Lagging indicators are relatively easy to define; Carsten acknowledges that identifying good leading indicators is genuinely hard and invites practitioners to share their approaches.
- Prompt teams to ask: "What can we measure *now* that will tell us where we're *heading*?" as a starting question for finding leading indicators.

## Best Used When
- Coaching teams or organisations on goal-setting frameworks (OKRs, team metrics, sprint goals).
- Helping a team move from reactive to proactive performance management.
- Designing measurement systems or dashboards for agile teams.
- Facilitating conversations about what "being on track" actually means.

## Related Concepts
- OKRs (Objectives and Key Results) — leading/lagging thinking maps onto key result design.
- Sales funnel metrics — used as a practical example in the video.
- Data-driven decision making in agile contexts.
- Rear-view mirror vs. windscreen metaphor for retrospective vs. forecasting thinking.
