# "ROTI" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten Lützen introduces ROTI (Return on Time Invested), a quick feedback technique used at the end of meetings or workshops to gauge whether participants felt their time was well spent. The video covers two simple variations and emphasizes the facilitator's responsibility to avoid wasting people's time.

## Methods & Techniques

### ROTI — Return on Time Invested (ab 00:15)
- **What:** A quick closing technique where participants rate whether the meeting or workshop was a good use of their time, typically on a 1–5 or 0–5 scale.
- **When to use:** At the end of any meeting or workshop to collect fast, honest feedback on perceived value.
- **Group size:** Any size
- **Duration:** A couple of minutes
- **How it works:**
  1. Choose a scale — either 1 to 5, or Fist of Five (0–5).
  2. Explain the anchors: 0 (clenched fist) = complete waste of time, no reason to repeat; 5 = excellent use of time, we need more of this.
  3. On a count of three, all participants simultaneously show their score with their hand.
  4. Optionally debrief the results briefly.
  5. **Alternative (silent/anonymous):** Draw a scale on paper and hang it near the door; participants place a dot on their score as they leave.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=CFpI_KoF4Vk&t=15

## Key Principles
- Simultaneous reveal (count of three) prevents anchoring — participants show scores at the same time rather than influencing each other.
- The anonymous dot-voting variant near the door allows quieter or more honest responses.
- According to Carsten, wasting people's time is the worst thing a facilitator can do — ROTI is a lightweight accountability mechanism.
- Even a two-minute closing check generates useful data about whether a session delivered value.

## Facilitation Tips
- Use the count-of-three reveal to ensure simultaneous display and avoid social influence on scores.
- If using the paper scale at the door, place it so it's easy to mark on the way out — low friction increases participation.
- Debrief outliers: if someone scores very low, invite a brief conversation about what would have made it more valuable.

## Best Used When
- Closing recurring meetings (standups, planning sessions, reviews, retrospectives) where continuous improvement of the format matters.
- Facilitating workshops where you want quick signal on perceived value without a lengthy feedback round.
- Building a habit of feedback culture within a team.

## Related Concepts
- **Fist of Five** — voting technique used as the scoring mechanism in one ROTI variant
- **Dot voting** — used in the paper-scale variant for anonymous scoring
- **Plus/Delta** — another lightweight closing feedback format
- **Retrospectives** — ROTI fits naturally as a closing activity
