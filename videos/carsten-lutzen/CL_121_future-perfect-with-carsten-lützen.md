# "Future Perfect" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten Lützen introduces "Future Perfect," a solution-focused coaching technique for one-on-one sessions. Rather than analysing a problem, the technique invites the coachee to speak from a future vantage point where the ideal solution has already been implemented — shifting language and mindset from "what would I do?" to "what did I do?" The result is a more concrete, action-oriented conversation that sidesteps the obstacles that typically block forward thinking.

## Methods & Techniques

### Future Perfect (ab 00:30)
- **What:** A solution-focused technique where the coachee imagines they are already in a future in which the ideal solution has been fully implemented, then describes — in past tense — what they did to get there.
- **When to use:** One-on-one coaching sessions where the coachee is stuck, overwhelmed by obstacles, or struggling to identify concrete next steps.
- **Group size:** Primarily designed for 1:1 coaching; can be adapted for small groups.
- **Duration:** Carsten notes it "doesn't necessarily take a long time" — suitable as a focused 10–20 minute coaching conversation segment.
- **How it works:**
  1. Ask the coachee to trust the process and suspend disbelief.
  2. Introduce a framing device — a "time quake" or "magic wand" — that causes time to jump forward so the solution is already in place (not a miracle, just accelerated time).
  3. Ask: *"What did you do?"* — using past tense deliberately, as if the solution is now history.
  4. Listen for concrete actions and changes the coachee describes ("I completed my Master's degree," "I set up these meetings").
  5. Explore what observable changes resulted from those actions.
  6. Find a clear way to "snap back" to the present, bringing the insights into the current conversation.

## Key Principles
- **Solution focus over problem focus:** The technique deliberately avoids analysing the problem. Instead, it looks for signs that a solution has already occurred and what enabled it.
- **Past tense as a cognitive lever:** Asking "what did you do?" (rather than "what would you do?") bypasses hypothetical anxiety and the "nitty-gritty details" that block action-oriented thinking.
- **Not a miracle — accelerated time:** The key distinction from the Miracle Question is causality. A miracle implies magic; Future Perfect implies that real effort happened, just faster than expected. This keeps the coachee's agency intact.
- **Coach curiosity is essential:** Carsten emphasises that the coach must genuinely buy into the frame and remain curious — the technique falls flat if the coach is not fully present and engaged.
- **Clean re-entry matters:** Rounding off the exercise with a deliberate return to the present is important so the insights can be applied practically.

## Facilitation Tips
- Choose your time-jump framing to suit the coachee — "time quake" works for some; a "magic wand that speeds up time" may land better for others. The specific metaphor is less important than landing the shift to past tense.
- Hold the past-tense frame firmly: if the coachee slips into "I would...," gently redirect — "you've already done it, so what *did* you do?"
- After the exercise, use a clear closing ritual or question to anchor the transition back to the present before working with the insights.
- Carsten has a related technique — **Future Campfire** — which shares similarities and may be worth pairing or comparing.

## Best Used When
- A coachee is stuck in problem analysis and struggling to imagine a path forward.
- Obstacle thinking is dominating the conversation ("yes, but...").
- You want to surface concrete, actionable steps rather than abstract goals.
- A 1:1 coaching session has space for a short, focused visioning exercise.

## Related Concepts
- **Miracle Question** (Solution-Focused Brief Therapy) — the closest relative; Future Perfect differs by grounding the shift in accelerated time rather than a miracle, preserving the coachee's sense of agency.
- **Future Campfire** — a related technique by Carsten Lützen with overlapping philosophy.
- **Solution-Focused Coaching** — the broader framework within which this technique sits.
