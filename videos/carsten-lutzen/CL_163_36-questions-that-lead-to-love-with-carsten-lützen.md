# "36 Questions that Lead to Love" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten introduces the "36 Questions That Lead to Love" — originally a social psychology research tool by Dr. Arthur Aron — and shows how to repurpose them as a facilitation technique for team connection. He demonstrates how a small, well-chosen subset of these questions can be embedded into Liberating Structures exercises like Impromptu Networking or Mad T (rebranded as "Mad Love") to help teams genuinely get to know one another without the technique feeling gimmicky or forced.

## Methods & Techniques

### 36 Questions as an Energizer / Mad Love (ab 00:30)
- **What:** A curated subset of the 36 Questions That Lead to Love used as an energizer or warm-up activity, often rebranded as "Mad Love" when used within the Mad T Liberating Structure format.
- **When to use:** When a team needs to deepen connection, especially before collaborative work. Works well at the start of a workshop or offsite.
- **Group size:** Any — pairs rotate through conversations.
- **Duration:** Flexible; Carsten typically uses 4–6 questions per round.
- **How it works:**
  1. Select 4–6 questions from the first set (the lighter, more accessible ones). Example: "Given anyone in the world, whom would you like to invite to dinner?"
  2. Display questions one at a time as prompts during paired conversations (e.g., in Impromptu Networking rounds).
  3. Do NOT announce upfront that these are the "36 Questions That Lead to Love" — just use them naturally.
  4. After the activity, reveal the origin: ask participants if they recognize the questions, and then share that these are actually the famous questions designed to create a feeling of love between two people.
  5. Optionally place printed copies of all 36 questions on tables so participants can explore more during breaks or take them home.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Fss2xvraX98&t=30

### 36 Questions in Impromptu Networking (ab 01:30)
- **What:** Using selected questions from the set as the conversation prompt in an Impromptu Networking session, replacing generic prompts with questions that naturally build rapport.
- **When to use:** When you want Impromptu Networking to go deeper than surface-level introductions, or when participants already know each other and need fresh connection triggers.
- **Group size:** Any even number; pairs.
- **Duration:** Depends on number of rounds; one question per pair rotation works well.
- **How it works:** Show one question per networking round as the shared prompt. Participants discuss it with their current partner, then rotate to a new partner with a new question.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Fss2xvraX98&t=90

## Key Principles
- The questions are originally from a 1997 scientific study by Dr. Arthur Aron, designed to generate interpersonal closeness — making them psychologically grounded, not just icebreaker fluff.
- Carsten was introduced to this application by Christian and Sperry from the Liberating Structures community.
- The power of the technique lies in the delayed reveal: participants experience genuine connection first, then the reframe ("you may have just fallen a little in love with each other") lands with humor and warmth.
- Not all 36 questions need to be used — a carefully chosen subset from Set 1 (the lightest questions) is enough to create value in a professional setting.
- The questions work outside facilitation too: Carsten genuinely recommends them for personal use with a partner at home.

## Facilitation Tips
- Never introduce these as "36 Questions That Lead to Love" at the start — let the experience speak first, then reveal the origin at the end for a playful, memorable moment.
- Use the reveal as a storytelling beat: "We're here to get to know each other so we can work better as a team — some might even say, fall a little in love with each other."
- Print the full list and place copies on tables after the reveal, so participants can continue exploring during breaks or take them home.
- Choose questions from Set 1 for professional contexts — they are lighter and more universally relatable.
- Be prepared: occasionally someone in the room will already know the questions. Have a simple response ready ("Yes! Do you want to share what they are?") and flow with it naturally.

## Best Used When
- A team needs to deepen connection beyond surface familiarity, especially before a day of collaborative or creative work.
- You want a fun, slightly subversive energizer with a memorable reveal moment.
- Running Impromptu Networking or Mad T and looking for richer conversation prompts than typical generic questions.
- The group is ready for a bit of playfulness and psychological openness.
