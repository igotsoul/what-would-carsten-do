# Retro Planning with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten shares his approach to retrospective planning as a deliberate, observation-driven process. Rather than picking exercises randomly, he emphasizes upfront team observation and intentional exercise selection to create the right experiences for the team's current needs.

## Methods & Techniques

### Observation-Driven Retro Preparation (ab 00:30)
- **What:** Before planning the retrospective, the Scrum Master/Coach actively observes the team — noting patterns, behaviors, and gaps — then selects exercises that directly address what was observed.
- **When to use:** Every sprint, as a standard preparation practice before designing a retrospective.
- **Group size:** Any team size
- **Duration:** Ongoing observation between retros; preparation time varies
- **How it works:**
  1. Walk around the team during the sprint — observe interactions, communication patterns, and behaviors
  2. Identify gaps (e.g., lack of conversation, no feedback culture, low transparency)
  3. Map observations to desired experiences (e.g., silent team → pick exercises requiring verbal interaction)
  4. Select exercises that teach or reinforce the missing behavior
  5. Optionally close the retro with a feedback round directed at you as facilitator
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=DG5x87KdFyI&t=30

### Facilitator-as-Role-Model Framing (ab 02:20)
- **What:** If the team lacks transparency, feedback, or openness, the facilitator explicitly designs the retro to model those values — not just talk about them.
- **When to use:** When there's a gap between the team's current culture and the values you want to cultivate.
- **How it works:** End the retro with a feedback exercise directed at yourself as facilitator. This demonstrates the behavior you're asking from the team.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=DG5x87KdFyI&t=140

## Key Principles
- The retrospective is the most important team meeting — it's where reflection and improvement happen
- Experiences created in retros are the foundation of team evolution; choose them deliberately
- Transparency, feedback, and openness should almost always be on the facilitation agenda
- The facilitator must embody the values they want to instill — "start the change with yourself"
- Know your audience: some teams want the hypothesis/rationale explained; others prefer to just do the exercises

## Facilitation Tips
- Match exercise type to the observed team gap: silent teams need verbal exercises, not more post-its
- If feedback culture is weak, select exercises that practice giving feedback — and model it yourself
- Adapt your communication of the plan to the team: analytical teams appreciate transparency about what you're testing; others just want to flow through the exercises
- Always close with a mechanism to receive feedback as a facilitator when working on feedback culture

## Best Used When
- Preparing a retrospective and unsure which exercises to select
- A team has a visible dysfunction or cultural gap (low communication, no feedback, low trust)
- You want to move beyond "same retro format every sprint" and make each one purposeful

## Related Concepts
- Agile values: transparency, inspection, adaptation
- Psychological safety (as a prerequisite for feedback culture)
- Servant leadership / facilitator-as-role-model
