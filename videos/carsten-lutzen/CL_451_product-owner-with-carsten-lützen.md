# Product Owner with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten offers a concise mental model for the Product Owner role, centering on one core principle: the PO owns *what* the team delivers, not *how*. He explains what this means in practice — sole ownership of backlog prioritization, the ability to say no, and the responsibility to align stakeholders while rallying the development team around a shared purpose.

## Methods & Techniques

### Single-Priority Ordering (ab 02:30)
- **What:** The Product Owner must assign strictly sequential priorities — one item at priority one, one at priority two, etc. There can never be multiple items sharing the same priority rank.
- **When to use:** Backlog refinement, sprint planning, or any stakeholder conversation where competing priorities need to be resolved.
- **Group size:** Product Owner + stakeholders
- **Duration:** Ongoing discipline, not a one-time exercise
- **How it works:**
  1. The PO lists backlog items.
  2. Each item receives a unique rank — no ties allowed.
  3. Stakeholders who challenge the order must convince the PO, not override them.
  4. The PO communicates the *why* behind the current top priority to both stakeholders and the development team.

## Key Principles
- The Product Owner is a **visionary**: they own the *why* and *where* the product is heading, not just the task list.
- **WHAT vs. HOW separation:** The PO owns what gets delivered; the development team owns how. Conflating these two responsibilities undermines both the PO and the team.
- **Backlog = To-Do list with business intent:** Most important items sit at the top. Prioritization method (priority score, gut feeling, etc.) is secondary — maximizing delivered value is the goal.
- **Right to say no:** The PO must be empowered to decline or defer ideas, including those from senior stakeholders, without that being seen as failure.
- **One priority one:** Forcing a single stack-ranked order surfaces real trade-off conversations that stakeholder groups often avoid.
- **PO ≠ less important than Scrum Master:** Carsten explicitly frames PO and SM as equally important, just playing different roles in a high-performing Scrum Team.

## Facilitation Tips
- When coaching a PO struggling with stakeholder pressure, Carsten suggests grounding the conversation in the "maximize delivered value" framing — it shifts the debate from politics to business outcome.
- Help POs practice articulating the *why* behind their top priority, both inward (to the dev team) and outward (to stakeholders) — the message needs to land differently in each direction.

## Best Used When
- Onboarding a new Product Owner or explaining the role to someone unfamiliar with Scrum.
- Coaching a PO who is either too passive (lets stakeholders re-prioritize freely) or too isolated (doesn't communicate priority rationale).
- Team retrospectives or health checks where the PO-team dynamic feels unclear or dysfunctional.
