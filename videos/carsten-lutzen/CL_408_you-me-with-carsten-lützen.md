# You & Me with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten shares a simple retrospective closing exercise called "You & Me," inspired by Retromat.org. Each team member writes a personal commitment toward the agreed improvement points and a thank-you note to a colleague. The exercise closes the retro on a positive, appreciative note while making individual contributions to team success visible.

## Methods & Techniques

### You & Me — Retro Closing Exercise (ab 00:30)
- **What:** A two-part silent writing activity that closes a retrospective: each participant writes one personal commitment and one thank-you note to a colleague.
- **When to use:** At the end of a retrospective, after improvement points have been agreed upon, to create personal accountability and appreciative closure.
- **Group size:** Any team size; Carsten notes a variant where each person writes one note *per* team member works well for smaller groups.
- **Duration:** Short — fits within the closing phase of a retro (implied ~5–10 min).
- **How it works:**
  1. After the team has agreed on process improvement points, Carsten asks: *"Given these focus points — what is one thing you will contribute to the success of the team?"*
  2. In silence, each person writes one post-it with their personal commitment. This can relate to the shared improvement points or be a personal development goal (e.g., "I want to get better at giving feedback").
  3. Also in silence, each person writes one thank-you note to *another* team member — specific, sincere, non-ironic, and not addressed to the facilitator.
  4. Post-its are shared/placed, making both commitments and appreciation visible to the team.
  - **Variant:** Each person writes one thank-you note *per* team member. This raises the bar — people must pay closer attention to how they've actually interacted with each colleague during the sprint.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=SABmj6qglO0&t=30s

## Key Principles
- Small personal commitments are more actionable than large shared ones — every individual leaves with something concrete to work on.
- Appreciation should be specific and grounded in real sprint interactions (e.g., "You helped me with the build pipeline").
- Closing a retro positively is a deliberate choice — it shapes how the team carries the experience forward.
- The thank-you constraint (only to people in the room, not the facilitator) keeps appreciation authentic and peer-to-peer.
- The "one note per person" variant increases team members' awareness of each other's contributions and interactions.

## Facilitation Tips
- Keep both writing phases in silence to allow genuine reflection without social pressure.
- Remind participants: thank-you notes must be sincere — no sarcasm, no irony.
- Notes are only for team members present, never for the facilitator.
- If the team is small enough, consider the "one note per member" variant to deepen mutual awareness.
- The exercise works even when some people get two notes and others get none — but the variant equalizes that.

## Best Used When
- Closing a retrospective and wanting to combine personal accountability with peer appreciation.
- The team needs a boost in psychological safety or mutual recognition.
- You want each team member to leave with a tangible, self-chosen micro-commitment.
- The retro has surfaced improvement points and you want to translate them into individual ownership.
