# Line of Accountability with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten introduces the "Above the Line / Below the Line" accountability model from the book *Pyramid of Results*. The video contrasts dysfunctional blame-avoidance behaviors with a healthy ownership mindset, arguing that accountability should be recognized not only when things go wrong, but also when people proactively drive solutions.

## Methods & Techniques

### Above the Line / Below the Line Model (ab 00:45)
- **What:** A visual framework that distinguishes between accountability-avoiding ("Below the Line") behaviors and ownership-taking ("Above the Line") behaviors, used to spark discussions about team culture and individual responsibility.
- **When to use:** When coaching teams or organizations that struggle with blame culture, learned helplessness, or low initiative. Useful as a diagnostic conversation starter in team retrospectives or leadership workshops.
- **Group size:** Works for individuals, teams, or entire organizations
- **Duration:** Model explanation fits in ~5 min; discussion can expand as needed
- **How it works:**
  1. Introduce the two zones — Above and Below the Line.
  2. Describe Below the Line behaviors: waiting to be told what to do, "not my job" responses, ignoring problems, deflecting blame, covering tracks, passively waiting.
  3. Describe Above the Line behaviors: owning problems (not necessarily as root cause, but as part of the solution), trying something, iterating incrementally if the first attempt fails.
  4. Facilitate a discussion: Where do we currently sit as a team? What behaviors do we want to reward?
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=exjJdiLS8W0&t=45

## Key Principles
- Accountability is not just relevant when things go wrong — it should also be recognized and rewarded when things go well and people show initiative.
- "Owning a problem" does not mean being the root cause — it means committing to being part of the solution.
- Below the Line behaviors (blame, passivity, covering tracks) are often learned responses to environments where initiative was previously punished.
- Change requires rewarding Above the Line behavior: acknowledging ideas, celebrating initiative, and recognizing solution ownership.
- Accountability should exist at all layers of an organization, not just at the top.

## Facilitation Tips
- Use this model to open discussions rather than point fingers — frame it as "where do we want to be?" not "look at what you're doing wrong."
- When introducing the model, Carsten suggests acknowledging that Below the Line behaviors are often rational responses to past experiences, not character flaws.
- Pair this model with retrospective formats that explicitly reward initiative and problem-solving to reinforce the cultural shift.

## Best Used When
- A team is stuck in blame cycles or low initiative after repeated failures.
- Leaders want to shift from a reactive accountability culture to a proactive one.
- Starting a team health or culture conversation in a coaching engagement.
- Onboarding a new team to agile ways of working and ownership mindsets.

## Related Concepts
- *Pyramid of Results* (book) — source of the Above/Below the Line model
- Psychological safety — a prerequisite for Above the Line behavior; people won't take initiative if they fear punishment
- Extreme Ownership (Jocko Willink) — similar framing of ownership without causality
- Growth mindset — connected to the willingness to try, fail, and iterate
