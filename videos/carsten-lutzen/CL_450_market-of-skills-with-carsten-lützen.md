# Market of Skills with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten introduces "Market of Skills", a team-building exercise for new or forming teams to discover each other's strengths, backgrounds, and needs. Each participant creates a personal "market booth" showcasing what they bring to the project, hidden gems from their background, and what they are looking for — framed through a solution-focused lens that highlights positives rather than gaps.

## Methods & Techniques

### Market of Skills (ab 00:15)
- **What:** Each team member builds a personal "market booth" poster with three layers — skills directly applicable to the project, background knowledge that shaped them, and what they are seeking. The group then presents, reacts with post-its, and briefly reflects.
- **When to use:** Ideal for new or recently formed teams who need to build mutual awareness of each other's capabilities and needs. Works especially well at project kickoffs.
- **Group size:** Small to medium teams (implied by the round-robin format)
- **Duration:** At least 2 hours total (Carsten explicitly warns it takes longer than expected)
- **How it works:**
  1. **Prepare (30 min):** Each person creates their market booth — a poster with three sections:
     - **On the counter:** Skills and knowledge directly applicable to this project (e.g., Scala developer, graphical facilitator)
     - **Under the counter:** Background that shaped you but may not be immediately relevant (e.g., music, UML knowledge, photography)
     - **Seeking:** What you want from this project or team (e.g., sparring partner, learning Rust, more feedback)
  2. **Present (3 min per person):** One person puts their booth on the wall and presents all three sections to the team.
  3. **Feedback round:** After the presentation, the rest of the team writes post-its in three categories:
     - "What I absolutely love that you bring to this project"
     - "I think you forgot to mention..." (additions the team noticed)
     - "I think I can help you with what you are seeking"
  4. **Reflection (1 min):** The presenter shares what they heard and liked from the feedback, then thanks the group.
  5. Repeat until everyone has presented.

## Key Principles
- Rooted in **Solution Focused Coaching**: the format deliberately surfaces strengths and opportunities rather than gaps or missing skills.
- The "under the counter" layer humanizes team members beyond their job titles — hidden skills often become valuable later.
- The "seeking" layer turns the exercise into a two-way market: selling and buying are both expected, normalizing asking for help from day one.
- Applies the **Vegas Rule**: everything shared in the session stays in the room. Not everyone is comfortable making their full booth public beyond the team.
- Attribution: exercise introduced to Carsten by **Bent Myllerup from Agile42**.

## Facilitation Tips
- Warn the team upfront that it takes longer than expected — **block at least 2 hours**.
- Strictly timebox presentations to **3 minutes** to keep energy up and ensure everyone gets a turn.
- Give the feedback post-its clear categories so participants know exactly what to write — don't leave it open-ended.
- The presenter gets only **1 minute** to respond to feedback: enough to acknowledge, not enough to defend or over-explain.
- Remind participants of the Vegas Rule explicitly before starting, so people feel safe sharing personal context.
- The booth doesn't need to literally look like a market booth — any poster format works.

## Best Used When
- Onboarding a new team or welcoming new members into an existing team
- Kickoff of a new project where people don't yet know each other's full range of capabilities
- When a team feels siloed and members aren't aware of what colleagues can offer
- As an alternative to dry "introductions" that only surface job titles and roles
