# "Drawing Emotions" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten Lützen demonstrates practical techniques for drawing expressive stick figures and characters for use in graphical facilitation. The video covers how small changes — mouth shape, eyebrows, eye placement, speech bubbles — dramatically alter the emotional expression of simple drawn figures. Viewers learn to build a personal visual language for representing roles and moods in workshop settings.

## Methods & Techniques

### Changing the Mouth to Show Emotion (ab 01:00)
- **What:** Modify only the mouth line of a simple stick figure to convey different emotional states — uncertainty, sadness, or anger.
- **When to use:** Any time you need to represent a person's emotional state on a whiteboard, flip chart, or sticky note during facilitation.
- **Group size:** N/A (facilitator skill)
- **Duration:** Seconds per drawing
- **How it works:**
  1. Draw a basic stick figure (circle head, two dots for eyes, simple body).
  2. Adjust the mouth: flat = neutral, curved up = happy, curved down = sad/angry, wavy = uncertain.
  3. The mouth change alone is enough to shift the perceived emotion significantly.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Cd3cMsRMIhc&t=60

### Adding Eyebrows for Intensity (ab 02:00)
- **What:** Introduce a simple angled line above the eyes to amplify an emotion — particularly anger or intensity.
- **When to use:** When you need to clearly distinguish between "sad" and "angry," or want to add stronger emotional signal to a figure.
- **Group size:** N/A (facilitator skill)
- **Duration:** Seconds per drawing
- **How it works:**
  1. Draw the basic face with the appropriate mouth.
  2. Add two short diagonal lines above the eyes — angled inward for anger, raised for surprise.
  3. Even a single eyebrow line transforms a neutral face into a visibly upset character.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Cd3cMsRMIhc&t=120

### Eye Placement to Change Perceived Mood (ab 02:30)
- **What:** Varying the spacing and position of eyes changes how happy or unsettling a face appears.
- **When to use:** When you want to fine-tune the "feel" of a drawn character beyond mouth shape alone.
- **How it works:** Eyes placed close together with a wide mouth read as more intense or cartoonishly happy; wider-spaced eyes feel calmer or more open.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Cd3cMsRMIhc&t=150

### Role-Specific Icon Sets (ab 03:00)
- **What:** Develop a small personal icon set for recurring roles (Scrum Master, Product Owner, developer, team member) so participants recognize them immediately.
- **When to use:** Agile ceremonies, team visualizations, process maps — any context where roles need to be visually distinct without labeling every figure.
- **How it works:**
  1. Assign a simple visual marker to each role (e.g., cap for Scrum Master, tie for Product Owner).
  2. Draw the same basic figure each time with only the marker changing.
  3. Repeat consistently so the team builds recognition — label only when introducing the icon for the first time.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Cd3cMsRMIhc&t=180

### Speech Bubbles for Questions and Statements (ab 05:00)
- **What:** Add speech or thought bubbles with a single symbol (?, !) to communicate what a figure is thinking or saying without writing full sentences.
- **When to use:** When visualizing team states, problem framing, or capturing a group's questions/concerns on a board.
- **How it works:**
  1. Draw a simple bubble shape attached to the figure's head.
  2. Place a question mark for uncertainty/inquiry, exclamation mark for assertion or alarm.
  3. Optionally add "steam lines" or scribbles around an angry figure for extra effect.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Cd3cMsRMIhc&t=300

### Inanimate Objects as Characters (ab 06:30)
- **What:** Draw everyday objects (e.g., a pencil) with a face and limbs to create a unique, ownable visual language.
- **When to use:** When you want a distinctive personal style or to represent abstract concepts as characters.
- **Group size:** N/A
- **How it works:** Take any simple object shape, add eyes, a mouth, and optional limbs. Use consistently so it becomes part of your visual vocabulary.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Cd3cMsRMIhc&t=390

## Key Principles
- Small changes have outsized impact: mouth, eyebrows, and eye placement are the three levers that carry almost all emotional information in a simple drawing.
- Consistency beats complexity: a simple icon used repeatedly becomes instantly recognizable; elaborate drawings slow you down and may confuse participants.
- Build muscle memory: Carsten recommends practicing a small set of figures until drawing them feels automatic, so you can focus on facilitation rather than drawing.
- Color is optional: a single accent color (e.g., yellow for skin tone) is sufficient; full color is a bonus for breaks, not a necessity.
- Comics are a learning resource: Carsten explicitly recommends studying comics to understand how professional illustrators convey emotion and energy with minimal lines.

## Facilitation Tips
- Label role icons the first time you use them; after that, trust the visual marker to do the work.
- Keep a personal "cheat sheet" of your standard icons (roles, emotions) when starting out — it reduces hesitation in live sessions.
- Simple figures are more inclusive: overly abstract characters can lose the audience; relatable stick figures keep everyone on the same page.
- Body posture (arm position, lean) adds another layer of expression beyond the face — combine with facial changes for richer storytelling.

## Best Used When
- Preparing visual agendas, process maps, or storyboards for workshops
- Live scribing during retrospectives, planning sessions, or team health checks
- Creating recurring role visualizations in agile team contexts (Scrum ceremonies, stakeholder maps)
- Facilitators building their graphic facilitation skills from scratch
