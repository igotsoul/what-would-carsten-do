# "ICE Score" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten introduces the ICE Score, a lightweight prioritization technique developed by Sean Ellis for scoring backlog items and product ideas. The method uses three factors — Impact, Confidence, and Ease — multiplied together to produce a ranking score. Carsten frames it primarily as a discussion starter rather than a definitive answer.

## Methods & Techniques

### ICE Score (ab 00:30)
- **What:** A prioritization framework where each backlog item or product idea is scored on Impact, Confidence, and Ease (each 0–10), then the three scores are multiplied to produce a single ranking number.
- **When to use:** When a team needs a quick, low-overhead way to prioritize backlog items or product ideas and wants to spark structured discussion around them.
- **Group size:** Not specified; suitable for product teams or backlog refinement sessions.
- **Duration:** Described as "super lightweight, easy and quick to do."
- **How it works:**
  1. List all backlog items or product ideas in a table.
  2. For each item, assign a score of 0–10 for each dimension:
     - **Impact** — How valuable is this to users? Is it something users clearly want, or just an assumption?
     - **Confidence** — How confident is the team that this will deliver the intended value and can actually be built?
     - **Ease** — How little effort does this require? Easy = high score.
  3. Multiply the three scores: ICE = Impact × Confidence × Ease.
  4. Sort items by ICE score.
  5. Use the ranked list as a starting point for team discussion.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=pshoNfC_RGA&t=30

## Key Principles
- Multiplying rather than adding means a zero in any dimension zeroes out the entire score — if impact, confidence, or ease is zero, the item should not be pursued.
- The method intentionally favors fast, low-effort experiments: items that are easy to execute and high in confidence score well, which reinforces an iterative, test-and-learn approach.
- Carsten treats ICE Score as a discussion starter, not a final verdict — the numbers create a common reference point, not a decision.
- Scoring forces teams to be explicit about assumptions, especially around user value (Impact) and delivery feasibility (Confidence).

## Facilitation Tips
- Use the sorted ICE Score table as the basis for a conversation, not as a mandate — let the scores surface disagreements and assumptions.
- If a team scores Impact low due to uncertainty, treat that as a signal to do more user research before committing.
- Ease scoring naturally surfaces quick experiments worth running first, which can be a useful entry point for teams new to iterative delivery.
- Carsten also references RICE (a related scoring model) as a variant worth exploring alongside ICE.

## Best Used When
- A team has a large backlog and needs a quick filter to focus discussion.
- There is disagreement about what to work on next and a neutral scoring mechanism would help surface assumptions.
- A team wants to encourage experimentation by making low-effort, high-confidence items visibly attractive.
- Backlog refinement sessions need more structure without adding heavy estimation overhead.

## Related Concepts
- **RICE Score** — a related prioritization metric (Reach, Impact, Confidence, Effort) mentioned by Carsten as a comparable tool.
- **Backlog refinement** — the broader practice this technique supports.
- **Iterative/experimental delivery** — the underlying approach ICE scoring is designed to reinforce.
