# "Noise Makers" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten shares a simple facilitation technique for reclaiming group attention without abruptly cutting off conversations. Using a noise-making instrument (such as a bell or prayer bowl), facilitators give participants an auditory cue that naturally signals when to finish their discussions — respecting cognitive closure rather than forcing a hard stop.

## Methods & Techniques

### Sustain-Based Attention Signal (ab 00:20)
- **What:** Use a bell, prayer bowl, or similar instrument with audible sustain as a soft signal for participants to wrap up conversations. As long as the sound is audible, people may keep talking; once the sound fades, they stop.
- **When to use:** Any facilitated session — large or small — where you need to bring a group back from parallel discussions or breakouts to plenary focus.
- **Group size:** Works for both small and large groups.
- **Duration:** Signal lasts only a few seconds; transition takes as long as the sustain.
- **How it works:**
  1. Tap the instrument (bell, prayer bowl, chime) to produce a sustained tone.
  2. Participants are allowed to continue talking as long as they can hear the sound.
  3. When the sound fades, conversations stop.
  4. Optionally, dampen the instrument (e.g., place the gavel on it) to shorten the sustain and reduce transition time.
  5. If participants finish before the sound fades, continue without waiting.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Av-TNnHa3ak&t=20s

## Key Principles
- Abrupt verbal commands ("Stop now!") leave participants with unfinished thoughts, reducing their ability to re-engage fully.
- Giving people a brief, bounded window to reach closure improves active participation in what follows.
- The auditory sustain acts as a natural, non-authoritative timer — participants self-regulate rather than being commanded.
- The technique is adjustable: dampening the instrument gives the facilitator control over transition speed.

## Facilitation Tips
- Choose an instrument with enough sustain to feel generous but not so long that the group loses momentum — Carsten demonstrates dampening as a way to tune this.
- The signal works best when it becomes a consistent ritual in recurring sessions; participants learn to associate the sound with wrapping up.
- No verbal instruction needed once participants understand the convention — the sound alone carries the message.

## Best Used When
- Facilitating workshops or plenary sessions with parallel small-group discussions that need to reconvene.
- Working with groups where hard verbal interruptions feel disruptive or disrespectful to the conversation culture.
- Running sessions of any size where consistent, calm attention signals are preferable to shouting or clapping.

- Psychological safety — soft signals reduce the authority gradient of "stop talking now"
