# "Christmas Tree" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten Lützen shares a Christmas-themed retrospective exercise designed for end-of-year team sessions. The activity uses a Christmas tree metaphor — star, ornaments, and presents — to structure reflection on team achievements, individual pride and appreciation, and hopes for the coming year. It works both co-located and online, though Carsten prefers the physical version.

## Methods & Techniques

### Christmas Tree Retrospective (ab 00:30)
- **What:** A visual, metaphor-driven retrospective that uses a Christmas tree drawing with three distinct elements — a star, ornaments, and wrapped presents — to guide team reflection at year's end.
- **When to use:** End of year or end of a significant season; when you want a positive, celebratory framing rather than a problem-focused retro.
- **Group size:** Not specified; suitable for teams of varying sizes.
- **Duration:** Not specified; implied 30–60 minutes based on the number of activities.
- **How it works:**
  1. **Energizer:** Ask everyone to complete the sentence "All I want for Christmas is…" — something personal or work-related, just to get everyone talking.
  2. **The Star (ab 01:10):** Using large gold (or gold-ish) sticky notes, ask the team: *What are we as a team most proud of achieving this year?* Place the star(s) at the top of the tree. Consider dot-voting if multiple candidates emerge.
  3. **The Ornaments (ab 02:00):** Using small sticky notes in different colors, team members decorate the tree with things they are proud of personally (e.g., "I learned X"), things the team shipped, or shout-outs to colleagues ("I'm amazed by how you did Y"). Positive appreciations only.
  4. **The Presents (ab 03:30):** Ask the team: *What do we look forward to next year? What's on our wish list?* Write these on sticky notes and place them as wrapped gifts under the tree. These become a forward-looking artifact the team can revisit throughout the year to "unwrap" items as they happen.
  5. **Follow-up:** At the next year-end, revisit the tree — check which presents were opened and carry forward anything still on the wish list.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=N05Cg3m4qJY

## Key Principles
- Carsten frames the ornaments step around both personal pride and peer appreciation — the exercise deliberately creates space to call out colleagues, not just self-reflect.
- The presents metaphor is intentionally forward-looking: it converts wishes into a visible, revisitable artifact rather than a one-time list.
- Carsten quotes a colleague — "feedback is a gift" — to connect the gift metaphor to the idea that looking forward together is itself an act of generosity within a team.
- The Christmas tree artifact can be preserved and physically revisited, giving it more longevity than a typical retro board.
- Co-located delivery is preferred by Carsten for the tactile quality of physical sticky notes, but the exercise works online as well.

## Facilitation Tips
- Use gold or gold-ish colored sticky notes for the star to reinforce the visual metaphor — small material choices deepen engagement.
- Use multiple sticky note colors for the ornaments to make the tree visually rich and easy to navigate.
- Keep the energizer ("All I want for Christmas is…") light and low-stakes — its purpose is simply to get every voice in the room before the substantive work begins.
- Save and display the completed tree somewhere the team can see it throughout the year so the "unwrapping" follow-up actually happens.
- At year-end follow-up, explicitly check: which presents did we open? What's left? This closes the loop and validates the exercise retrospectively.

## Best Used When
- Closing out a calendar year or a major project phase with a team.
- The team needs a positive, energizing retrospective rather than a problem-focused one.
- You want a single artifact that covers celebration (star + ornaments) and forward intention (presents) in one session.
- Psychological safety is sufficient for peer appreciation to feel genuine rather than performative.
