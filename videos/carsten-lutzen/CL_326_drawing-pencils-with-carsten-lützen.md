# "Drawing Pencils" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten introduces a simple drawing exercise he calls an "anti-energizer" — designed not to excite or activate the group, but to gently bring people's attention to a single, focused task. Participants draw pencils or pens step-by-step, then personalize them with character features. The activity is low-barrier, requires no artistic skill, and can serve as a quiet transition into deeper workshop work.

## Methods & Techniques

### Drawing Pencils — Anti-Energizer (ab 00:20)
- **What:** A guided step-by-step drawing exercise where participants draw pencil or pen characters on paper. Carsten calls it an "anti-energizer" — it slows people down and focuses attention rather than raising energy.
- **When to use:** At the start of a session when you want to transition from distracted/scattered to calm and focused. Works well before reflective or creative activities.
- **Group size:** Any — works individually; everyone draws independently at the same time.
- **Duration:** ~5–10 minutes
- **How it works:**
  1. Each participant gets a blank piece of paper (A4) and a pen.
  2. Facilitate step-by-step: draw the pencil tip, the body, the sharpened top (half-circle marks for depth), and an eraser.
  3. Add shadow lines with a thinner pen for depth — Carsten notes the brain fills in incomplete lines.
  4. Invite participants to give the pencil a personality: add arms, legs, feet, a face.
  5. Optional: add a second character (e.g., a pear or banana) peeking out from behind.
  6. Color and add shadows at the end — this can be fast-forwarded or done individually in silence.

## Key Principles
- **Anti-energizer as a category:** Not all warm-ups should raise energy. Sometimes a group needs to slow down and arrive mentally — drawing is a vehicle for that.
- **Low barrier to entry:** Carsten deliberately picks something "known to everybody" and guides it step by step, so nobody feels excluded by lack of drawing skill.
- **The brain fills in the rest:** Incomplete lines still read as complete shapes — this gives participants permission to draw imperfectly and still produce something satisfying.
- **Personalization adds engagement:** Inviting people to define their pencil's "character" (what is it doing? what's its personality?) adds a creative and projective layer without requiring more skill.

## Facilitation Tips
- Guide participants step by step — don't just say "draw a pencil." Lead through tip, body, eraser, details in sequence.
- Keep your own drawing visible (on screen or in the room) so participants can follow along without feeling lost.
- The coloring/shading phase can be done in silence — it's inherently calming and doesn't need facilitation.
- Adding a second object (fruit, banana, pear) next to the pencil character gives faster finishers something to continue with.

## Best Used When
- Opening a workshop where participants arrive mentally elsewhere and need a gentle landing.
- Before reflective exercises that require calm and focus (retrospectives, coaching sessions, strategy work).
- As a low-stakes creative warm-up when the group is unfamiliar with drawing activities.
- When you want a non-verbal, individual activity that still creates a shared experience.
