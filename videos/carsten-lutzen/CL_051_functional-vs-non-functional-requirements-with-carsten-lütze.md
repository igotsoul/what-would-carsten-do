# Functional vs Non Functional Requirements

## Summary
Carsten Lützen provides a concise explanation of the difference between functional and non-functional requirements in systems and product development. He explains when each type applies, how they relate to agile refinement practices, and why non-functional requirements deserve extra attention because they affect the entire system.

## Methods & Techniques

### Distinguishing Functional from Non-Functional Requirements (ab 00:30)
- **What:** A mental model for categorizing requirements into two types — what a system must *do* (functional) vs. how it must *perform or behave* (non-functional).
- **When to use:** During backlog refinement, sprint planning, or any requirements discussion to ensure teams are addressing both dimensions.
- **Group size:** Any team doing product or systems development work.
- **Duration:** Can be introduced in minutes; ongoing discipline throughout development.
- **How it works:**
  1. Identify whether the requirement describes a specific action or output the system must produce — that is a **functional requirement**.
  2. Identify whether the requirement describes system-wide behavior, performance, or compliance — that is a **non-functional requirement**.
  3. For non-functional requirements (e.g., GDPR compliance, response time under 2 seconds), break them down further into concrete functional requirements that can actually be implemented.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zmr_r3Y_x4Q&t=30

### Refining User Stories into Functional Requirements (ab 01:30)
- **What:** The practice of taking a user story and refining it into specific, implementable functional requirements with acceptance criteria.
- **When to use:** During backlog refinement sessions.
- **How it works:** Teams take a user story and add acceptance criteria — Carsten frames adding acceptance criteria as the act of specifying non-functional requirements alongside functional ones.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zmr_r3Y_x4Q&t=90

## Key Principles
- Functional requirements define *what* a system must do — specific enough to be implemented directly by developers.
- Non-functional requirements define *how* a system should perform or behave across all situations — system-wide behaviors.
- Non-functional requirements require significantly more effort and energy because they affect the entire system, not just one component.
- Non-functional requirements are often costly or impossible to retrofit after a system is built — they must be identified early.
- A non-functional requirement (e.g., GDPR compliance) often needs to be decomposed into multiple concrete functional requirements before it can be acted upon.

## Facilitation Tips
- When running refinement sessions, explicitly prompt teams to check both dimensions: "What must it *do*?" and "How must it *behave* or *perform*?"
- Use classic examples like "the system must respond within two seconds" or "the system must comply with GDPR" to make non-functional requirements tangible for teams unfamiliar with the concept.
- Encourage teams to share how they work with both types of requirements — cross-team learning surfaces practical approaches.

## Best Used When
- Onboarding new team members to product development practices.
- Facilitating a refinement or backlog grooming session where requirements are poorly defined.
- Coaching teams who consistently miss system-wide quality concerns (performance, security, compliance) in their planning.
- Running a workshop on requirements engineering or definition of ready.
