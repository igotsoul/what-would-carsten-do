# Simple Ethnography

## Summary
Carsten Lützen walks through Simple Ethnography, the final liberating structure in his series covering all 33 structures. The method involves silent observation of people in their natural context, followed by expert interviews, group sense-making, and prototyping. It's particularly useful for understanding how people actually use products or navigate processes before designing improvements.

## Methods & Techniques

### Simple Ethnography (ab 00:45)
- **What:** A structured observation method where participants silently watch people with relevant experience, take notes, interview those experts afterward, then collectively sense-make and prototype based on findings.
- **When to use:** When you need to understand how people actually behave in a context — e.g., how users interact with a product, how a team runs a meeting, or how a customer navigates a service interaction. Ideal when assumptions need grounding in direct observation.
- **Group size:** Observers work individually or in pairs; broader group reconvenes for sense-making
- **Duration:** 75 minutes to 7–8 hours depending on scope of observation and sense-making time needed
- **How it works:**
  1. **Stage-set (~10 min):** Define the problem or challenge being observed. Identify the site or people to observe. Agree on who observes whom, and what medium to use for notes (written, photos, video — with expert consent).
  2. **Silent observation (~10 min to 1 hour):** Observe without interfering. Take notes, photos, or video as agreed. Aim to disturb the system as little as possible.
  3. **Expert interviews:** After observing, interview the people you watched. Use clarifying questions to make sense of what you saw but couldn't fully interpret.
  4. **Reconvene and sense-make (~30+ min):** Use 1-2-4-All or "What? So What? Now What?" to process the data collectively. Put observations on a wall, sticky notes, or shared digital space.
  5. **Prototype:** Translate learnings into a prototype — doesn't need to be a finished product; a paper prototype or role play works. Carsten suggests a product interaction, customer touchpoint, or meeting format as common targets.
  6. **Iterate:** Repeat the cycle as needed to validate whether changes made a difference.

## Key Principles
- Observation should be as non-interfering as possible — the goal is to see the system as it naturally operates
- Expert interviews after observation fill the interpretation gaps that silent watching can't answer
- Sense-making is a group activity; one person's observations are incomplete without collective processing
- A prototype doesn't need to be high-fidelity — the point is to translate insight into a testable artifact
- The cycle (observe → interview → sense-make → prototype) can repeat multiple times to validate learning

## Facilitation Tips
- Get explicit agreement with the people being observed on what note-taking medium will be used (written, photo, video) before the observation begins
- Budget generously for sense-making — Carsten notes that half an hour passes quickly when working through rich observational data
- Use "What? So What? Now What?" as a sense-making frame: what did we observe, what does it mean (to us and to the expert), what are good next steps
- 1-2-4-All is Carsten's recommended default for the group sense-making step
- Physical wall space with sticky notes works well for sense-making; adapt to virtual equivalents as needed

## Best Used When
- Designing or improving a product and you want to understand actual usage patterns
- A team suspects their mental model of a process doesn't match reality
- You need to build shared understanding across a group before designing solutions
- Running discovery or research phases in agile product development
- Facilitating service design or customer journey work
