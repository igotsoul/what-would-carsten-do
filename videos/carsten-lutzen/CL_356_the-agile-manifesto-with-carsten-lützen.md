# "The Agile Manifesto" with Carsten Lützen.

## Summary
Carsten walks through the Agile Manifesto — its historical context, the four core values, and the often-overlooked nuance that the right-hand side items still have value. He also offers a practical retrospective idea: using the four values as a team reflection exercise to assess where the team currently stands.

## Methods & Techniques

### Four Values Retro Exercise (ab 06:30)
- **What:** Use the four Agile Manifesto values as reflection prompts in a retrospective — asking the team where they fall on each spectrum and whether that's where they want to be.
- **When to use:** In team retrospectives, especially when the team has lost sight of agile principles or is stuck in process-heavy or documentation-heavy patterns.
- **Group size:** Any team size
- **Duration:** Can be done as part of a regular retro slot; no fixed duration given
- **How it works:**
  1. Present each of the four values as a spectrum (not a binary yes/no).
  2. Ask: "Where are we on this spectrum right now?"
  3. Ask: "Is that where we want to be?"
  4. Discuss what's driving the team's current position and what adjustments make sense.

## Key Principles

- The Agile Manifesto was written by practitioners actively doing software development — not academics. The values reflect lived experience, not theory.
- The right-hand side of each value pair is not worthless. Carsten emphasizes: "While there is value in the items on the right, we value the items on the left more." This nuance is frequently lost.
- **Individuals and interactions over processes and tools:** Mature agile teams have processes and tools — but those are derived from and adapted by the team, not imposed. The processes serve the people, not vice versa.
- **Working software over comprehensive documentation:** If it's not in the hands of the customer, it's not adding value — it's waste. Document what's necessary, not for the sake of documentation.
- **Customer collaboration over contract negotiation:** Contracts are at best incomplete and often solve the wrong problem. Getting customer/end-user involvement as early and continuously as possible beats a detailed spec.
- **Responding to change over following a plan:** Plans exist in agile (e.g., sprint plans, high-level roadmaps), but the team retains the ability to adapt when circumstances change — a new competitor, new information, shifted priorities.

## Facilitation Tips

- When introducing the Agile Manifesto in a team setting, don't just present the four values — highlight the qualifying statement ("while there is value in the items on the right...") explicitly. It's the part most people overlook.
- Use the four values as a non-binary spectrum discussion, not a checklist. Teams rarely sit at one extreme; the interesting conversation is about *where* they are and *why*.
- Revisiting the manifesto periodically — even for experienced teams — surfaces drift and realigns shared understanding.

## Best Used When

- Onboarding new team members to agile ways of working
- Teams are over-indexed on documentation, tooling, or rigid process and need a reset
- Retrospectives where the team wants to reflect on their agile maturity or values alignment
- Coaching conversations about the "why" behind agile practices
