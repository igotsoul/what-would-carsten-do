# Servant Leadership with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten shares his personal, lived definition of Servant Leadership — moving beyond theoretical Wikipedia definitions toward a practical coaching philosophy. The core idea: a Servant Leader's motivation is to enable others to succeed by removing obstacles, leaving ego at the door, and focusing entirely on helping people reach their potential.

## Methods & Techniques

### The "How Can I Make You Better?" Question (ab 00:45)
- **What:** A personal guiding question that Carsten uses as his daily motivation as a Scrum Master and coach — actively asking how to improve the people around him.
- **When to use:** As a mindset anchor before entering any interaction with team members, stakeholders, or individuals you support.
- **Group size:** 1-on-1 or team
- **Duration:** Ongoing practice, not a one-time exercise
- **How it works:**
  1. Before each interaction or workday, ask yourself: "How can I make this person better?"
  2. Direct the question specifically: "How can I make you a better Product Owner / Scrum Master / Developer?"
  3. Let the answer inform where you focus your energy and what you remove from their path.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=cMzmzNjaTho&t=45s

### Optimizing the Environment (ab 01:30)
- **What:** Reframing the Servant Leader role as an environment optimizer — removing road bumps (small and large) so people can perform at their potential.
- **When to use:** When a team is underperforming, frustrated, or blocked — rather than adding process, look for what to remove.
- **How it works:**
  1. Identify obstacles in the team's environment (process friction, unclear ownership, missing tools, interpersonal tension).
  2. Prioritize removal of those obstacles over directing work.
  3. Keep fun and engagement as a signal — if people are not enjoying the work, that itself is a road bump.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=cMzmzNjaTho&t=90s

## Key Principles
- Servant Leadership is not a theoretical concept — it is a lived daily practice centered on enabling others.
- The classic inversion: the leader is not at the top of the hierarchy but beneath the team, supporting them.
- Leaving ego at the door is a prerequisite, not just a nice-to-have — the servant leader's agenda must be the team's success, not their own visibility.
- Fun is a performance signal: Carsten explicitly connects enjoyment at work with higher output and quality.
- Acknowledging others as the experts and yourself as a facilitator of their learning is the foundation of the coaching stance.

## Facilitation Tips
- Use specific, role-directed questions rather than generic ones — "How can I make you a better Product Owner?" lands differently than "How can I help you?"
- Treat learning coaching tools (solution-focused questions, NVC, etc.) as part of your Servant Leadership practice — the better your toolkit, the better you can enable others.
- Use the question "How can I optimize the environment?" as a diagnostic lens during retrospectives or 1-on-1s.

## Best Used When
- Onboarding new Scrum Masters or Agile Coaches who need a grounded, non-theoretical definition of Servant Leadership
- Reflecting on your own coaching stance and whether ego or agenda is getting in the way
- Facilitating a conversation with a team or leader about what support they actually need
- Preparing for sense-making workshops on leadership styles in agile contexts
