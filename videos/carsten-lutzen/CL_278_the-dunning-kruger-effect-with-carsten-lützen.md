# "The Dunning Kruger Effect" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten explains the Dunning-Kruger Effect — a well-documented psychological phenomenon describing the gap between perceived and actual competence during skill acquisition. Using a relatable learning curve illustration, he walks through the four key stages people pass through when learning a new skill, and reflects on how coaches and facilitators can use this model to understand and support others.

## Methods & Techniques

### The Dunning-Kruger Learning Curve (ab 00:45)
- **What:** A model describing how confidence and competence evolve — often out of sync — as someone learns a new skill or tool. Confidence typically rises faster than competence early on, then crashes before recovering.
- **When to use:** When coaching someone who is overconfident, stuck in frustration, or struggling to see their own growth. Also useful for self-reflection and explaining team dynamics.
- **Group size:** Applicable in 1-on-1 coaching, team settings, or as a teaching concept in workshops.
- **Duration:** Conceptual — can be introduced in 5–10 minutes as a framing tool.
- **How it works:**
  1. **Start:** Low confidence, low competence — the learner begins with little knowledge and appropriate humility.
  2. **Peak of Mount Stupid:** Confidence spikes rapidly, outpacing actual competence. The learner feels they "get it" and applies the skill broadly — often with poor results.
  3. **Valley of Despair:** Reality sets in. Confidence crashes as the learner realizes their limitations. They may question whether the skill was worth learning at all.
  4. **Slope of Enlightenment:** With continued effort, both competence and confidence gradually rise together. The learner begins to understand the real use cases and nuances of the skill.
  5. **Plateau of Sustainability:** Competence and confidence stabilize at a high, realistic level. The skill is integrated into the learner's toolkit. Over time, if the skill stops being used, it may gradually retire.

## Key Principles
- Confidence and competence are not the same thing — and they rarely grow at the same rate.
- The "Peak of Mount Stupid" is characterized by enthusiasm and overconfidence — the learner treats the new skill as a silver bullet ("one tool to rule them all").
- The "Valley of Despair" is not a sign of failure; it's a natural and necessary phase of learning. Quitting here is the real risk.
- According to Carsten, understanding where someone is on the curve helps explain seemingly irrational behavior — someone acting "unknowingly incompetent" is likely at the Peak of Mount Stupid.
- The model applies broadly beyond tools and skills — Carsten encourages exploring its many applications across different domains.

## Facilitation Tips
- Use the Dunning-Kruger curve as a coaching conversation starter: "Where do you think you are on this curve right now?"
- When someone in your team or group seems frustratingly overconfident, resist the urge to confront them directly. Carsten suggests focusing on helping them make a "safe descent" from the peak — gradually exposing gaps without humiliating them.
- When someone is in the Valley of Despair, your role as coach or facilitator is to rebuild confidence in a way that matches their actual competence — not to inflate it artificially.
- Carsten notes the importance of identifying what triggered the drop in confidence (the "event"), as this helps inform how to support the recovery.

## Best Used When
- A team member or coachee is resistant to feedback or blind to their own skill gaps.
- Someone is visibly frustrated or disengaged after an initial burst of enthusiasm for a new approach or tool.
- You want to normalize the learning process in a team — especially when introducing new practices (Scrum, facilitation techniques, agile tools).
- During 1-on-1 coaching conversations about growth, imposter syndrome, or skill development.

## Related Concepts
- **Unconscious incompetence** — the state of not knowing what you don't know (linked to the Peak of Mount Stupid)
- **Imposter syndrome** — often emerges in the Valley of Despair or on the Slope of Enlightenment
- **Growth mindset** — the disposition needed to persist through the Valley of Despair
- **Situational Leadership** — matching support style to the learner's competence/confidence level (similar underlying logic)
