# "Sprint Review" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten challenges the widespread misconception of the Sprint Review as a "demo" and reframes it as an interactive working session focused on gathering real feedback. He argues that calling it a demo leads teams to over-prepare polished presentations instead of getting hands-on input from stakeholders and end users. He offers Liberating Structures' Shift & Share as a concrete format to run a more effective Sprint Review.

## Methods & Techniques

### Shift & Share for Sprint Review (ab 02:45)
- **What:** A science-fair-style format where participants rotate between stations, each showcasing a specific feature or part of the sprint goal. Stakeholders and end users move around, try things out, and give direct feedback at each station.
- **When to use:** At the end of a sprint as the primary Sprint Review format — especially when multiple features or areas of work need feedback simultaneously.
- **Group size:** Works well with mixed groups (team + stakeholders + end users); scales to larger groups naturally due to the station-rotation structure.
- **Duration:** Fits within the standard Sprint Review timebox (typically 1–4 hours depending on sprint length).
- **How it works:**
  1. Set up stations — one per feature, user story cluster, or part of the sprint goal.
  2. Team members (or pairs) stand at their station ready to demonstrate and discuss.
  3. Stakeholders and end users rotate through stations, try the product, and give feedback directly.
  4. Optionally combine with 1-2-4-All to surface themes and prioritize next steps.

## Key Principles
- The Sprint Review is a **working session**, not a presentation — the Scrum Guide explicitly calls for minimizing presentations.
- "Sprint Demo" is a misnomer that leads to the wrong behavior: over-preparation, PowerPoint decks, and one-way information flow.
- The goal is **feedback and learning**, not showcasing polished work.
- **End users** are the most valuable attendees — if real users can't attend, bring proxies or representatives who understand the user's domain and context.
- Teams often live in a "bubble" — they don't experience the product the way users do, so outside input is essential to avoid building in an echo chamber.
- The Sprint Review is a great moment to **co-create the next sprint goal** with end users and the Product Owner together.
- Do **not** spend days preparing the Sprint Review — the informal, hands-on nature is the point.

## Facilitation Tips
- Replace slides with a working prototype or live system — even a paper prototype is more valuable than a polished deck.
- Use Shift & Share (from Liberating Structures) to make the session inherently interactive and multi-directional.
- Actively recruit end users to attend; if that's not possible, identify the best available proxy.
- Keep preparation lightweight — the session should feel like a collaborative working meeting, not a performance.
- Use the review output directly to inform and ideally co-formulate the next sprint goal.

## Best Used When
- Planning or redesigning a Sprint Review that has drifted into "demo theater."
- Coaching a Scrum team or Product Owner who sees the review as a reporting ceremony rather than a feedback loop.
- Looking for a concrete Liberating Structures format to make agile ceremonies more participatory.
- Onboarding new team members or stakeholders to what a healthy Sprint Review looks like.
