# "Working Agreements" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten explains working agreements as explicit, shared team agreements that define what must be true to move work from one stage to the next. He walks through how to use them across a typical workflow — from idea to backlog, backlog to in-progress, and in-progress to done — and emphasizes that they should evolve with the team rather than stay fixed.

## Methods & Techniques

### Working Agreements via Workflow Stages (ab 00:20)
- **What:** Define agreements for each transition in a workflow (e.g., To Do → In Progress → Done) by asking "What needs to be true for X to move to Y?"
- **When to use:** When forming new teams, or when recurring bottlenecks suggest the team lacks shared expectations around quality or readiness
- **Group size:** Full team
- **Duration:** Not specified; can be a short workshop segment
- **How it works:**
  1. Map out your workflow stages (e.g., To Do / In Progress / Done)
  2. For each transition, ask the team: "What needs to be true before something moves to the next stage?"
  3. Capture the answers as checklists — these become your Definition of Ready and Definition of Done
  4. Start with what you have, apply it, then refine over time — add what's missing, remove what's unnecessary
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=YE0k9mikMTs&t=20s

### Definition of Ready (ab 01:20)
- **What:** A checklist defining the minimum conditions a backlog item must meet before the team begins work on it
- **When to use:** To reduce turnaround time and avoid mid-sprint blockers caused by missing information or unclear dependencies
- **How it works:** Agree as a team on what "ready" means — e.g., design is in place, external dependencies are mapped, acceptance criteria exist
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=YE0k9mikMTs&t=80s

### Definition of Done (ab 01:00)
- **What:** A shared checklist of conditions that must be met before work is considered complete
- **When to use:** Anytime a team needs shared quality standards and clear completion criteria
- **How it works:** Agree on conditions such as: accepted by the Product Owner, tested, reviewed, deployed to test environment — whatever fits the team's context
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=YE0k9mikMTs&t=60s

### Generating Agreements with Liberating Structures (ab 01:45)
- **What:** Use formats like Conversation Café or 1-2-4-All to facilitate the discussion that surfaces working agreements
- **When to use:** When a team needs structured conversation to surface diverse opinions and reach shared understanding
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=YE0k9mikMTs&t=105s

## Key Principles
- Working agreements answer the question: "What needs to be true for X to move to Y?" — they make implicit expectations explicit
- They can exist at multiple stages: idea → backlog, backlog → in progress, in progress → done, or even a Definition of Ready for testing
- Start rigid, then iterate — apply them strictly at first, then loosen or tighten based on what the team actually experiences
- Working agreements are living documents — update them whenever the team agrees a change is needed
- From a coaching perspective, the ambition level visible in a team's Definition of Done or Definition of Ready reveals how high the team has set its quality bar — a useful diagnostic signal

## Facilitation Tips
- Carsten suggests using Liberating Structures (Conversation Café, 1-2-4-All) to generate the agreements in a participatory way
- Use the agreements as a coaching lens: observe whether the team actually lives up to what they agreed on, and intervene when there's a gap
- Don't let the agreements become stale — make reviewing and updating them a normal part of team life

## Best Used When
- Onboarding or forming new teams that need to establish shared norms quickly
- Teams experiencing recurring quality issues, unclear handoffs, or slow cycle times
- Agile coaches or Scrum Masters wanting a structured way to make team quality standards visible and discussable
