# "Dot Voting" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten Lützen walks through dot voting — a quick technique for gauging group sentiment and prioritizing items in retrospectives and workshops. Beyond the basics, he highlights two underappreciated pitfalls (marker bleed-through and the bandwagon effect) and shares practical setup tips that make the technique run more smoothly in physical settings.

## Methods & Techniques

### Dot Voting (ab 00:30)
- **What:** Participants place a limited number of dots (stickers or marker marks) on items they want to prioritize, creating a visible heat map of group sentiment.
- **When to use:** Retrospectives (selecting focus items for the next sprint), workshops (deciding on next steps), or any meeting where the group needs to surface shared priorities quickly.
- **Group size:** Works for small teams and larger groups; Carsten notes the bandwagon effect is more pronounced in larger, less psychologically safe groups.
- **Duration:** Quick by design — exact timing depends on number of items and group size.
- **How it works:**
  1. Collect all items to vote on (e.g., sticky notes on a board).
  2. Cluster similar items to reduce overchoice before voting begins.
  3. Agree on the number of votes per person (commonly 3 or 5).
  4. Decide the rule: multiple votes on one item allowed, or max one vote per item per person.
  5. Distribute pre-cut sticker strips (one strip per person showing their allotted votes) or markers.
  6. Participants place dots; results are read as a heat map.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=HK_BoALpTVU&t=30

### Pre-Vote Clustering (ab 01:30)
- **What:** Before voting, participants group duplicate or related sticky notes together to reduce visual noise and avoid split votes on the same idea.
- **When to use:** Any time there are many items on the board — especially with large flip charts covered in sticky notes.
- **How it works:** Facilitate a brief clustering pass before distributing dots. Carsten notes this is "time well spent" and prevents the group from being overwhelmed by overchoice.

## Key Principles
- Dot voting is a heat map tool, not a precise democratic vote — it surfaces energy and sentiment, not definitive consensus.
- Reliability is limited: a cluster of dots may represent two enthusiastic people rather than broad group agreement. Carsten recommends interpreting results with that in mind.
- The technique is most trustworthy within psychologically safe, familiar teams where social pressure to conform is lower.
- Virtual tools can hide votes until revealed, which neutralizes the bandwagon effect — a meaningful advantage over physical voting in large or unfamiliar groups.

## Facilitation Tips
- **Use pre-cut sticker strips:** Cut a sheet of stickers into strips of the agreed vote count (e.g., three dots per strip) and hand one to each participant. This makes it easy for people to track how many votes they have left without asking.
- **Watch your markers:** In physical settings, marker ink can bleed through sticky notes onto walls. Check pen quality before distributing them — use markers that won't cause damage or leave marks on surfaces.
- **Account for the bandwagon effect:** The first few voters create visible momentum that pulls subsequent voters toward the same items. In groups with lower psychological safety, consider using virtual tools that hide votes until all are cast, or at minimum acknowledge the effect to participants.
- **Set ground rules explicitly:** Before voting starts, state the number of votes and whether multiple votes on one item are allowed. Don't assume participants know the rules.

## Best Used When
- Running retrospectives and needing to select 1–3 focus items from a large set of observations.
- Facilitating workshops where a team needs to align on next steps without lengthy discussion.
- Working with a team that has reasonable psychological safety (reducing bandwagon risk in physical settings).
- Time is limited and a quick, visual signal of group preference is sufficient.

## Related Concepts
- **Overchoice** — too many options slowing down decision-making; clustering mitigates this before voting.
- **Bandwagon effect** — social conformity bias where visible early votes influence later voters.
- **Heat mapping** — the broader principle dot voting applies: making group sentiment visible spatially.
- **Virtual facilitation tools** — platforms that hide votes until reveal address the bandwagon effect inherent in physical dot voting.
