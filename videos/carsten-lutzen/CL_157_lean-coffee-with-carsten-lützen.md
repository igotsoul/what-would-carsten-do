# "Lean Coffee™" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten introduces Lean Coffee, a trademarked facilitation format for structured, participant-driven conversations. He explains how it works step by step and shares how he uses it as a lightweight retrospective or team alignment tool. The format ensures discussions focus on what matters most to participants through silent brainstorming, dot voting, and time-boxed conversations.

## Methods & Techniques

### Lean Coffee (ab 00:30)
- **What:** A structured, agenda-less meeting format where participants generate topics, vote on priorities, and discuss in time-boxed rounds — ensuring conversations focus on what the group actually cares about.
- **When to use:** Informal meetups, lightweight retrospectives, team alignment sessions, or any situation where you want participant-driven topic selection.
- **Group size:** Works for small teams as a single group; larger groups split into smaller discussion clusters (unconference-style).
- **Duration:** Flexible — each discussion round is time-boxed (Carsten uses 8 minutes per topic).
- **How it works:**
  1. **Brainstorm (individually, in silence):** Each participant writes down topics they want to discuss — 2 to 5 minutes.
  2. **Cluster (optional):** If there are many topics, group similar ones together before presenting.
  3. **Quick presentation:** If the group is diverse or topics aren't self-explanatory, give a brief overview of each topic.
  4. **Vote:** Each participant gets 2 votes to cast on the topics they most want to discuss. This builds a prioritized list.
  5. **Time-boxed discussion:** Start with the highest-voted topic. Discuss for 8 minutes, then check in: did the group get what it needed? If yes, move to the next topic. If no, take another 8-minute round. Repeat until resolved or the group decides to move on.

## Key Principles
- The silent, individual brainstorm prevents groupthink and ensures all voices contribute topics, not just the loudest.
- Voting creates a participant-owned priority list — the group decides what matters, not the facilitator.
- Time-boxing (8 minutes) drives focus and directness; people stay on point when they know time is limited.
- The check-in after each time box ("did we get what we needed?") gives the group agency over whether to continue or move on.
- The format is intentionally lightweight — Carsten describes it as "super simple" and usable with minimal preparation.

## Facilitation Tips
- Use 2 votes per person in the voting step — Carsten finds this works well in practice.
- For small, familiar teams, skip the clustering and presentation steps and go straight from brainstorm to voting.
- For larger groups, split into smaller sub-groups for discussion and allow people to move between groups (unconference-style).
- Eight minutes per time box is Carsten's default, but this can be adjusted to context.
- The time box is a focusing tool, not a hard cutoff — the group explicitly decides whether to continue after each round.

## Best Used When
- You want participant-driven agendas with no pre-set topics.
- Running a lightweight retrospective with a small team.
- Facilitating informal meetups or communities of practice where engagement and relevance matter.
- A team needs to align quickly and you want to surface what's actually on people's minds.
- You want to prevent one or two voices from dominating topic selection.
