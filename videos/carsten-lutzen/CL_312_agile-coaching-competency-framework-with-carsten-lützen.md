# "Agile Coaching Competency Framework" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten walks through the Agile Coaching Competency Framework created by Liz Atkins, explaining its eight core competency areas. The model provides a structured view of what effective agile coaches need in their toolbox — useful both for personal development and for helping others grow as agile coaches. Carsten shares his own experience of encountering the framework and realizing he was "unknowingly incompetent" at the time.

## Methods & Techniques

### Agile Coaching Competency Framework (ab 00:30)
- **What:** A visual model by Liz Atkins that maps the key competency areas an agile coach should develop, spanning practitioner skills, coaching stances, and mastery tracks.
- **When to use:** Personal growth reflection, coaching others through their agile coaching journey, or assessing where to invest in development next.
- **Group size:** Individual or small groups (coaching/mentoring context)
- **Duration:** Reflective tool — no fixed session duration
- **How it works:**
  1. **Agile-Lean Practitioner** — The foundation: living the agile values yourself, not just preaching them.
  2. **Professional Coaching** — Partnering with clients (1-on-1 or small groups) in a creative process to inspire personal and professional potential (aligned with ICF definition). No opinion on the "right" answer.
  3. **Facilitation** — Neutral process facilitation for groups. You do not advocate for specific outcomes; you help the group find their own value. Overlaps with group coaching.
  4. **Teaching** — Bringing in new knowledge when coaching alone cannot transfer it. Carsten notes you can then transition back into a coaching stance.
  5. **Mentoring** — Sharing your own experience. Carsten warns: what worked for you may not work in their context — always be context-aware.
  6. **Technical Mastery** — Deep knowledge of technology and software craftsmanship (e.g., engineering practices, automation). You don't need to code all day, but you need a package of knowledge to help engineers improve.
  7. **Business Mastery** — Knowing the business side deeply: portfolio, products, go-to-market plans, processes.
  8. **Transformation Mastery** — Organizational-level systemic thinking: how teams work together, reducing friction, recognizing patterns at scale.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=JbcWosWEA6s

## Key Principles
- The framework distinguishes between stances where you are **neutral** (facilitation, professional coaching) and stances where you **bring yourself in** (teaching, mentoring) — knowing which mode you are in matters.
- You cannot coach someone into new knowledge they don't have yet — sometimes teaching is the right stance.
- Mentoring requires contextual humility: what worked for you in the past may not transfer to another person's context.
- The three mastery tracks (technical, business, transformation) are specializations — Carsten suggests depth in at least one, while maintaining baseline competency in the core stances.
- The framework is useful for surfacing "unknown incompetence" — areas you didn't know you were missing.

## Facilitation Tips
- When in a teaching stance, use deliberate techniques to transition back into a coaching stance afterward if that is the desired setting.
- Be explicit about which stance you are operating from — it affects how your counterpart relates to your input.
- Use the framework as a conversation tool when coaching other agile coaches: map where they currently are and where they want to grow.

## Best Used When
- Reflecting on your own development as an agile coach or Scrum Master
- Structuring growth conversations with junior coaches or aspiring Scrum Masters
- Diagnosing why a coaching engagement feels stuck (e.g., defaulting to mentoring when facilitation is needed)
- Building a personal development plan for agile coaching roles
