# "Four Positions for a Host Leader" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten introduces the four positions of a Host Leader — a model from the book *Host* by Mark McKergow — using the metaphor of a party host. He explains how facilitators and leaders can use these four positions (In the Spotlight, With the Guests, In the Gallery, In the Kitchen) to stay intentional about their role at any given moment in a session or meeting, and connects each position to facilitation practice and Liberating Structures.

## Methods & Techniques

### The Four Positions of a Host Leader (ab 00:30)
- **What:** A framework from Host Leadership describing four distinct positions a host (leader or facilitator) can occupy, each serving a different purpose. The core metaphor is a party host who moves fluidly between roles throughout an event.
- **When to use:** Any time you are facilitating, leading, or hosting a group — workshops, meetings, coaching sessions, or team events. Useful as a real-time self-check during facilitation.
- **Group size:** Any
- **Duration:** Ongoing — used as a continuous awareness practice, not a time-boxed exercise
- **How it works:**
  1. **In the Spotlight** — You are directive and visible. You address the whole group, give instructions, or set something in motion. In Liberating Structures terms, this is when you deliver the invitation. Carsten recommends rehearsing and warming up so you can be confident and deliver the invitation with just the right amount of ambiguity.
  2. **With the Guests** — You step back and become a participant among equals. You listen, empower others to take ownership, and resist the pull to reclaim the spotlight. In Liberating Structures, this means joining an exercise as a regular participant rather than hovering as the facilitator.
  3. **In the Gallery** — You observe from a slight distance without directly intervening. Carsten uses this position during pair or small-group exercises (e.g., Impromptu Networking) to read the room: Are people engaged? Zoned out? Do they need more time? This informs when to step back into the spotlight.
  4. **In the Kitchen** — You remove yourself from the party entirely to regenerate, reflect, and prepare. Carsten emphasises this is the position people most often forget, yet it is essential for energy management, self-coaching, and planning the next intervention.

## Key Principles
- A facilitator is always a Host Leader — adopting this identity helps you stay intentional about your role throughout a session.
- The biggest trap is accidentally slipping into the spotlight when you should be with the guests. Carsten explicitly warns facilitators to remain aware of this boundary.
- The Kitchen (retreat) is not optional downtime — it is where energy is restored and reflection happens. Neglecting it leads to burnout and poor preparation.
- Liberating Structures can serve as a built-in prompt for which position you are in: the invitation = spotlight; running the exercise = with the guests or gallery.
- Confidence in the spotlight comes from preparation and rehearsal — not from improvisation under pressure.

## Facilitation Tips
- Before a session, identify which position you will be in at each stage. Knowing this in advance prevents accidental spotlight-hogging.
- When in the gallery during small-group work, read energy cues: disengagement is a signal to change something; high engagement is a signal to give more time before interrupting.
- When delivering an invitation (Liberating Structures or otherwise), craft it with deliberate ambiguity — enough to activate participants' thinking without over-directing.
- Schedule Kitchen time after sessions, not just before. Reflection on what just happened is as valuable as preparation for what comes next.
- Practice the transition from spotlight to with-the-guests: once you give the instruction, fully let go and join as a participant.

## Best Used When
- Preparing for a facilitation session and wanting a simple self-awareness framework to guide your presence.
- Debriefing a session where you felt "off" — the four positions help diagnose what went wrong (e.g., stayed in the spotlight too long).
- Coaching leaders or Scrum Masters on facilitative leadership presence.
- Introducing Host Leadership concepts to someone unfamiliar with the model.
- Reflecting on energy and sustainability as a facilitator or coach.
