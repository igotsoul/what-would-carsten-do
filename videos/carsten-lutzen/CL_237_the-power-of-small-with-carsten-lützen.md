# "The Power of Small" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten shares a collection of small, low-effort interventions that introduce playfulness and fun into meetings and workshops without requiring big structural changes. He argues that even 5–10 minutes of well-chosen playful activity can meaningfully shift the energy and psychological safety in a session. The video walks through a range of techniques from micro-level tiebreakers to full energizer exercises.

## Methods & Techniques

### Playful Tiebreakers (ab 01:00)
- **What:** Instead of defaulting to "you go first," use a quirky criterion to decide who speaks first in pairs or small groups — e.g., the person with the most colors on their clothes, the largest shoe size, or who traveled the longest to get here.
- **When to use:** At the start of pair/group discussions to reduce awkwardness about who speaks first; doubles as a light psychological safety booster.
- **Group size:** Pairs or small groups
- **Duration:** 5–10 seconds per group
- **How it works:** Before giving a discussion prompt, announce the tiebreaker criterion. Participants use it to determine speaking order, and the unusual criteria often spark a brief non-work-related exchange.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=o97xPve-b1s&t=60

### Personal Quiz Energizer (ab 02:30)
- **What:** Carsten keeps a ready-made quiz about himself on hand. The winner of the quiz then gets to create and share their own quiz about themselves, using questions that reveal personal quirks not normally discussed at work.
- **When to use:** At the start of any meeting or workshop; particularly useful for distributed or mixed teams.
- **Group size:** Any
- **Duration:** ~5 minutes
- **How it works:** Facilitator presents a short multiple-choice quiz about themselves (e.g., "What do I put on my pizza bread?"). After scoring, the winner gets to make a quiz about themselves for the next session. Questions are intentionally surprising or mundane-but-personal to spark conversation.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=o97xPve-b1s&t=150

### MATT-E / Mad Love (ab 03:00)
- **What:** A Liberating Structure-inspired exercise (Carsten calls the online variant "Mad Love") where participants receive a list of prompts and only discover the framing at the end.
- **When to use:** Works both in-person (with tools that support it) and online; good for creating connection.
- **Group size:** Any
- **Duration:** ~10 minutes
- **How it works:** Participants are given a list of questions or prompts to respond to. At the end, the facilitator reveals the context or title ("Mad Love"), creating a reframe moment. Carsten has a dedicated video on this exercise.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=o97xPve-b1s&t=180

### Would You Rather (ab 03:20)
- **What:** Classic party-game format adapted for meetings. Participants choose between two absurd or funny hypothetical scenarios and briefly explain their reasoning.
- **When to use:** Any meeting opener; especially effective with teams that don't know each other well.
- **Group size:** Any
- **Duration:** 5–10 minutes
- **How it works:** Facilitator selects questions appropriate for the audience (e.g., "Would you rather be covered in fur or fish scales? Why?"). Participants share their choice and reasoning. Questions can be tailored to be more or less wild depending on the group.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=o97xPve-b1s&t=200

### Pancake Break (ab 03:50)
- **What:** For fully remote sessions, task participants to go make a pancake during a 15-minute break.
- **When to use:** Online meetings and workshops where people are at home; works as a mid-session re-energizer.
- **Group size:** Any (remote)
- **Duration:** 15 minutes
- **How it works:** Announce a 15-minute break and give the explicit task of making a pancake. Participants get away from the screen, do something physical, get a sugar boost, and return with renewed energy. Sounds strange — Carsten acknowledges this — but he reports it is "really really effectful."
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=o97xPve-b1s&t=230

### Dad Jokes (ab 04:20)
- **What:** Starting or ending a meeting with a single, deliberately terrible dad joke.
- **When to use:** Any meeting; especially useful as a low-risk, low-effort way to end on a light note.
- **Group size:** Any
- **Duration:** 30 seconds
- **How it works:** Facilitator shares one dad joke. The badness of the joke is part of the point — it gets people to smile or groan, which is enough. Carsten has a dedicated video on this.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=o97xPve-b1s&t=260

### Gamestorm-Inspired Facilitation (ab 04:50)
- **What:** Gamifying goal-setting and task framing in larger sessions, inspired by the Gamestorm framework.
- **When to use:** Bigger workshops or planning sessions where you want to increase engagement through game mechanics.
- **Group size:** Larger groups
- **Duration:** Varies
- **How it works:** Carsten references his own facilitation video inspired by Gamestorm. The core idea is using game-like structures to make facilitation and goal-setting more engaging. Works online and in person.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=o97xPve-b1s&t=290

## Key Principles
- Small tweaks can have a tremendous effect — you don't need to redesign the whole session to shift its energy.
- Playfulness supports psychological safety by giving people low-stakes, non-work topics to connect over briefly.
- There is no silver bullet: what's funny or playful for one group may not land with another — choose interventions thoughtfully.
- When a joke or activity offends someone, own it, apologize sincerely, and make it right. The intent to not hurt people matters, but so does the impact.
- Even serious meetings benefit from a short playful moment — pick the energizer wisely rather than skipping it entirely.

## Facilitation Tips
- Keep a personal quiz ready at all times — it costs nothing to prepare and is reusable across different groups.
- Use tiebreakers not just to decide speaking order but as a micro-moment of human connection before the real work begins.
- Match the energy and weirdness of your energizer to your audience; "would you rather" questions can be dialed up or down.
- The pancake break sounds absurd — lean into that. The absurdity is part of what makes it memorable and effective.
- Dad jokes work precisely because they are bad. Don't try to find a clever one; find the worst one you can.

## Best Used When
- You want to increase energy or psychological safety without redesigning a whole session
- Teams are stuck in a low-energy, heads-down, all-business mode
- You're running recurring meetings that have become stale or transactional
- Onboarding new team members who don't yet know each other
- Remote sessions where connection and presence need an intentional boost
