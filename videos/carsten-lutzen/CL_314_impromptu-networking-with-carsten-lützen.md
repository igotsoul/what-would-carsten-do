# "Impromptu Networking" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten introduces Impromptu Networking, a Liberating Structure that works both as an energizer and as a tool to orient a group around a bigger theme. Participants pair up repeatedly, respond to prompts, and rotate partners — creating energy, connection, and shared reflection in just a few minutes.

## Methods & Techniques

### Impromptu Networking (ab 00:20)
- **What:** A structured pairing exercise where participants repeatedly pair up with someone new, respond to a facilitator prompt, and rotate — typically for 3 rounds.
- **When to use:** As an opening energizer to warm up a group, or to prime participants to reflect on a bigger theme before diving into session content.
- **Group size:** Any size; works well with larger groups since rotating partners is easy.
- **Duration:** Typically 10–15 minutes depending on number of rounds (3–5 rounds standard).
- **How it works:**
  1. Ask participants to find a partner they haven't spoken to yet today.
  2. Give the pair a prompt (fun/personal for energizer use, thematic for content-focused use).
  3. Use a tiebreaker to decide who speaks first (e.g., "the person with the most colors in their clothing", "the person with the longest hair", "the person with the most pets").
  4. Each person gets 2 minutes to respond to the prompt while the other listens.
  5. When time is up, the second person shares.
  6. After both have spoken, ask everyone to find a new partner and give the next prompt.
  7. Repeat for 3–5 rounds, observing energy levels to decide when to stop.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=4EzVAIzOnEM&t=20s

## Key Principles
- Many facilitators have run this exercise intuitively without knowing its name — it's a formalized version of something that works naturally.
- The tiebreaker to determine who speaks first serves double duty: it removes friction and gets participants into a playful, creative mindset right away. (Credit to "Barry" for this tip, per Carsten.)
- Watching the energy in the room is the key facilitation skill here — switch rounds when energy dips, not on a fixed clock.
- Prompt design determines the purpose: lighthearted prompts energize, thematic prompts build shared context.

## Facilitation Tips
- Prepare your prompt list in advance — both for energizer use and for thematic use.
- Use creative tiebreakers ("most colorful outfit", "most fish at home") to spark laughter and ease people into participation.
- Don't rigidly stick to 3 rounds — Carsten suggests anywhere from 3 to 5 depending on the questions and desired outcomes.
- Observe the crowd; when energy visibly drops, that's the signal to rotate partners or wrap up.

## Best Used When
- Opening a workshop or training session and needing to quickly build connection and energy.
- Priming a group to think about a shared theme before a deeper conversation or activity.
- Working with groups where participants don't know each other well.
