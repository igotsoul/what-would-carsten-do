# "Dart" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten introduces "Dart" — a simple visual technique using concentric circles (like a dartboard) to help teams evaluate how close they came to hitting a target. It works equally well in-person and online, and opens up rich retrospective conversations about alignment, expectations, and improvement.

## Methods & Techniques

### Dart — Bullseye Evaluation (ab 00:30)
- **What:** Draw three (or more) concentric circles representing levels of success — bullseye (fully on target), middle ring (close but not quite), outer ring (some results but off-target) — and invite participants to place sticky notes where they feel the outcome landed.
- **When to use:** Sprint retrospectives, mission statement evaluations, retrospective-of-the-retrospective, or any situation where a team wants to assess how well they hit an intended goal.
- **Group size:** Any; works for small and larger groups
- **Duration:** 5–15 minutes depending on discussion depth
- **How it works:**
  1. Draw three concentric circles on a whiteboard or digital canvas.
  2. Label the zones: bullseye = fully on target, middle = close, outer = some progress but off.
  3. Optionally add a zone completely outside the circles for "completely off."
  4. Ask participants to place a sticky note where they think the outcome landed.
  5. Facilitate a discussion: Why did you place it there? What would have moved it closer to the center?
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=rHgwdC9aVSc&t=30

## Key Principles
- A simple visual metaphor can carry rich conversation — the dartboard makes abstract assessments concrete and discussable.
- It's okay if things land completely outside the target; misalignment is a valid data point worth exploring.
- The real value isn't in where stickies land, but in the "why" conversation that follows.
- Carsten emphasizes that the number of concentric rings can be adapted freely to the team's needs.

## Facilitation Tips
- After stickies are placed, ask: "What would have moved this into the red (bullseye) zone?" to drive forward-looking discussion.
- Allow stickies to be placed completely outside the dartboard — normalizing this removes stigma around acknowledging failure.
- Works both physically (whiteboard + sticky notes) and digitally (e.g., Miro, MURAL).
- Stage-setting and framing the invitation matters — Carsten hints that *how* you introduce the exercise shapes the quality of the responses.

## Best Used When
- A team wants to evaluate a sprint, project, or session against an original intention.
- You need a quick, low-prep visual check-in that still opens up meaningful dialogue.
- Alignment or shared understanding of "what success looks like" is in question.
