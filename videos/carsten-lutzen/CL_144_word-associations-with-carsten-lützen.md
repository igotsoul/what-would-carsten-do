# "Word Associations" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten demonstrates a creative self-introduction technique using word association. Rather than giving a standard bio, the speaker presents abstract or seemingly unrelated words that describe themselves, invites the audience to shout out associations, and then reveals the personal connection — creating an engaging, memorable opening for presentations or workshops.

## Methods & Techniques

### Word Association Self-Introduction (ab 00:30)
- **What:** The speaker presents 3–5 words or short phrases that describe aspects of their life/identity, invites the audience to associate freely, and then reveals that all words describe the speaker themselves.
- **When to use:** Opening a presentation, conference talk, or workshop where the facilitator wants to introduce themselves in an engaging, non-standard way.
- **Group size:** Any — works in plenary, small or large groups
- **Duration:** 3–5 minutes
- **How it works:**
  1. Choose 3–5 words or short phrases that each describe something real about you (profession, hometown, hobby, background).
  2. Deliberately include at least one word with a more negative or unexpected connotation to create surprise.
  3. Present the words one at a time to the audience and invite them to shout out their associations (mentally or verbally).
  4. After all words have been shared, reveal: "Actually, all of these describe me."
  5. Pick up on associations the audience shared and weave them into your intro — go with the flow.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=folm3tQ985E&t=30

## Key Principles
- Using indirect or abstract cues before revealing context creates curiosity and engagement — people are invested before they know what they're investing in.
- Including a negatively-coded word (e.g., a rough neighborhood, a prison) before the reveal adds humor and surprise, making the introduction more memorable.
- The technique was originally used by a facilitator named Ahmed at a conference — Carsten adapted it after attending that session, illustrating how facilitation techniques spread through communities of practice.
- The audience's associations become part of the introduction, making it interactive rather than one-directional.

## Facilitation Tips
- Choose words that are evocative and slightly ambiguous — bland or obvious words reduce the surprise effect.
- Deliberately include at least one word that carries unexpected or edgy associations to maximize the reveal moment.
- After the reveal, actively pick up on what the audience said and incorporate it — this rewards participation and builds rapport.
- Keep the word list short (3–5 items) to maintain pace and not dilute the effect.

## Best Used When
- You want to break away from the standard "let me tell you about myself" opener.
- The audience doesn't know you well and you want to create an immediate connection.
- You're facilitating a session where psychological safety or energy at the start matters.
- You want the group to be active and vocal from the very first minute.
