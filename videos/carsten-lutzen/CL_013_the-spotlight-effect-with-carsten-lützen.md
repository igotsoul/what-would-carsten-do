# "The Spotlight Effect" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten introduces the spotlight effect — a cognitive bias that makes us believe others are constantly watching and judging us — and explains how it shows up during facilitation and workshops. He offers two concrete facilitation strategies to reduce its impact and create a more inclusive environment where participants feel safe to contribute.

## Methods & Techniques

### Normalize the Spotlight Effect (ab 02:30)
- **What:** Name the spotlight effect explicitly in the room to reduce its power over participants.
- **When to use:** At the start of a workshop or session where participation and speaking up is expected, especially with groups that may feel anxious.
- **Group size:** Any
- **Duration:** 1–2 minutes
- **How it works:** Simply acknowledge out loud that most people feel like everyone is watching them — but in reality, everyone else is busy thinking about what *they* themselves should say next. Naming this shared experience normalizes it.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=WBlrlM9pUrI&t=150

### 1-2-4-All to Lower Visibility Pressure (ab 02:45)
- **What:** Use the Liberating Structures format 1-2-4-All to progressively build participant confidence before full-group sharing.
- **When to use:** When you want broader participation and suspect people are holding back due to self-consciousness or fear of judgment.
- **Group size:** Any (works best with 8+ people)
- **Duration:** 10–20 minutes depending on topic
- **How it works:**
  1. **Individual silence** — give everyone time to think and formulate their thoughts privately
  2. **Pairs** — share with one other person to begin building confidence
  3. **Groups of four** — expand to a small group, further building confidence
  4. **Plenary** — bring insights to the full group
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=WBlrlM9pUrI&t=165

### Go First Imperfectly (ab 03:15)
- **What:** As a facilitator, deliberately model imperfection by making mistakes openly and calling them out.
- **When to use:** Whenever you want to signal psychological safety and lower the bar for participation.
- **Group size:** Any
- **Duration:** Ongoing practice, not a discrete activity
- **How it works:** Rather than striving for perfectly polished language, allow yourself to fumble, self-correct out loud ("Oops — what I actually meant was…"), and move on. This role models that mistakes are acceptable and helps shift the group's spotlight effect.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=WBlrlM9pUrI&t=195

## Key Principles
- The spotlight effect is a cognitive bias: we believe others are watching and judging us far more than they actually are.
- We feel our nerves viscerally — so we assume others can see them too. They usually can't.
- We over-expect that others notice our mistakes and share our inner critic's opinions.
- In reality, everyone else is preoccupied with their own thoughts: "What should I say next? Do I look stupid?"
- Facilitators are not exempt — Carsten notes he often falls victim to it himself.
- Reducing the spotlight effect is not just personal comfort; it directly improves inclusion and participation quality.

## Facilitation Tips
- Name the bias explicitly — saying it out loud disarms it for the whole room.
- Use progressive structures (like 1-2-4-All) to build confidence gradually before asking for public contribution.
- Role model imperfection: correct yourself visibly and casually rather than hiding or glossing over mistakes.
- Avoid the trap of over-polishing your facilitation language — some fumbling is humanizing and trust-building.

## Best Used When
- Opening workshops where psychological safety or participation is a concern
- Working with groups that seem hesitant to speak up or share
- Facilitating retrospectives or feedback sessions where vulnerability is required
- Onboarding new facilitators who struggle with performance anxiety
