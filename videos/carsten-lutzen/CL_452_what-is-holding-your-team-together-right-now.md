# What is holding your team together right now?

## Summary
Carsten explores how teams and coaches often suffer from "expert blindness" — taking for granted the things that already work well. In times of high uncertainty, he argues the most valuable focus is identifying what holds a team together and protecting it, rather than defaulting to control or chasing change. He offers three practical questions to surface this hidden glue.

## Methods & Techniques

### Three Questions to Uncover Team Glue (ab 01:30)
- **What:** A set of three reflective questions designed to surface implicit, taken-for-granted practices that give a team stability and cohesion — things that often go unnoticed precisely because they work so well.
- **When to use:** During periods of uncertainty, organizational change, or when a team is being asked to adapt rapidly. Also useful onboarding new members or during retrospectives.
- **Group size:** Works for any team size; suitable for 1-on-1 coaching or group reflection
- **Duration:** Can be used as a quick retrospective activity or a deeper coaching conversation (5–30 min depending on context)
- **How it works:**
  1. **"When you onboard a new team member, what do you often forget to mention?"** — What you forget to mention is likely something so embedded and functional that it's invisible. That's the glue.
  2. **"Where do you harvest reliable and repeatable value?"** — Identify practices that consistently produce predictable output. These need to be protected, especially when capacity is under pressure.
  3. **"What part of our process should we keep exactly as is?"** — Explicitly name what should not change, even as experiments and improvements happen around it.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=DWS84lHAjnk&t=90

### "Continue" Column in Retrospectives (ab 03:40)
- **What:** A dedicated column in a retrospective format that captures what the team should actively keep doing — not just what to change or experiment with.
- **When to use:** Any retrospective, but especially valuable when a team is under pressure to change or when stability is at risk.
- **Group size:** Any
- **Duration:** Embedded within a retro (5–10 min of focus)
- **How it works:** Add a "Continue" column alongside the typical retrospective columns. Treat it as a deliberate safeguard conversation: what must we protect and consciously keep doing, even under pressure?
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=DWS84lHAjnk&t=220

## Key Principles
- **Expert blindness:** The more skilled we become, the more we stop noticing what is already working — we take it for granted. This is a coaching blind spot.
- **Stability as strategy:** In uncertain times, doubling down on control or chasing every new trend are common but potentially harmful responses. Identifying and protecting what already works is an underutilized alternative.
- **Solution-focused lens:** Carsten grounds this in solution-focused coaching — always asking what is already working and how to build on it, rather than starting from problems.
- **The invisible glue:** A team's cohesion often lives in practices and norms so natural they never get named — until they disappear.
- **Protect before you improve:** Before running experiments and changes, teams need a clear sense of what is non-negotiable to protect.

## Facilitation Tips
- Use the onboarding question as a warm entry point — it feels concrete and story-like, and quickly surfaces implicit norms without feeling threatening.
- Frame the "what should we keep exactly as is" question explicitly in retros — it gives permission to name value in the status quo, which teams rarely feel invited to do.
- As a coach, notice when you yourself have grown blind to what's working in a team. Apply these questions to your own coaching practice.
- Resist the pull to make the "continue" conversation less interesting than the "change" conversation — frame it as protection and stewardship, not stagnation.

## Best Used When
- A team is experiencing rapid change, uncertainty, or pressure to adapt
- A coach or team lead is preparing to onboard a new team member and wants to surface implicit culture
- Retrospectives feel stuck on problems and experiments, with no recognition of what's already strong
- An organization is pushing change initiatives that risk destabilizing high-performing team habits
