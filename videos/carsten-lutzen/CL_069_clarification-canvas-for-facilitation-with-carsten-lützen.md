# "Clarification Canvas for Facilitation" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten introduces the Clarification Canvas for Facilitation, a tool developed by Krina Kelson, designed to help facilitators plan and align on the key elements of any workshop, meeting, or event before executing it. The canvas guides facilitators through a structured set of questions — from identifying the sponsor to clarifying the outcome and the "why" — ensuring that all critical assumptions are made explicit and agreed upon upfront.

## Methods & Techniques

### Clarification Canvas for Facilitation (ab 00:30)
- **What:** A structured canvas with multiple fields that a facilitator works through when planning a facilitation event. It surfaces assumptions, aligns sponsor and participants, and validates whether facilitation is even the right approach.
- **When to use:** Before planning or running any workshop, meeting, or facilitation event — especially when commissioned by a sponsor or stakeholder.
- **Group size:** Used by the facilitator (possibly together with the sponsor), not a group exercise per se.
- **Duration:** Ongoing reference tool during planning; not a one-time fill-in.
- **How it works:**
  1. **Sponsor** — Identify who is commissioning the event and establish a clear facilitation contract with them. What is their responsibility?
  2. **Participants** — Who needs to be in the room to achieve the desired outcome? Validate that the right people will actually attend.
  3. **Problem/Context** — What problem is being addressed, for whom? Is facilitation actually the right approach? If not, stop and redirect the sponsor.
  4. **Output** — What tangible deliverables or artefacts should the session produce (e.g., a plan, a decision, a prioritised list)?
  5. **Outcome/Value (the Why)** — What goals, behaviour changes, or deeper value is this event meant to create? This is the most important field. Articulate it clearly or people won't show up.
  6. **Location/Environment** — Does the sensitivity or nature of the topic require a different setting (offsite, online, neutral space)?
  7. **Iteration** — The canvas is not a one-time checklist; as activities and outputs are designed, revisit the outcome fields to check alignment.

## Key Principles
- Facilitation is not always the answer — recognising when something else is needed is a sign of professional maturity, not failure.
- The "why" (outcome value) is the most critical field: if you cannot articulate why participants should care, they won't show up or engage.
- The canvas is iterative, not linear — designing activities may cause you to revisit and revise the intended outcomes.
- All elements of the canvas are interconnected; a change in one field ripples through the others.
- A solid facilitation contract with the sponsor is foundational to everything else.
- Credit for the canvas goes to **Krina Kelson**, who developed it and taught it in a course Carsten attended.

## Facilitation Tips
- Use the canvas as a living document throughout planning, not just at the start.
- When the sponsor arrives with a preset format ("we need a two-hour workshop"), use the canvas to test whether that format actually fits the problem and outcome.
- Check in with participants — not just the sponsor — to gather their perspective on the "why."
- If the canvas reveals that facilitation is not the right tool, use that as a coaching moment with the sponsor rather than treating it as a failure.

## Best Used When
- You are commissioned by a sponsor to facilitate a session and need to align on expectations before designing anything.
- There is ambiguity about what the session should achieve or who should attend.
- You want to avoid designing a workshop that solves the wrong problem or delivers outputs nobody actually needs.
- You are onboarding a new client or working with a sponsor who has not worked with a facilitator before.
