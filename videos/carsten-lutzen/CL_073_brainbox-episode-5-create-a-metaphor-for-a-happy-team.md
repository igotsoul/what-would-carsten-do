# Brainbox Episode 5 - Create a Metaphor for a Happy Team

## Summary
This is a short creative drawing exercise episode where Carsten Lützen and guests have exactly 7 minutes to visually represent a randomly selected prompt — in this case, "create a metaphor for a happy team." Each participant draws independently without seeing the others' work, then reveals results at the end. The series is designed to spark curiosity and lower the barrier to using visual communication in professional contexts.

## Methods & Techniques

### Brainbox Drawing Sprint (ab 00:30)
- **What:** A timed (7-minute) independent drawing exercise where participants each illustrate the same randomly selected prompt, then share and compare results.
- **When to use:** As a creative warm-up, icebreaker, or team energizer; useful for getting people comfortable with visual thinking and metaphorical reasoning.
- **Group size:** 2 or more participants (works individually or in small groups)
- **Duration:** 7 minutes drawing + reveal/debrief
- **How it works:**
  1. Collect a pool of prompts in advance (participants can submit their own).
  2. Randomly select one prompt — everyone sees it at the same time.
  3. Start a 7-minute timer. Everyone draws independently and privately.
  4. When time is up, reveal all drawings simultaneously.
  5. Invite reflection: "What did you think of when you saw the prompt?"

### Metaphor Generation for Team Concepts (ab 01:30)
- **What:** A thinking technique where you deliberately seek an indirect, creative image (metaphor) rather than a literal representation of an abstract concept like "team" or "happiness."
- **When to use:** When facilitating conversations about team culture, values, or identity — metaphors surface richer shared meaning than direct descriptions.
- **How it works:** Carsten models the thinking process aloud — first listing literal associations (people rowing, Pokémon teams), then pushing toward more abstract or unexpected combinations (a burger combo as a team, a battery representing energy). The goal is to commit to one metaphor and develop it visually within the time constraint.

## Key Principles
- The intent is not artistic quality — "it's not art, it's just simply drawing to illustrate something." Lowering this bar is essential to psychological safety in visual exercises.
- Thinking out loud through word associations (team → collaboration → energy → smiles → battery) is a legitimate and productive ideation method before committing to a visual.
- Metaphors work best when they are not the first, most obvious image — the extra cognitive step creates more interesting and memorable representations.
- Independent parallel creation (no peeking) ensures diverse outputs and richer comparison during debrief.
- Randomised prompts remove ownership anxiety — no one chose the topic, so no one is precious about the "right" answer.
- Time pressure is generative, not punishing — constraints force commitment and prevent over-thinking.

## Facilitation Tips
- Invite participants to submit prompts in advance; this increases buy-in and generates a larger, more varied prompt pool.
- Model the messy thinking process openly, as Carsten does — verbalising dead ends ("beer with pizza doesn't really give the impression of team") normalises iteration.
- Keep the debrief question open and curiosity-led: "What did YOU think of when you saw this prompt?" rather than asking people to explain or defend their drawing.
- The reveal moment (seeing what everyone drew for the same prompt) is the payoff — make it simultaneous for maximum impact.

## Best Used When
- You want to warm up a group for creative or visual work without requiring drawing skills.
- You are exploring shared mental models of abstract team concepts (happiness, trust, collaboration, safety).
- You need a short, low-prep energiser that still produces meaningful conversation.
- You are building a culture of visual communication in an agile or coaching context.
