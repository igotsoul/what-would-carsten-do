# "Raising Hands" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten shares a simple, low-overhead technique for self-organized pair formation during exercises like Impromptu Networking or Critical Uncertainties. Rather than the facilitator directing partner changes, participants signal readiness by raising their hand and pointing to match with another available person. The technique reduces facilitator load and increases group autonomy.

## Methods & Techniques

### Raising Hands Pairing (ab 00:30)
- **What:** A self-organizing signal system where participants raise their hand when they need a new partner, then point to another raised hand to confirm the pairing.
- **When to use:** Any exercise requiring repeated pair formation or partner swaps — e.g., Impromptu Networking, Eco Cycle, Critical Uncertainties, or any open reflection format.
- **Group size:** Works for any group size where participants are spread around a room and need to find each other visually.
- **Duration:** Adds no extra time — pairing happens organically as participants finish conversations.
- **How it works:**
  1. When a participant is ready for a new partner, they raise their hand visibly.
  2. They scan the room for another raised hand.
  3. When two people spot each other, they point at one another to confirm the match.
  4. Both walk toward each other and begin their next conversation.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=SUW7QQF10PU&t=30

## Key Principles
- Self-organization is preferable to facilitator-directed coordination when the group can manage it themselves.
- Visual signals lower the cognitive load of pair formation — no need for verbal announcements or counting.
- Simple, body-based cues (raise hand, point) are universally readable and require no explanation beyond a quick demo.
- Carsten credits this technique to Barry, emphasizing the value of borrowing and sharing small facilitation gems.

## Facilitation Tips
- Introduce the technique with a brief demo before the exercise starts so participants know what a raised hand means.
- As facilitator, you can step back once the signal system is in place — the group handles partner changes at their own pace.
- Works best in a room where participants can see each other; less effective in large venues or online settings without adaptation.

## Best Used When
- Running Liberating Structures or similar exercises that involve multiple rounds of paired conversation.
- You want to reduce facilitator interruptions and let the group self-pace their partner transitions.
- The group is physically co-located and can scan the room visually.
