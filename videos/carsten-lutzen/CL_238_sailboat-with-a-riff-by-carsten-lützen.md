# Sailboat... with a riff

## Summary
Carsten introduces the classic Sailboat retrospective exercise and adds a clever third element — the **reef** — to the traditional wind/anchor format. This twist extends the exercise beyond reflection on the past into forward-looking risk identification, making it useful not just for retros but also as a feed-in to sprint planning.

## Methods & Techniques

### Sailboat Retrospective (ab 01:00)
- **What:** A metaphor-based retrospective exercise where the team's product or project is represented as a sailboat. Participants identify forces helping and hindering progress, and — in Carsten's extended version — upcoming risks.
- **When to use:** During the "Gather Data" phase of a retrospective, after the energizer and stage-setting.
- **Group size:** Any team size (standard retro format)
- **Duration:** Not specified; fits within a standard retrospective slot
- **How it works:**
  1. Draw a sailboat on a whiteboard or flip chart.
  2. Ask the team: **What is the wind?** — Things that are working, pushing the team forward, that should be continued.
  3. Ask the team: **What are the anchors?** — Things holding the team back, not working, that should be stopped.
  4. *(Carsten's riff)* Ask the team: **What are the reefs ahead?** — Potential risks or obstacles in the upcoming sprint/iteration the team should be aware of.
  5. Use the reefs as input into the planning session to address and dismantle those risks proactively.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=7-u6o8uEjbM&t=60

### The Reef Extension (ab 02:45)
- **What:** A third parameter added to the classic Sailboat exercise. Reefs represent upcoming risks or concerns visible to the team — things that could block or derail the next sprint if not addressed.
- **When to use:** Particularly valuable when the team has a planning session coming up immediately after or alongside the retrospective.
- **How it works:** After capturing wind and anchors, invite team members to place sticky notes on the reef area for anything they foresee as a risk in the next iteration. The metaphor works naturally: a boat stuck on a reef (shallow water or anchor caught on reef) cannot move forward.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=7-u6o8uEjbM&t=165

## Key Principles
- Developers and team members hold enormous knowledge about what could go wrong — the reef element gives that knowledge a structured place to surface.
- Simple metaphors lower the cognitive barrier for reflection; people connect more easily to a concrete image than abstract categories.
- Retrospectives should not only look backward (what worked / didn't work) but also forward (what should we watch out for).
- The reef output naturally feeds into sprint planning as a risk register, bridging the retro and planning ceremonies.

## Facilitation Tips
- Run this after an energizer and stage-setting — it fits the "Gather Data" phase of the standard retro format.
- The exercise also works in a Lightning Decision Jam context with just wind + anchor (two parameters), but adding the reef makes it more complete for sprint-based teams.
- The metaphor is intentionally simple — let it do the work. Avoid over-explaining; the visual of a boat, wind, anchor, and reef is largely self-explanatory.
- Reefs collected during the retro should be explicitly carried into the next planning session to be addressed — communicate this linkage to the team upfront so the exercise feels purposeful.

## Best Used When
- The team is about to enter a sprint planning session and you want to surface risks early.
- You want a lightweight, metaphor-driven alternative to more abstract retro formats like Start/Stop/Continue.
- The team is comfortable with retros but needs a nudge toward forward-looking thinking, not just retrospection.
