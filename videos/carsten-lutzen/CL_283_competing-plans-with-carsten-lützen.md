# "Competing Plans" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten introduces "Competing Plans," a workshop exercise shared with him by his colleague Fleming. Groups simultaneously develop independent plans for a shared challenge, pitch them to each other, and then converge on the strongest elements — resulting in a final plan that has broad team buy-in. It's a fast, energizing way to move a group from problem to actionable plan.

## Methods & Techniques

### Competing Plans (ab 00:30)
- **What:** Small groups independently create plans for the same challenge, pitch them, rate them, and then identify overlaps to converge into one shared plan with broad confidence.
- **When to use:** When a group needs to create an initial plan or roadmap for a challenge, product, or obstacle — especially when you want energy, ownership, and fast convergence. Works virtually and in-person.
- **Group size:** Total group split into sub-groups of 4–8 people each. Any number of sub-groups works (not limited to three).
- **Duration:** ~30–45 minutes total (10–15 min for planning, 2 min per pitch, plus debrief)
- **How it works:**
  1. **Set the pitch:** Facilitator or sponsor presents the challenge — e.g. "What are the first steps for our new product?" or "How do we overcome this obstacle?"
  2. **Form groups:** Split participants into small groups of 4–8. Groups don't need to be carefully constructed — random assignment works fine.
  3. **Create the plan:** Each group has 10–15 minutes to develop a plan they could start executing tomorrow. Not a perfect plan — just a starting point.
  4. **Pitch the plan:** Each group presents their plan as a 2-minute elevator pitch, selling why it's the best approach.
  5. **Rate the plan:** After each pitch, everyone rates from "yea" to "nay." In person: participants stand on a physical line on the floor. Virtually: place sticky notes on a virtual whiteboard scale.
  6. **Hear from outliers:** Ask both ends of the spectrum — what do they love about the plan, and what would need more work (not "what's wrong").
  7. **Repeat** until all plans have been presented.
  8. **Converge:** Identify overlaps across all plans. Take the highest-rated plan as the base and augment it with strong elements from others. The final plan often emerges as the intersection — the actions everyone feels confident about.

## Key Principles
- The real value is in the **intersection**, not the best individual plan — overlapping actions across groups signal broad confidence and shared direction.
- Keep the pace high: short planning windows (10–15 min) and capped pitches (2 min) prevent overthinking and maintain energy.
- Framing feedback as "what would need more work" rather than "what's wrong" keeps the conversation constructive and forward-looking.
- The process surfaces multiple routes and options organically, creating a backlog of alternatives even as the group converges.
- Random group formation is intentional — it removes hierarchy and ensures no pre-aligned coalition starts with an advantage.

## Facilitation Tips
- Use a physical line on the floor (in-person) or sticky notes on a spectrum (virtual) for rating — visible, spatial voting is more honest and more engaging than a show of hands.
- Time-box everything strictly: planning, pitching, and rating. The energy comes from pace.
- Don't ask groups to create a *perfect* plan — frame it explicitly as a rough starting point for tomorrow. This lowers the stakes and increases creative risk-taking.
- When debriefing outliers, give equal airtime to enthusiasts and skeptics. Both perspectives strengthen the final plan.
- The convergence step (identifying overlaps) can be done as a whole-group activity on a whiteboard — making the shared ground visible is powerful.

## Best Used When
- A team needs to move quickly from problem to action plan without getting stuck in analysis paralysis.
- You want high ownership of the plan — having people co-create it builds commitment.
- There are multiple valid directions and you want to surface and stress-test them before committing.
- The group has energy but lacks alignment.
- Kicking off a new product, project, or sprint cycle.
