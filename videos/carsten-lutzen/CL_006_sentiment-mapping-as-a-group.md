# "Sentiment Mapping" as a Group

## Summary

Carsten Lützen introduces Sentiment Mapping, a visual retrospective technique where individuals draw their emotional journey across a shared timeline, then compare notes to surface trends and outliers. The exercise works at any scale — from a single sprint to a multi-year project — and pairs naturally with follow-up activities like "What, So What, Now What" or group brainstorming.

## Methods & Techniques

### Sentiment Mapping (ab 00:30)

- **What:** Participants draw a personal sentiment curve across a shared timeline — tracking how their feelings about the project or period rose and fell — then annotate turning points with sticky notes explaining what drove each shift.
- **When to use:** Retrospectives, after-action reviews, or any reflection on a shared experience. Particularly valuable when the team has been through a long or emotionally significant journey.
- **Group size:** Not specified; works for small teams and larger groups alike.
- **Duration:** Not specified for the full exercise; individual drawing phase is brief (a few minutes); full exercise with follow-up likely 30–60 minutes.
- **How it works:**
  1. **Set the timeline (ab 00:45):** Draw a timeline on the X-axis and sentiment (positive/negative) on the Y-axis. Decide the time scope together — one sprint or a multi-year arc.
  2. **Pre-populate events (ab 01:00):** Either a sponsor/select group pre-loads the timeline before the session, or use a 1-2-4-all round (1 min individual → 2 min pairs → 4 min groups of four → plenary) to identify and agree on the major milestones and events to mark on the timeline.
  3. **Draw individual sentiment curves (ab 01:20):** Each person silently draws their own curve across the agreed events — starting high or low, dipping and recovering as they experienced it.
  4. **Annotate turning points (ab 01:45):** Add sticky notes at each significant shift: Why did sentiment start here? What happened to make it drop? What caused a recovery? Carsten emphasizes doing this individually first, before sharing.
  5. **Compare in pairs, then group (ab 02:10):** Pair up and compare curves. Slowly surface shared trends and any outliers. Someone ending noticeably lower than everyone else is worth exploring — that's a meaningful discussion to have.
  6. **Follow up (ab 02:30):** Move into "What, So What, Now What" for structured reflection, or open brainstorming on what actions to take based on the patterns surfaced.

## Key Principles

- Sentiment is personal and individual first — let people draw in silence before any sharing, to avoid anchoring on each other's perceptions.
- The turning points are where the real insight lives. The curve itself is a prompt; the sticky notes on the *why* are the substance.
- Outliers are as valuable as trends. A person who ended significantly lower than the group deserves curiosity, not averaging out.
- The timeline scope shapes what gets surfaced. A sprint-length lens will surface tactical friction; a multi-year lens surfaces relational and cultural patterns.
- Pre-populating the timeline (before the session or via 1-2-4-all) ensures the group has shared reference points before drawing individual experiences onto them.

## Facilitation Tips

- Use 1-2-4-all to build the shared event timeline — it avoids one voice dominating what gets marked as significant.
- Give people a simple template with pre-drawn axes so they can focus on mapping, not constructing the chart.
- When reviewing curves in pairs and groups, explicitly invite people to name what drove each turning point — don't let the curve stand alone without narrative.
- If someone's curve is a significant outlier, treat it as a gift to the group: "This is an interesting discussion to have," not a deviation to explain away.
- Plan a follow-up activity in advance. Sentiment Mapping generates energy and material — it needs somewhere to go (brainstorming, action planning, "What So What Now What").

## Best Used When

- A team or group has been through a meaningful shared experience — a sprint, a project, an org change — and needs to process it collectively before moving forward.
- Retrospectives where simple "went well / improve" formats feel insufficient for the emotional weight of what happened.
- After-action reviews where understanding the human experience of events matters alongside the factual timeline.
- You want to surface both shared patterns *and* individual outliers in a psychologically safe way.
