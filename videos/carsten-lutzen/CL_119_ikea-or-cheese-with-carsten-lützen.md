# "IKEA or Cheese" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten introduces a quick and playful team energizer called "IKEA or Cheese," where participants guess whether a given word is an IKEA product name or a type of cheese. It requires no facilitation setup beyond a list of words and works as a light, fun warm-up activity.

## Methods & Techniques

### IKEA or Cheese Energizer (ab 00:20)
- **What:** A quiz-style energizer where participants guess whether a presented word is the name of an IKEA product or a type of cheese.
- **When to use:** As a quick warm-up, icebreaker, or energy boost during a workshop or team session.
- **Group size:** Not specified; suitable for small to large groups.
- **Duration:** A few minutes; length is flexible depending on number of questions used.
- **How it works:**
  1. Present a word to participants (e.g., "Smårå").
  2. Participants guess: IKEA product or cheese?
  3. Reveal the answer with a brief description or picture of the item.
  4. Repeat with additional words.
- **Materials:** Carsten shares a PDF with questions, descriptions, and pictures (link referenced in video).
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=p10AyhHI2Ao

## Key Principles
- Simple, low-prep energizers can be highly effective — this one requires nothing more than a word list.
- Humor and surprise (the ambiguity between IKEA names and cheese names is genuinely funny) create psychological safety and lighten the mood.
- Carsten notes the original creator is unknown; he credits the "big internet" and invites viewers to help attribute it properly.

## Facilitation Tips
- Use the PDF Carsten prepared, which includes descriptions and pictures for each item — this makes reveal moments more engaging.
- Note that some IKEA products in the list are discontinued, but Carsten considers this part of the fun rather than a problem.
- No flip chart or materials needed beyond the question list — easy to run in remote or in-person settings.

## Best Used When
- You need a 3–5 minute energizer at the start of a session or after a break.
- The group needs a low-stakes, playful moment to loosen up.
- You want something that requires zero preparation from participants.
