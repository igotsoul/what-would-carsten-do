# "Faces" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten demonstrates simple, fast techniques for drawing expressive faces on flip charts and posters. The video covers three eye styles, three mouth styles, and how to combine them for different emotional expressions — all designed to be quick enough for live facilitation use.

## Methods & Techniques

### Drawing Eyes — Three Styles (ab 00:30)
- **What:** Three minimal eye styles that are fast to draw and easy to vary for expression.
- **When to use:** Any time you need a face on a flip chart, poster, or visual artefact during facilitation.
- **How it works:**
  1. **Arc** — a simple curved line; pairs naturally with a smiley mouth for a happy face
  2. **Two short lines** — easy to offset left/right or up/down to simulate gaze direction
  3. **Dots** — the simplest option; Carsten notes his own tendency to draw them too small — make them larger than feels natural
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=yRtNXIbKqWs&t=30

### Drawing Mouths — Three Styles (ab 01:30)
- **What:** Three mouth shapes that convey different moods and levels of detail.
- **When to use:** Pair with any eye style to build a complete face expression.
- **How it works:**
  1. **Simple arc** — minimal, neutral
  2. **Cartoonish arc** — more pronounced curve, more character
  3. **Two cheeks + mouth** — takes slightly longer but adds realism and warmth; can be extended upward for a broader smile
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=yRtNXIbKqWs&t=90

### Combining Features for Emotion (ab 02:15)
- **What:** Mixing and matching eye and mouth styles, and adjusting their placement, to create a wide range of facial expressions.
- **When to use:** When you want faces on visuals to convey specific emotional states (happy, sad, neutral, worried).
- **How it works:**
  - Swap arc eyes for downturned lines to shift from happy to sad
  - Move eyes closer together or farther apart — Carsten highlights this as having a surprisingly large impact on the feel of the face
  - No nose needed; keeping it minimal keeps drawing fast
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=yRtNXIbKqWs&t=135

### More Realistic Portrait Style (ab 03:30)
- **What:** A slightly more detailed face style for when you want a recognizable, character-like drawing rather than a pure icon.
- **When to use:** Personas, role cards, or any visual where individuality matters.
- **How it works:**
  - Outline the face shape, add capital-G shapes for ears
  - Use the cheeks-and-mouth style for the mouth
  - Add hair, a simple nose mark, and color
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=yRtNXIbKqWs&t=210

## Key Principles
- **Speed over perfection:** The primary criterion for a facilitation drawing technique is that it is fast — detailed anatomy is irrelevant on a flip chart.
- **Size matters for dots:** Small dots read as lifeless; make dot eyes bigger than your instinct suggests.
- **Placement changes everything:** The relative position of eyes and mouth on the face has more emotional impact than the shapes themselves.
- **Practice offline:** Carsten recommends sitting down with paper and drawing many emojis and expressions to build muscle memory before using in live sessions.

## Facilitation Tips
- Skip the nose entirely — it adds complexity without meaningfully improving readability at flip-chart scale.
- Use color at the end (not during) to keep your drawing flow uninterrupted.
- Offsetting the two-line eyes slightly in one direction is a quick trick to show a character "looking" somewhere, which can add narrative to your visuals.

## Best Used When
- Preparing flip charts, posters, or visual artefacts for workshops
- Creating personas, emotion check-ins, or empathy maps that need expressive faces
- Building a visual facilitation toolkit from scratch
