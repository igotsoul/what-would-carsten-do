# "Minimal Viable Product" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten explores the often-misunderstood concept of Minimum Viable Product (MVP), arguing that the term has become overloaded and watered down in practice. He introduces an alternative framing — Earliest Testable, Earliest Usable, and Earliest Lovable Prototype/Product — as a more precise way to define and sequence product increments without over-building before releasing to users.

## Methods & Techniques

### Skateboard-to-Car Analogy (ab 00:40)
- **What:** Henrik Kniberg's classic visual model for iterative product development — instead of building parts of a car in sequence (wheels, then chassis, then body), you deliver usable value at each stage: skateboard → scooter → bicycle → car.
- **When to use:** When explaining MVP thinking to teams or stakeholders who conflate "building parts of the end product" with "delivering value incrementally."
- **Group size:** Any
- **Duration:** A few minutes as a framing exercise
- **How it works:** Draw or reference the progression from skateboard to car. Each step solves the core user need (getting from A to B) and is usable on its own. The point is that user value is present from day one, not only at the end.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=jDQTJoA6p5w&t=40

### Earliest Testable / Usable / Lovable Prototype (ab 02:45)
- **What:** A three-stage framework to replace or clarify "MVP" — breaking product evolution into Earliest Testable Prototype, Earliest Usable Prototype/Product, and Earliest Lovable Product.
- **When to use:** When teams are struggling to agree on what "minimum" means, or when MVP discussions get stuck due to differing stakeholder perspectives (technical, marketing, UX, etc.).
- **Group size:** Any product or cross-functional team
- **Duration:** Can be used as a framing discussion or workshop input
- **How it works:**
  1. **Earliest Testable:** Often just paper — a click-through mockup or prototype that lets you validate assumptions before any code is written. Not usable, not lovable — just testable.
  2. **Earliest Usable:** Something you can actually interact with. The experience may be rough, but users can start generating value from it. This is where you learn if value is being created at all.
  3. **Earliest Lovable:** A refined, streamlined experience. The danger Carsten warns against is skipping straight here and "polishing everything before putting it into the wild."
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=jDQTJoA6p5w&t=165

## Key Principles
- The fundamental idea behind MVP is sound — the problem is in how it is applied and interpreted in practice.
- **Perspective matters:** What counts as an MVP differs depending on whether you're looking from a technical, marketing, or UX lens. Without a shared definition, teams talk past each other.
- The risk is not under-building — it's over-building before releasing. Many things labeled MVP are not MVP at all; they are closer to "Earliest Lovable" or even full product.
- Paper prototypes are a legitimate and underused testable artifact. You can validate assumptions before writing a single line of code.
- Carsten distinguishes between "prototype" and "product" — earliest testable/usable/lovable can apply to either, depending on the maturity of the work.

## Facilitation Tips
- Use the Testable / Usable / Lovable framework to facilitate prioritization conversations across perspectives — it gives teams a shared vocabulary to debate what "minimum" actually means.
- When stakeholders or team members disagree on MVP scope, surface the question: *Whose perspective defines the MVP?* That is where the real prioritization work lives.
- Reference Story Maps as a complementary tool for visualizing what could constitute an MVP slice.
- Mention Gojko Adzic's "Elephant Carpaccio" exercise and other MVP framing tools (e.g., magic estimation) as additional options for teams needing more structured approaches.

## Best Used When
- A team is debating what to include in a first release and cannot align on "minimum."
- Stakeholders from different functions (tech, marketing, design) are in conflict about MVP scope.
- A team is at risk of over-engineering before releasing — building the "lovable" version before validating anything.
- Onboarding new team members or stakeholders to iterative product thinking.
