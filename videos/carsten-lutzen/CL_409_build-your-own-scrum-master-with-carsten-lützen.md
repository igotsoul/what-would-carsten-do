# Build Your Own Scrum Master

## Summary
Carsten introduces a team exercise from retromat.org called "Build Your Own Scrum Master" — a structured format for aligning team expectations when a new Scrum Master joins. Through three rounds of silent reflection and group sharing, the team surfaces what they need from the Scrum Master and what they can offer in return, making the onboarding a two-way conversation.

## Methods & Techniques

### Build Your Own Scrum Master (ab 00:30)
- **What:** A three-round workshop exercise that helps teams and new Scrum Masters align on mutual expectations using the metaphor of head (brain), heart, and stomach.
- **When to use:** When a new Scrum Master joins an existing team; also useful when re-clarifying an existing Scrum Master's role.
- **Group size:** The whole Scrum team
- **Duration:** Not specified; estimated 30–60 min depending on team size and discussion
- **How it works:**
  1. Introduce three categories: **Brain** (how the SM thinks and reasons), **Heart** (values, people orientation), **Stomach** (resilience, ability to solve impediments)
  2. **Round 1 — Expectations:** Ask silently: "What would the perfect Scrum Master be for you as a team?" Participants write post-its for each category individually.
  3. **Round 2 — Context:** Ask: "What knowledge or context would help the new Scrum Master work efficiently with you from day one?" (e.g., team communication norms, preferences, quirks)
  4. **Round 3 — Team responsibility:** Ask: "What can *you* do to help the new Scrum Master become efficient?" — explicitly frames onboarding as a two-way responsibility.
  5. Collect all post-its, display them, then use dot voting or similar to prioritize the most important traits per category.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=ZYKrlwFJwRM

## Key Principles
- Onboarding is a **two-way street** — the team must create space for the Scrum Master, not just wait for the SM to adapt.
- The brain/heart/stomach metaphor gives people an intuitive structure to articulate expectations that might otherwise stay implicit.
- Silent individual reflection before sharing prevents groupthink and surfaces diverse perspectives.
- Saving all post-its for a combined reveal at the end (rather than sharing after each round) keeps energy and attention high.
- The exercise is sourced from retromat.org — Carsten encourages practitioners to explore that resource.

## Facilitation Tips
- Don't show or discuss post-its between rounds — collect everything and present all at once at the end.
- Round 3 is the most important to not skip: teams often forget their own responsibility in making the SM successful.
- Use dot voting after the reveal to identify the top priorities in each of the three categories.
- Prompt examples for Round 2 help participants get specific: "We're not talkative in the morning", "we dislike back-to-back meetings", "we love cats" — concrete team norms are gold for a new SM.

## Best Used When
- A new Scrum Master is joining an existing team
- A team and SM want to reset or explicitly negotiate their working relationship
- There is ambiguity or tension around the Scrum Master role
