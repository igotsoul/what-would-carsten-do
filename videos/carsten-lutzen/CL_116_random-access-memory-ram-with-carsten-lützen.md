# "Random Access Memory (RAM)" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten Lützen introduces Random Access Memory (RAM), a Liberating Structure in development (not yet officially part of the toolbox) designed as a debrief or closing reflection activity. Participants write down what stood out for them, physically throw their notes into a circle, then randomly pick up and read each other's reflections before discussing patterns in pairs. It's a playful, low-bias way to surface collective takeaways from a session.

## Methods & Techniques

### Random Access Memory (RAM) (ab 00:30)
- **What:** A debrief activity where participants write reflections, throw them into a shared space, then randomly read each other's notes and discuss patterns — creating an unbiased, serendipitous exchange of takeaways.
- **When to use:** As a closing debrief at the end of a workshop, training, or any session where you want to surface what participants found most memorable or meaningful.
- **Group size:** Any size that can stand in a circle; works well in groups where pair discussion is feasible.
- **Duration:** Not specified, but the format suggests 15–30 minutes depending on group size and discussion depth.
- **How it works:**
  1. Ask participants to stand in a circle facing *outward* (away from the center).
  2. Everyone has pen and paper.
  3. Pose a reflection prompt — e.g., *"What stood out? What will you remember from today?"*
  4. Participants write their responses (one or more), crumple the paper, and throw it over their shoulder into the center of the circle.
  5. Participants turn around, walk into the circle, and randomly pick up notes from the floor — reading them, putting them down, picking up others.
  6. Optionally, participants take notes on what they read.
  7. Participants pair up and discuss the patterns they noticed across the notes they read.
  8. Optional whole-group debrief (plenary) depending on session format.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=MbuHHZOXI7M&t=30s

## Key Principles
- The "random" element is intentional — participants read notes they didn't write, removing ownership bias and creating fresh perspective on collective insights.
- Standing *outward* in the circle during the writing phase encourages individual reflection before the collective sharing.
- The physical act of crumpling and throwing adds a playful, low-stakes quality to sharing reflections.
- Pattern recognition emerges naturally in the pair discussion phase — participants surface themes without a facilitator imposing categories.
- RAM is part of the *Liberating Structures in Development* collection — high-quality, experimentally refined structures not yet officially published in the core toolbox.

## Facilitation Tips
- Keep the reflection prompt open and memorable — Carsten's default is *"What stood out? What will you remember from today?"*
- Allow participants to write more than one note if they have multiple reflections.
- During the random reading phase, you can optionally invite participants to take notes on what they encounter — useful if a plenary discussion follows.
- Pair discussion works well before a plenary: it gives everyone a chance to articulate patterns before sharing with the full group.
- Carsten encourages facilitators to riff and adapt the format — sharing variations is welcomed.

## Best Used When
- Closing a workshop, training day, or multi-hour session and wanting a debrief that feels energizing rather than routine.
- You want to surface collective learning without a facilitator steering the narrative.
- The group benefits from hearing each other's perspectives anonymously and without attachment to who wrote what.
- You want a physical, embodied activity to shift energy at the end of a session.
