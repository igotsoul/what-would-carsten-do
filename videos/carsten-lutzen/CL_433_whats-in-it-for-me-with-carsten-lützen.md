# What's In It For Me with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten introduces a simple flipchart-based technique for aligning participant expectations at the start of a workshop or longer meeting. By collecting and displaying hopes before the agenda is revealed, facilitators surface gaps early and can close the loop at the end of the session.

## Methods & Techniques

### What's In It For Me — Expectation Flipchart (ab 00:20)
- **What:** A pre-agenda expectation-gathering exercise where participants write their hopes for the session on post-its and place them on a dedicated flipchart.
- **When to use:** At the very start of a workshop or longer meeting, before introducing the agenda — to surface expectations and create shared alignment.
- **Group size:** Any group size that fits a workshop setting.
- **Duration:** ~5–10 minutes for the opening round; reviewed again mid-session and at close.
- **How it works:**
  1. Before showing the agenda, ask participants to grab a post-it and pen.
  2. Each person writes down what they hope the session will cover.
  3. One by one, participants come up, place their post-it on the flipchart, and briefly present their expectation.
  4. Apply the "Bingo Rule" — if someone hears an expectation already mentioned, they call out "Bingo!" to signal overlap (no duplicate presentation needed).
  5. During the session, refer back to the flipchart to check which topics have been covered.
  6. At the end, review remaining post-its: discuss uncovered topics and decide whether a follow-up session is needed or if it's acceptable to leave them out.

## Key Principles
- Expectations should be surfaced *before* the agenda is introduced — this prevents the agenda from anchoring what participants allow themselves to hope for.
- Closing the loop at the end of the session is as important as the opening — it turns the flipchart into a shared accountability tool, not just a warm-up.
- A reusable physical artifact (blank flipchart template) is more practical than writing directly on it — post-its allow reset between workshops.

## Facilitation Tips
- Carsten keeps the flipchart template blank and reusable — he brings the same sheet from workshop to workshop and only sticks post-its on it, never writing directly on the paper.
- Use the "Bingo Rule" (covered in a separate video) to keep the sharing round energetic and avoid repetitive presentations when expectations overlap.
- Revisit the flipchart mid-session, not just at the end — it keeps the group aware of their own stated goals throughout the day.

## Best Used When
- Running full-day workshops or multi-hour meetings where participant buy-in and clarity of purpose matter.
- Working with groups whose expectations may diverge significantly from the planned agenda.
- Starting any session where psychological ownership and engagement are important from the first minute.
