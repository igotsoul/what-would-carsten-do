# "Safe-to-fail vs Fail-safe" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten Lützen unpacks the critical distinction between "safe-to-fail" and "fail-safe" in the context of product development and organisational culture. He argues that teams should deliberately design for safe-to-fail conditions — short learning cycles and small bets — rather than investing enormous resources in making everything fail-safe. He also surfaces the cultural gap between organisations that claim psychological safety but punish failure in practice.

## Methods & Techniques

### One-Two-Four-All Reflection on Safe-to-Fail (ab 04:00)
- **What:** A Liberating Structures format used to prompt teams to reflect on whether their most recent product development work was set up as safe-to-fail or fail-safe.
- **When to use:** During retrospectives, team health checks, or coaching conversations where you want to surface the team's actual experience of psychological safety and risk culture.
- **Group size:** Any team size; works well in groups of 4+
- **Duration:** 15–30 minutes depending on debrief depth
- **How it works:**
  1. Pose the question individually (1 min silent reflection): "Was your last piece of work set up as safe-to-fail — or was it more about covering your own back?"
  2. Pairs discuss (2 min)
  3. Groups of four share and synthesise (4 min)
  4. Whole group shares key insights

## Key Principles

- **Safe-to-fail ≠ fail-safe:** Fail-safe means designing something so it cannot fail catastrophically (e.g. aircraft landing gear). Safe-to-fail means designing so that when something fails, it doesn't sink the organisation. Both are valid — but they require very different investments.
- **Fail-safe is expensive:** Making something truly fail-safe requires enormous resources — clinical drug trials are Carsten's example. This approach is often the wrong choice for product development.
- **Small bets over big bets:** Carsten emphasises designing product development as a series of small, safe-to-fail increments rather than large single bets where failure means layoffs or company collapse.
- **Short learning intervals are non-negotiable:** The faster a team can learn and share learnings, the cheaper each failure becomes.
- **Design for learning, not for success:** The goal of each increment should be to generate a learning, not to guarantee an outcome.
- **The cultural gap is real:** Many organisations claim it is safe to fail, but in practice punish failure through career consequences, withheld projects, or redundancy. Carsten names this as a systemic problem facilitators and coaches need to surface.
- **Sunk cost fallacy as a failure mode:** Delaying feedback and continuing to invest in something that isn't working is a direct consequence of not designing for safe-to-fail conditions.
- **Embarrassment as a feedback signal:** Carsten cites the principle — if you are not embarrassed by your first version when you ask for feedback, you waited too long. Early, uncomfortable feedback is cheaper than late, polished failure.

## Facilitation Tips

- Use the One-Two-Four-All structure to make the safe-to-fail vs. fail-safe distinction concrete and personal — ask about the team's *last* piece of work, not a hypothetical.
- When exploring psychological safety in an organisation, Carsten suggests asking a diagnostic question: "Will I be punished if this fails — even if the failure wasn't my fault?" This surfaces the gap between stated and lived culture.
- Avoid letting teams conflate the two concepts. Naming the difference explicitly early in a session prevents confusion and creates a shared vocabulary for the rest of the conversation.

## Best Used When

- Coaching a team or organisation that says it values experimentation but whose behaviour suggests otherwise
- Running a retrospective focused on learning culture or psychological safety
- Facilitating a product team conversation about how to size bets and structure development cycles
- Helping leaders understand why their teams are risk-averse despite stated values of innovation
