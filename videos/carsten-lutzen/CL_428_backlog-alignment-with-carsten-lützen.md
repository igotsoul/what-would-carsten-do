# Backlog Alignment with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten introduces a physical backlog alignment technique for Scrum Teams dealing with large, unwieldy backlogs. The format gets the entire team — developers and Product Owner — into one room to sort printed backlog items by importance, surface outdated or already-done items, and create shared understanding. It combines individual scoring with team discussion and optional Story Mapping to reach prioritization alignment.

## Methods & Techniques

### Physical Backlog Alignment (ab 00:30)
- **What:** A whole-team workshop where printed backlog items are physically sorted into importance categories, followed by a structured alignment discussion between the Development Team and the Product Owner.
- **When to use:** When the backlog has grown large and unwieldy, when the team lacks shared understanding of what is important and why, or when a general backlog health check is overdue.
- **Group size:** Full Scrum Team (developers + Product Owner)
- **Duration:** Not specified; Carsten notes energy can be sustained for an extended period. Scales to 100+ items.
- **How it works:**
  1. Print the entire backlog — title, description, and relevant details — one item per sheet.
  2. Bring the full Scrum Team into the same room.
  3. Each team member picks up items and independently sorts them into four categories:
     - **Important** — high priority from their perspective (e.g., technical debt)
     - **Nice-to-have** — low urgency, not business-critical
     - **Not Needed** — the team has pivoted away; can be removed
     - **Done** — already completed but not closed in the backlog
  4. Once the Development Team has sorted all items, the Product Owner and team discuss: reviewing placements, advocating for business value, and surfacing misalignments.
  5. Optionally, overlay Story Mapping: draw a sprint line to informally sketch what a near-term sprint could look like based on the sorted priorities — without committing to it.

## Key Principles
- Individual scoring first, then discussion — separating personal assessment from group pressure surfaces honest perspectives before social alignment kicks in.
- The Product Owner's job is partly "sales": if the Development Team rates a business feature low, that signals the PO hasn't yet made the value case clearly enough.
- Physical movement (standing, picking up paper, moving items) sustains energy longer than screen-based refinement with one person typing.
- Backlog hygiene is a team responsibility: surfacing "already done" or "no longer needed" items is a legitimate outcome of the session, not a distraction.
- Carsten frames sprint planning previews as exploratory ("it would make sense if…"), not commitments — keeping the exercise low-stakes and revisable.

## Facilitation Tips
- Print all backlog items with enough detail (title + description) so items can be evaluated without needing to open a tool.
- Let team members self-select which items to pick up rather than assigning them — this keeps the energy distributed and avoids bottlenecks.
- Use the sorting result as a conversation starter, not a final decision. The real value is in the discussion triggered by disagreements between categories.
- After sorting, revisit Story Mapping lightly to connect priorities to near-term sprints without overcommitting.
- Keeping it physical — away from keyboards — is intentional. Carsten specifically highlights this as energizing and worth the setup effort.

## Best Used When
- The backlog hasn't been groomed in a while and contains stale, completed, or deprioritized items.
- There is a mismatch between what the Development Team and Product Owner consider important.
- The team needs a shared mental model of the full backlog scope before planning.
- You want to run a high-energy, participatory session rather than a passive backlog review.

## Related Concepts
- **Backlog Refinement** — this technique extends standard refinement practices with a physical, whole-team format.
- **Story Mapping** (Jeff Patton) — referenced as an optional overlay to visualize sprint slicing after the sorting phase.
- **MoSCoW Prioritization** — the four sorting categories (Important / Nice-to-have / Not Needed / Done) loosely echo MoSCoW logic.
