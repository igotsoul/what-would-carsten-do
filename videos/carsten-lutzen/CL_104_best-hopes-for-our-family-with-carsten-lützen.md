# "Best Hopes for Our Family" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten Lützen, an agile coach and Scrum Master, shares how he brought workplace facilitation practices into his family life by running a collaborative goal-setting session for 2024. Inspired by a book on happiness and Scrum, he adapted the 1-2-4-All structure for a family setting, using sticky notes to surface individual wishes and create a shared visual goal board with a "done" section to celebrate progress.

## Methods & Techniques

### Family Goal-Setting with 1-2-4-All (ab 00:30)
- **What:** A structured goal-setting session adapted from the Liberating Structure 1-2-4-All, run at home with family members to define shared aspirations for the year.
- **When to use:** Start of a new year or any natural transition point when a family (or small group) wants to align on shared intentions and personal wishes.
- **Group size:** Small groups — Carsten ran this with his wife and kids (4 people).
- **Duration:** Not specified; likely 30–60 minutes for a family setting.
- **How it works:**
  1. Each family member individually and silently writes wishes on sticky notes — "What would you really like to have happen this year?"
  2. Pair up: one adult pairs with one child to discuss and refine.
  3. Share in plenary — everyone presents their sticky notes to the whole group.
  4. Post all wishes visibly (e.g., on a window).
  5. As wishes are fulfilled throughout the year, physically move sticky notes to a "done" section.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=epRpKkvbmak&t=30

### Visual Progress Board — Wishes to Done (ab 01:45)
- **What:** A two-zone visual board (wishes / done) where family members track goal completion by physically moving sticky notes when something is achieved.
- **When to use:** Any time you want to make progress visible and create moments of celebration, especially with children.
- **Group size:** Any family or small team.
- **Duration:** Ongoing throughout the year.
- **How it works:** Post all goal sticky notes in a "wishes" area. When a goal is met, the person who wrote it moves it to the "done" zone. Carsten notes the visible pride in his kids' eyes when they get to move a note.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=epRpKkvbmak&t=105

## Key Principles
- Agile facilitation techniques are not limited to the workplace — they transfer naturally into personal and family life.
- The physical act of moving a sticky note from "wishes" to "done" creates an emotional moment of recognition and pride, especially for children.
- Starting individually and in silence before discussing in pairs and then groups (1-2-4-All) ensures every voice is heard, even in a family context.
- Shared goals created collaboratively carry more meaning than goals imposed top-down.

## Facilitation Tips
- Use the silent individual writing phase to prevent groupthink — let each person form their own wishes before hearing others.
- Pair an adult with a child in the pair discussion phase to give kids a safe space to refine their ideas before the full group share.
- Keep the goal board visible year-round (e.g., on a window) so it stays top of mind.
- Let the person who wrote the sticky note be the one to move it to "done" — ownership and the physical gesture matter.

## Best Used When
- Kicking off a new year with a family, team, or small group and wanting to create shared direction.
- Looking for ways to bring facilitation and agile practices into personal life contexts.
- Working with mixed groups that include children or non-practitioners who benefit from simple, visual, tangible formats.
- Wanting to build a habit of celebrating small wins visibly over time.
