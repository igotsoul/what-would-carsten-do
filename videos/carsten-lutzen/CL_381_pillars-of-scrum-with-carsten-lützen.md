# Pillars of Scrum with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten walks through the three empirical pillars of Scrum — Transparency, Inspection, and Adaptation — and argues they are not Scrum-specific but universal principles for any sensible process. He extends each pillar beyond the textbook definition into team culture, including sharing personal challenges and career aspirations as acts of transparency.

## Methods & Techniques

### The Three Pillars as a Daily Practice (ab 00:30)
- **What:** Transparency, Inspection, and Adaptation are the empirical foundation of Scrum, meant to be lived continuously — not just in ceremonies.
- **When to use:** As a mental model when designing or evaluating any team process, regardless of framework.
- **Group size:** Any team size
- **Duration:** Ongoing practice, not a time-boxed activity
- **How it works:**
  1. **Transparency** — Make everything visible: product priorities, team direction, Retrospective outcomes, career aspirations, and even personal circumstances affecting work capacity.
  2. **Inspection** — Review regularly (e.g., Sprint Review every 1–2 weeks) both product output and process efficiency. Identify waste and question whether existing practices still deliver value.
  3. **Adaptation** — When inspection reveals something not working, change it. Don't preserve rituals out of habit; replace them with what actually adds value.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=FilRStMP4Dw&t=30

### Daily Scrum as a Pillar Microcycle (ab 02:20)
- **What:** Carsten uses the Daily Scrum as a concrete example of all three pillars in action within a single short event.
- **When to use:** When explaining empiricism to teams new to Scrum or struggling to see the purpose of the Daily Scrum.
- **How it works:** In the Daily Scrum, the team surfaces what they plan to do (Transparency), checks whether the plan is still valid (Inspection), and adjusts if something blocks progress (Adaptation).
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=FilRStMP4Dw&t=140

## Key Principles
- Transparency extends to personal life: sharing when something at home is difficult helps the team support each other and calibrate expectations.
- Career aspirations belong in team conversations — they are the only way teams can help individuals grow toward their goals.
- Waste in process should be removed without hesitation. If an event or artifact no longer delivers value, replace or drop it.
- These three pillars are not Scrum-exclusive. Carsten frames them as "Pillars of a Sensible Process" applicable to Kanban, Scrum, or any framework.
- The Scrum Guide's strict definition ("only Scrum if everything is done exactly as stipulated") is acknowledged but treated as a floor, not a ceiling for thinking.

## Facilitation Tips
- When introducing the pillars, ground each one in a concrete ceremony (Daily Scrum, Sprint Review, Retrospective) so they feel tangible rather than abstract.
- Invite teams to share what values *they* live by — Carsten models this by asking viewers directly, which works well as a Retrospective closing question too.
- Use the "Pillars of a Sensible Process" reframe to make the concept accessible to teams not doing Scrum.

## Best Used When
- Onboarding a new team to Scrum or empirical thinking
- A team is going through the motions of Scrum ceremonies without understanding the underlying purpose
- Coaching a team to evaluate and adapt their own process rather than blindly following a framework
- Cross-framework conversations where Scrum purists and Kanban practitioners need common ground

## Related Concepts
- Scrum Guide (empirical process control)
- Kanban (mentioned as an alternative framework the pillars apply to equally)
- Sprint Review and Retrospective as primary Inspection + Adaptation events
- Psychological safety (implied by the call to share personal challenges openly with the team)
