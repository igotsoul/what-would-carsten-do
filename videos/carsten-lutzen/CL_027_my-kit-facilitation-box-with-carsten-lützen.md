# "My Kit - Facilitation Box" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten Lützen, an agile coach and facilitator, gives a tour of his physical facilitation box — the portable kit he brings to every workshop. The video is a practical inventory of materials that experienced facilitators carry, with brief notes on why each item earns its place. Useful for anyone building or refining their own facilitation kit.

## Methods & Techniques

### Dot Voting Setup (ab 04:10)
- **What:** Using physical dot stickers rather than marker dots to let participants vote on options, priorities, or ideas.
- **When to use:** Prioritization exercises, decision-making with groups, identifying where energy or value lies.
- **Group size:** Any
- **Duration:** A few minutes as part of a larger session
- **How it works:** Distribute a fixed number of dot stickers per person; participants place dots on options they prefer or value most. Tally the dots to surface group priorities.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=NMScN6zQFlA&t=250

### Gold Coins & Play Money as Betting Props (ab 04:20)
- **What:** Using fake gold coins or play money as physical tokens when participants need to allocate value or place bets in an exercise.
- **When to use:** Value prioritization workshops, portfolio decisions, exercises asking "where is the biggest value?" or similar trade-off discussions.
- **Group size:** Any
- **Duration:** Situational
- **How it works:** Carsten distributes fake coins or play money and asks participants to place their currency on the options they believe hold the most value. The physical prop adds tangibility and a sense of stakes to an otherwise abstract exercise.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=NMScN6zQFlA&t=255

### Ball Toss for Debrief (ab 04:55)
- **What:** Passing or throwing a ball around the group to structure turn-taking during a debrief or check-in.
- **When to use:** One-word debrief rounds, check-ins, any moment where you want organic but structured participation without a fixed speaking order.
- **Group size:** Small to medium groups
- **Duration:** A few minutes
- **How it works:** Whoever holds the ball speaks; they then throw it to the next person. Keeps energy up and avoids the stiffness of going around a circle.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=NMScN6zQFlA&t=295

### Blank Paper for Sketching / Crazy Eights (ab 01:10)
- **What:** Standard blank A4/letter paper kept on hand for freeform sketching exercises.
- **When to use:** Ideation exercises like Crazy Eights, journey mapping, quick sketching rounds.
- **Group size:** Any
- **How it works:** Distribute sheets on demand. Having blank (not lined) paper ready removes the friction of searching for it mid-session.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=NMScN6zQFlA&t=70

## Key Principles

- **Always carry more than you think you need.** Carsten's box is deliberately overstocked — multiple colors of sticky notes, several types of tape, spare batteries — because running out mid-workshop breaks flow.
- **Physical props create psychological reality.** Fake coins and play money are not gimmicks; they make abstract value trade-offs feel concrete and raise participant engagement.
- **Ask the venue before you use anything on surfaces.** Sticky tack and tape can leave oil or residue. Carsten explicitly asks venues which surfaces can be written or stuck on, and what materials are acceptable — before the session starts.
- **Error recovery is part of the kit.** Carsten carries blank white sticker labels specifically to cover flip chart mistakes. He normalizes this: "when, not if, I make a mistake."
- **A kit is built over time, not bought all at once.** Carsten notes his box has been assembled gradually over years. The right kit is personal and iterative, not a one-size-fits-all purchase.

## Facilitation Tips

- Keep white cover-up stickers (blank label stickers) in your kit. When you make a typo or error on a flip chart, place one over it rather than crossing out or starting over. Cut them to size as needed.
- Painters tape is the safest general-purpose mounting tape — it works on most surfaces and is less likely to damage walls. Keep it as your default; ask the venue if anything stronger is needed.
- A noise maker (e.g., a loud clicker or bell) with a long sustain is more effective than clapping or calling out to reconvene a group. The sustained sound carries across a noisy room.
- Whiteboard markers can double as large flip chart markers when you have many participants and need bold, readable text from a distance.
- Small sticky notes (smaller than standard size) are useful for quick additions, traffic light indicators, or annotation layers — stock multiple sizes.
- Carry a clicker for presentations even if you rarely use it. The one time you need it and don't have it is a preventable problem.

## Best Used When

- Looking for low-cost prop ideas to make value prioritization or decision exercises more tangible and engaging.
