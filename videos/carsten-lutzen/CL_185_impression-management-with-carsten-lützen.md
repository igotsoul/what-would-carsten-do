# "Impression Management" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten Lützen explores impression management — the conscious or unconscious shaping of how others perceive us — through the lens of facilitation and coaching. He distinguishes three levels: good (intentional and values-aligned), bad (inauthentically acting to meet perceived expectations), and ugly (manipulative). Carsten argues that impression management is neither inherently good nor bad; what matters is awareness, intentionality, and alignment with your values.

## Methods & Techniques

### The Three-Level Impression Management Framework (ab 01:30)
- **What:** A mental model for categorizing impression management behavior into good, bad, and ugly — based on intent, authenticity, and impact.
- **When to use:** As a reflective tool for facilitators and coaches to audit their own behavior and to recognize manipulation patterns in groups.
- **Group size:** Individual reflection; applicable to any group setting
- **Duration:** Not a standalone exercise — an ongoing awareness practice
- **How it works:**
  1. **Good** — Conscious, values-aligned choices about behavior or appearance designed to create the right environment (e.g., removing shoes to signal informality and approachability).
  2. **Bad** — Acting in ways that don't reflect your true beliefs because you assume that's what others expect; includes sheep mentality or going along with the majority without voicing genuine views.
  3. **Ugly** — Deliberate manipulation: lying, withholding information, coercing agreement, shifting blame, or facilitating people "into submission."
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=hAxT_5cs0p0&t=90

### Intentional Appearance as Environmental Design (ab 01:00)
- **What:** Deliberately choosing attire and physical cues (e.g., t-shirts, removing shoes) to shape the atmosphere and signal accessibility.
- **When to use:** Before any facilitated session where psychological safety and a non-hierarchical feel are important.
- **Group size:** Any
- **Duration:** Ongoing practice; decision made before the session
- **How it works:** Carsten consciously chooses casual clothing to signal a down-to-earth, approachable presence, with the explicit goal of lowering perceived hierarchy and building trust in the room.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=hAxT_5cs0p0&t=60

### One-on-One Intervention for Manipulative Behavior (ab 05:30)
- **What:** When a facilitator detects manipulation in a session, address it privately with the individual rather than calling it out publicly.
- **When to use:** When you observe someone trying to steer a group toward an outcome not in the group's best interest — through framing, pressure, or withholding.
- **Group size:** 1-on-1 debrief with the individual
- **Duration:** Brief, outside the main session
- **How it works:** Pull the person aside, ask open questions ("What happened? What can we do about it?"), and invite behavior change — rather than confronting them in front of the group.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=hAxT_5cs0p0&t=330

## Key Principles
- Everyone does impression management — the differentiator is whether you are conscious of it.
- Good impression management is intentional, values-aligned, and transparent to yourself; it builds trust and psychological safety.
- When impression management diverges from authentic values, it creates a disconnect between who you are and who you appear to be — eroding credibility over time.
- "Sheep mentality" (going along with the group) is a form of bad impression management — a failure to create space for dissenting or minority views.
- The ugliest form — manipulation — causes long-term relational damage and is fundamentally incompatible with the role of a facilitator.
- Carsten's test for good impression management: Is it intentional? Does it align with my values? Is there anything I should do differently?

## Facilitation Tips
- Audit your own impression management before sessions: what signals are you sending, why, and are they serving the group?
- Create psychological safety explicitly so participants feel safe voicing unpopular opinions — this counteracts bad impression management in the group.
- Never call out manipulative behavior publicly in a session; address it in private with curiosity, not accusation.
- Be honest with yourself when you notice you're acting out of perceived expectations rather than genuine belief — that's the warning sign of "bad" impression management.
- Treating "facilitate them into submission" as a clear red line: if asked to do this, refuse. It is a breach of facilitator ethics.

## Best Used When
- Preparing for a facilitated session and reflecting on your facilitator presence and choices
- Coaching a facilitator or team member who is struggling with authenticity or trust
- Debriefing a session where group dynamics felt off — where conformity, silence, or manipulation may have been at play
- Developing a personal facilitation philosophy around presence, ethics, and authenticity

## Related Concepts
- Psychological safety (Amy Edmondson) — the precondition for participants to voice authentic views
- Authenticity and congruence in facilitation — the alignment between inner values and outward behavior
- Groupthink / sheep mentality — a passive form of bad impression management at the group level
- Facilitator ethics — the boundary between shaping environment (good) and coercing outcomes (ugly)
- Presence and facilitator stance — related to how a facilitator shows up physically and energetically in the room
