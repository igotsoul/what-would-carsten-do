# "Anxiety Circus" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten introduces Anxiety Circus, an unofficial "under development" variant of the Liberating Structure 25/10 Crowd Sourcing, sourced from a Holistic Agile card deck. The technique surfaces and prioritizes fears and anxieties within a group by combining anonymous writing, physical card-passing, and peer voting. Carsten explains the full facilitation sequence and emphasizes closing with actionable sense-making to avoid the exercise becoming mere fearmongering.

## Methods & Techniques

### Anxiety Circus — a variant of 25/10 Crowd Sourcing (ab 00:30)
- **What:** A structured group exercise where participants write down their biggest fear or anxiety, pass the cards around, vote on each one, and then surface the most widely shared concerns for collective action.
- **When to use:** When a team or group needs to surface hidden fears, risks, or anxieties related to a project, product, or any specific challenge — useful in kick-offs, risk workshops, or retrospectives where psychological safety may be low.
- **Group size:** Works with groups large enough to benefit from crowd-sourced prioritization; no explicit minimum stated, but designed for groups where anonymous input adds value.
- **Duration:** 10 minutes for the voting rounds (5 rounds × 2 minutes); additional time needed for debrief and sense-making.
- **How it works:**
  1. Each participant writes their biggest fear or anxiety on a piece of paper or index card. Optionally, anchor it to a specific problem, project, or product depending on workshop scope.
  2. Cards are passed around. Every 2 minutes, a bell or signal is given.
  3. At each signal, participants read the card in their hand and rate their own level of fear on a scale of 1–5 (5 = most scary, 1 = not very scary). They write the number on the back of the card and pass it on.
  4. This is repeated 5 times, so each card receives 5 votes. Maximum score is 25.
  5. After 10 minutes, collect cards. Sum the numbers on the back of each card.
  6. Count down from 25: "Does anyone have a card with 25? 24? 23?" — read out the fears at the top scores and work down until time runs out or the most significant fears are covered.
  7. Close with a debrief: What stood out? Are there common themes? Use a tool like 1-2-4-All to turn insights into tangible actions.

## Key Principles
- The power of 25/10 Crowd Sourcing lies in the invitation — changing what you ask people to write shifts the entire dynamic of the exercise.
- Anonymous card-passing creates psychological safety, allowing fears that might never surface in open discussion to become visible.
- Scoring by peers (rather than self-ranking importance) reveals collective resonance: a fear rated highly by many carries different weight than one person's top concern.
- According to Carsten, the debrief is non-negotiable — without it, the exercise risks becoming "fearmongering" rather than productive work.
- Tweaking both the invitation and the reveal mechanism (counting down from max score) makes this a meaningfully different tool from the standard 25/10, not just a thematic riff.

## Facilitation Tips
- Carsten candidly notes that facilitating this cleanly is difficult — errors happen, and that is acceptable. Don't let perfectionism stop you from running it.
- Use a bell, clap, or clear audio signal to mark the 2-minute transitions so energy stays consistent across rounds.
- If cards end up with fewer than five votes (e.g., due to group size or movement errors), find a fair way to normalize before summing — don't just discard them.
- Close with a convergence move: Carsten suggests a 1-2-4-All on what stood out, specifically looking for similarities and common elements across the fears surfaced.
- Anchor the invitation to a specific scope (project, product, sprint) if you want focused output; leave it open if the workshop is broader.

## Best Used When
- A team is about to start something new and unspoken risks need to surface before they derail progress.
- Psychological safety is uncertain and anonymous input is needed to get honest responses.
- You want a fast, energetic way to prioritize concerns without lengthy open debate.
- Running a risk identification session that needs to feel human and emotionally honest rather than purely analytical.
