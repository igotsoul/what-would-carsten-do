# "Drawing Together" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten introduces the Liberating Structure "Drawing Together," a visual sensemaking exercise that uses only five simple shapes to help groups express ideas, patterns, and meaning without requiring artistic skill. The activity invites participants to create metaphorical drawings around a central question, share them with others, and collectively identify patterns — making abstract themes visible and discussable.

## Methods & Techniques

### Drawing Together (ab 00:30)
- **What:** A Liberating Structure where participants draw responses to a framing question using only five predefined symbolic shapes, then share and interpret their drawings together.
- **When to use:** When a group needs to surface implicit assumptions, explore a complex topic metaphorically, or make sense of a shared experience — especially useful when verbal discussion alone feels limiting.
- **Group size:** Any; pairs or small groups for sharing, then whole group for pattern discussion.
- **Duration:** ~25–30 minutes total (5 min practice + 10 min first draft + 10 min second draft + sharing/reflection)
- **How it works:**
  1. **Stage setting** — Introduce the framing question that participants will respond to visually.
  2. **Practice the five shapes** (5 min):
     - Circle — wholeness, being part of something
     - Rectangle — support
     - Triangle — a goal
     - Spiral — change
     - Star figure (person) — relationship, connection
  3. **First draft** — Participants create an initial drawing using the shapes (time-boxed).
  4. **Second draft** — Refine or deepen the drawing (10 min).
  5. **Share** — Present your drawing to at least one other person.
  6. **Display and reflect** — Post drawings visibly (wall or virtual whiteboard). Step back and look for patterns across the group's work.
  7. **Discuss** — Use a format like 1-2-4-All to debrief what patterns emerge and what they mean.

## Key Principles
- The exercise is deliberately low-barrier — only five shapes are needed, so no artistic ability is required. Framing this upfront reduces resistance.
- The shapes are symbolic, not literal; meaning is co-constructed through interpretation, so multiple valid readings are expected and welcomed.
- Displaying all drawings together (at a distance) reveals collective patterns that individual drawings alone don't show — the group picture is the real artifact.
- The framing question anchors interpretation; a shared reference frame helps participants "decipher" each other's drawings meaningfully.
- Carsten notes that interpretation varies — the same drawing can mean different things to different people, which is a feature, not a bug.

## Facilitation Tips
- Normalize the discomfort early: explicitly tell participants they do not need to be artists.
- In virtual settings, a shared digital whiteboard (e.g., Miro, Mural) works well — everyone can see all drawings simultaneously.
- In-person: post drawings on a wall and have participants step back to view them from a distance before discussion.
- Consider pairing the debrief with 1-2-4-All to structure the pattern-finding conversation.
- The framing question is critical — it determines the interpretive frame for all drawings, so choose it carefully.

## Best Used When
- A team needs to reflect on a complex or emotionally loaded topic (e.g., change, uncertainty, team health).
- Verbal discussion has become stuck or overly analytical and a shift in modality would help.
- You want to surface diverse perspectives without privileging articulate speakers.
- Remote or hybrid teams need an engaging visual collaboration activity.
