# "Late Cake" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten introduces "Late Cake," a lighthearted visual accountability tool for tracking repeated undesired team behaviors such as showing up late to standups. The technique uses a drawn cake divided into slices to make patterns visible in a playful way — but only works safely in psychologically safe teams.

## Methods & Techniques

### Late Cake (ab 00:20)
- **What:** A visual tracking tool where a hand-drawn cake is placed in the team space. Each time someone exhibits an agreed-upon undesired behavior (e.g., arriving late to standup), their name is written on a slice. When the cake is full, the person with the most slices faces a fun, agreed consequence.
- **When to use:** When a stable team with high psychological safety wants to create awareness around a recurring behavioral pattern in a non-punitive, playful way.
- **Group size:** Team-sized (implied: Scrum team or similar small group)
- **Duration:** Ongoing visual artefact; consequence triggered when the cake fills up
- **How it works:**
  1. Draw an empty cake divided into slices on a whiteboard or paper
  2. Define the target behavior to track (e.g., arriving late to standup)
  3. Each occurrence: write the responsible person's name on one slice
  4. When the cake is full, count who has the most slices
  5. That person fulfills the agreed consequence — e.g., bringing actual cake, or running the Energizer at the next retrospective
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=rEbywrs6Ssg&t=20

## Key Principles
- Psychological safety is a hard prerequisite — without it, the tool risks becoming blame and finger-pointing rather than awareness-building
- The goal is transparency and awareness of behavior, not punishment
- The "currency" (cake, running an Energizer, etc.) should feel fun and low-stakes, not shaming
- Keep it internal to the team; Carsten explicitly advises against displaying it where people outside the team can see and ask about it

## Facilitation Tips
- Only introduce this in stable teams where trust is already established
- The consequence doesn't have to be cake — choose something the team finds genuinely fun
- Keep the artefact visible to the team but not to outsiders to avoid external judgment or embarrassment
- Frame it as a tool for creating behavioral awareness, not a scoreboard

## Best Used When
- A mature, psychologically safe team has developed a recurring habit they collectively want to address (e.g., chronic lateness to ceremonies)
- The team is looking for a light, self-regulating norm rather than a top-down rule
- There is already enough trust that being "named on the cake" feels playful, not punitive
