# Why Sharing is Good

## Summary
Carsten reflects on his personal journey of sharing knowledge through weekly YouTube videos and explains why he believes practitioners should start sharing their own experiences. The video covers the fears around going public, the unexpected rewards of sharing, and a practical framing tool — the Best Hopes Question — to guide what you put out into the world.

## Methods & Techniques

### The Best Hopes Question as a Framing Tool (ab 05:10)
- **What:** Before creating or sharing content, ask yourself: "When I'm done with this, what do I hope the audience is saying?" This focuses your intent and shapes the output toward a concrete desired outcome.
- **When to use:** When preparing a video, post, coaching session, or any sharing moment — to clarify purpose before starting.
- **Group size:** Individual (self-reflection)
- **Duration:** 1-2 minutes of reflection
- **How it works:**
  1. Before creating content or facilitating, pause and ask: "What do I hope the audience says when this is over?"
  2. Let that answer shape your preparation and focus.
  3. Use it as a north star to avoid perfectionism or scope creep.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=iTXIPqTxjIg&t=310

### Start Imperfect, Start Authentic (ab 02:30)
- **What:** Deliberately share imperfect work — typos, hesitations, rough edges included — to model authenticity and lower the barrier to engagement for others.
- **When to use:** When starting a public sharing practice or helping others overcome the fear of going public with their knowledge.
- **How it works:**
  1. Accept that the first piece of content will feel uncomfortable.
  2. Post it with the assumption that people are kind and value the effort over the polish.
  3. Treat feedback as fuel rather than judgment.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=iTXIPqTxjIg&t=150

## Key Principles
- Sharing creates reciprocity: the more you share, the more you receive — feedback, connections, ideas, and encouragement from people you would never have met otherwise.
- "If you're not embarrassed the first time you ask for feedback, you've waited too long." Carsten frames early discomfort as a signal that you're moving at the right pace, not a reason to wait.
- Knowledge hoarding as "job security" is a losing mindset. Carsten acknowledges some people resist sharing out of self-protection, but frames this as a position you simply cannot argue everyone out of — so focus on those you can help.
- Even saturated topics benefit from a fresh angle. Carsten encourages contributors to add their own perspective even when a subject feels fully covered.
- Sharing serves the sharer too: Carsten uses video creation to pace his own learning, develop communication and visual facilitation skills, and build a reusable reference library he can send to coaching clients.

## Facilitation Tips
- Use the Best Hopes Question before any session or content creation to anchor intent and guide preparation.
- Send clients links to relevant videos after coaching conversations so they can revisit the exercise without needing to take detailed notes during the session.
- Design your output for authenticity, not perfection — audiences connect more with real than polished.

## Best Used When
- You are coaching someone who is hesitant to share their knowledge or start a public practice.
- You want to introduce the Best Hopes Question as a pre-session framing tool in a coaching or facilitation context.
- You are reflecting on your own practice and looking for a frame to articulate the value of knowledge sharing.
