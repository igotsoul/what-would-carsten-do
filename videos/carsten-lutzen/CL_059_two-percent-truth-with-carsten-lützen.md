# "Two Percent Truth" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten Lützen shares a short but practical mental model called "Two Percent Truth" for working with feedback — especially feedback that triggers a defensive reaction. The idea is that even in feedback we strongly disagree with, there is almost always a small kernel of truth worth finding and using. Carsten encourages viewers to actively search for that truth rather than dismiss the feedback entirely.

## Methods & Techniques

### Two Percent Truth (ab 00:15)
- **What:** A reframing technique for receiving difficult feedback. Rather than rejecting feedback that feels wrong or unfair, you ask yourself: "Where is the truth in this?" — even if only 2% of it applies to you.
- **When to use:** When receiving feedback that triggers a defensive response ("That's not me at all"), or when coaching others who struggle to receive critical feedback.
- **Group size:** Individual reflection, but applicable in 1:1 coaching or team feedback sessions.
- **Duration:** A momentary mindset shift; no formal time requirement.
- **How it works:**
  1. Receive feedback — including feedback you instinctively want to reject.
  2. Notice the defensive reaction without acting on it.
  3. Actively search for where the feedback might contain a grain of truth.
  4. Extract that small truth and use it for growth or improvement.
  5. For the remainder of the feedback you don't connect with, treat it as a gift — choose whether to use it or set it aside, but do so consciously.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=d-q9GrGG_PM&t=15

## Key Principles
- Defensiveness is a natural reaction to challenging feedback — the technique doesn't try to eliminate it, but works alongside it.
- Even feedback that feels completely wrong usually contains something useful; the job is to find it.
- Feedback is a gift: you are not obligated to use all of it, but you should consciously decide what to do with it rather than dismissing it reflexively.
- The value of the technique lies in the searching, not necessarily in finding a large truth — 2% is enough to make it worthwhile.

## Facilitation Tips
- Carsten suggests introducing this as a named mental model ("Two Percent Truth") so people have a shared phrase to invoke when defensiveness arises in the room.
- In coaching conversations, you can use this as a prompt: "Where might there be two percent truth in what you just heard?"
- Useful as a debrief frame after feedback exercises — ask participants to share what their "two percent" was.

## Best Used When
- Coaching individuals who habitually dismiss critical feedback.
- Preparing teams for peer feedback sessions where defensive reactions are likely.
- Debriefing after 360-degree feedback or performance reviews.
- Building a team culture where feedback is treated as a gift rather than an attack.
