# "TRIZ" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten introduces TRIZ, a Liberating Structures exercise that uses inversion to surface counterproductive behaviors a team may already be doing. By first listing what would cause the worst possible outcomes, participants often have an "aha moment" recognizing their own actions in that list — creating a powerful, non-defensive opening for change.

## Methods & Techniques

### TRIZ (ab 00:30)
- **What:** An inversion exercise where participants first brainstorm what would guarantee the worst results, then honestly assess which of those things they are already doing — and finally commit to stopping them.
- **When to use:** When a team needs to surface counterproductive patterns that are hard to name directly; works best the first time a group encounters it, as the inversion reveal has maximum impact.
- **Group size:** Any; the 1-2-4-All structure scales from small teams to large workshops.
- **Duration:** Fits within a typical meeting slot; each reflection round is roughly 1 minute individually.
- **How it works:**
  1. Invite participants to write a list: "What are the worst results imaginable for this topic?"
  2. Run **1-2-4-All**: 1 min solo → pairs → groups of 4 → full group sharing.
  3. Reveal the key question: "From this list, what are we actually doing right now?"
  4. Repeat 1-2-4-All for this second question (individual reflection is essential here).
  5. Close with action: "What first steps will I take to stop doing these counterproductive things?"
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=82gHVeDXG4M&t=30

## Key Principles
- Inversion bypasses defensiveness — asking "what causes failure?" is less threatening than "what are you doing wrong?"
- The moment of recognition ("we are actually doing this") creates intrinsic motivation to change, without external pressure.
- Individual silent reflection before group discussion is critical for the second question — it gives people space to be honest with themselves first.
- TRIZ originates from a larger innovation toolbox and was later incorporated into Liberating Structures.

## Facilitation Tips
- Don't reveal the purpose upfront. According to Carsten, the exercise works best when participants don't know the inversion reveal is coming — just invite them to list worst-case outcomes.
- The second question ("what are we actually doing?") is the heart of the exercise — give it enough time and don't rush past it.
- You can skip some steps of the 1-2-4-All sequence depending on group size, but always preserve individual reflection on the second question.
- Works especially well the first time a team experiences it; the surprise factor drives the aha moment.

## Best Used When
- A team is stuck in patterns they intellectually know aren't working but haven't named openly.
- You want to create psychological safety around discussing failure without blame.
- Opening a retrospective or improvement workshop where direct criticism might feel risky.
- The group needs to move from awareness to concrete action on counterproductive behaviors.
