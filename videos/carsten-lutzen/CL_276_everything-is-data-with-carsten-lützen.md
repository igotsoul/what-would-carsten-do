# "Everything is Data" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten introduces a mindset shift for agile coaches and Scrum Masters: everything you observe — and everything you *don't* observe — is data. By expanding what counts as a data point beyond direct interactions, coaches can build richer hypotheses about team dynamics and make more informed interventions.

## Methods & Techniques

### Observational Data Collection (ab 00:30)
- **What:** Systematically insert yourself into as many team situations as possible to observe how people interact — in meetings, at their desks, during lunch — and use these observations to form hypotheses about team behavior.
- **When to use:** When onboarding with a new team or trying to understand an unfamiliar team dynamic before making interventions.
- **Group size:** Any team size
- **Duration:** Ongoing, especially in early engagement with a team
- **How it works:**
  1. Observe how people talk to each other in meetings and informal settings.
  2. Note whether people work in pairs/small groups or default to individual tasks and status reporting.
  3. Look beyond formal meetings — do people talk at lunch? In the office generally?
  4. Compile observations into hypotheses about the team's working culture and relationships.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=IA6UtZcmKqM&t=30

### Reading Closed Doors (ab 01:40)
- **What:** Treat restricted access and lack of transparency as meaningful data points. When you are kept out of certain conversations or meetings, that itself tells you something about the team's openness, decision-making structure, and psychological safety.
- **When to use:** When you sense information is being withheld or when certain meetings are off-limits to you as a coach or facilitator.
- **Group size:** N/A — observational technique
- **Duration:** N/A
- **How it works:**
  1. Notice which situations you are *not* invited into or actively excluded from.
  2. Ask: How open is this team? How transparent is their way of working? Who is making decisions, and is it visible to the team?
  3. Use these "closed doors" as data points alongside your positive observations.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=IA6UtZcmKqM&t=100

### Noticing the Absence of Interaction (ab 02:30)
- **What:** Silence, isolation, and lack of spontaneous interaction are as informative as the interactions themselves. Whether people sit quietly at their desks or whether lunch is a solo activity can reveal how team members perceive their relationship to each other.
- **When to use:** When trying to assess psychological safety, team cohesion, or whether a group has formed into a real team.
- **Group size:** N/A — observational technique
- **Duration:** N/A
- **How it works:**
  1. Observe whether people initiate informal conversation or default to silence.
  2. Ask yourself: Do they see this as a working group, a team, or just people who happen to be together?
  3. Feed the absence of interaction into your overall hypothesis alongside positive observations.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=IA6UtZcmKqM&t=150

### Role Modeling the Behavior You Want to See (ab 04:00)
- **What:** Rather than staying in passive observation mode, actively role model the behaviors you want to see — initiating conversations, showing openness, having fun — and observe whether the system responds.
- **When to use:** When lack of interaction or closed-door culture is identified as a problem. Use as a gentle, non-confrontational intervention.
- **Group size:** Any
- **Duration:** Ongoing
- **How it works:**
  1. Identify the behavior you want to encourage (e.g., more informal interaction, openness).
  2. Model that behavior yourself — talk to people beyond work topics, be visible and approachable.
  3. Observe whether the team's behavior shifts in response.
  4. If nothing changes, treat that as another data point.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=IA6UtZcmKqM&t=240

## Key Principles
- **Everything is data** — not just what happens, but what *doesn't* happen and what you're *not allowed* to see.
- Closed doors and restricted access are themselves diagnostic signals about transparency and decision-making culture.
- The absence of interaction (silence, solo work, isolated lunches) can reveal as much as the presence of interaction.
- Data collection should inform hypotheses, not conclusions — use observations to ask better questions, not to judge.
- Don't stay in observation mode indefinitely. At some point, shift to active role modeling to test whether the system can change.
- If you only have partial data (because of closed doors), acknowledge that your picture may be incomplete — and factor that limitation into your coaching.

## Facilitation Tips
- Carsten recommends deliberately inserting yourself into as many team situations as possible early in an engagement — not just formal meetings.
- Don't treat an "off-limits" situation as unimportant. Note it, name it (to yourself at minimum), and use it.
- Role model informally — having fun, starting conversations — rather than announcing you're trying to change culture.
- Balance data collection mode with active participation. Passive observation alone is insufficient for building trust or creating change.

## Best Used When
- Starting work with a new team and trying to build a baseline understanding of their dynamics.
- Diagnosing why a team feels "stuck" or disconnected, even when nothing is overtly wrong.
- Preparing for a deeper coaching conversation or retrospective by gathering observational evidence first.
- When standard metrics (velocity, board hygiene) don't explain team behavior.
