# Talking Behind Peoples Back...

## Summary
Carsten introduces a two-part facilitation pattern that bookends a workshop. It opens with a counterintuitive energizer — "Talking Behind People's Back" — where participants share positive expectations about each colleague while that person literally turns away. It closes with a "Feed Forward Loop" where participants give appreciative feedback face-to-face in a round-robin. Together, the pair creates a visible arc from indirect anticipation to direct appreciation.

## Methods & Techniques

### Talking Behind People's Back (ab 00:30)
- **What:** A positive-gossip energizer where each person turns their back to the group while the rest openly share what they look forward to that person bringing to the session.
- **When to use:** At the start of a workshop or meeting to surface expectations and create alignment in a low-pressure way.
- **Group size:** Works best with roughly 10 participants or fewer.
- **Duration:** Allow 1.5–2 minutes per participant.
- **How it works:**
  1. Go round-robin — one person at a time turns their back to the group.
  2. The remaining participants have a brief conversation: "What do we look forward to seeing [Name] bring to today's session?"
  3. The person with their back turned listens in without responding.
  4. Rotate until everyone has had a turn.
  5. Carsten recommends including the facilitator — participants can also share what they hope the facilitator brings.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=gBK6ueOqv3s&t=30

### Feed Forward Loop (ab 03:00)
- **What:** A closing appreciation round where each participant shares specific positive feedback directly with the person next to them, then the round advances to the next pair.
- **When to use:** At the end of a workshop as a closing ritual, especially when paired with the opening energizer above.
- **Group size:** Same as above — round-robin format, works well for groups of ~10.
- **Duration:** 1–2 minutes per person.
- **How it works:**
  1. Go round-robin — each person turns to the participant next to them.
  2. They share: "I was really glad that you brought [specific contribution] to this workshop today."
  3. The recipient listens without responding, defending, or saying thank you.
  4. When time is up, move to the next person in the circle.
  5. Continue until everyone has given and received appreciative feedback.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=gBK6ueOqv3s&t=180

## Key Principles
- Indirect framing lowers the social cost of stating expectations — it is easier to say what you hope someone brings when that person is not directly facing you.
- Getting expectations out in the open before a session creates alignment and psychological safety for participation.
- The facilitator joining the energizer (rather than staying outside it) models inclusion and gives participants a voice over the facilitation style they want.
- The two exercises mirror each other intentionally: the workshop opens with indirect positive anticipation and closes with direct face-to-face appreciation, making the growth arc tangible.
- Removing the recipient's option to respond (no "thank you," no defense) keeps the feedback flowing and prevents deflection.

## Facilitation Tips
- Carsten normally stays out of energizers, but deliberately includes himself in this one because it serves an alignment purpose — participants can shape what they want from the facilitator too.
- Frame the opening prompt with forward-looking language: "What do we *look forward* to seeing [Name] bring?" — the future tense keeps it aspirational rather than evaluative.
- The no-response rule in the Feed Forward Loop is important to enforce; the value is in the uninterrupted listening, not the exchange.
- Treat the two exercises as a pair when possible — the contrast between indirect (back turned) and direct (face-to-face) reinforces how the group dynamic has shifted over the day.

## Best Used When
- Opening a workshop where participants may not know each other well or where expectation-setting matters.
- Closing a full-day or multi-hour session where you want to end on appreciation rather than tasks.
- Groups where direct positive feedback feels awkward — the indirect opening lowers the barrier and the structured closing makes directness feel earned.
- Teams that benefit from hearing what others value in them, especially in contexts like agile retrospectives, coaching workshops, or team development days.
