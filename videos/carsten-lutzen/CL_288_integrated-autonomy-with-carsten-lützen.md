# "Integrated Autonomy" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten introduces the Liberating Structure "Integrated Autonomy," a method for resolving apparent either/or tensions by surfacing actions that serve both sides simultaneously. It is typically used after Wicked Questions and pairs well with 1-2-4-All or 15% Solutions as a follow-up.

## Methods & Techniques

### Integrated Autonomy (ab 00:20)
- **What:** A Liberating Structure that takes a polarized either/or tension (e.g., standardization vs. team autonomy) and generates actions that serve both sides — turning an apparent dilemma into a "both/and" solution space.
- **When to use:** When a team or organization faces a tension that seems like a trade-off but may actually be solvable through creative integration. Often follows Wicked Questions, which provides the either/or prompt.
- **Group size:** Groups of ~4 people; works with multiple small groups in parallel.
- **Duration:** Approximately 11–15 minutes for the core structure (1 min individual + 5 min group for each side + both-sides round).
- **How it works:**
  1. Identify an either/or tension as the invitation (e.g., "How can we both standardize our way of working AND maintain team autonomy?").
  2. Draw or visualize three columns: Left (e.g., standardization), Right (e.g., team autonomy), and Middle (both).
  3. Individuals silently list actions that support the Left side, then the Right side (~1 min each).
  4. In groups of ~4, aggregate and collect the Left and Right side actions (~5 min).
  5. Individually and in silence, identify actions that could serve both sides simultaneously — place these in the Middle column.
  6. Collectively explore: Can any Middle actions be pulled toward the Left or Right to strengthen those sides? Prioritize the most promising solutions.
  7. Follow up with 15% Solutions to make the prioritized ideas actionable.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Xa_lgukGNZw

## Key Principles
- Integrated Autonomy reframes either/or tensions into both/and possibilities — the goal is not to choose a side but to transcend the dilemma.
- The structure works best when seeded with a genuine tension, ideally surfaced through Wicked Questions beforehand.
- The silent individual work before group discussion ensures diverse ideas surface before social convergence.
- There is no single canonical facilitation sequence — Carsten notes that the official Liberating Structures definition and various articles describe different steps; what matters is the left/right/both logic.

## Facilitation Tips
- According to Carsten, the structure "can be a bit difficult at first" — set expectations that it requires some cognitive effort before the insight emerges.
- Use a visual three-column layout (Left / Both / Right) to make the structure tangible and easy to follow.
- Follow up Integrated Autonomy with 15% Solutions so that the "both/and" ideas don't stay abstract but get translated into concrete small experiments.
- Invite participants to share their own facilitation riffs and variations — the structure has intentional flexibility.

## Best Used When
- A team is stuck in a perceived trade-off between two organizational values or goals (e.g., alignment vs. autonomy, speed vs. quality, consistency vs. innovation).
- After a Wicked Questions session has surfaced the tension and the group is ready to explore resolution.
- In retrospectives, team alignment sessions, or organizational design workshops where polarities need to be integrated rather than resolved by picking a side.

## Related Concepts
- **Wicked Questions** — typically precedes Integrated Autonomy by generating the either/or tension that serves as input.
- **15% Solutions** — recommended follow-up to translate the "both/and" ideas into actionable experiments.
- **Polarity Management** — underlying mental model: some tensions are not problems to solve but polarities to manage and integrate.
