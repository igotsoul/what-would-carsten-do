# Undercover Boss with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten introduces the "Undercover Boss" retrospective exercise from Retromat.org, designed to help teams shift from an internal to an outside-in perspective. It's particularly useful when a team keeps focusing on things outside their control — by imagining how a senior leader (CEO, VP, manager) would perceive the team, members gain a fresh, more objective view of their own dynamics and work.

## Methods & Techniques

### Undercover Boss Retrospective (ab 00:30)
- **What:** A perspective-taking exercise where the team imagines that a senior leader (e.g., the CEO) secretly joined the team for the sprint and asks: "What would they have seen?"
- **When to use:** When the team repeatedly focuses on things outside their control, or when they need a fresh outside-in perspective on their own strengths, pain points, and dynamics.
- **Group size:** Standard team size (not specified, works for typical Scrum teams)
- **Duration:** Not specified; fits within a standard retro slot
- **How it works:**
  1. Set the scene: Tell the team that a new member joined at the start of the sprint — and reveal it was actually the CEO (or another senior leader).
  2. Prompt the team to reflect through that person's eyes:
     - What pain points did they notice?
     - What did the team do well?
     - What values or behaviors bind this team together?
     - What drives progress in this team?
  3. Collect observations and insights from the team.
  4. Derive actionable items from the reflections.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=PS5FQRelhEI&t=30s

## Key Principles
- Teams that fixate on external annoyances benefit from being nudged into an outside-in perspective rather than continuing to circle the same complaints.
- The "senior leader lens" creates a sense of obligation and opportunity — it prompts more honest, distanced self-reflection than direct introspection alone.
- The persona doesn't have to be the CEO; any figure above the team (manager, VP) works — the key is choosing someone who represents an external, authoritative viewpoint.
- Perspective-taking exercises can unlock team insights that direct questioning misses.

## Facilitation Tips
- Carsten suggests framing the setup as a reveal: "I don't know if you noticed, but the new team member who joined this sprint was actually our CEO." This narrative framing draws the team in before the exercise begins.
- Keep the persona relevant but slightly removed — someone the team respects and would want to impress, but who isn't in the room.
- Use guiding questions to structure the reflection: team values, strengths, progress drivers, and pain points.
- Transition from observations to actionable items at the end — don't let it stay purely reflective.

## Best Used When
- A team is stuck in a pattern of discussing things they cannot influence.
- The team needs a mirror held up without it feeling like direct criticism.
- You want to surface team strengths alongside challenges in the same exercise.
- The team has done several retros and needs a fresh format to re-engage.
