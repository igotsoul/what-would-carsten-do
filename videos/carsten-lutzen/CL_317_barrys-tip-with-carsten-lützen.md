# "Barry's Tip" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten shares a micro-tweak to instruction-giving language — attributed to Barry Overeem — that prevents participants from starting activities before the facilitator has finished explaining. A single phrase change eliminates the common chaos of people jumping ahead mid-instruction.

## Methods & Techniques

### "Once I'm Done Explaining" Instruction Framing (ab 00:30)
- **What:** Instead of saying "What I want you to do is [instruction]", say "What I want you to do **once I'm done explaining** is [instruction]". This signals to participants that they should hold their action until the full instruction is delivered.
- **When to use:** Any time you give instructions to a group before an exercise, activity, or discussion — especially in workshops with physical materials or tasks that participants are eager to start.
- **Group size:** Any size
- **Duration:** Adds zero time — it's a single phrase insertion
- **How it works:**
  1. Begin your instruction with: *"What I want you to do, once I'm done explaining, is…"*
  2. Deliver the full instruction
  3. End with "…so please do that now."
  4. Participants have a clear trigger to start acting: the word "now" after the full explanation.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=AWAMktCjIXw&t=30s

## Key Principles
- Small language changes can have disproportionately large effects on group behavior.
- Participants often start acting on partial instructions — not out of defiance, but because the default phrasing ("What I want you to do is…") implies the action should follow immediately.
- Adding a timing signal ("once I'm done explaining") shifts the group from reactive to attentive mode.
- The tip originates from Barry Overeem, shared during a Liberating Structures immersion workshop.

## Facilitation Tips
- Pair the opening phrase with a clear action trigger at the end ("…so please do that now") to create a complete instruction container.
- This works for any exercise type — physical, reflective, digital.
- It requires no preparation or materials change — purely a language habit to build.

## Best Used When
- Facilitating groups that tend to start too early and miss part of the instructions.
- Running exercises with multiple steps where order matters.
- Working with large or energetic groups where managing attention is a challenge.
- Any workshop context where instruction chaos costs time and flow.
