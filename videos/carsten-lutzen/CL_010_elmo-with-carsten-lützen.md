# "ELMO" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten introduces ELMO (Enough, Let's Move On) — a group signal technique for redirecting repetitive or off-track conversations in workshops and meetings. He also offers three softer, flow-preserving alternatives for facilitators who want to move the group forward without the abruptness of a hard cut.

## Methods & Techniques

### ELMO — Enough, Let's Move On (ab 01:00)
- **What:** A shared group signal — verbal, physical, or object-based — that any participant can invoke to indicate the current conversation is no longer adding value and the group should move on.
- **When to use:** Whenever a discussion loops, loses relevance, or causes participants to zone out. Most effective in workshops, stand-ups, and structured sessions.
- **Group size:** Any; works for teams and larger workshop groups.
- **Duration:** The signal itself is instantaneous; the technique is introduced at session start.
- **How it works:**
  1. Introduce ELMO as a session norm at the very beginning — frame it as "it's okay to say when something isn't adding value."
  2. Agree on the signal: saying "Elmo" aloud, raising a hand, throwing an ELMO teddy bear toward the speaker, or using a bell/whistle.
  3. When someone invokes ELMO, the group acknowledges it and moves on without debate.
  4. As facilitator, role model it first — use it yourself before expecting the group to use it.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=H2Rvk5UVRT8&t=60

### Parking Lot Redirect (ab 03:45)
- **What:** Instead of cutting a conversation, the facilitator offers to "park" the topic and ensure it gets picked up later — either in a break or after the session.
- **When to use:** When ELMO feels too abrupt, or when the conversation has genuine value but is derailing the current flow.
- **How it works:** Observe that some participants have zoned out, then ask the group: "Is it okay if we park this?" Commit to reminding the relevant people to continue the conversation at a better moment.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=H2Rvk5UVRT8&t=225

### Best Hopes Check (ab 04:30)
- **What:** A redirecting question that reconnects the group to the session's stated purpose, surfacing whether the current conversation is still aligned.
- **When to use:** When the group has drifted onto a tangent; especially effective when best hopes or session goals were established at the start.
- **How it works:** Ask: "Is this conversation still moving us closer to our best hopes?" If the answer is no, use that as a natural pivot point to return to the original path.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=H2Rvk5UVRT8&t=270

### "What Do We Need Right Now?" (ab 05:15)
- **What:** An open question that invites the group to self-assess where their energy and attention are best invested — without implying the current conversation is wrong.
- **When to use:** When a conversation may still have value, but the group needs to make a conscious choice about where to focus.
- **How it works:** Ask: "What do we need right now to move us forward? Is it this conversation, or something else? Where is the biggest return on the time and energy we're investing?"
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=H2Rvk5UVRT8&t=315

## Key Principles
- Introduce session tools like ELMO upfront as explicit norms — never mid-session, when it can feel like a manipulation.
- A tool introduced but never used loses its power. As facilitator, you must model it first to make it socially acceptable for others.
- ELMO can be goofy or playful (teddy bear, bell) — this lowers the social cost of invoking it.
- Psychological safety determines which tool fits: ELMO works well in safe groups; softer alternatives are better when trust is still developing.
- Carsten distinguishes between cutting a conversation (ELMO) and redirecting it gracefully — both are valid, and skilled facilitators choose based on context and group energy.

## Facilitation Tips
- Frame ELMO in your session contract as a shared norm, not a facilitator override: "We all have permission to say this."
- If using a physical prop (teddy bear, bell), having it visible on the table primes participants to remember it.
- The "best hopes" check only works if you've established best hopes at the start — invest in that opening step.
- Carsten's personal preference is to use the flow-preserving alternatives more often than ELMO itself, because he values conversational momentum.
- When parking a topic, always make a visible, explicit commitment (e.g., write it on the parking lot flip chart) — vague promises to "come back to it" erode trust.

## Best Used When
- A workshop or meeting has a defined agenda and limited time.
- Conversations regularly go off-track or loop without resolution.
- You want to give participants shared ownership over session pacing, not just the facilitator.
- You're working with a psychologically safe group where direct signals won't feel like attacks.
- Sessions have been set up with explicit norms or a "working agreement" at the start.
