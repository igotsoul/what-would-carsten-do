# "The Wave" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten shares a metaphor — introduced to him by Simon Powers — of viewing Agile as a wave: powerful, transformative, but possibly cresting. He explores the underlying currents that shaped the Agile wave (continuous learning, trust, purpose) and identifies three emerging currents he believes may form the next wave: human-centeredness, complexity thinking, and purpose-driven collaboration. He closes with a personal reflection on what it means to name one's role after a wave versus the deeper currents beneath it.

## Methods & Techniques

### The Wave Metaphor as a Sense-Making Tool (ab 00:45)
- **What:** A conceptual framework for understanding movements like Agile as waves — visible, powerful, temporary — while recognizing the underlying currents (values, needs, beliefs) that generate and outlast them.
- **When to use:** When coaching teams or organizations that are questioning the relevance of Agile, or when helping people reconnect with the "why" behind practices and frameworks.
- **Group size:** Works for individual reflection, team conversations, or leadership workshops.
- **Duration:** Can anchor a short 20-minute conversation or a longer strategic dialogue.
- **How it works:**
  1. Introduce the wave metaphor: a wave is visible and impactful, but it's created by deeper underwater currents.
  2. Name the "wave" the group has been surfing (e.g., Agile, digital transformation, lean).
  3. Ask: what were the underlying currents that created this wave? (e.g., desire for better ways of working, shorter feedback loops, human over process)
  4. Ask: is this wave still building, peaking, or calming? What evidence do we see?
  5. Explore: what new currents are forming beneath the surface that might become the next wave?
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=mSBHH5EPpwY&t=45s

### Identifying Emerging Currents (ab 03:30)
- **What:** A reflective practice of scanning for early-stage movements or values gaining momentum before they crystallize into named frameworks or methodologies.
- **When to use:** Strategic conversations about the future of work, team culture, or organizational design. Useful for coaches and consultants reflecting on their own positioning.
- **How it works:** Carsten identifies three currents he's watching:
  1. **Human-centered work** — psychological safety, empathy, authentic self at work
  2. **Complexity thinking** — frameworks like Cynefin, embracing emergence, resilience over control and predictability
  3. **Purpose-driven collaboration** — connecting individual and team motivation to meaningful work
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=mSBHH5EPpwY&t=210s

### Role Identity Reframing (ab 05:30)
- **What:** A coaching prompt for practitioners to reconsider how they name their role — moving from wave-based labels (e.g., "Agile Coach") to current-based ones that reflect deeper, more durable value.
- **When to use:** Career coaching, professional identity conversations, or team discussions about evolving roles in organizations.
- **How it works:** Ask: "Are you named after the wave, or after the current?" Explore alternative titles such as Team Coach, Organizational Coach, Facilitator of Change, Complexity Sherpa, Catalyst of Learning, Sense-Making Partner.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=mSBHH5EPpwY&t=330s

## Key Principles
- Waves (movements, methodologies) are powerful but temporary. The underlying currents — values, human needs, organizational desires — are more durable.
- The Agile wave brought vital language and structure around collaboration, adaptability, and customer focus, but may be calming. Its value was in surfacing currents that were always there.
- Continuous learning, trust, and purpose were the core currents beneath Agile — and they persist regardless of what the next "wave" is called.
- Carsten argues that practitioners risk becoming irrelevant if they identify too closely with a wave rather than with the deeper work it represented.
- "Stop being a surfer and figure out how scuba gear works" — dive into the currents instead of riding the surface.
- Psychological safety is not optional: it's a prerequisite for people to bring their authentic selves to work and deliver excellent work.

## Facilitation Tips
- Use the wave/currents metaphor to defuse defensiveness around "Agile is dead" conversations — it reframes decline as natural, not as failure.
- When introducing this metaphor, ask the group to name the wave first before diving into currents — this grounds the conversation in shared experience.
- Allow space for personal reflection on purpose: Carsten models vulnerability by sharing that purpose-driven work is deeply personal to him, which invites others to do the same.
- The "Complexity Sherpa" framing (attributed to a colleague of Carsten's) is a useful provocation in identity workshops — the guide can show the path but the group must make the decisions.

## Best Used When
- Teams or organizations are questioning whether Agile still applies to them
- A coach or facilitator is helping a leadership group think about organizational transformation beyond current frameworks
- Someone is navigating a professional identity shift (e.g., from Scrum Master to something broader)
- You want to open a conversation about psychological safety or purpose without leading with those terms directly
