# Powerful Questions Part 2

## Summary
Carsten continues his series on powerful questions, this time showcasing the Zombie Scrum First Aid Kit card deck as a practical source. He explores how powerful questions can break the monotony of retrospectives and routine ceremonies by shifting from post-it brainstorming to dialogue-based reflection. He also demonstrates how to embed powerful questions into everyday team rituals like stand-ups, not just retrospectives.

## Methods & Techniques

### Powerful Questions Card Draw (ab 00:45)
- **What:** Participants draw a card from a prepared deck of powerful questions and reflect on or answer them, either individually or in turn.
- **When to use:** Retrospectives, team meetings, stand-ups, or as an icebreaker to get people mentally present.
- **Group size:** Any team size
- **Duration:** Flexible — can be a quick 5-min check-in or a longer dialogue exercise
- **How it works:**
  1. Prepare a deck of powerful questions (e.g. Zombie Scrum First Aid Kit, Liberating Structures list, or self-authored cards)
  2. Pick a card at random — or have participants take turns picking
  3. Read the question aloud
  4. Participants either write down their answer, share in turn, or pass a token to signal who speaks next
  5. Keep it lightweight — one question can be enough to spark meaningful dialogue
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=HSCx8etynNw&t=45s

### Stand-Up Concept Jar (ab 02:30)
- **What:** After each stand-up, a team member draws a concept from a jar and explains it to the group — used to reinforce shared understanding of technical or domain terms.
- **When to use:** Development teams with a shared tech stack; any team wanting to build collective knowledge incrementally
- **Group size:** Small dev teams
- **Duration:** 2–5 min, appended to the daily stand-up
- **How it works:**
  1. Fill a jar with notes, each containing a concept from the team's language, tech stack, or domain
  2. After the stand-up, one person draws a note and explains the concept
  3. Rotate responsibility over time
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=HSCx8etynNw&t=150s

### Powerful Question as Stand-Up Closer (ab 03:00)
- **What:** End each stand-up by reading a single powerful question and having a brief team discussion around its relevance.
- **When to use:** Teams where stand-ups feel routine or disconnected from deeper team issues
- **Group size:** Any team
- **Duration:** 2–5 min add-on to stand-up
- **How it works:**
  1. Keep a card deck or printed questions near the stand-up space
  2. After the standard stand-up, draw one card (e.g. "Gently Challenged Belief")
  3. Read the question aloud
  4. Ask: "What does this mean for our team? Is it relevant? Anything we should do?"
  5. Keep it short — this is a nudge, not a full retrospective
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=HSCx8etynNw&t=180s

## Key Principles
- Powerful questions break the monotony of recurring ceremonies — they shift the mode from reporting to reflecting
- Moving from post-it brainstorming to dialogue-based exercises changes the quality of engagement
- A well-prepared question deck lets the facilitator participate as an equal — you start the exercise, then join in rather than staying in observer mode
- Reflection doesn't need a dedicated session: embedding one question into everyday rituals (stand-up, team meetings) creates continuous learning habits
- Participants can also contribute questions in advance, which can then be shuffled into the deck

## Facilitation Tips
- Let participants take turns drawing and reading cards — distributes ownership and removes the facilitator as the sole driver
- Pass a physical token to signal whose turn it is to speak — helps with orderly dialogue in groups
- Use powerful questions as icebreakers at the start of meetings to ensure participants are mentally present before the main agenda
- Prepare questions in advance so you can attend as a participant yourself — particularly valuable when you are part of the team you're facilitating

## Best Used When
- Retrospectives feel stale or predictable
- A team needs a low-effort way to introduce reflection into daily rituals
- You want to shift from broadcasting to dialogue in stand-ups or check-ins
- You are both facilitator and team member and want to participate on equal terms
