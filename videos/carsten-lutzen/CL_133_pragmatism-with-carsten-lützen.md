# "Pragmatism" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten Lützen, agile coach and Scrum Master, makes the case that pragmatism — not frameworks — should drive how teams work. He argues that "Scrum is never the answer" and that coaches should start with lightweight iterative loops, experiment freely, and let appropriate practices emerge from the team's real context rather than imposing prescriptive frameworks from day one.

## Methods & Techniques

### Minimal Iterative Loop as Starting Point (ab 00:35)
- **What:** Instead of implementing Scrum wholesale, Carsten starts teams with a stripped-down iterative loop: set a goal → do the work → review how you did → retrospect on process → set a new goal.
- **When to use:** When onboarding a new team or starting a change initiative where full Scrum adoption might be premature or forced.
- **Group size:** Any team size
- **Duration:** One iteration/sprint length; built up incrementally over time
- **How it works:**
  1. Set an iteration goal (or "Sprint Goal" if Scrum terminology fits)
  2. Do the work
  3. At the end: review — did we achieve the goal? Why or why not?
  4. Retrospect on the process and how the team worked
  5. Set a new goal and repeat
  6. Let structure (Scrum or otherwise) emerge from what the team learns
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=rG5VtNadOYs&t=35

### Experiment-and-Learn on Work Item Formats (ab 02:30)
- **What:** Treat backlog item formats (user stories, problem stories, use cases) as experiments — try a format, assess if it helps articulate what you want to achieve, and swap it out if it doesn't.
- **When to use:** When a team is struggling with how to express requirements or when user stories feel forced.
- **How it works:**
  1. Try a format (e.g. user stories)
  2. Assess: does this format help us describe what we want to achieve?
  3. If not, try an alternative (problem stories, use cases, etc.)
  4. Choose based on the problem at hand, not framework convention

## Key Principles
- "Scrum is never the answer" — frameworks are a source of inspiration, not a destination. Carsten uses Scrum as a reference but never mandates it upfront.
- Complex problems (per the Cynefin framework) don't respond well to plan-driven or prescriptive approaches. They require experimentation to surface emerging practices — not best practices or even good practices.
- Scrum works by putting constraints on a system, but you need to understand the problem before you can design useful constraints. Pragmatism fills that gap.
- A backlog is never "100% user stories." Whatever format makes sense for the team and context is the right format.
- Avoid blindly following scaling frameworks. Step back, ask what you could try, and identify leading and lagging indicators of a good solution.
- Iterative and incremental work is itself a form of pragmatism — each step builds on what was learned before.

## Facilitation Tips
- When introducing agile to a team, resist naming the framework. Start with the minimal iterative loop and let the team discover what works.
- Use "what could we try out?" and "how would we know it's working?" as facilitation questions to guide teams away from prescription and toward experimentation.
- Invite teams to reflect on leading and lagging indicators of success — this builds shared understanding of what "good" looks like before committing to a method.

## Best Used When
- Coaching a team new to agile where framework imposition risks resistance or cargo-culting
- Working in product development or IoT contexts where problems sit in the "complex" domain of Cynefin
- Facilitating a process design session where the team needs to define their own way of working
- Reflecting on your own coaching practice and whether you are being prescriptive vs. adaptive

## Related Concepts
- **Cynefin Framework** — referenced to explain why complex problems require emergent rather than prescriptive practices
- **Scrum** — used as an inspirational framework, not a mandate; Sprint Goals and retrospectives are referenced as useful starting-point elements
- **User Stories / Problem Stories / Use Cases** — discussed as interchangeable formats depending on context
- **Scaling Frameworks** — cautioned against blind adoption; Carsten advocates stepping back before committing to any large framework
