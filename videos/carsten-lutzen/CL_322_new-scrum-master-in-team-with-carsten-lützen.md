# "New 'Scrum Master' in Team" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten shares his approach for a Scrum Master joining a new or existing team. The core message: resist the urge to immediately change things. Instead, observe, form hypotheses, run small experiments, and help the team discover better ways of working themselves.

## Methods & Techniques

### Silent Observer Approach (ab 00:35)
- **What:** Before intervening, spend time watching team dynamics without judgment or premature conclusions.
- **When to use:** First days/weeks with a new team, before making any structural changes.
- **Group size:** Any team size.
- **Duration:** Ongoing in early phase of engagement.
- **How it works:**
  1. Attend events without facilitating — just observe.
  2. Note patterns (e.g., low attendance, low board usage).
  3. Form a hypothesis about the underlying cause.
  4. Gather data by asking targeted questions.
  5. Run a small experiment to test the hypothesis (e.g., try stand-up without the board).
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=4XIMbTwhkQw&t=35

### Small Step Change (ab 01:20)
- **What:** Introduce changes incrementally rather than attempting a full transformation from day one.
- **When to use:** Any time a Scrum Master is tempted to overhaul team practices quickly.
- **How it works:** Identify one friction point, experiment with a small change, observe the result, then move to the next.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=4XIMbTwhkQw&t=80

### One-on-Ones with Team Members (ab 02:45)
- **What:** Individual conversations with each team member to build trust and surface hidden perspectives.
- **When to use:** Early in a new engagement; also valuable when team dynamics feel stuck.
- **Group size:** 1-on-1.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=4XIMbTwhkQw&t=165

## Key Principles
- **Be curious, not prescriptive.** Ask questions to understand, not to demonstrate expertise.
- **Assume positive intent.** People are doing what they believe is best right now — the Scrum Master's job is to help them see a better way, not to judge current practices.
- **Empirical process control as a compass.** Carsten anchors to the three pillars: Transparency (e.g., daily stand-up), Inspection (product and process), Adaptation (adjusting both product and process based on what you learn).
- **Humility before change.** Rushing too many changes risks losing people's trust and buy-in.

## Facilitation Tips
- Use stand-up variations as low-risk experiments: try it with the board, then without — observe what changes.
- Don't ask questions just to ask questions ("why are you doing it like this?") — ask to genuinely understand and generate a testable hypothesis.
- One-on-ones create psychological safety before group-level interventions.

## Best Used When
- A Scrum Master is joining an unfamiliar team.
- A coach is trying to support a team that has established (possibly dysfunctional) habits.
- You need to build trust before introducing change.

## Related Concepts
- Empirical process control (transparency, inspection, adaptation)

- Servant leadership
- Change management (incremental vs. big-bang change)
