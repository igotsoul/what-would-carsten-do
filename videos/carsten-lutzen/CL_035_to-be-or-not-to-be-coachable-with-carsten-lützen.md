# "To be, or not to be... Coachable" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten Lützen explores what it means to be coachable — a quality he considers essential for any productive coaching relationship. Drawing on personal experience as a competitive dancer who resisted feedback, he outlines five key traits that make someone genuinely coachable. The video is a short reflective piece aimed at both coaches and those being coached.

## Methods & Techniques

### The Five Traits of Coachability (ab 01:30)
- **What:** Carsten identifies five concrete behaviors that define a coachable person, framed as a practical checklist for self-assessment.
- **When to use:** When evaluating whether a coaching relationship is likely to be productive, or when preparing individuals or teams to get value from coaching.
- **Group size:** Individual reflection, but applicable to teams
- **Duration:** Ongoing self-assessment practice
- **How it works:**
  1. **Hearing feedback and working with it** — not just nodding, but actively integrating feedback from coaches, peers, or any source
  2. **Actively seeking feedback** — proactively asking "how could I do this better?" rather than waiting for feedback to arrive
  3. **Willingness to experiment** — stepping outside comfort zones to try new behaviors, knowing you can always revert
  4. **Acting like a sponge, not a mirror** — absorbing feedback and sitting with it, rather than deflecting it back ("it's not me, it's something else")
  5. **Choosing to pause and reflect** — deliberately reviewing interactions: what could I do differently? How could I improve the experience for others?
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=f0dlWSyFW2I&t=90

### Pause and Reflect Practice (ab 03:30)
- **What:** A simple reflective habit applied after meetings or facilitated sessions to build coachability as a discipline.
- **When to use:** After any significant interaction — a meeting, a facilitation session, a stakeholder conversation
- **How it works:** Ask yourself: What could I do differently or better next time? How could I make a better experience for participants? How could I ensure stakeholders are on board?
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=f0dlWSyFW2I&t=210

## Key Principles

- Coachability is not passive — it requires active effort: seeking feedback, experimenting, and persevering through difficulty
- If change were easy, it would have already happened. The hard, subtle behaviors are precisely what coaching targets
- Being honest with yourself is as important as being open to others — don't tell the coach what you think they want to hear
- A mirror deflects; a sponge absorbs. Coachable people take feedback in, even when it's uncomfortable
- Willingness to experiment is coachability in action: try something new, observe what happens, and you can always revert
- Humility and honesty are the foundation — without them, the other traits don't take hold
- According to Carsten, coaches have a responsibility to assess coachability early; investing effort in someone who isn't coachable wastes everyone's time

## Facilitation Tips

- When entering a new coaching engagement, Carsten implicitly recommends assessing coachability upfront — is this person/team ready to work with feedback?
- Frame experimentation as low-risk: "try it once, you can always go back to how you did it before" lowers resistance to change
- When giving feedback, watch for the "talking to a door" dynamic — head-nodding without behavioral change is a signal of low coachability
- Encourage coachees to self-assess against the five traits rather than diagnosing them yourself — it builds self-awareness

## Best Used When

- Preparing a team or individual for a coaching engagement
- Diagnosing why a coaching relationship isn't producing results
- Facilitating a conversation about team learning culture or growth mindset
- Onboarding a new coachee and setting expectations about what productive coaching requires from both sides
