# "Retro Dart" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten introduces Retro Dart, a short retrospective evaluation exercise sourced from retromat.org. Teams use a dartboard visual to rate how well the retrospective itself went across three dimensions: whether people said what was important, whether they spoke openly, and whether they're confident progress will follow. The exercise surfaces psychological safety issues and helps the facilitator improve future retros.

## Methods & Techniques

### Retro Dart (ab 00:20)
- **What:** A visual retrospective-of-the-retrospective exercise where participants place sticky notes on a dartboard to indicate how much they agree with three reflective statements. Bullseye = 100% agreement, outer edge = 0%.
- **When to use:** At the end of a retrospective, to evaluate the quality of the session itself — especially useful when the facilitator suspects psychological safety or follow-through might be issues.
- **Group size:** Any team size; each person places their own marker independently.
- **Duration:** ~5–10 minutes (implied by simplicity and video length).
- **How it works:**
  1. Draw a dartboard with three concentric rings on a whiteboard or flipchart.
  2. Label the bullseye as 100% (full agreement) and the outer edge as 0%.
  3. Introduce three statements (classic set below), one at a time:
     - *"Did I manage to say what was important to me?"*
     - *"Did I manage to openly say what was on my mind?"*
     - *"How confident am I that we're actually going to make progress in the next sprint?"*
  4. Participants place a small sticky note (or dot sticker) on the dartboard for each statement.
  5. Debrief the patterns: clustering near the bullseye vs. the edges reveals team sentiment.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=5fYYhZ8H-OY&t=20

## Key Principles
- The three classic questions target three distinct failure modes of retrospectives: voice (did people speak up?), safety (did people feel free to be honest?), and effectiveness (do people believe anything will change?).
- If people felt safe to speak but don't expect progress, the facilitator needs to improve follow-through and commitment — not the exercises.
- If people couldn't speak openly, psychological safety is the root issue and needs direct attention.
- If people couldn't say what was important, the chosen exercises may have been a poor fit for the team's needs.
- The questions are variable — Carsten explicitly encourages adapting them to the current team focus.

## Facilitation Tips
- Use small sticky notes or dot stickers instead of actual darts (Carsten jokes: "not actual darts, please").
- Have participants place markers simultaneously or one statement at a time to avoid anchoring.
- Read the spatial pattern before interpreting: a split distribution tells a richer story than a cluster.
- Use the results to diagnose your own facilitation, not to judge the team.
- Vary the three statements to reflect what matters most right now — the format is the constant, the questions are flexible.

## Best Used When
- Closing a retrospective and wanting structured feedback on the session itself.
- The facilitator senses the retro didn't land well and wants concrete signal.
- Psychological safety or follow-through are ongoing concerns with the team.
- You want a quick, low-barrier check-in that doesn't require much setup.

## Related Concepts

- **Retrospective effectiveness:** Connects to the broader question of whether retros create real change, not just conversation.
