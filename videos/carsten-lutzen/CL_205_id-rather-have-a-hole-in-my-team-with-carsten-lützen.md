# "I'd rather have a hole in my team..." with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten shares a favorite quote — "I'd rather have a hole in my team than an asshole" — and unpacks why a single negative team member can erode psychological safety faster than any structural fix can repair it. He explores the contagious nature of emotions, the danger of normalizing low-grade toxicity, and how coaches and facilitators can intervene before lasting damage is done.

## Methods & Techniques

### Coaching the Negative Team Member (ab 03:30)
- **What:** Instead of removing a disruptive team member immediately, coach them to recognize the impact their behavior has on others — helping them grow both professionally and personally.
- **When to use:** When one person's negativity or toxic behavior is shrinking the psychological safety of the rest of the team.
- **Group size:** 1-on-1 coaching conversation
- **Duration:** Ongoing; not a one-time intervention
- **How it works:**
  1. Observe the behavior and its effect on the group.
  2. Create space for the individual to reflect: "What is it that your interactions are doing to the rest of the team?"
  3. Help them understand how others perceive their comments or tone.
  4. Explore what might be pushing their own buttons — frustration or unmet needs often drive negative behavior.
  5. Set clear boundaries collaboratively, not unilaterally.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=BItraJy4fpQ&t=210

### Calling Out Low-Grade Toxicity (ab 04:00)
- **What:** Naming and addressing small, seemingly harmless negative comments (sarcasm, teasing, put-downs) before they normalize into a toxic team culture.
- **When to use:** Whenever you observe recurring "fun" comments that subtly demean others, even in teams that feel psychologically safe.
- **Group size:** Team setting or 1-on-1
- **Duration:** In-the-moment intervention, brief
- **How it works:**
  1. Notice the comment without immediately escalating.
  2. Reflect it back: "How is this making the receiver feel? How does it shape how they see the world?"
  3. Check whether the whole team genuinely consents to this tone — don't assume shared comfort.
  4. Help the group recalibrate if the tone has drifted too far (Carsten shares an example of an external facilitator pointing this out during a retrospective).

## Key Principles
- **Negativity spreads faster than productivity.** One person can undo the psychological safety of an otherwise high-performing team.
- **Emotions are contagious** — in both directions. An enthusiastic person lifts the room; a chronically negative one drains it. As facilitators, we are emotion-setters too.
- **Diversity and inclusion is not a blank check for toxic behavior.** Including different viewpoints is essential, but delivery and respect matter just as much as the viewpoint itself.
- **The hidden cost of silence.** If one person makes the rest of the team afraid to speak up or pitch ideas, the team has already lost more than that one person could ever contribute.
- **Role-model what you want to see.** Coaches, leaders, and facilitators have a unique lever: they can actively demonstrate the tone, openness, and respect they want the team to embody.
- **Don't normalize low-grade toxicity.** Small digs and "harmless" jokes are a gradient — left unchecked, they shift what the team considers acceptable.

## Facilitation Tips
- When you observe low-grade toxicity in a team, don't ignore it hoping it will pass — name it gently and help the group reflect on the effect.
- Use questions rather than judgments when coaching a disruptive person: "What do you think this comment does to the person who receives it?"
- Before assuming a team's rough tone is healthy, bring in an outside perspective — a second facilitator or observer can surface patterns that insiders have normalized.
- Check consent explicitly: don't assume everyone is equally comfortable with a team's humor or communication style.
- Intervening early is far cheaper than rebuilding psychological safety after it has collapsed.

## Best Used When
- You are coaching a Scrum Master, team lead, or manager who is struggling with one persistently negative team member.
- You are facilitating a retrospective and notice recurring sarcasm, dismissiveness, or side comments that chill participation.
- A team is reflecting on why their psychological safety has declined despite no obvious structural changes.
- You want to build the case — for a leader or HR — for why "just tolerating" a difficult personality is not a neutral choice.
