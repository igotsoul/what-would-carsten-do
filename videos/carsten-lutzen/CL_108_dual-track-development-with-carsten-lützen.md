# "Dual Track Development" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten Lützen introduces dual track development as a mental model for running product discovery and delivery in parallel. He explains how discovery cycles — short, time-boxed experiments — feed validated ideas into the main delivery engine, and shares practical prototyping techniques for learning fast and cheaply.

## Methods & Techniques

### Dual Track Development (ab 00:30)
- **What:** A model where product discovery and iterative delivery run as two parallel tracks simultaneously, with discovery feeding validated ideas into the delivery cycle.
- **When to use:** When teams need to balance predictable delivery with continuous exploration of new ideas and product bets.
- **Group size:** Typically the full product team; discovery work can involve a subset (e.g., tech lead, developers, UX designer).
- **Duration:** Discovery cycles are short time-boxes — an hour, a day, or two days. Delivery cycles follow the team's existing rhythm (e.g., sprints).
- **How it works:**
  1. Run the main delivery cycle (e.g., Scrum) for predictable value delivery.
  2. In parallel, run short time-boxed discovery experiments on new ideas.
  3. Evaluate learnings: if the idea shows promise, iterate on it with another small time-box; if not, scrap it without attachment.
  4. Once an idea is validated, feed it into the main delivery engine.
  5. Keep both tracks in sync — the delivery team should be aware discovery is ongoing and vice versa.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=jjqWeovoCqU&t=30

### Paper Prototyping (ab 02:30)
- **What:** Creating quick hand-drawn sketches of UI flows to test whether a concept makes sense before investing in digital implementation.
- **When to use:** Early-stage discovery for websites or applications when you want to validate flow and usability cheaply.
- **Group size:** Small — one or a few people.
- **Duration:** Very short; the intent is to produce something testable within hours.
- **How it works:** Sketch the UI screens or flow on paper, walk through them to check if the flow makes sense, then use the sketches to test with users or iterate further.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=jjqWeovoCqU&t=150

### Fake Door Testing (ab 03:00)
- **What:** Presenting users with a feature or option that doesn't yet exist to measure genuine interest before building it.
- **When to use:** When you want to validate demand for a feature without committing development resources.
- **How it works:** Show the user a link, button, or option as if it exists. When they engage with it, inform them it hasn't been built yet and thank them for showing interest. The click-through signals real intent.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=jjqWeovoCqU&t=180

### Wizard of Oz Prototyping (ab 03:10)
- **What:** Simulating a product experience manually behind the scenes so the user believes they are interacting with a real system.
- **When to use:** When you want to test a user experience or workflow before automating or engineering it.
- **How it works:** A team member manually performs the "backend" actions while the user interacts with a front-end that appears functional, generating fast learning about desirability and usability.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=jjqWeovoCqU&t=190

## Key Principles
- Iterative delivery optimizes for predictability and value; discovery optimizes for learning — you need both running simultaneously.
- There is no shame in cheap, low-fidelity prototypes. Speed of learning matters more than polish in the discovery track.
- Validated ideas should flow from discovery into the delivery engine — discovery without delivery is worthless and vice versa.
- Keep the two tracks in sync: the delivery team should always know discovery is happening, and discoverers should stay connected to the delivery reality.
- Rotate who participates in discovery work to distribute the energy and avoid frustration when ideas don't pan out.
- Expect to explore many ideas before finding one worth building. Carsten frames this as normal, not as failure.
- "Test fast and cheap" — Carsten's stated mantra for the discovery track.

## Facilitation Tips
- Frame discovery as a no-judgment space: Carsten emphasizes "no hard feelings" when an idea gets scrapped after testing. Establishing psychological safety around failure is essential for discovery to work.
- Avoid separating discovery and delivery into isolated sessions with different people. Carsten recommends keeping them integrated so both sides remain aware of and connected to each other.
- Use time-boxing aggressively in discovery — define upfront how far you want to get with an idea in a fixed window (an hour, a day) to keep experiments lean.
- Rotate team members through discovery work to share the cognitive and emotional load of exploring uncertain territory.

## Best Used When
- A team has a stable delivery rhythm but no structured way to explore new product ideas.
- There is pressure to keep delivering while also needing to de-risk future work.
- Teams are struggling with the tension between "just build it" and "we need to learn first."
- Product managers or coaches want to introduce lightweight discovery practices without disrupting existing delivery processes.

## Related Concepts
- **Scrum** — mentioned as a common delivery engine that dual track can run alongside.
- **Infinity loop / double diamond** — Carsten references the classic infinity-style lifecycle (discovery → delivery → discovery) as a related model.
- **Iterative development** — the foundational practice that both tracks build on.
- **UX research** — closely related; UX designers are cited as natural contributors to the discovery track.
- **Lean Startup / Build-Measure-Learn** — not named, but the underlying logic of fast cheap experiments maps directly to this framework.
