# "Reframing" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten introduces reframing as a core coaching technique for shifting perspective on problems — moving from the problem space to the solution space. He explains how to apply different lenses (user, other team member, family) to help individuals and teams see an issue in a new context, and connects it to the Undercover Boss retrospective exercise.

## Methods & Techniques

### Reframing via Perspective Shifts (ab 00:30)
- **What:** Deliberately viewing a problem through someone else's eyes to surface new insights and shift focus toward solutions.
- **When to use:** In 1-on-1 coaching sessions, team retrospectives, or group coaching when a team is stuck on a problem or in conflict.
- **Group size:** Works for individuals, pairs, or full teams.
- **Duration:** Not prescriptive — used as a questioning technique within a broader session.
- **How it works:**
  1. Identify the problem or tension the person/team is focused on.
  2. Ask them to view it through a different lens — the user's perspective, a colleague's perspective, the rest of the team's perspective.
  3. For conflicts: ask how the other person sees it, and how the rest of the team perceives it.
  4. For heavy personal burden: ask how a significant other or family member would notice the change if the problem were resolved — shifting focus to signs of improvement.
  5. Keep redirecting toward "what would it look like if this were solved?" rather than dwelling on the problem itself.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=QrpxRHTG318&t=30

### Undercover Boss Retrospective Exercise (ab 02:20)
- **What:** A retrospective prompt that asks the team to imagine an outside observer (e.g., the company CEO, an undercover user) watching them work — what would that person say?
- **When to use:** Retrospectives where the team is too internally focused and needs an external frame of reference.
- **Group size:** Team retrospective setting.
- **Duration:** Not specified; used as a prompt within a retro.
- **How it works:**
  1. Pose the question: "Imagine a new team member who is the CEO of the company joined today — what would they say about how we work?"
  2. Alternatively: "Imagine an undercover user sat with us — what feedback would they give?"
  3. Use the responses to surface blind spots and shift perspective on team habits or problems.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=QrpxRHTG318&t=140

## Key Principles
- Reframing means putting a problem into a different context or phrasing — the facts don't change, but the perspective does.
- Some problems feel real internally but have no impact on the user; the user's perspective can reveal this quickly.
- In conflict situations, both parties are often locked in their own view — asking "how does the other person see this?" breaks the loop.
- Asking "how would your family notice things are better?" brings the whole person into the conversation and acknowledges that work problems follow people home.
- The goal of reframing is always to move toward signs of a solution, not to deepen analysis of the problem.

## Facilitation Tips
- Use reframing questions naturally within a session rather than announcing "we will now reframe."
- For conflicts, layer the perspectives: self → other party → rest of team → outside observer.
- The family/home perspective question ("how would your partner notice a change?") is particularly powerful for someone who is emotionally burdened — use it carefully and with empathy.
- Always connect back to solutions: reframing is a means to an end, not a reflection exercise in itself.

## Best Used When
- A coachee or team is stuck in problem-focused thinking and struggling to see options.
- There is interpersonal conflict and both sides are entrenched in their own perspective.
- A retrospective discussion is going in circles without producing actionable insights.
- Someone is carrying work stress into their personal life and needs to reconnect with what "better" would look like.

## Related Concepts
- **Undercover Boss Exercise** — retrospective format that uses an external observer persona as a reframing device.
- **Solution-Focused Coaching** — the broader approach that reframing supports; focus on signs of solution rather than root cause analysis.
- **Group Coaching** — Carsten positions team retrospectives as a form of group coaching where reframing is a core tool.
