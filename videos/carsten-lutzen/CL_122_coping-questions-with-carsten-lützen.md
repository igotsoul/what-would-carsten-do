# "Coping Questions" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten introduces the Coping Question, a technique from the Solution Focused coaching toolbox. It helps a coachee who is struggling with a difficult situation reconnect with their own past experiences and resources to find a way forward. The technique is simple, non-directive, and rooted in the belief that the coachee already has the answers within them.

## Methods & Techniques

### Coping Question (ab 00:45)
- **What:** A coaching question that asks the coachee how they have previously managed or coped with a similar difficult situation, redirecting attention from the problem to their own proven resources.
- **When to use:** When a coachee feels stuck, overwhelmed, or unable to see a way forward in a challenging situation — particularly in 1-on-1 coaching conversations.
- **Group size:** 1-on-1 (coach and coachee)
- **Duration:** A single question within a coaching conversation; reflection time varies
- **How it works:**
  1. Listen to the coachee describe their current difficulty (e.g., a problem with a specific person or situation).
  2. Ask: *"How have you coped with this in the past?"* — explicitly anchoring it in the past, not the future.
  3. Allow the coachee space to reflect on how they previously handled similar situations.
  4. Help them identify which resources, strategies, or strengths helped before.
  5. Explore together how those same approaches might apply to the current challenge.

## Key Principles
- The coachee is the expert on their own life — the coach's role is to channel the coachee's inner resources, not to provide answers.
- Most people have already faced something similar to their current challenge. The fact that they are still here is evidence of resilience.
- Looking backward (to past coping) is a powerful way to unlock forward movement — it is concrete and grounded in real experience rather than hypothetical future action.
- Solution Focused coaching deliberately avoids problem analysis; instead it surfaces what has already worked.

## Facilitation Tips
- Phrase the question specifically to the situation at hand — don't use a generic formula; tailor it so the coachee feels heard.
- After asking, stay silent and give the coachee genuine space to reflect. Don't rush to follow up.
- The question itself is simple; the skill lies in the timing and the trust you place in the coachee to find their own answer.

## Best Used When
- A coachee feels stuck or overwhelmed and can't see options.
- You want to shift focus from problem-dwelling to resource-activation.
- The coachee has been through similar challenges before (which, according to Carsten, is almost always true).
- You are working within a Solution Focused or strengths-based coaching framework.
