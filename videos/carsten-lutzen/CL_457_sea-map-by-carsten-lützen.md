# Sea Map by Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten Lützen presents the Sea Map (also called Treasure Map or Nautical Map) — a visual retrospective exercise where the team draws a nautical scene representing their journey toward a shared goal. The team sketches their boat, a treasure representing their ideal future state, obstacles like storms and sea monsters, and other crews they interact with. Using a solutions-focused coaching lens, the exercise helps teams chart a course, identify impediments optimistically, and maintain a running visual of their progress over 2–4 sprints.

## Methods & Techniques

### Sea Map Retrospective (ab 00:30)
- **What:** A visual, metaphor-driven retrospective exercise where the team draws a nautical map representing their current state, desired future (the treasure), obstacles along the way, and other teams they interact with.
- **When to use:** As a retrospective format — especially useful when a team needs a shared vision of where they are heading, wants to surface impediments in a creative way, or would benefit from a multi-sprint focus artifact.
- **Group size:** Works for a typical Scrum team (implied 5–10 people)
- **Duration:** Fits within a standard retrospective timebox; the map can persist and be revisited across 2–4 sprints
- **How it works:**
  1. Place a large sheet of paper on the table or wall and hand out pens — let the team draw freely.
  2. The team draws their boat (representing themselves and their current state).
  3. They identify and draw the treasure — what does success look like? Use solutions-focused questions: *"How do we know we have arrived at the treasure?"*
  4. They add obstacles: storms (e.g., reorganizations, deprecated tech), sea monsters (e.g., technical debt), other boats (teams they depend on).
  5. For each obstacle, ask: *"How do we know when we have steered clear of this?"* rather than *"What do we do when we hit this?"*
  6. The team votes on which obstacle or stop to tackle first and lays out a course — typically one or two stops at a time.
  7. In the next retrospective, revisit the map: add new elements, remove stale ones, and move the course marker.
  8. After 2–4 iterations, the map grows stale — create a fresh one and continue.

### Country Map Variation (ab 01:10)
- **What:** An alternative version where the team draws their team as a country, with neighboring nations representing other teams. Relationships are framed diplomatically — good allies, neutral neighbors, or "at war."
- **When to use:** When inter-team dependencies and relationships are the primary focus of the retrospective.
- **Group size:** Standard team size
- **Duration:** Similar to the Sea Map
- **How it works:** Draw the team as a country, add neighboring nations (other teams), annotate the quality of relationships, and explore how to become a more influential or well-connected country.

## Key Principles
- Retrospectives are one of the most important Scrum ceremonies — the place to inspect the process, surface impediments, and co-create improvements.
- The solutions-focused coaching approach reframes questions from "what went wrong?" to "how do we know we've overcome this?" — shifting the team toward optimism and agency.
- Drawing together generates richer discussion than talking alone; the act of drawing something forces the team to make tacit ideas explicit and negotiate meaning.
- The map doesn't need to be pretty — the value is in the conversation, not the artifact.
- Predict only one or two steps ahead; the map gives direction without demanding a fixed long-term plan.
- The treasure definition should be concrete and observable: *"We know we've arrived when..."*

## Facilitation Tips
- Carsten deliberately does not draw anything himself — he hands out pens and lets the team go. This keeps ownership with the team.
- Use solutions-focused questions consistently: instead of "what can we do about this storm?", ask "what are the sure signs we steered clear of it?"
- Use dot voting or a quick team discussion to agree on which stop to tackle first — this gives the team a clear sprint focus.
- After defining a stop to focus on, generate post-its listing observable signs that the obstacle has been overcome. This becomes the team's working agreement for the sprint.
- Explicitly name when the map is getting stale (2–4 sprints) and frame making a new one as progress, not failure.

## Best Used When
- A team needs a shared, visual representation of where they are and where they want to go.
- The team is dealing with multiple ongoing impediments and needs help prioritizing.
- Engagement in retrospectives has been low — the drawing activity re-energizes participation.
- You want to maintain continuity across multiple retrospectives without starting from scratch each time.
- The team benefits from a more optimistic, forward-looking framing rather than a problem-focused one.

## Related Concepts
- **Solutions-Focused Coaching** — the questioning approach underpinning this exercise; ask about signs of success rather than symptoms of failure.
- **Miracle Question** — a solutions-focused technique closely related to defining the treasure ("how do we know we've arrived?").
- **Team Radar / Health Check** — another visual, multi-sprint retrospective artifact for tracking team state over time.
- **Technical Debt** — explicitly named in the example as a "sea monster" that grows stronger until tackled directly.
