# "Scrum Mom" by Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten introduces the "Scrum Mom" as a classic Scrum Master anti-pattern — well-intentioned over-involvement that erodes team accountability and ownership. Drawing on Barry Overeem's Scrum Master stances, he explains why helping too much can harm team development and offers two practical countermeasures.

## Methods & Techniques

### The "What if I'm not there?" Test (ab 04:30)
- **What:** A self-check for Scrum Masters to gauge whether the team has internalized process ownership or is dependent on the SM's facilitation.
- **When to use:** Regularly, especially when working with newer teams or when you sense you may be over-facilitating.
- **Group size:** Individual reflection (SM self-assessment)
- **Duration:** A few minutes of honest reflection
- **How it works:**
  1. Ask yourself: "If I don't show up tomorrow, do they still run stand-ups? Do they still hold a retrospective?"
  2. If the answer is "probably not," you may be acting as a Scrum Mom.
  3. Use the insight to delegate facilitation, co-create clearer process rationale, or gradually step back.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=WqYmk0enDVI&t=270

### Deliberate Fail-Fast Facilitation (ab 03:30)
- **What:** Consciously allowing the team to encounter and learn from failure rather than preemptively solving problems for them.
- **When to use:** When the SM senses the urge to swoop in and fix something the team could resolve themselves.
- **Group size:** Any team
- **Duration:** Ongoing stance, not a one-time exercise
- **How it works:**
  1. Resist the impulse to intervene before a problem materializes.
  2. Let the team attempt their own solution — even if you believe it won't work.
  3. When they fail, be present to help extract the learning (not to say "I told you so").
  4. Keep failures small and recoverable so trust in the SM is maintained.
  5. Stay open: Carsten notes that team solutions often turn out better than the SM's own ideas.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=WqYmk0enDVI&t=210

## Key Principles
- The Scrum Mom anti-pattern is defined by good intentions, not malice — making it hard to self-diagnose.
- Overstepping to help removes the team's experience of *why* a practice matters, not just the knowledge that it matters.
- A team that hasn't internalized ownership will revert to "zombie scrum" the moment the SM is absent.
- The SM's preferred solution is not automatically the best solution for the team's specific context.
- Accountability disappears when the team learns the SM will fix problems before they fully surface.
- The pattern is especially likely when onboarding new teams, where the SM feels pressure to demonstrate value quickly.

## Facilitation Tips
- Reference Barry Overeem's Scrum Master stances framework when coaching SMs on role identity — it provides useful vocabulary for this conversation.
- When working with new teams, be explicit with yourself about *why* you're intervening: is this a genuine blocker, or is it impatience?
- Delegate facilitation of ceremonies to team members as an early structural way to reduce SM dependency.
- Build shared understanding of the *why* behind process elements — teams with internalized rationale are more resilient when the SM steps back.

## Best Used When
- Coaching or mentoring Scrum Masters who tend toward over-facilitation
- Retrospectives focused on the SM's own role and effectiveness
- Team health checks where autonomy and accountability are in question
- Onboarding conversations with new Scrum Masters about role boundaries
