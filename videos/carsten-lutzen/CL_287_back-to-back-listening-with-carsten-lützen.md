# "Back to Back Listening" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten introduces "Back to Back Listening," an unofficial Liberating Structure where pairs stand back-to-back, lean against each other, and listen to a piece of music together. The exercise works as an energizer, a trust-building activity, and a powerful segue into conversations about empathy and differences in perception.

## Methods & Techniques

### Back to Back Listening (ab 00:20)
- **What:** Pairs stand back-to-back, leaning against each other while listening to a selected piece of music. They then share what they felt and noticed — first individually, then with awareness of their partner's perspective.
- **When to use:** As an energizer to get people moving; to build trust through physical contact; to prime a session on empathy; or to illustrate that people perceive the same situation in fundamentally different ways.
- **Group size:** Any group that can be split into pairs
- **Duration:** ~10–15 min (implied by structure)
- **How it works:**
  1. Participants pair up and stand back-to-back, leaning against each other.
  2. Play a piece of music.
  3. After the music ends, each person shares what they felt or noticed — either still back-to-back or after turning around.
  4. Partners switch roles: the second person now listens, keeping in mind what their partner just shared.
  5. Play the same piece of music again.
  6. Pairs debrief: How did it change things to listen through your partner's frame?
  7. Full group debrief: What came up? What does this tell us about perception?
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=J30jPgh5kdE&t=20s

## Key Principles
- The exercise operates on two levels simultaneously: the music itself and the physical experience of leaning against another person.
- Perception is selective — Carsten listens for drums because he plays drums; his partner may attend to entirely different elements of the same piece. This makes the exercise a natural metaphor for how people interpret the same event differently.
- The second listen (with the partner's frame in mind) is the key move: it invites participants to actively try on another person's perspective, not just hear about it.
- Emotional or "corny" music tends to work better — it triggers stronger feelings and richer reflection.
- The physical closeness (leaning) pushes comfort zones, but a well-facilitated debrief makes this worthwhile.

## Facilitation Tips
- Acknowledge upfront that standing back-to-back may feel unusual — normalize the discomfort before starting.
- Choose music with strong emotional texture; Carsten notes that overtly emotional ("corny") pieces tend to generate the richest responses.
- Use the debrief to explicitly surface the perception theme: "We all heard the same music — what did you each pay attention to?"
- The debrief is where the value is unlocked; don't skip or rush it.
- If using as a session opener on perspective-taking or empathy, the music metaphor carries directly into the content discussion.

## Best Used When
- Opening a session where perspective-taking, empathy, or differing viewpoints are central themes.
- The group needs an energizer that also does conceptual work (not just fun movement).
- Building psychological safety or trust within a team early in a workshop.
- Priming participants to hold multiple viewpoints before diving into complex topics.
