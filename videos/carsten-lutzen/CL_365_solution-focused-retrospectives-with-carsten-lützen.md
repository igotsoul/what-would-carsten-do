# "Solution Focused Retrospectives" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten introduces a solution-focused approach to retrospectives as an alternative to the classic "what worked / what didn't" format. He shares three concrete tips for shifting the team's attention away from problems and toward strengths, desired outcomes, and actionable improvements. The core idea: amplify what works, and when something isn't working, focus on what "working" would look like instead.

## Methods & Techniques

### Solution-Focused Retrospective — Tip 1: Amplify What Works (ab 00:40)
- **What:** Instead of cataloguing problems, identify what succeeded and ask how the team can do more of it.
- **When to use:** Any retrospective, especially with teams that tend to dwell on failures or have low morale.
- **Group size:** Any team size
- **Duration:** Fits within a standard retrospective timebox
- **How it works:**
  1. Ask: "What worked well this sprint?"
  2. Follow up: "How can we do even more of that? How can we amplify the benefits?"
  3. Turn successes into explicit actions to repeat or scale.
- **Why it matters:** According to Carsten, building on successes grows team self-confidence and equips the team to tackle harder challenges over time.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=qkVvoMyUZV4&t=40

### Solution-Focused Retrospective — Tip 2: Define What "Fixed" Looks Like (ab 01:30)
- **What:** When a problem exists, don't analyze the problem — instead define observable signs that the problem is gone.
- **When to use:** When the team keeps cycling on the same issues without finding traction.
- **Group size:** Any team size
- **Duration:** Fits within a standard retrospective timebox
- **How it works:**
  1. Identify a recurring issue (e.g., Merge Requests staying open too long).
  2. Ask: "What would we like to have happen instead?"
  3. Ask: "How would we *know* it's no longer a problem?" (e.g., a flatter control chart, no age indicators on the board)
  4. Work backwards from those observable signs to define concrete actions.
  5. Once progress appears, amplify it.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=qkVvoMyUZV4&t=90

### Solution-Focused Retrospective — Tip 3: Focus on Team Strengths and Internal Leverage (ab 02:45)
- **What:** Redirect teams — especially younger ones — away from blaming external dependencies toward identifying their own capabilities and influence.
- **When to use:** When teams frequently focus on what other teams, people, or systems are blocking them.
- **Group size:** Any team size
- **Duration:** Fits within a standard retrospective timebox
- **How it works:**
  1. Ask: "What resources, capabilities, or strengths does this team already have?"
  2. Ask: "Where can *we* move the needle — without depending on anyone outside this team?"
  3. Identify improvement areas within the team's control and build actions around those.
  4. For issues outside team control: define what a small improvement would look like, and involve the Scrum Master or Team Lead to resolve external blockers.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=qkVvoMyUZV4&t=165

## Key Principles
- The classic "what worked / what didn't" framing pulls teams into a problem-focused mindset — which is the opposite of what drives improvement.
- Solution-focused facilitation asks teams to define the *desired future state* rather than analyze the current failure.
- **Amplify what works** is the core loop: find a success → understand why it worked → do more of it → amplify the results.
- Teams that build on strengths develop self-confidence, which in turn makes them more resilient when facing genuinely difficult challenges.
- For external blockers, the Scrum Master's role is to step in — but the team should still define what "better" looks like before handing off.

## Facilitation Tips
- Replace "What didn't work?" with "What would you like to have happen instead?" — this single reframe shifts the whole conversation.
- Make the desired outcome *observable*: ask teams to describe concrete, visible signs that a problem is resolved (metrics, board states, behaviors), not just feelings.
- When a team fixates on external problems, redirect with: "Where is it that *we* can improve, without depending on others?"
- Once positive signs appear, don't move on — explicitly amplify: "How can we make this happen on an even broader scale?"

## Best Used When
- A team is stuck in a blame or complaint loop during retrospectives.
- Team morale is low and needs a confidence boost.
- The same problems keep surfacing sprint after sprint without resolution.
- Working with a younger or less experienced team that hasn't yet developed a habit of self-reflection.
- The facilitator wants to build team agency and psychological ownership.

## Related Concepts
- Solution-Focused Brief Therapy (SFBT) — the therapeutic tradition behind the "solution-focused" approach
- Appreciative Inquiry — a related strengths-based facilitation philosophy
- "What would you like to have happen?" — a core Clean Language / SFBT question
- Control chart / cumulative flow diagrams — referenced as observable outcome indicators
- Scrum retrospective format (classic "what worked / what didn't")
