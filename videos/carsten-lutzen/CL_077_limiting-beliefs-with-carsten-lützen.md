# "Limiting Beliefs" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten Lützen introduces the concept of limiting beliefs — false or restrictive thoughts that prevent individuals and teams from achieving their goals. He explains why these beliefs exist (often as self-preservation), and walks through a simple four-step process to identify, acknowledge, reframe, and act on them. The approach is applicable both in personal coaching contexts and in team retrospectives.

## Methods & Techniques

### Limiting Beliefs Reframing Process (ab 01:30)
- **What:** A four-step coaching technique to surface, understand, and reframe restrictive thoughts that hold individuals or teams back.
- **When to use:** In 1:1 coaching conversations, team retrospectives, or whenever someone (or a team) is stuck in "we can't do this" thinking.
- **Group size:** Individual or team
- **Duration:** Not specified; adaptable to conversation length
- **How it works:**
  1. **Identify** the limiting belief — notice where you or the team are holding back, feeling "not good enough" or "not smart enough"
  2. **Acknowledge** why the belief exists — what experiences created it? Carsten emphasizes these beliefs often serve a self-preservation function, so understanding their origin is important before challenging them
  3. **Change the perspective** — reframe the belief into a positive affirmation (e.g., "I'm not smart enough" → "I am capable of growing and learning")
  4. **Find evidence and take action** — look for signs of positive change that validate the new narrative, then define concrete actions (e.g., "I want to learn one skill for this new role — which skill would that be?")
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=2KDVzjN-89A&t=90s

## Key Principles
- Limiting beliefs are not inherently bad — they often function as self-protection mechanisms that prevent us from damaging our self-worth and self-image
- Because of this protective function, awareness (not criticism) is the right entry point — understanding why a belief exists makes it easier to work with
- Teams can hold collective limiting beliefs, not just individuals — watch for team-level patterns in retrospectives
- Positive affirmations are not denial; they create a new lens through which to notice real evidence of growth
- Changing the narrative is the goal — concrete actions reinforce the new story

## Facilitation Tips
- In retrospectives, Carsten suggests watching for issues that are actually team-level limiting beliefs in disguise — the team saying "we can't" may signal a belief worth surfacing
- When reframing, help the person or team find visible, concrete evidence of the positive affirmation being true before jumping to action — this builds believability
- Keep actions small and specific: "learn one person from the group" or "learn one skill" rather than broad commitments

## Best Used When
- A team or individual is repeatedly stuck on the same obstacles and reframes them as fixed limitations
- Retrospectives surface themes of helplessness, low confidence, or "that's just how it is here"
- Coaching conversations where someone attributes underperformance to identity ("I'm not a people person", "I'm not technical enough")
- Onboarding or role transitions where imposter syndrome is likely

## Related Concepts
- Growth mindset (Dweck) — the affirmation step maps directly to a growth mindset reframe
- Psychological safety — teams with low safety are more likely to surface collective limiting beliefs
- Positive affirmations as a coaching tool
- Comfort zone models — limiting beliefs are often what defines the edge of someone's perceived comfort zone
