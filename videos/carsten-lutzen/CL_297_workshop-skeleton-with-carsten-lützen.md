# "Workshop Skeleton" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten shares his go-to workshop structure — a reusable skeleton he applies to physical and online sessions alike. The video walks through each phase of the skeleton in sequence, explaining the reasoning behind the ordering. The key insight is that participant voices should come before facilitator framing, not after.

## Methods & Techniques

### Workshop Skeleton — Full Structure (ab 00:30)
- **What:** A sequenced prototype for planning workshops or longer meetings, designed to maximize participant ownership and psychological safety from the start.
- **When to use:** Any workshop or extended meeting, physical or online. Works as a planning template.
- **Group size:** Any
- **Duration:** Adaptable — the skeleton is format-agnostic
- **How it works:**
  1. **Visible agenda** — post it (A5 paper or digital equivalent) but do NOT walk through it at the start
  2. **Welcome** — brief, no stakeholder speech; just a warm welcome
  3. **Energizer** — silly, fun, quiz-based, or reflective (e.g., "What superpower are you bringing?")
  4. **Best Hopes** — before revealing the agenda, collect participant hopes for the session
  5. **Agenda walkthrough** — now present the agenda, tied to the best hopes collected
  6. **Session Rules** — establish ground rules, especially the Vegas Rule
  7. **Workshop program** — with breaks structured for context (online: 5–10 min every 45 min; in-room: movement and breakouts)
  8. **Revisit Best Hopes** — at the end, check what was covered and address gaps
  9. **Session Evaluation** — e.g., Perfection Game
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=LCglZJtAobE

### Best Hopes (ab 02:10)
- **What:** A short opening exercise where participants individually write what would make this session the best session ever, before hearing the agenda.
- **When to use:** At the start of any workshop, before the agenda is presented — to get unbiased participant input and set a shared direction.
- **Group size:** Any; review all responses in small groups, sample in large ones
- **Duration:** 5–10 minutes
- **How it works:** Ask "After this session, what would have made it the best session ever?" → participants write on post-its → post and review → revisit at the end of the session
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=LCglZJtAobE&t=130

### Vegas Rule (ab 03:20)
- **What:** A session ground rule: what happens in this session stays in this session, unless the group agrees otherwise.
- **When to use:** At the start of any session requiring openness, vulnerability, or honest feedback.
- **Group size:** Any
- **Duration:** < 2 minutes to introduce
- **How it works:** State the rule explicitly on a visible flip chart or slide. Do not assume people expect it — make it visible and named. Honor it strictly.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=LCglZJtAobE&t=200

### Perfection Game (ab 05:30)
- **What:** A simple closing evaluation format: What made this session work? What could be improved next time?
- **When to use:** End of any workshop or meeting as a lightweight retrospective on the session itself.
- **Group size:** Any
- **Duration:** 5–10 minutes
- **How it works:** Ask two questions — (1) What made this session valuable? (2) What could be improved for next time? Collect responses verbally or in writing.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=LCglZJtAobE&t=330

## Key Principles
- **Minimize priming before participant voice:** Don't walk through the agenda before collecting Best Hopes — it biases responses. Let participants speak first, then frame.
- **Sequence matters:** The skeleton is deliberately ordered so that participant ownership is established before facilitator structure is imposed.
- **Revisit what you opened with:** Closing with Best Hopes creates a feedback loop and ensures participants leave with a sense of value delivered.
- **Psychological safety is structural, not assumed:** The Vegas Rule should be stated explicitly and visibly — not left implicit.
- **Online vs. in-room fatigue is different:** Online sessions need scheduled breaks every 45 minutes; in-room sessions can manage energy through movement and breakouts instead.

## Facilitation Tips
- Post the agenda visibly from the start but don't reference it until after Best Hopes — it signals transparency without front-loading your framing.
- Keep the welcome short. No stakeholder speeches. A warm, brief welcome is enough.
- Choose energizers that match the group and context — silly, reflective, or quiz-based all work. The goal is activation, not a specific format.
- When presenting the agenda after Best Hopes, explicitly connect agenda items to what participants expressed — "I can see many of you asked for X, and that's covered here."
- At session close, if Best Hopes weren't fully addressed, name it and agree on next steps (follow-up materials, new session, etc.) rather than leaving it implicit.

## Best Used When
- Planning a workshop from scratch and needing a reliable sequence to build from
- Running sessions where psychological safety and participant buy-in matter
- Facilitating groups where you want to avoid the facilitator's framing dominating the room
- Onboarding new facilitators who need a starting template
