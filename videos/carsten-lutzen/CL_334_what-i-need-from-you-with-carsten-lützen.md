# "What I Need From You" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten introduces the Liberating Structure "What I Need From You" (WINFY), a structured format for surfacing inter-team dependencies in a psychologically safe way. The video walks through a full facilitation sequence — from individual reflection to cross-team declarations and structured responses — making it practical for Scrum Masters and agile coaches working with multiple product teams.

## Methods & Techniques

### What I Need From You / WINFY (ab 00:30)
- **What:** A Liberating Structure that enables teams to explicitly declare what they need from other teams, and receive one of four structured responses — creating transparency around dependencies without spiraling into discussion or blame.
- **When to use:** When multiple product teams are working toward a shared goal and dependencies or unspoken needs are getting in the way. Especially useful as a first step before building a dependency map.
- **Group size:** Multiple teams (3+ recommended); each team sends one representative to a central "stage" moment.
- **Duration:** ~25 minutes for the core sequence.
- **How it works:**
  1. **Frame the shared goal** — Define a concrete product goal all teams are working toward (e.g., target retention rate, number of downloads).
  2. **Individual reflection (1-2-4-All, ~10 min)** — Each person writes silently: "What do I need from others outside my product team to be successful?" Teams then consolidate into 1–2 items per other team, phrased as "We/I need from you [team] [specific thing]."
  3. **Stage round — needs declared (no comments)** — One representative per team stands in the center. Each team reads their needs to all other teams in sequence. No comments, no questions, not even clarifying questions during this round.
  4. **Teams respond internally** — Each team reviews the incoming needs and assigns one of four responses:
     - **Yes** — We can commit to that.
     - **No** — We cannot meet that need.
     - **I will try** — We'll investigate / timebox a spike, but can't promise an outcome.
     - **Whatever** — *Not* dismissal — means "I need clarification before I can give you one of the other three answers." Carsten stresses facilitators must explicitly explain this distinction.
  5. **Stage round — responses shared (no comments)** — Representatives return to center and deliver responses: "Your need #1 — yes. Your need #2 — I will try." All in the open, no discussion.
  6. **Debrief with W² (What? So What? Now What?)** — Teams reflect on what they learned and decide what to do next.
  7. **Handle "whatevers"** — If many whatevers remain, run a second round for clarification, or collect them on a list and follow up.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=s0x094D9Mqw

### W² / What-So What-Now What Debrief (ab 06:30)
- **What:** A structured debrief format Carsten references for closing the WINFY session. Teams reflect on what they learned and what actions to take.
- **When to use:** After the WINFY response round to convert insights into commitments.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=s0x094D9Mqw&t=390

## Key Principles
- **Baby steps over big dependency maps** — Carsten recommends WINFY as a starting point before building full dependency maps, especially for teams new to this kind of cross-team work.
- **Silence and structure create safety** — The no-comments rule during declarations reduces defensiveness and allows needs to surface that might otherwise stay hidden.
- **"Whatever" ≠ dismissal** — The fourth response option must be reframed by the facilitator as "I need more information." Without this, it reads as indifference and breaks trust.
- **Use product teams, not functional areas** — Carsten adapts the official WINFY description to use cross-functional product teams as the unit, not technical functional areas (e.g., "database team"). Teams should reflect how people actually sit and work day-to-day.
- **No clarifying questions in declaration rounds** — Even clarifying questions derail the structured flow. Save all clarification for the "whatever" follow-up.

## Facilitation Tips
- Explicitly explain the four response options — especially "whatever" — before the response round begins. The framing of "whatever = I need clarification" must be made crystal clear.
- Keep the stage rounds tight: no comments, no questions. Enforce this actively as the facilitator.
- If time is short and whatevers remain unresolved, capture them on a list and schedule follow-up — don't skip the debrief.
- 1-2-4-All is embedded in the opening phase — use individual silence before pair/team discussion to avoid groupthink in the needs-gathering step.

## Best Used When
- Multiple product teams share a common goal and inter-team dependencies are unclear or unspoken.
- Teams have not yet tried any structured dependency mapping and need a safe entry point.
- A Scrum of Scrums or PI Planning-style alignment is needed but the group isn't ready for heavyweight formats.
- There's a sense that teams are blocked by each other but no one is naming it explicitly.
