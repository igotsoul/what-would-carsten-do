# "Segmentation" by Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten introduces a simple but versatile facilitation technique — placing people on a physical or virtual scale based on a question — and then using that positioning intentionally to form pairs or groups for richer conversation. The technique works both in-person and remotely, and is inspired by a colleague (Fleming) as well as a training course Carsten attended years ago. The key insight is that the line-up is not just an exercise in itself, but a springboard for purposeful grouping and dialogue.

## Methods & Techniques

### Segmentation / Standing on a Line (ab 00:20)
- **What:** Participants position themselves on a physical or virtual scale in response to a question (e.g., "How many years have you been at the company?" or "How much trust do you have in the product we're building?"). Their position becomes the basis for structured conversation or intentional pairing.
- **When to use:** As a warm-up or check-in at the start of a workshop or meeting; when you want to surface diversity of experience, perspective, or opinion; when you need a low-friction way to form groups with a meaningful rationale.
- **Group size:** Any size; works for both small teams and larger groups
- **Duration:** Flexible — the line-up itself takes only a few minutes; follow-up conversations can be extended as needed
- **How it works:**
  1. Create a scale — on the floor with tape in person, or on a virtual whiteboard remotely
  2. Ask a question with a clear spectrum (e.g., years of experience, level of confidence in the product, number of fish owned)
  3. Have participants position themselves on the scale
  4. Use their positions intentionally: form three groups (high / mid / low), pair the extremes (most vs. least experienced), or create mixed groups to find commonalities
  5. Give groups or pairs a follow-up question to discuss: "Why are we standing here?" or "What makes you confident in your position?"
  6. Consider using a 1-2-all structure: individual reflection → pair/small group → full group

## Key Principles
- The line-up is a means, not an end — Carsten emphasizes using it *for something*: intentional pairing, surfacing commonalities, or bridging experience gaps
- Psychological safety matters: questions touching on trust or confidence require a safe environment for honest answers
- Start playful, go deeper — silly questions (e.g., "How many fish do you own?") get people talking first, making them more willing to engage with more meaningful questions afterwards
- Think of it like Liberating Structures: frame participation as an invitation, not a directive
- Group formation doesn't have to be random counting (1-2-3-4) — the positioning itself can inform smarter, more intentional groupings

## Facilitation Tips
- Use the positions to form groups with purpose: senior ↔ junior pairs, or clusters by similarity to find shared patterns
- Be playful with your questions — lighter questions lower the threshold and warm people up for deeper ones
- In virtual settings, replicate the scale on a digital whiteboard (e.g., Miro, MURAL)
- Sequence conversations using 1-2-all or 1-2-all variants depending on whether you pair extremes or group by proximity
- Don't prepare the groupings upfront — the in-the-moment grouping based on where people stand is part of what makes it feel organic and relevant

## Best Used When
- You want a quick, visual way to surface diversity of experience or perspective in a group
- You need to form breakout groups with a meaningful rationale rather than random assignment
- You're opening a workshop and want people talking before diving into heavier content
- You want to create senior-junior dialogue or cross-pollinate perspectives across experience levels

## Related Concepts
- **Liberating Structures** — Carsten explicitly draws a parallel to the invitation-based participation philosophy of LS
- **1-2-4-All** — the follow-up conversation structure mirrors this LS format
- **Spectrogram** — a closely related facilitation technique (positioning on a line based on agreement/experience) used in many workshop contexts
