# "Waltz" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten Lützen demonstrates how to teach basic waltz steps as an energizer for workshops and sessions. The technique requires no dance experience and takes just 5–10 minutes, giving participants a way to get movement into their bodies during longer gatherings.

## Methods & Techniques

### Waltz Energizer (ab 00:20)
- **What:** A simple 6-step waltz pattern taught to a group as a physical energizer during workshops. No dance experience required — only the basic box-step pattern is covered.
- **When to use:** When participants need a movement break, as an opener to shift energy, or to loosen up a group before or between sessions.
- **Group size:** Any size; works in a room where everyone can face the same direction.
- **Duration:** 5–10 minutes.
- **How it works:**
  1. Have everyone face the same direction — ideally facing the facilitator, who then turns their back to the group and mirrors movements.
  2. Avoid saying "left" and "right" — instead use fixed room landmarks (e.g., "the foot closest to the window," "the foot closest to the parking lot") to prevent confusion from mirroring.
  3. Count loudly and slowly throughout.
  4. First half (counts 1–3): Start with feet together → step the foot closest to the door forward (1) → move the other foot across (2) → close feet together (3).
  5. Second half (counts 4–6): Start with the foot closest to the parking lot → step (4) → bring the other foot across (5) → close (6).
  6. The full pattern traces a square on the floor. Repeat continuously.
  7. Optional advanced variation: pair people up facing each other, with each partner starting on the opposite foot.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=gkwKrDxg2wA&t=30

## Key Principles
- Physical movement energizers don't need to be complex — even a basic pattern creates value by getting people out of their heads and into their bodies.
- Removing left/right orientation cues and replacing them with room landmarks drastically reduces participant confusion, especially when the facilitator is mirroring the group.
- Counting loudly and slowly is as important as the steps themselves — it holds the group together and keeps the pace accessible.
- Dancing together (even badly) creates a light, playful atmosphere that can shift group dynamics.

## Facilitation Tips
- Turn your back to the group so your movements mirror theirs directly — but shout louder to compensate for the turned position.
- Use the room's physical features (doors, windows, whiteboards, parking lot view) as directional anchors. Identify these before starting.
- Don't worry about perfection — Carsten explicitly models that being a non-expert dancer is fine and even reassuring for participants.
- For the paired version, have one group start with one foot and the other group start with the opposite foot, keyed to the same room landmark, so orientation differences become irrelevant.

## Best Used When
- A group needs a physical break during a long workshop or session.
- You want to inject playfulness or laughter into a group without requiring preparation or materials.
- Participants are sitting for extended periods and energy is dropping.
- You want a simple, inclusive activity where skill level is irrelevant.
