# "Caravan" by Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten introduces "Caravan," a liberating structure in development that combines Wise Crowds and Shift & Share into a rotating peer-consultation format. Participants rotate between stations where clients share their challenges to successive groups of consultants, gaining both diverse input and the opportunity to refine how they communicate their problem over multiple iterations.

## Methods & Techniques

### Caravan (ab 00:30)
- **What:** A hybrid liberating structure combining Wise Crowds and Shift & Share, where multiple "clients" host stations and rotating groups of "consultants" move round-robin between them, each spending ~8 minutes consulting on the client's challenge.
- **When to use:** When multiple people each have a challenge they want input on, and you want to generate diverse perspectives without heavy preparation or lengthy plenary sharing.
- **Group size:** Works with enough participants to form several consultant groups; Carsten uses six groups as an example.
- **Duration:** ~8 minutes per station plus buffer time, multiplied by the number of stations (e.g., 6 stations ≈ ~50–60 minutes total).
- **How it works:**
  1. Set up one station per challenge/client — either prepared in advance or as part of a larger workshop string.
  2. Remaining participants become "consultants" divided into groups, one group per station to start.
  3. At each station, the client shares their challenge (~brief presentation).
  4. Consultants ask clarifying questions only — no advice yet.
  5. The client turns their back; consultants discuss observations, ideas, and suggestions among themselves (mirroring the Wise Crowds fishbowl mechanic).
  6. Client turns back and briefly shares what they heard and what they're taking away.
  7. Consultant groups rotate to the next station; repeat until all groups have visited all stations.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=j8yvcfM096Q&t=30

## Key Principles
- Caravan is explicitly positioned as a blend of two existing liberating structures (Wise Crowds + Shift & Share), not a fully standalone structure — Carsten is transparent about this hybrid nature.
- Repeated telling of the same challenge is itself a learning mechanism: by the sixth iteration, clients have usually refined their narrative, discovered where they were over- or under-emphasizing, and can articulate the problem more precisely.
- The format separates listening (client faces away) from receiving, which reduces defensiveness and encourages consultants to speak candidly.
- High input density with low preparation cost — clients receive ideas from every consultant group without anyone needing to prepare a presentation or facilitation sequence.

## Facilitation Tips
- Encourage clients to notice what changes in their own storytelling between rounds — the evolution of the narrative is part of the value, not just the consultant feedback.
- Keep the clarifying-questions-only rule strict before the client turns away; this prevents premature advice and keeps the fishbowl discussion richer.
- Build in buffer time between rotations — 8 minutes is the station time, but transitions add up across many stations.
- Works well embedded in a larger liberating structures string or workshop, where clients and challenges are already defined from upstream activities.

## Best Used When
- Multiple team members or stakeholders each have a distinct challenge or problem they want peer input on.
- You want to maximize the number of perspectives each person receives without running sequential full-group discussions.
- The goal is both problem-solving *and* communication skill development — helping people learn to frame and articulate their challenges clearly.
- Time or energy is limited and you need an efficient, structured format that doesn't exhaust participants.
