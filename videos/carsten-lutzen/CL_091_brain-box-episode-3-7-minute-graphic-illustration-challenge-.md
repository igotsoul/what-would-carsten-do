# Brain Box Episode 3: 7-Minute Graphic Illustration Challenge ft. Paddy Dhanda

## Summary
Carsten and guests Paddy Dhanda and Francesco take part in a live 7-minute visual illustration challenge, each drawing the same randomly selected concept (public transport) independently. The episode demonstrates how the same prompt yields different visual interpretations and serves as a model for using timed graphic challenges as creative warm-up or visual-thinking exercises in group settings.

## Methods & Techniques

### Brain Box — Timed Graphic Illustration Challenge (ab 00:20)
- **What:** A structured creative exercise where participants each illustrate a shared concept in exactly 7 minutes using only visuals, then compare results. A spinner randomly selects the prompt, removing facilitator bias.
- **When to use:** As a visual-thinking warm-up, to build sketchnoting confidence, or to explore how different people conceptualise the same idea. Works well at the start of workshops or as a recurring team ritual.
- **Group size:** 2–5 participants (demonstrated with 3); can be scaled for larger groups with gallery-style sharing.
- **Duration:** 7 minutes drawing + debrief time (full episode runs ~9 minutes).
- **How it works:**
  1. Use a random spinner (physical or digital) to select the concept prompt.
  2. All participants draw simultaneously — no peeking.
  3. Each person produces one visual representing the overall concept.
  4. Reveal and compare illustrations together.
  5. Invite reflection: "What did you notice? What surprised you?"
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=BQhHCY9u9hI&t=20

### Iterative On-Canvas Storytelling (ab 04:30)
- **What:** Rather than drawing a static snapshot, Paddy progressively adds narrative layers to a single illustration — showing time passing (a person ageing at the bus stop), cause and effect (a splodge blocking the bus), and emotional arc — turning the image into a mini-story.
- **When to use:** When coaching teams to make visual metaphors richer and more memorable, or when demonstrating that sketchnotes can convey sequence and emotion, not just labels.
- **How it works:** Start with the literal (a bus, a train), then add a character, then add a conflict or tension, then show resolution or consequence — all within the same frame.

## Key Principles
- A random prompt selector (spinner) democratises the exercise — no one can prepare, which levels the playing field and reduces perfectionism.
- Carsten frames the challenge with "what's the worst thing that can happen?" — normalising imperfection and lowering psychological barriers to visual expression.
- The same prompt yields genuinely different outputs; this divergence is the point, not a problem. It makes the debrief rich.
- Greyscale or minimal colour can be a deliberate aesthetic choice, not a limitation — Paddy works intentionally in black/grey.
- Time pressure (7 minutes) forces prioritisation and inhibits over-thinking, making it a useful constraint rather than a stressor.

## Facilitation Tips
- Invite the audience or team to suggest future prompts (comments/sticky notes) — this builds ownership and ensures prompts are contextually relevant.
- Credit prompt contributors by name during the session to reward participation and model appreciation.
- Run the exercise in parallel (everyone draws at the same time) rather than sequentially to maintain energy and prevent anchoring on others' ideas.
- When time runs short, encourage participants to keep going and show whatever partial work exists — partial sketches are still valid data for debrief.

## Best Used When
- Opening a workshop and you want to warm up visual and creative thinking quickly.
- Building psychological safety around "not being an artist" — the timed format and peer participation normalise imperfect drawing.
- Exploring how a team conceptualises a shared concept (e.g. "draw what our product does") as a diagnostic tool.
- Running recurring team rituals that build sketchnoting vocabulary over time.
