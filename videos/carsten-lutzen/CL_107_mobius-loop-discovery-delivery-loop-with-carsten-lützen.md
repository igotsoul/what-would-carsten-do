# "Mobius Loop / Discovery-Delivery Loop" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten Lützen introduces the Mobius Loop (also known as the Discovery-Delivery Loop), a visual model for understanding how product discovery and delivery work together as a continuous feedback cycle. The video explains each phase of the loop and makes the case for keeping discovery and delivery within the same team rather than splitting responsibilities. It serves as a practical mental model to help teams articulate where they are in the product development process at any given time.

## Methods & Techniques

### Mobius Loop / Discovery-Delivery Loop (ab 00:30)
- **What:** A figure-eight shaped model that visualizes the continuous cycle between product discovery (understanding problems and prototyping solutions) and product delivery (building, measuring, and validating). Often drawn as a heart shape, sometimes called the "pivotal area."
- **When to use:** When a team needs a shared language to describe whether they are currently in problem exploration or solution building. Useful for structuring product conversations, sprint planning, and team alignment.
- **Group size:** Any team size; Carsten notes it may apply even when part of the team is in discovery while others are in delivery.
- **Duration:** Model is ongoing — teams move through it continuously across multiple cycles.
- **How it works:**
  1. **Understand** — Identify the underlying user or business need, not the solution. Focus on the "why," not the "what."
  2. **Define** — Clarify what success looks like and what can be measured. Describe the ideal outcome without constraints.
  3. **Prototype** — Build a lightweight prototype (paper, wizard-of-oz, etc.) to test the proposed solution direction.
  4. **Evaluate feedback** — If the prototype fails, loop back to Understand. If it validates, move into delivery.
  5. **Build** — Develop the actual solution in the delivery phase.
  6. **Validate** — Check whether the built solution still meets the original intent.
  7. **Measure impact** — Assess the effect on users.
  8. **Feed back into Discovery** — Use what was learned to identify new needs and restart the loop.

## Key Principles
- The loop is fundamentally about feedback — the figure-eight shape makes the feedback mechanism visible and central.
- The underlying need (e.g., "I need to get from A to B") must be separated from the proposed solution (e.g., "a car"). Carsten references the classic Nika illustration of the skateboard-to-car progression as an example.
- Discovery and delivery should be handled by the same team. Splitting them across two teams causes knowledge loss at the handover point.
- Multiple iterations within the discovery half of the loop are normal — not all prototypes are good solutions.
- Having a named model gives teams language to articulate their current state: "We're in the build phase — what does that mean for us right now?"
- It can be healthy to balance discovery and delivery within the team, allowing some members to experience the satisfaction of delivery while others explore new problems.

## Facilitation Tips
- Use this model as a conversation starter with product teams about how they are currently doing discovery and whether it is deliberate or ad hoc.
- When teams are stuck or frustrated in discovery, Carsten suggests "dosing" the exploration — rotating some members into the build phase so the team experiences forward momentum.
- Presenting multiple models (this alongside Dual Track Agile, for example) gives teams richer vocabulary and helps different people connect with different framings of the same idea.

## Best Used When
- A team is unclear about whether they are solving the right problem before building.
- There is a risk of premature delivery — jumping to solutions before validating the underlying need.
- Teams have siloed discovery and delivery and are losing context at handovers.
- Coaching conversations need a visual anchor to discuss product development rhythm.
