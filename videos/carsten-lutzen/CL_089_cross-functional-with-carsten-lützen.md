# "Cross Functional" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten Lützen clarifies a common misconception about the term "cross functional team." He distinguishes between the actual definition — a team with different functional expertises that shares one goal — and the often-assumed requirement that team members must have T-shaped profiles. T-shaped profiles are a nice-to-have, not a requirement.

## Methods & Techniques

### Defining Cross Functional Teams (ab 00:30)
- **What:** A cross functional team is a group with different functional expertises that shares a single goal and can take work from idea to production.
- **When to use:** Use this definition when structuring teams or evaluating whether a team qualifies as cross functional.
- **How it works:**
  1. Each team member brings a distinct functional skill (skill A, B, C, D, E).
  2. Together, the team covers all capabilities needed to deliver end-to-end.
  3. The team shares one goal — this is what distinguishes it from a working group.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zsyPZwqz1qQ&t=30

### Cross Functional vs. Working Group (ab 00:50)
- **What:** A working group is people working in parallel without a shared goal. A cross functional team has a shared goal.
- **When to use:** Use this distinction when explaining team structures to stakeholders or when diagnosing collaboration problems.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zsyPZwqz1qQ&t=50

## Key Principles
- Cross functional does **not** require T-shaped profiles — that is a common misconception. A team where each person holds one deep skill is still cross functional as long as they share a goal and collectively cover the full delivery chain.
- T-shaped profiles (one deep skill + breadth across other areas) are a "very nice to have" in cross functional teams, but not a definitional requirement.
- The shared goal is the critical differentiator between a cross functional team and a working group.
- Carsten openly shares that he had been redefining the term incorrectly himself — a useful reminder that even experienced practitioners carry imprecise mental models worth revisiting.

## Facilitation Tips
- When introducing cross functional team concepts, explicitly separate the definition (shared goal + diverse expertise) from the ideal (T-shaped profiles) to avoid conflating the two.
- Use this distinction as a prompt for team reflection: "Are we a working group or a cross functional team — do we actually share one goal?"

## Best Used When
- Designing or restructuring agile teams.
- Coaching teams or leaders who conflate "cross functional" with "everyone must be a generalist."
- Running discussions about team topology, skill coverage, or hiring strategy.
