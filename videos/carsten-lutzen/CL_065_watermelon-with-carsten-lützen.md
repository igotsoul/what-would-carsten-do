# "Watermelon" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten Lützen explores the watermelon as a powerful metaphor in agile and project contexts. He covers two distinct applications: watermelon reporting (where projects appear green on the outside but are red on the inside) and a structured watermelon retrospective format. The video offers a ready-to-use retrospective technique with four prompts for examining a team's work from multiple perspectives.

## Methods & Techniques

### Watermelon Reporting (ab 00:30)
- **What:** A metaphor for status reporting where the outward-facing status appears green/healthy, but the internal reality is yellow or red — problems are hidden from stakeholders.
- **When to use:** When coaching teams or organizations on transparency, honest reporting, or surfacing hidden project risks. Useful as a conversation starter with leadership about reporting culture.
- **Group size:** Any
- **Duration:** N/A — this is a metaphor/concept, not a structured exercise
- **How it works:** Use the watermelon image to name the pattern: the outside is green, the inside is red. Invite reflection on whether current reporting practices reflect internal reality.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=DF-33SdqLSU&t=30

### Watermelon Retrospective (ab 01:15)
- **What:** A structured retrospective format from retr-o-mat (exercise #142) that uses the watermelon metaphor to examine a project from four distinct angles.
- **When to use:** Retrospectives where a team wants to explore the gap between internal experience and external perception, and identify actionable improvements.
- **Group size:** Any team size; works well with standard retrospective formats (e.g., 1-2-4-All for brainstorming)
- **Duration:** Fits a standard retrospective slot; no specific duration mentioned
- **How it works:**
  1. **Inside view** — How does the project look internally? What does the team see?
  2. **Outside view** — How does the project appear to the outside? What do stakeholders perceive?
  3. **Bothersome seeds** — Which small irritants (seeds) would the team like to get rid of? Focus on what is within the circle of influence.
  4. **Potential seeds** — Which seeds, if nurtured correctly, could grow into something valuable in the future?
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=DF-33SdqLSU&t=75

## Key Principles
- Effective metaphors make abstract problems immediately tangible — the watermelon works because almost everyone has a shared mental image of its inside/outside contrast.
- The distinction between **inside and outside perception** is a powerful lens for teams that may be shielding stakeholders from problems.
- Using the **circle of influence** as a filter (the middle layer of the watermelon) helps teams focus retrospective energy on what they can actually change.
- Seeds serve a dual purpose: bothersome seeds to remove, and potential seeds to cultivate — this reframes problems as both obstacles and opportunities.

## Facilitation Tips
- According to Carsten, the four prompts work well when combined with a diverge-converge technique like 1-2-4-All to generate ideas before discussing as a group.
- The metaphor is especially useful when introducing the concept of watermelon reporting to stakeholders — it names a recognizable pattern without assigning blame.
- Carsten recommends using this retrospective format specifically when there is a felt or suspected gap between what the team experiences and what is communicated externally.

## Best Used When
- A team suspects their project reporting is not reflecting reality
- There is a need to surface hidden risks or team frustrations in a psychologically safe way
- A retrospective format is needed that goes beyond the team's internal dynamics to include external perception
- Coaching leaders or stakeholders on the risks of filtered status updates
