# "Folding Spectogram" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten Lützen introduces the Folding Spectrogram, a liberating structure variation designed as a workshop energizer or opener. By having participants physically position themselves along a line based on experience or affinity, then folding the line once or twice, the technique creates intentionally mixed pairs or small groups that blend high and low experience levels — sparking peer learning and surfacing group expectations before the main session begins.

## Methods & Techniques

### Folding Spectrogram (ab 00:15)
- **What:** A physical activity where participants stand on a line representing a spectrum of experience or affinity with a topic, then the line is "folded" to pair or group people across the experience spectrum.
- **When to use:** As a workshop energizer or opener when you want to activate participants, create mixed-experience groups, and surface expectations or knowledge gaps before diving into content.
- **Group size:** Any group that can stand in a line; works well for small to medium workshop groups.
- **Duration:** Short — can serve as a standalone energizer or lead directly into Impromptu Networking.
- **How it works:**
  1. **Invitation / prompt** — Give participants a question tied to the session topic (e.g., "How familiar are you with working with OKRs?"). Avoid trivial distance-type questions if you want meaningful pairings.
  2. **Line up** — Participants position themselves along a physical line or scale: one end represents high experience/affinity, the other end represents little to none.
  3. **First fold** — Ask the group to fold the line at its midpoint. The person with the most experience now faces the person with the least, and so on down the line, creating matched pairs across the spectrum.
  4. **Pair conversation** — Pairs talk briefly: share their experience, what they hope to get from the session, or relevant questions. This is a natural entry point into Impromptu Networking.
  5. **Second fold (optional)** — Fold the line again to form groups of four, each containing a deliberate mix of seniority or affinity levels. Groups can then discuss the topic together.

## Key Principles
- Mixing experience levels intentionally creates richer group dynamics — participants realize they are at different places and have different needs, which surfaces expectations naturally.
- Peer learning emerges organically: in a well-mixed group, participants can answer most of each other's questions without facilitator intervention.
- The physical movement of lining up and folding is itself an energizer — it gets people out of their seats and engaged before content begins.
- The spectrogram prompt should be topic-relevant and concrete (e.g., experience with a specific framework) rather than trivial, to generate meaningful pairings and conversations.
- The first fold naturally sets up pairs for Impromptu Networking, making it easy to chain activities.

## Facilitation Tips
- Choose your invitation prompt carefully — concrete, session-relevant questions (e.g., OKR experience) produce more useful groupings than generic ones.
- After the first fold, you can stop and transition directly into Impromptu Networking using the established pairs, saving time while still getting the mixing benefit.
- The second fold (groups of four) is optional but valuable when you want small group discussion with built-in diversity of perspective.
- As facilitator, observe the groupings — the mixed groups will reveal pain points and knowledge gaps that help you calibrate the session in real time.

## Best Used When
- Opening a workshop where participants have varying levels of experience with the topic.
- You need a quick energizer that also does useful grouping work.
- You want to activate peer learning and set the expectation that the group itself holds much of the knowledge.
- You plan to follow up with Impromptu Networking or a small group discussion activity.
