# Agile Principles with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten presents a retrospective exercise that helps teams reconnect with the 12 Agile Principles from the Agile Manifesto. Teams rank the principles on a relative scale to surface which ones deserve more attention, then identify concrete actions for the next sprint or iteration.

## Methods & Techniques

### Agile Principles Ranking Exercise (ab 00:25)
- **What:** Teams draw and rank all 12 Agile Principles on a relative axis (more focus ↔ less focus), then commit to actions for the top 2–3 that need the most attention.
- **When to use:** In retrospectives when a team has drifted away from Agile values or needs a structured way to reflect on what "being Agile" actually means in practice.
- **Group size:** Small to medium team (typical Scrum team size)
- **Duration:** Fits within a standard retrospective timebox (~30–45 min implied)
- **How it works:**
  1. Prepare a flipchart with a vertical axis labeled "needs more focus" at the top and "needs less focus" at the bottom.
  2. Print the 12 Agile Principles on individual slips of paper and shuffle them.
  3. The first team member draws a card, reads the principle aloud, and places it in the middle — no judgment yet since it's the reference point.
  4. Each subsequent team member draws a card, reads it aloud, and the team has a short discussion before placing it above or below existing cards.
  5. Move cards around as needed to make room as the axis fills up.
  6. Once all 12 are placed, identify the top 2–3 that need the most focus.
  7. Dive into those: What concrete actions can the team take in the next sprint or iteration to strengthen these principles?
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=ia9O6de_tbU&t=25s

## Key Principles
- The exercise uses relative ranking, not absolute scoring — each principle is only placed in relation to the others, which makes discussion easier and avoids analysis paralysis.
- The goal is not to say "we are Agile" but to reflect on what the Agile values and principles actually require in day-to-day work.
- Carsten emphasizes closing the loop with concrete actions, not just awareness.

## Facilitation Tips
- Shuffle the cards before drawing so the sequence is random — avoids bias toward "famous" principles like working software.
- The first card always goes in the middle by design; set that expectation upfront so teams don't overthink it.
- Keep individual discussions short before placing each card; the goal is relative prioritization, not a deep dive on every principle.
- After placement, focus the action conversation on the top 2–3 only — going deeper on fewer items beats shallow discussion of many.

## Best Used When
- A team is going through the motions of Scrum without reflecting on the underlying values.
- Retrospectives feel stale and need a content-focused format rather than a process-focused one.
- A team is newly formed and needs a low-stakes way to discuss what Agile actually means to them.
