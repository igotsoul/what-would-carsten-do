# 10 Years Ago with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten shares a two-part icebreaker introduced by Scrum Master Marlene Topholdt Andersen. The exercise uses a time-travel framing to help team members — even long-standing ones — learn something new about each other by introducing themselves as they were 10 years in the past, and optionally imagining themselves 10 years into the future.

## Methods & Techniques

### "10 Years Ago" Icebreaker (ab 00:20)
- **What:** Participants pair up and introduce themselves not as they are today, but as they were 10 years ago — using a fictional "time travel" framing to make it playful.
- **When to use:** Retrospective opening or any session where the team knows each other well and standard introductions would feel stale.
- **Group size:** Any; works in pairs
- **Duration:** ~5 minutes
- **How it works:**
  1. Tell participants: "As you entered the room, something funny happened — we traveled 10 years back in time. You don't know each other yet."
  2. Pairs introduce themselves as their past selves (job, life situation, what they were doing).
  3. Optionally extend: "Now we just jumped forward — it's 10 years in the future. With no limitations, what does your life look like?"
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=iv-J3mMlCkM&t=20

### Future-Self Extension (ab 01:30)
- **What:** After the past-self introduction, participants imagine and share their life 10 years from now — with no constraints.
- **When to use:** When you want to add a forward-looking, aspirational dimension to the icebreaker.
- **Group size:** Any; same pairs as the first part
- **Duration:** ~3–5 additional minutes
- **How it works:** Ask pairs: "It's now [current year + 10]. What are you doing? Where are you living? With no limitations, what would your world look like?"
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=iv-J3mMlCkM&t=90

## Key Principles
- Familiar teams benefit from icebreakers that surface *new* information — standard "who are you" prompts yield nothing when people already know each other.
- A fictional framing (time travel) lowers the barrier to sharing personal stories, making it fun rather than forced.
- Combining past and future perspectives creates both warmth (shared memories, humor) and inspiration (aspirations, possibilities).

## Facilitation Tips
- Commit to the framing: announce it as if something actually happened when they entered the room — this sells the playfulness.
- The past prompt tends to generate more laughter and surprise; lead with it before the future prompt.
- No prep or materials needed; works as a drop-in opener.

## Best Used When
- A team has been together long enough that standard introductions feel pointless.
- You want a low-effort, high-energy retro opener that creates genuine connection.
- You need something quick (< 10 min) that still surfaces real personal content.
