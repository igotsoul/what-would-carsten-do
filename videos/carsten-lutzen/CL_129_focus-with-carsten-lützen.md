# "FOCUS" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten introduces the FOCUS mnemonic — originally coined by Martin in a book on "Scrum Sprinkles" — as a practical framework for crafting better Sprint Goals. Each letter guides teams toward goals that are memorable, outcome-driven, co-created, meaningful, and unified. The video is a short, opinionated walkthrough of what each letter means in practice, with concrete examples from Carsten's own coaching experience.

## Methods & Techniques

### The FOCUS Mnemonic for Sprint Goals (ab 00:30)
- **What:** A five-part checklist for evaluating and crafting Sprint Goals, where each letter stands for a quality the goal should have: Fun, Outcome-oriented, Collaborative, Ultimate, Singular.
- **When to use:** During Sprint Planning when defining or reviewing the Sprint Goal with the team. Also useful as a coaching tool to assess the quality of existing goals.
- **Group size:** Whole Scrum team (product owner + development team)
- **Duration:** Part of Sprint Planning; no fixed duration for applying the mnemonic itself
- **How it works:**
  1. **F — Fun:** Give the Sprint a memorable name or theme. Carsten recommends adjective + animal sequences through the alphabet (e.g., "Boozy Bear", "Amazing Antelope") or movie titles (e.g., working toward "Mission Impossible"). Sequential naming also creates a natural sprint timeline — you know Armageddon came before Frozen.
  2. **O — Outcome-oriented:** The Sprint Goal must express *what outcomes* the team is after, not *what tasks* will be done. The backlog serves the goal, not the other way around.
  3. **C — Collaborative:** The goal must be negotiated and co-created between the product owner and the team. Everyone on the Scrum team needs to be able to stand behind it.
  4. **U — Ultimate:** Articulate *why* this goal matters — to the team, to the company. Carsten frames it provocatively: "Why is this the single most important thing since the invention of sliced bread?" The absurdity of the analogy gets people to engage with the real answer.
  5. **S — Singular:** One goal for the whole team to work toward as a unified group. Breaking it into multiple disconnected sub-goals creates mini-teams running in different directions. If breakdown is pragmatically needed, sub-goals must still feed into one overarching goal.

### Sprint Naming Conventions (ab 00:45)
- **What:** Giving Sprints fun, sequential names using a shared theme — cycling through the alphabet across sprints.
- **When to use:** At the start of a new team engagement or when Sprint names feel generic and forgettable.
- **How it works:** Pick a category (animals, movies, adjective+animal combos) and assign names alphabetically. Teams can immediately place any sprint in relative order just from the name.

## Key Principles
- The Sprint backlog is a *tool* for the team to figure out what work is needed to hit the Sprint Goal — it is not the goal itself.
- Fun is not a nice-to-have: it aids memory and brings the goal into everyday team conversation.
- A Sprint Goal that nobody helped craft is a goal nobody owns.
- The "ultimate" question forces teams to articulate business value, not just describe work.
- Pragmatism is allowed on singularity — multiple sub-goals can coexist if they converge on one overarching outcome. Three disjoint goals are a sign of a fragmented team, not a complex sprint.

## Facilitation Tips
- Use the FOCUS letters as a review checklist after a draft Sprint Goal is written — run through each letter and ask whether the goal satisfies it.
- The "sliced bread" framing for the Ultimate question is deliberately silly; Carsten uses it intentionally to lower the threshold for teams to engage with the *why*.
- When introducing sprint naming, let the team pick the theme — ownership of the naming convention increases engagement with it.
- The alphabet sequencing of sprint names is a lightweight, low-overhead way to maintain a shared sense of sprint history without any tooling.

## Best Used When
- Teams are producing Sprint Goals that are task-lists in disguise ("we will build X, Y, Z") rather than outcome statements
- Sprint Goals feel like they belong to the product owner, not the team
- Teams struggle to remember or reference past sprints
- A team is newly forming and needs lightweight rituals to build shared identity
