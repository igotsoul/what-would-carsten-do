# "Circle of Influence" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten introduces the Circle of Influence as a practical mental model for categorizing action items and concerns during retrospectives or other facilitated sessions. The model helps teams develop a shared language around what they can control, influence, or only be concerned about — preventing wasted energy on things outside the team's reach.

## Methods & Techniques

### Circle of Influence (ab 00:20)
- **What:** A three-zone mental model that sorts topics, action items, or concerns into: things you can **control**, things you can **influence** (via others), and things you are merely **concerned** about but cannot act on.
- **When to use:** After exercises like "What? So What? Now What?" or at the end of a retrospective when reviewing a list of action items — especially when team members keep raising topics that are outside their power to change.
- **Group size:** Works for any team size
- **Duration:** Can be used as a short sorting activity within a longer session (a few minutes per item)
- **How it works:**
  1. Present the three concentric zones: Control (innermost), Influence (middle), Concern/Soup (outer)
  2. For each action item or topic, ask the team: "Where would you place this on the circle?"
  3. If something sits in the outer "concern" zone, acknowledge it — then explore whether anyone in the room can move it inward via influence or connection to someone with control
  4. Use it visually (e.g., drawn on a whiteboard or flipchart) to make the sorting tangible

## Key Principles
- The model exists in multiple variations; Carsten uses a three-zone version: **control → influence → concern**
- The outermost zone is sometimes called "the soup" — things that are simply given and cannot realistically be changed
- Venting is valid: teams sometimes just need to express concerns, and this model gives that expression a legitimate place without letting it derail the session
- The primary value is creating a **shared language**: teams become more precise about what they can and cannot act on, reducing frustration and unfocused discussions
- Even items in the "concern" zone are worth naming — it opens a conversation about whether they can be moved inward over time

## Facilitation Tips
- When an action item keeps resurfacing that no one can act on, use the circle to explicitly park it in "concern" rather than ignoring it — this validates the concern while closing the loop
- Make it visual: draw the circles on a board and physically place sticky notes or write items in each zone
- Use the model as a debrief filter after exercises like "What? So What? Now What?" to triage the resulting action list
- Invite the team to challenge placements: "Can anyone influence someone who has control over this?" — this often reveals hidden agency

## Best Used When
- A retrospective produces action items that feel overwhelming or outside the team's control
- Team members are repeatedly raising the same unresolvable complaints
- A team lacks shared language for distinguishing ownership from concern
- You want to redirect energy toward actionable items without dismissing legitimate frustrations
