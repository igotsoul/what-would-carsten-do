# "Cheerleader Rock Paper Scissors" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten Lützen demonstrates Cheerleader Rock Paper Scissors, a high-energy in-person energizer that builds group enthusiasm through a simple elimination tournament. Starting as paired duels, losers become cheerleaders for the winner, creating two large cheering crowds by the final round. It's a quick, inclusive activity that gets people moving, laughing, and genuinely rooting for each other.

## Methods & Techniques

### Cheerleader Rock Paper Scissors (ab 00:30)
- **What:** A Rock Paper Scissors elimination tournament where losers become enthusiastic cheerleaders for the winner, culminating in two large rival crowds cheering for the final two champions.
- **When to use:** At the start of a workshop or meeting to energize a group, break the ice, and create a playful, high-energy atmosphere. Best used when the group needs to loosen up or when energy is low.
- **Group size:** Works best with larger groups (10+), though any size that allows for at least a few rounds works.
- **Duration:** Approximately 5–10 minutes depending on group size.
- **How it works:**
  1. Ask everyone to find a partner.
  2. Briefly explain Rock Paper Scissors rules (even if most know them — it sets the tone).
  3. Pairs play one round. The loser becomes a dedicated cheerleader for the winner.
  4. Winners find other winners and play again, now each accompanied by their personal cheering section.
  5. After each round, the losing group joins the winning side's cheering crowd.
  6. Continue until two large groups remain, each cheering for their champion.
  7. Final round is best of three for extra excitement.
  8. Winner gets a high five (or similar light celebration).

## Key Principles
- The energy of the activity comes from the cheering, not the game itself — Carsten emphasizes that cheering should be loud and genuinely enthusiastic, not polite.
- Keeping the underlying game (Rock Paper Scissors) extremely simple removes any barrier to participation — everyone can play, regardless of background.
- "A bit silly but not too far out" — the activity hits the sweet spot of playful without being uncomfortable or alienating.
- Physical togetherness is a prerequisite; this energizer only works when people are in the same room.

## Facilitation Tips
- Set the expectation for loud, energetic cheering explicitly — Carsten models it himself and emphasizes "really loud, really energetic," not just a polite "yay."
- Wait for each round to fully resolve before giving the next instruction — let the pairs settle before moving on.
- Go through the Rock Paper Scissors rules even if you think everyone knows them; it normalizes participation and buys time for pairs to form.
- Make the final round best of three to build more suspense and crowd investment.
- Keep the winner's reward light (a high five) — the fun is the activity, not the prize.

## Best Used When
- Opening a full-day workshop or off-site where people need to shake off formality.
- The group doesn't know each other well and needs a low-stakes way to interact physically.
- Energy in the room is flat and you need a quick reset.
- You have an in-person group of mixed backgrounds — the simplicity of Rock Paper Scissors makes it universally accessible.
