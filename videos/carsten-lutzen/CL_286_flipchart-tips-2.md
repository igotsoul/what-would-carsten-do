# Flipchart Tips 2

## Summary
Carsten shares six practical tips for working with flipcharts in facilitation settings, covering pen selection, color usage, on-the-fly drawing, and storage. The video is a follow-up to an earlier flipchart tips video and focuses specifically on how to fill a flipchart page effectively. Someone watching will learn concrete, low-effort techniques to make their flipcharts more readable, visually structured, and engaging.

## Methods & Techniques

### Wedge-tip pen as primary marker (ab 00:40)
- **What:** Use a wedge-tip marker as your default flipchart pen — it allows both small and large writing by simply rotating the pen.
- **When to use:** Any time you're writing on a flipchart, especially when you need to vary font size on the same page.
- **How it works:** Hold the pen on the narrow edge for smaller text; rotate to the broad face for larger, more readable text. Keep two identical pens as backup — running out mid-sentence is a real risk.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=E2NUAYO-vGg&t=40

### Round-nip pen for details and arrows (ab 01:30)
- **What:** A secondary pen with a round nib for finer details, small text, and smooth curved arrows.
- **When to use:** When drawing arrows, fine lines, or annotations that need a consistent line thickness — especially curves, which look uneven with a wedge tip.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=E2NUAYO-vGg&t=90

### Three-color system for structure (ab 02:00)
- **What:** Limit yourself to three colors maximum: one for body text, one for structural elements (dividers, arrows, headers), and one for emphasis.
- **When to use:** Any flipchart with more than a few lines of content — helps viewers scan and parse the page without cognitive overload.
- **How it works:** Carsten uses black for text, orange for structural elements, and blue for emphasis. Instead of writing emphasized words in a different color, he sometimes writes in black and underlines with the emphasis color for dramatic effect.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=E2NUAYO-vGg&t=120

### Big marker for color fills and headers (ab 03:30)
- **What:** Use a large marker — ideally matching the structural color — to fill areas of color, underline whole words, or highlight headers quickly.
- **When to use:** When you want to draw visual attention to a specific section without spending time on careful coloring.
- **How it works:** The big marker lets you block-color a word or section in one pass, creating strong visual contrast with minimal effort.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=E2NUAYO-vGg&t=210

### Simple on-the-go illustrations (ab 04:30)
- **What:** Add small, rough figures or doodles directly on the flipchart to inject personality and humor — no artistic skill required.
- **When to use:** When a flipchart feels too dry or text-heavy; also useful to make content more memorable.
- **How it works:** Carsten draws a simple stick-figure-style character looking at the content, giving a thumbs up. He then adds a color detail (e.g., a colored shirt) with the structural color. The bar is low — the point is personality, not precision.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=E2NUAYO-vGg&t=270

### Pre-made flipcharts in a transport canister (ab 06:00)
- **What:** Store and transport pre-prepared flipcharts rolled up in a dedicated canister with a carrying strap.
- **When to use:** When illustrations or titles are too complex to draw on the spot, or when you want reusable flipcharts ready to hang at any venue.
- **How it works:** Carsten keeps reusable flipcharts on his wall at home, rolls them up before a workshop, and carries them in the canister. This separates the "creative work" from the "facilitation work" — so he can focus on the room, not the marker.
- **Source:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=E2NUAYO-vGg&t=360

## Key Principles
- If people can't read it, it doesn't exist — legibility trumps aesthetics.
- Writing large consumes a lot of ink; always have duplicate pens.
- Color serves structure, not decoration — each color should have a consistent role on the page.
- Simple drawings don't need to be good; they just need to add personality and warmth.
- Separate preparation from execution: pre-make complex visuals so you stay present during the workshop.

## Facilitation Tips
- Rotate the wedge-tip pen rather than switching pens when you need to vary text size.
- Underline with a color (rather than writing in it) for a dramatic emphasis effect that still reads cleanly.
- Use your structural color consistently for arrows, dividers, and section markers — visual consistency helps participants navigate the page.
- Don't aim for artistic quality in on-the-go drawings; a rough figure with one color accent is enough.
- Roll pre-made flipcharts and store them upright in a canister — this protects them and makes transport practical.

## Best Used When
- Preparing for in-person workshops where you'll be using physical flipcharts.
- You want to improve the visual quality and readability of your flipchart work without formal design training.
- You facilitate regularly and want reusable, pre-made visual assets.
