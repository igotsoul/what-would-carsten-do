# "Online Treasure Hunt" with Carsten Lützen

## Summary
Carsten shares a remote energizer called the Online Treasure Hunt, credited to his colleague Søren Urenfeldt. Participants race to find specific household items during a video call, turning the home environment — and even kids — into an asset. It's a playful, competitive warm-up that gets people away from their screens and boosts social energy in distributed teams.

## Methods & Techniques

### Online Treasure Hunt (ab 00:20)
- **What:** A competitive energizer where the facilitator prepares a list of everyday household items; participants race to find each item and return to the camera first.
- **When to use:** Remote team meetings, virtual kick-offs, or any online session that needs social energy and a physical break from screens. Especially effective when participants are working from home with family.
- **Group size:** Any; works well in larger groups due to the competitive scoring element.
- **Duration:** Flexible — scales with the number of items (e.g., 9–10 items typically fits in 10–15 minutes).
- **How it works:**
  1. Facilitator prepares a slide deck with each item shown as both an image and text (images help if children are participating).
  2. Reveal one item at a time — e.g., "Find a bottle of ketchup", "Find a book whose title or author contains the letter A", "Find a toothbrush".
  3. Participants run to find the item and return to the camera; first back earns 2 points, second earns 1 point.
  4. After all items are found, tally scores. The winner earns the title (e.g., "Pirate of All Time") and the responsibility to design the next online game.

## Key Principles
- Physical movement during virtual sessions counteracts screen fatigue and lifts energy.
- Embracing the home environment (kids, household chaos) as a feature rather than a distraction strengthens social connection.
- Handing ownership of the next game to the winner creates continuity and engagement beyond a single session.
- Simple scoring mechanics (2 pts / 1 pt) keep competition lighthearted and inclusive.

## Facilitation Tips
- Prepare a presentation with both images and text for each item — images make it more accessible and faster to parse, especially for participants with kids.
- Be creative with item descriptions: mix concrete items ("a pair of jeans") with playful constraints ("a book where the author's name starts with A") to vary difficulty.
- Encourage family involvement explicitly — framing kids as a competitive advantage removes the awkwardness of home life bleeding into work calls.
- Keep the item list unpredictable; avoid items that clearly advantage certain home setups.

## Best Used When
- Opening a remote team session that needs energy and laughter.
- Teams working from home with family around — the format normalizes the home environment.
- Groups that have been together for a while and can handle a playful, competitive format.
- Any moment a facilitator needs to get people physically moving during a virtual call.
